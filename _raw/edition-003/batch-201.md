- title: AI-Driven Computational Approaches for Health Equity and Social Well-Being: A Survey and Perspective Through a Three-Layer Framework
- authors: Leo Wang, Ernie Lin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6761819
- keyword hits: large language model, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) and computational methods are increasingly deployed to address challenges in public health, social welfare, and clinical medicine. However, research efforts across natural language processing, computer vision, causal inference, and health informatics remain largely siloed, with limited cross-pollination of methods, evaluation standards, or equity considerations. This paper presents a survey and perspective that synthesizes recent advances under a unifying three-layer framework: (1) Sensing and Representation, which addresses how signals about health and social conditions are captured from heterogeneous data sources; (2) Analysis and Reasoning, which encompasses the AI and machine learning methods that transform raw data into actionable knowledge; and (3) Intervention and Support, which examines how computational insights are translated into tools that directly serve patients, caregivers, and vulnerable populations. Cutting across all three layers, we foreground health equity as an architectural design principle rather than a post-hoc audit. Drawing on representative case studies spanning social media analysis for domestic violence detection, topic modeling for short texts, health-wealth gradient modeling, causal super-resolution, vision-language models for medical diagnosis, retrieval-augmented generation evaluation, medication adherence informatics, and large language model bias mitigation, we demonstrate how the framework organizes diverse contributions and reveals critical gaps. We conclude with a discussion of open challenges in data governance, regulatory alignment, participatory design, and cross-layer integration, and we propose a set of design principles for building equity-aware AI systems for health and social well-being.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6806067`

- title: Enriching Few-Shot Retrieval for Log Parsing via Structured Data Augmentation
- authors: Huiqi Zou, Yicheng SUN, Jacky Keung, Yishu Li, Xiaoxue Ma
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6806067
- keyword hits: in-context learning, large language model, llm

### abstract

Context: Log parsing transforms raw log messages into structured templates and underpins downstream tasks such as anomaly detection and root-cause analysis. Recent large language model (LLM)-based parsers commonly rely on few-shot in-context learning, where parsing quality depends heavily on the diversity and representativeness of retrieved examples. However, public log datasets often provide limited template coverage, leaving rare or structurally complex templates under-represented in the candidate pool. Objective: This study aims to improve the accuracy and efficiency of few-shot LLM-based log parsing by enriching the retrieval candidate pool with structurally faithful and semantically diverse log variants.Method: We propose a semantic-preserving augmentation framework for few-shot LLM-based log parsing. The framework applies token-level semantic annotation to classify tokens by structural role (core structures, modifiers, fillers, and variable placeholders), guiding the generation of syntactically faithful log variants while preserving template identity. The augmented candidates are further integrated into a cluster-aware parallel parsing workflow to reduce retrieval overhead and accelerate inference without modifying parser internals. Results: Experiments on six datasets with two representative LLM-based parsers, DivLog and LILAC, show that the framework improves template-level parsing quality, particularly on high-diversity datasets with under-represented templates. In addition, the cluster-aware workflow reduces total parsing time by 2.26--5.51x across datasets. Conclusion: These results show that controlled, semantic-aware augmentation is effective for improving both the accuracy and practicality of few-shot LLM-based log parsing.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6766938`

- title: Epistemic Coprocessors and the Emergence of Stable Human-model Dyads: Toward an Architecture of Asymmetric Human-AI Interaction
- authors: Thomas A. Blüm
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6766938
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, prompting

### abstract

Recent generations of Large Language Models have enabled a new form of human–machine interaction: relatively stable epistemic dyads between human actors and probabilistic language systems. This paper introduces the concept of the asymmetric epistemic dyad as a preliminary analytical framework for describing recursive human–model interaction architectures within probabilistic epistemic environments. The paper argues that the decisive transition is not anthropomorphic “machine intelligence,” but the emergence of interaction architectures in which models function as epistemic coprocessors . Within such dyads , meaning integration, normative evaluation, intentional direction, and epistemic responsibility remain located on the human side, while models contribute probabilistic structural processing, recursive reconstruction, semantic variation, and stabilization of extended epistemic operations. The framework distinguishes these interaction architectures from prompting, conventional software augmentation, and earlier models of distributed cognition. The paper does not claim that language models possess consciousness, intentionality, autonomous understanding, or independent epistemic agency. Nor does it claim finalized empirical validation for the proposed framework. The analysis is exploratory, architectural, and conceptual. Its primary contribution lies in defining a conceptual observation and analysis space for investigating long-form recursive human–AI interaction under conditions of sustained epistemic continuity. The document should therefore be understood as an exploratory working framework situated within the broader discourse on human–AI interaction, epistemic infrastructure, and probabilistic cognitive environments.

### abstract

### abstract

Rajendra (2026a, 2026b, 2026c) established that belief-misalignment pathology is a structural failure mode in human-AI hybrid organisations whose two-stage contamination process degrades organisational fitness while internal quality metrics remain high. Those papers treat the organisational boundary as given. This paper introduces the boundary as a governance variable. Drawing on Coase (1937) and Williamson (1975, 1985) alongside the March-Simon-Cohen-March-Olsen tradition, we argue that the firm boundary in the human-AI context is a process topology question: the point in the BPMN process at which alignment governance is applied determines whether belief-misalignment can be detected and corrected at all. We formalise three BPMN topology conditions (flat, sequential gateway, parallel gateway), a role oscillation trichotomy (RPA, assistant, autonomous), a four-component cost model (compute, governance, misalignment opportunity cost, and Stage-2 remediation), and the IT:HR ratio as a governance architecture diagnostic. AI agents are neither opportunistic nor cognitively limited in the relevant senses; their governance problem is architectural rather than behavioural or cognitive, and existing governance instruments calibrated to the first two ontologies cannot detect or correct it. Four propositions are derived and tested against 30-seed Monte Carlo experiments across three topology conditions, three role modes, and three landscape ruggedness levels. All four propositions are supported. The central finding is that governance architecturetopology, role mode, reviewer quality, and probe frequency-is a first-class design variable whose cost consequences are quantifiable, and that unified IT+HR governance consistently outperforms either attribution architecture alone because it implements a two-stage alignment filter that LLM-only evaluation cannot replicate.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6734338`

- title: Individual Bodily Injury Loss Assessment in Auto Insurance: A Group-adaptive Quantile Combination Approach
- authors: Jian Cao, Hailiang Yang, Jing Yao, Yang Yang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6734338
- keyword hits: large language model, text embedding

### abstract

Bodily injury (BI) claims are a major source of tail risk in auto insurance, but individual-level BI loss assessment has received limited attention in the actuarial literature. BI losses are difficult to predict because they are heavy-tailed, heterogeneous across claim types, and strongly influenced by medical information often recorded as unstructured diagnostic text. We propose a group-adaptive quantile combination framework for individual BI loss assessment. The method estimates multiple conditional quantiles of BI losses using gradient-boosted decision trees with the pinball loss and then combines these quantiles using entropy-regularized weights learned separately for outpatient, inpatient, and disability claims. We also incorporate medical diagnostic text through semantic embeddings from a domain-specific Chinese medical large language model. Using 80,426 individual BI claim records from a major Chinese property and casualty insurer, we find that the proposed framework substantially improves out-of-sample performance. Relative to a generalized linear model, it reduces mean squared error by 42.5% and mean absolute error by 28.7%, while increasing the Gini index from 0.677 to 0.747. The gains are especially pronounced for high-severity claims. The learned weights are actuarially interpretable: outpatient claims rely mainly on central quantiles, inpatient claims combine central and upper-tail information, and disability claims place dominant weight on the highest quantile. Ablation analysis shows that medical diagnostic text provides substantial incremental predictive value. The results demonstrate how quantile-based statistical learning, group-adaptive combination, and medical-text embeddings can support case reserving, claims triage, and tail-risk monitoring for BI claims.

## uid: `arxiv:2605.22892v2`

- title: Is TabPFN the Silver Bullet for Insurance Pricing?
- authors: Bruno Deprez, Wouter Verbeke, Tim Verdonck
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22892v2
- keyword hits: foundation model, in-context learning

### abstract

Modelling claim frequency and severity for non-life insurance pricing predominantly relies on generalised linear models, with gradient-boosted machines as the leading machine learning alternative. Tabular foundation models (TFMs) present a fundamentally different inference paradigm. By pre-training on large collections of synthetic datasets, TFMs enable inference on new data through in-context learning, without any dataset-specific fitting or hyperparameter tuning. This paper presents a first empirical evaluation of TabPFN for motor insurance pricing, benchmarking it against GLM and XGBoost on two publicly available MTPL datasets. Our results show that TabPFN does not consistently outperform established baselines, exhibits substantially longer inference times, and is sensitive to the size of the in-context training set. While tabular foundation models represent a promising direction, particularly in data-scarce settings, their current performance does not offer a viable replacement for established actuarial methods.
