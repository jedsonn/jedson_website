## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6614321`

- title: Governing the Agentic Enterprise: Guardrails for Autonomous AI Agents in B2B Financial Transactions
- authors: Syed Muhammad Khuzaima Alam
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6614321
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

The emergence of autonomous ("agentic") artificial intelligence systems capable of independently executing financial decisions represents a fundamental shift in enterprise operations. In B2B financial environments, such agents are increasingly deployed for payments, liquidity management, and transaction optimization. However, their ability to operate without continuous human oversight introduces significant risks, including financial loss, regulatory non-compliance, and systemic instability. Existing AI governance frameworks and financial control systems are not designed for real-time, adaptive, decision-executing agents. This paper proposes FASTRAC (Financial Agent Safety, Trust, Risk, and Compliance), a control-theoretic governance framework that models autonomous financial agents as bounded, risk-scored actors operating within dynamically enforced constraints. FASTRAC introduces a formal governance function G(A) = {C, V, R, M, L} integrating constraints, validation, risk scoring, monitoring, and learning into a closedloop system. By defining execution as a constrained optimization problem under risk thresholds, the framework ensures that agent actions remain compliant, auditable, and controllable. Through formal modeling and a B2B payment execution scenario, this study demonstrates how adaptive governance mechanisms-such as dynamic risk thresholds and trust-calibrated autonomy-can mitigate risks while preserving operational efficiency. The findings suggest that effective deployment of agentic systems in finance requires a hybrid governance paradigm combining AI intelligence with deterministic control structures.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6716257`

- title: Leveraging Generative AI in the Development, Adaptation, and Evaluation of EAP
- authors: Maimoona Sayeed
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6716257
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

This study explores teacher and student perspectives on the use of Generative AI in the development, adaptation, and evaluation of English for Academic Purposes (EAP) materials. Using a mixed-method, exploratory design, data were collected through questionnaires, interviews, focus groups, and analysis of AI-generated materials. The findings reveal that while both teachers and students recognise the efficiency and accessibility of AI tools such as ChatGPT, their perceptions differ significantly. Teachers emphasised accuracy, pedagogical value, and ethical concerns, whereas students prioritise usability and convenience. AI was found to enhance engagement and support material development but also posed risks of over-reliance and reduced critical engagement. The study highlights the need for structured integration, teacher training, and digital literacy development. It concludes that AI can be an effective support tool when balanced with human judgment, promoting meaningful learning while maintaining academic integrity and learner autonomy.

## uid: `arxiv:2605.03310v1`

- title: Coordination as an Architectural Layer for LLM-Based Multi-Agent Systems
- authors: Maksym Nechepurenko, Pavel Shuvalov
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03310v1
- keyword hits: claude, llm

### abstract

Multi-agent LLM systems fail in production at rates between 41% and 87%, mostly due to coordination defects rather than base-model capability. Existing responses split between cataloguing failure modes empirically and shipping declarative orchestration frameworks as engineering tools; neither delivers a principled mapping from coordination configuration to predictable failure-mode signature. We argue that coordination should be treated as a configurable architectural layer, separable from agent logic and from information access, enabling architectural reasoning rather than only engineering productivity. We instantiate this with an information-controlled design on prediction markets: a single LLM, fixed tools, fixed per-call output cap, and fixed prompt template across five reference coordination configurations, with total compute per question treated as an endogenous architectural output. The Murphy decomposition of the Brier score separates calibration from discriminative power, so configurations leave distinguishable signatures even when aggregate scores coincide. On 100 Polymarket binary markets resolved after the model's training cutoff (claude-opus-4-6) we report Murphy signatures, a cost-quality Pareto frontier, category-conditioned analysis, and a bootstrap power-projection. Three of five pre-specified predictions are upheld in direction; two configurations dominate the Pareto frontier within this regime; exploratory bootstrap intervals separate consensus alignment from others, though pairwise tests do not survive Bonferroni correction at n=100. We also deploy the same configurations as live agents on Foresight Arena under web-search-enabled conditions, as an on-chain replication channel accumulating in parallel. Harness, trace dataset, and production agents are released. We position this as a methodology-validating first instantiation, not a general cross-model claim.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6722712`

- title: Ideology-Driven Specification Selection in Applied Econometrics
- authors: Fabio Motoki, Valdemar Pinho Neto, Victor Rangel
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6722712
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

We use 600 large language model agents as synthetic analysts across four applied economics papers. We organise agents into balanced cells of 50 runs per ideology (left, neutral, right) and introduce the persona through an identity-framing clause in an otherwise identical system prompt. Each agent chooses from a pre-enumerated menu of defensible specifications and retrieves coefficients from a frozen database, so the only margin of variation is selection among legitimate specifications. In every paper, ideology shifts which specifications agents select, and a variance decomposition identifies the analytical dimension on which agents concentrate their choices. The mean of the chosen estimates orders in the predicted direction in all four papers, although the shift ranges from clean ordered separation in the voter identification paper to a near-null gap in the DACA paper. Ideology also shapes how agents summarise a given estimate, a margin that operates even when the coefficient gap is small.

- title: The Human Data Collective: A Standard for Open, Sustainable, and Equitable Artificial Intelligence With Transparency and Compensation Optionality for Large Language Model Inference
- authors: Austin Harshberger
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6639918
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Several of the biggest challenges plaguing the development and utilization of large language models in 2026, notably bias, the difficulty of monitoring undesirable and illegal content, the psychological safety risks associated with human-led alignment, and severe environmental impact, can be partially or completely mitigated through the careful curation of high-quality human-generated content paired with fair incentives. This paper offers an alternative to the existing paradigm of extraction and privatization by proposing a decentralized network of human-generated content created, curated, and labeled specifically for the purpose of training large language models. By introducing a new inference standard rooted in cryptographically verifiable tokens tied to environmental impact, those who do the creating, curating, and labeling can be fairly compensated, the carbon and water footprint of large language models can be addressed at compute time, and the artificial intelligence industry can be returned to its original mission of open access and equality for all while preserving productive free-market competition.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6639820`

- title: Between Tool and Crutch: AI, Student Assessment, and the Crisis of Deep Learning in UK Higher Education
- authors: Vaishnavi Bhavsar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6639820
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid proliferation of generative artificial intelligence (AI) tools-most notably OpenAI's ChatGPT-has fundamentally disrupted the landscape of student assessment in UK higher education. Drawing on both empirical literature and the author's reflective practitioner experience as a Lecturer and Module Leader at a London-based higher education institution, this paper examines the dual nature of AI in academic contexts: its genuine pedagogical potential as a tool for personalised learning, formative feedback, and academic skill development, and its growing misuse as a mechanism for bypassing authentic intellectual engagement. The paper argues that the central challenge facing higher education is not AI itself, but the conditions-motivational, structural, and institutionalthat drive students to use AI as a substitute for learning rather than a supplement to it. Critically engaging with the concepts of cognitive offloading (Gerlich, 2025), cognitive debt (Kosmyna et al., 2025), and AI literacy (Ng et al., 2021), the paper proposes a balanced pedagogical framework that neither prohibits AI nor permits its uncritical use, but instead redesigns assessment and teaching practice to cultivate AI-literate, critically engaged learners. The paper concludes with practical recommendations for educators, curriculum designers, and institutional policymakers in the UK higher education context.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6726105`

- title: A Five-Domain Research Agenda for AI-Augmented Legal Practice: Translating the Anthropic Architect Framework
- authors: Thomas Potter
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6726105
- keyword hits: claude

### abstract

Contemporary legal AI commentary is fragmented across four genres. Bar association ethics opinions articulate duties without specifying architecture. Vendor marketing describes products and integration surfaces. Practitioner case studies report local successes that do not generalize to a framework other firms can adopt. The academic computer science tradition formalizes legal rules in code without addressing how legal practice should consume probabilistic AI systems. No single genre supplies the architectural vocabulary the field needs. The Anthropic Claude Certified Architect Foundations curriculum, released in early 2026 as the first comprehensive professional certification Anthropic has issued for production AI system design, can serve as a useful scaffold for the missing framework. Law is well suited to this translation because law and code share the structural properties of intentional, structured language. An existing line of computational law scholarship has run from law to code, representing legal rules as executable programs. This paper proposes the inverse direction: applying production AI architecture frameworks to legal practice operations. The contribution is the systematic translation, not the underlying analogy. The paper anchors the project in existing computational law scholarship, surveys the contemporary legal AI commentary that has begun engaging individual concepts, maps the five Architect domains to legal practice with sample translations drawn from production deployments, and identifies a research agenda of open questions. Detailed development of each subcategory is forthcoming in companion working documents. The framework also carries implications for professional competency standards in AI-augmented legal practice, including law school curriculum design and bar examination content.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6723042`

- title: Earth Observation and Global Scale Modelling Tools Meeting Township Needs: Community Resilience Framework Incorporating Weather Prediction, Flood Forecasting, Inundation Mapping and Ground Motion Detection
- authors: Bashudev Neupane, Bamikole Akinsehinde, Manmeet Singh, Amrit  Prasad Sharma, Chengye Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6723042
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Flood-prone communities require integrated tools to assess environmental risks, infrastructure exposure, and socio-economic vulnerability. In this interdisciplinary study, considering Palmer Township, Pennsylvania, as a case, we present a comprehensive ‘risk mapping framework’ combining physically consistent, locally referenced hydroclimatic conditions, climate-scale wet- and dry-season information, land use landcover and geological datasets, hydrologic simulation, insights from earth observation, and vulnerability modelling to map current and future risks from flooding and land movement. The framework links the risk matrix to community and infrastructure resilience attributes, strengthening the decision-support system for planners and resilience actors to develop effective flood mitigation strategies. During the development of an auditable casebook-based workflow, the comparison against ECMWF Reanalysis v5 (ERA5): an internally consistent reference product, CMIP6 EC-Earth3 daily native-grid precipitation showed weak correspondence with same-day township-scale totals but retained useful information about broader multi-day wet and dry regimes, with interpretation improving under a ±1-day phase tolerance and across 3- to 7-day accumulation windows. A fuzzy clustering-based feature-ranking procedure identified convective rain rate, mean total precipitation rate, total cloud cover, evaporation, and top-layer soil moisture as the most informative ERA5 predictors for local rainfall variability. Taken together, results suggest that CMIP6 is more appropriate here as a source of climate-scale regime context than as a day-ahead township rainfall predictor. While the contribution of ERA5-based hydroclimatic diagnostics and CMIP6 regime signals remained methodological, future flood scenarios are simulated using HEC-RAS-based hydraulic modelling to assess inundation extents. Additionally, Sentinel-1A SAR imagery is used for flood inundation mapping using backscatter thresholding and change-detection techniques. Using Dynamic World land cover data, including water mask layers, and generative AI-based downscaling, we analyse water body expansion and validate SAR-derived flood extents across flood events. Based on the extent of inundation, risk zones are delineated, and the risk interpretations are performed regarding land cover classes. Sentinel-1-based OPERA product reveals land surface displacements ranging from –10 mm/year to +2.8 mm/year, particularly along the Bushkill Creek corridor, highlighting areas of potential concern for infrastructure stability.Results demonstrate that disaster risk mapping is most effective when interdisciplinary science, spanning climate modelling, hydrology, remote sensing, and community analysis, aligns with local relevance and supports anticipatory governance and disaster resilience at the municipal level.
