## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7134678`

- title: RP-LLM: A Semantically Conditioned Cross-Modal Large Language Model Framework for Remaining Useful Life Prediction
- authors: Zimeng Fan, Lei Song, Naipeng Li, Yaguo Lei, Hongfei Wang, Xuzhi Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7134678
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Remaining useful life (RUL) prediction supports maintenance decision-making and risk control in industrial systems, but its performance is often limited by few-shot and cross-domain scenarios. Recent progress in large language models (LLMs) offers a flexible interface for instruction generation and contextual modeling. However, current LLM-based studies for RUL prediction either use large models as powerful regressors or rely on textualized signal descriptions, while the intermediate organization of degradation states before value generation remains insufficiently explored. To address this issue, this paper develops RP-LLM, a cross-modal large language model framework with health-state semantic conditioning for multivariate time series RUL prediction. RP-LLM first learns degradation representations through contrastive pretraining between time series windows and structured textual descriptions. It then maps these representations to an ordered health prototype space encoded by text, where the resulting membership distribution serves as a semantic bridge between temporal patterns and value generation. Finally, a Prompt Learner converts the prototype distribution into soft prompts, and an LLM adapted with LoRA generates structured RUL responses under a numeric consistency objective. The framework is evaluated under few-shot and cross-domain transfer protocols on public battery and aero-engine datasets, together with a strict zero-shot case study on a private on-orbit cryocooler trajectory. Across the evaluated protocols, it achieves up to 27.1% lower prediction error while supporting natural-language RUL queries and structured textual prediction beyond conventional scalar regression.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7071718`

- title: Generative AI Mania and Macro Vulnerability: Asymmetric Volatility Dynamics of Tech Giants via EGARCH-X (2023-2026)
- authors: Fuli Yang, Shiyu Lin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7071718
- keyword hits: deepseek, generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The period from January 2023 to July 2026 witnessed an unprecedented surge in investor enthusiasm surrounding generative artificial intelligence (AI), propelling technology-sector equities-most notably NVIDIA Corporation (NVDA)-to extraordinary cumulative excess returns. This paper examines whether the AI innovation narrative confers immunity to macroeconomic systemic risk transmission. Using 866 daily observations, we estimate Exponential GARCH models augmented with exogenous macro-stress variables (EGARCH-X), incorporating the ICE BofA MOVE Index as a proxy for sovereign bond market dislocation. Our cross-sectional analysis covers five assets: NVDA, AMD, MSFT, GOOGL, and the S&P; 500 (SPY). Three findings emerge. First, the leverage effect is monotonically weaker as assets move closer to the AI narrative core: SPY exhibits the strongest leverage (γ =-0.198, p < 0.001), while AMD shows no significant asymmetry (γ = +0.028, n.s.). This cross-sectional gradient is consistent with a behavioral hypothesis: momentum-driven buy-the-dip dynamics suppress the institutional fear-leverage channel in high-narrative assets. Second, NVDA's ARCH coefficient (α = 0.142) substantially exceeds SPY's (0.088), confirming greater sensitivity to information shocks. Third, a structural break analysis centered on the January 27, 2025 DeepSeek announcement reveals a fundamental regime shift in NVDA's volatility dynamics, while SPY parameters remain stable-evidence of AI-specific structural fragility.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6991379`

- title: Foundation Models For 3D Point Cloud Understanding: A Survey
- authors: Jing Du, John Zelek, Dening Lu, Kyle Gao, Qiming Xia, Ting Han, Jingxiang Li, Quanyun Wu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6991379
- keyword hits: foundation model, large language model, llm

### abstract

Research on foundation models (FMs) has changed 3D point cloud understanding from task-specific 3D backbones into a compositional design space in which pretrained vision, language, promptable, generative, and multimodal models enter 3D pipelines through different routes. Existing surveys tend to follow a single task family, foundation-model family, or data regime, making it difficult to compare why a rendering method based on Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP), a Segment Anything Model (SAM)-lifted mask pipeline, a native 3D language encoder, and a 3D large language model (LLM) fail in different ways. This survey organizes the literature on foundation models for 3D point cloud understanding using a two-axis taxonomy: the role of the foundation model in each method and the route by which 3D information is connected to foundation-model knowledge. The review synthesizes a curated corpus that includes 204 method-level works assigned to the resulting role-access matrix, together with additional substrate, benchmark, boundary, and outside-grid works used as contextual evidence. This taxonomy supports synthesis across indoor RGB plus depth (RGB-D), driving light detection and ranging (LiDAR), aerial, mobile, and airborne point clouds, and broader geospatial settings. Progress remains uneven across the corpus: many gains still depend on 2D image-language supervision, pre-foundation-model benchmarks, projection or distillation bottlenecks, and incomplete outdoor, temporal, and geospatial coverage. The survey closes with a capability-gap agenda built on five coupled programs: 3D-native geometric foundation models, physically grounded multi-FM composition, temporal and geospatial world models, reliable 3D-language systems with traceable evidence, and open-world task-compositional evaluation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6985126`

- title: Structuring Product Management Theory for AI Reasoning: A Knowledge Graph Schema and Compilation Pattern for Domain-specific Intelligence
- authors: Talal Ahmad Gondal
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6985126
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Large language models are now routinely used as decision-support tools in product management, but their reasoning over domain knowledge is only as good as the format that knowledge arrives in, and that format is almost always unstructured prose. The dominant workaround, retrievalaugmented generation (RAG), slices text into arbitrary chunks and, in doing so, discards the very thing that makes a product recommendation useful: the relationships among constructs, frameworks, and lifecycle phases. This paper sets out a formal ontology and knowledge graph schema for encoding product management theory as machine-actionable knowledge that keeps those relationships intact through retrieval. The schema defines four entity types (Concepts, Frameworks, Artifacts, Metrics), a set of directed relations (phase_requires, mechanism_produces, construct_extends, among others), and provenance links back to source material. Alongside it I describe a compilation pattern, the LLM-Wiki approach, which turns linear scholarly prose into interlinked, structured pages that a model can actually reason across. My claim is narrow but, I think, consequential: domain-specific AI needs structured knowledge representations, accumulated institutional memory, and outcome feedback loops if it is to give phase-aware, context-sensitive advice, and current prompt-based tools have none of the three. Though the worked example here is product management, the argument travels to any domain where AI reasoning has to be grounded in accumulated organizational knowledge.

- affiliations: not stated

- source: SSRN

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) have recently demonstrated strong capabilities in reasoning and tool use, opening new opportunities for AI-assisted scientific discovery. However, applying LLM-based agents to drug discovery remains challenging because they must integrate heterogeneous biomedical data, coordinate domain-specific computational tools, and maintain consistent reasoning across multi-step research workflows. In this work, we present DrugPilot, a parameterized reasoning agent designed to support structured drug discovery workflows. DrugPilot introduces a parameterized memory architecture that transforms heterogeneous biomedical information from public databases and user inputs into standardized representations, enabling consistent reasoning and tool coordination across iterative research steps. DrugPilot supports multi-stage scientific interactions while preserving contextual knowledge during complex decision processes. To facilitate systematic evaluation, we construct a drug discovery instruction dataset spanning eight representative tasks in computational drug research. Experimental results demonstrate that DrugPilot achieves strong performance, outperforming existing widely used agent frameworks across simple, multi-tool, and multi-turn scenarios. These results suggest that structured parameterized reasoning architectures can substantially improve the effectiveness of LLM-based agents in complex scientific workflows.

Large language models (LLMs) offer new opportunities for intelligent decision support in logistics network modeling and optimization, yet existing studies mainly treat them as algorithmic assistants, direct optimizers, or end-to-end generators. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7138662`

- title: Robustness of Information Retrieval Evaluation Conclusions underPerturbations of Relevance Judgments: Implications forLLM-as-a-Judge
- authors: Imane belahayne, Abdelmalek tamim, sanaa aidi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7138662
- keyword hits: gpt-4, large language model, llama, llm

### abstract

Evaluation of Information Retrieval (IR) systems, increasingly conducted with large language model (LLM) judges, rests on the assumption that small disagreements in relevance judgments do not alter ranking conclusions. This paper tests that assumption directly. We combine statistical power and equivalence analysis, calibration against real TREC Deep Learning (DL) ranking distributions, a team-independence correction, and a judgment-level perturbation mechanism grounded in the Graded Response Model (GRM) applied to 9,260 real human relevance judgments from TREC DL 2019 (43 topics), to quantify when a perturbation of relevance judgments changes which system is ranked best. We find that a perturbation of odds ratio (OR = 1.30) – where OR quantifies the multiplicative shift in the probability of a higher relevance grade assigned by a biased judge, with OR = 1 denoting the absence of bias – a magnitude our design can neither statistically detect nor exclude with confidence, produces ranking-reversal rates of 8.3% (NDCG@10) and 8.0% (MRR) under tight-gap conditions, rising to 54.7% (NDCG@10) at OR = 1.86; MAP is substantially less sensitive (≤ 2% across the same range) owing to its coarser binary relevance formulation. An empirical validation with two real LLM judges – GPT-4o (𝑛 = 1,200; weighted Cohen’s 𝜅 = 0.545) and Llama 3.3 70B (𝑛 = 656; 𝜅 = 0.516) – shows moderate agreement with human assessors (bootstrap 95% confidence intervals overlap; Δ𝜅 = 0.029 is not statistically significant), with a three-way Krippendorff 𝛼 = 0.594 on the shared subset (𝑛 = 323), placing currently deployed judges inside the agreement regime where such undetectable perturbations are plausible and consequential. These findings suggest that IR evaluation campaigns relying on LLM judges should routinely report ranking-reversal sensitivity alongside point-estimate performance differences, particularly when adjacent system scores differ by less than 0.01 NDCG@10.
