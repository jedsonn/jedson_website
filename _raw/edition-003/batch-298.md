## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6838662`

- title: Natural Language Input, Semantic Track Representation, and LLM Inference: Making the Maritime Information Exchange Model Tractable
- authors: Rick Roth
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6838662
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

We describe a practical architecture for making the Maritime Information Exchange Model (MIEM) and the broader Rich Semantic Track model tractable using current large language model (LLM) technology. The barrier to adoption of semantic track models in defense and law enforcement has been the requirement that operators learn formal ontology languages and manually encode observations as typed logical assertions. We propose eliminating this barrier entirely: operators contribute observations in natural language; an LLM translates these into typed Semantic Assertion Records (SARs), which are named case frames that capture n-ary relations in a single compact structure; a knowledge graph accumulates the SARs; and a second LLM pass performs inference, anomaly detection, and hypothesis ranking over the graph. We work through two detailed examples (a 9/11-era pre-attack indicator scenario and a maritime cargo inspection scenario) showing the full pipeline from natural language input to SAR representation to inference output. We argue that this architecture makes the Track Model and MIEM immediately deployable with current technology, establishes prior art against proprietary enclosure of the approach, and grounds the method in a theoretical framework connecting semantic track representations to neural manifold geometry.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6802961`

- title: The Crisis of Political Visibility in the AI Age AI Infrastructure and the Hollowing-out of Democratic Visibility
- authors: Jimmy Chow
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6802961
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

This paper introduces political visibility as a foundational concept for democratic theory in the AI age, and argues that the progressive migration of political decisions into AI infrastructure constitutes a historically distinctive mechanism of democratic hollowing-out. Political visibility is defined as the condition in which the decisions that constitute collective governance occur within a public domain that is identifiable, contestable, participable, and accountable. These four dimensions are not procedural niceties but necessary conditions of democratic legitimacy: the absence of any single dimension is sufficient to produce a visibility deficit that no institutional form of democracy can compensate. The paper distinguishes political visibility from the established tradition of the "politics of visibility"-which concerns who is seen within politics and argues that the present crisis concerns whether politics itself remains visible at all. The central argument is that AI infrastructure systems-foundation models, recommendation architectures, RLHF alignment procedures, and government AI procurement-constitute a new mechanism of the infrastructuralisation of politics: the migration of substantive political decisions from visible democratic governance into a technical layer that is structurally beyond the reach of democratic procedure. Drawing on the infrastructure studies tradition (Bowker and Star; Larkin; Plantin et al.), the paper identifies how this mechanism operates, situates it within the established hollowing-out thesis (Mair; Crouch; Wolin; Brown; Urbinati), and identifies what is historically distinctive about its AI-age instantiation. The paper closes by positioning political visibility as the first principle from which AI-era democratic governance must be reconstructed, and previews a broader research programme in AI-era state theory.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6842561`

- title: Trust Experience Glitches: AI Agents, Delegated Autonomy, and the Breakdown of Legal Assumptions
- authors: Helena Rong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6842561
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

Artificial intelligence is shifting from a technology of output generation to a technology of delegated action. Beyond answering queries in a chat window, contemporary AI agents can call APIs, manage credentials, write and deploy code, move money, negotiate with other agents, and act across digital environments autonomously with limited human supervision. This shift unsettles legal frameworks that assume clear delegation, identifiable actors, traceable causation, revocable authority, and enforceable defendants. This paper introduces the concept of the trust experience glitch: a discrete rupture in a stakeholder's reasonable trust expectations at the moment an autonomous AI system acts. A trust experience glitch is not merely a bug, hallucination, or AI incident, but rather a relational failure that exposes a mismatch between the trust signals emitted by a system or platform and the behavior stakeholders actually experience. Drawing on documented agentic AI failures, this paper develops a six-part taxonomy of trust experience glitches: (1) agency assertion, (2) epistemic camouflage, (3) delegation ambiguity, (4) opacity at scale, (5) cascading autonomy, and (6) revocation failure. It then maps these failure modes onto legal assumptions under strain: authority, veracity, causation, consent, and enforceability. This paper argues that agentic AI does not simply create new liability risks, but reveals a deeper mismatch between ex-post legal remedies and systems whose harmful actions may be distributed, opaque, emergent, or difficult to stop. The appropriate governance response must therefore move upstream, from after-the-fact attribution to protocol-level constraints. It concludes by pointing to a set of protocol primitives as the "traffic lights" of the agentic web.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6801178`

- title: The Oracle Problem in Autonomous Agent Commerce: Why Semantic Truth Verification Is Computationally Intractable and What to Build Instead
- authors: René Dechamps Otamendi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6801178
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Autonomous agent commerce — where software agents hire, pay, and evaluate other agents at micropayment scale — creates a verification problem that existing approaches cannot solve. When Agent A pays Agent B $0.01 for a translation, who determines whether the translation is actually good? Human review is economically impossible. A central LLM evaluator is non-deterministic, non-reproducible, and empirically unreliable on ambiguous cases. The problem is not engineering — it is epistemological. Tarski (1936) proved that truth in a formal system cannot be defined within that system. Gödel (1931) proved that any consistent system contains true statements it cannot prove. Every content moderation system that has attempted automated truth verification confirms the theory: precision drops below 60% on context-dependent content. This paper argues that the correct response to the Oracle Problem in agent commerce is not better computation but better incentives. We propose a two-layer architecture: (1) deterministic validators that verify contract compliance — postconditions in the sense of Hoare (1969) and Meyer (1992) — handling the cases with zero ambiguity; and (2) Quality Markets, a competitive market of verification agents with reputational stake, grounded in prediction market theory (Wolfers & Zitzewitz, 2004), peer prediction (Miller et al., 2005), and the economics of information asymmetry (Akerlof, 1970). The design separates what can be verified mechanically from what requires judgment, and delegates judgment to economic competition rather than algorithmic authority. We analyze the mechanism's incentive properties, identify its limitations, and situate it within the broader Oracle Problem literature from philosophy, computer science, and decentralized finance.

- title: The Apprenticeship Externality: Generative AI, Entry-Level Work, and the Future Supply of Expertise
- authors: Mustafa Seref Akin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6809082

### abstract

Task-based models of automation typically assume that human comparative advantage is replenished through the creation of new labor-intensive tasks. This paper argues that generative AI may weaken that assumption by automating the entry-level tasks through which workers acquire the expertise needed to perform future complex tasks. We call this the apprenticeship externality: firms choosing to automate junior tasks do not internalise the social cost of eliminating the learning pathway that produces tomorrow's senior experts, generating systematic overautomation relative to the social optimum. We formalise this in a two-period overlappinggenerations model with heterogeneous junior tasks, each characterised by a net private automation gain g(i) and a task-specific learning value ℓ(i). The decentralised firm automates task i whenever g(i) > 0; the social planner automates only when g(i) exceeds the discounted learning cost βℓ(i)MP_H. The over-automation region 𝓞 = {i : 0 0): expert wages rise while junior wages fall. When AI substitutes for expert deliverables directly (γ ≤ 0), expert wages need not rise, but the pipeline consequence holds regardless. In the long run, absent alternative formation channels, the expert stock decays geometrically. The welfare loss equals the discounted value of human capital destroyed on tasks in 𝓞. Policy implications include apprenticeship subsidies, tax credits for preserving human training tasks, and protected junior task rotations in AI-exposed occupations.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6867318`

- title: The Silicon Strain: A Structured Review of Supply Chain Resilience Frameworks for AI Infrastructure Constraints
- authors: Snehaseel Naidu Anugonda
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6867318
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The accelerating deployment of Generative Artificial Intelligence has produced unprecedented demand for specialised compute hardware, imposing systemic stress on global semiconductor supply chains, municipal power grids, and data-centre thermal management infrastructure. This paper presents a structured review of four primary structural constraints—GPU and high-bandwidth memory (HBM) scarcity, energy-grid strain, thermal density limitations, and accelerated capital obsolescence—drawing on peer-reviewed literature and empirically grounded industry analyses published between 2019 and 2026. GPU thermal design power has risen fourfold from 300 W to 1,200 W between 2017 and 2024, with roadmaps projecting values exceeding 2,000 W, rendering legacy air-cooling systems structurally inadequate. U.S. data-centre electricity consumption reached 183 TWh in 2024 and is projected to reach 426 TWh by 2030, creating grid-interconnection queue backlogs exceeding 36 months in high-density technology regions. Against this empirical backdrop, the paper proposes a six-vector integrated resilience framework encompassing hardware diversification, on-site microgrid deployment, direct-to-chip liquid cooling, edge-computing decentralisation, AI-driven predictive procurement, and circular economy integration. A stylised cost-benefit case study for a 20 MW AI data centre demonstrates a base-case payback period of 3.8 years on the microgrid infrastructure premium. A parametric sensitivity analysis across grid electricity rates, power usage effectiveness, and interconnection queue delay scenarios shows positive total net outcomes in all cases when queue-avoidance revenue benefits are included, confirming queue avoidance as the dominant value driver. The paper contributes an empirically grounded and practically actionable roadmap for organisations navigating hardware-constrained AI deployment environments.
