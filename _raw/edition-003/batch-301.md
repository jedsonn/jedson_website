## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6879250`

- title: AI Fairness in ESP Assessment: GPT-Generated Writing and L2 Learner Discourse
- authors: Shin Yu Kyoung, Isaiah Yoo, Koo Myoung-Wan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6879250
- keyword hits: generative ai, gpt-4

### abstract

As generative AI becomes increasingly integrated into assessment practices, concerns about fairness – particularly bias toward dominant data sources – have become more prominent. In educational settings, these concerns are especially pressing in English for Specific Purposes (ESP) assessment, where validity depends on the authentic representation of learner discourse and context-specific language use. Learners of English as a foreign language may therefore be disadvantaged by models primarily trained on native-speaker data. This study examines how a GPT-4 based model represents novice academic writing across L1 and L2 contexts. Using essays written by L1 and L2 university students on identical prompts, we compare AI- and human-written texts to assess whether the model authentically reflects features of both groups, including formulaic language. Results show that while the model effectively replicates L1 writing, it only partially reflects L2-specific traits such as learner language and characteristic errors, as well as developmental patterns in lexical bundle use, revealing limitations in the representation of nonnative writing. Given the role of ESP assessment in high-stakes educational decisions, ensuring fair representation of L2 writing is essential for valid and equitable evaluation. This study highlights the need for more learner-sensitive and developmentally representative generative AI in ESP assessment contexts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6866865`

- title: Governing the Scam Ecosystem: Policy Responses to Generative AI-Enabled Deepfake Financial Fraud
- authors: Alice E. Marwick, Schiffrin Anya, Kaylee Williams
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6866865
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

AI-enabled scams represent one of the most consequential and underregulated harms of the generative AI era. This paper examines how generative AI, and deepfakes in particular, have transformed fraud into a globalized, industrialized threat, and evaluates the adequacy of existing regulatory responses across platforms, telecommunications, and financial systems. Drawing on a comparative analysis of policy frameworks from more than a dozen jurisdictions, we find that current approaches are fragmented, reactive, and structurally misaligned with the transnational, multi-actor nature of the scam pipeline. Individual-responsibility models have consistently failed; shared liability frameworks, mandatory data-sharing requirements, and cross-border enforcement remain underdeveloped. We argue that effective governance requires a shift toward institutional accountability, anchored by the principle of the “cheapest cost avoider”: social platforms, as the primary infrastructure through which scammers reach victims at scale, are best positioned to prevent harm and should bear commensurate liability when they do not. Where platforms fail to act, financial institutions represent the next critical intervention point. AI-enabled scams are not merely a consumer protection problem but a stress test of AI governance architecture, one that current frameworks are failing.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6876395`

- title: Beyond random splits: Temporal robustness of vision foundation models for lettuce nutritional diagnosis
- authors: Misael Hernández-Sandoval, Sebastian Salazar-Colores, Alfonso Ramírez-Pedraza, Geminiano Martinez-Ponce, Juvenal Rodríguez, Luis Valentín-Coronado
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6876395
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Lettuce (Lactuca sativa L.) is one of the most widely cultivated leafy vegetables worldwide, with growing adoption in greenhouse hydroponic systems where precise nutrient management is essential. Crop production in these controlled environments is highly sensitive to nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium deficiencies. The resulting foliar symptoms, such as chlorosis, necrosis, and morphological alterations, evolve alongside the growth cycle, making early detection vital for optimized crop management. Yet, traditional diagnosis relies on specialized visual expertise or destructive chemical analyses, both of which are costly and incompatible with continuous monitoring. RGB-image classification with deep learning has emerged as a non-invasive alternative, with Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)-based studies reporting accuracies above 96 %. However, these figures come from random-split protocols that may place near-identical images of the same plant in both training and test sets, leaving open the question of whether such models generalize to later phenological stages unseen during training. We address this question with twocontributions. First, we introduce a chronological-split protocol that assigns early growth-stage images to training and late-stage images to testing, quantifying the temporal robustness that random splits fail to measure. Second, we introduce LettuceDeF, a public dataset of 7,297 RGB images spanning four cultivars and seven diagnostic classes, with ground-truth labels validated by Inductively Coupled Plasma Optical Emission Spectroscopy (ICP-OES). Using this protocol and dataset, we compare four vision foundation models (DINOv3, EVA-02, SigLIP, and BEiT-3) against nine representative CNNs from the literature. Each of the 26 model–protocol combinations is trained with 12 independent seeds, and comparisons use paired t-tests with Bonferroni correction. Under the random split, the three best vFMs and ConvNeXt Base were statistically indistinguishable (F1-macro ≈ 0.997). The chronological split broke this parity: EVA-02 led with F1 = 0.928 and the lowest degradation (7.03 percentage points, pp), while the nine CNNs degraded by 14.26 pp on average—1.6× more than the top three vFMs (8.71 pp). EfficientNet-B3 was the notable exception, degrading only 9.55 pp and rivalling SigLIP and DINOv3. The chronological split provides a more realistic estimate of deployment performance and reveals robustness differences between model families that random splits cannot detect.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6879985`

- title: Selective Disclosure in Online Knowledge Communities: The Impact of Enterprise AI on Public Information Externalization
- authors: Mengke Li, Ziqiong Zhang, Rob Law, Zili Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6879985
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence is changing how users externalize their information needs by allowing them to obtain solutions through private AI-assisted interactions. This study explores the impact of enterprise AI on users’ question-posting activity and the composition of questions that remain visible in public knowledge communities. Building on the cost–benefit logic embedded in information foraging theory, we conceptualize enterprise AI as an alternative information source that can alter users’ public information externalization motivations. By using the introduction of OverflowAI in Stack Overflow for Teams as a quasi-experimental setting and user–week panel data, we employ a difference-in-differences design to assess the impact of enterprise AI on users’ question-posting behavior in the public Stack Overflow community. Empirical results indicate that the introduction of OverflowAI is associated with a decrease in the volume of questions and downstream response outcomes in the public knowledge community. The questions that are posted publicly are highly information rich and linguistically elaborated. The reduction can be seen in the low-scored and less elaborated questions and is highly pronounced among the users with considerable community experience and a good reputation. This study contributes to the literature on online knowledge communities and enterprise AI by emphasizing that the introduction of enterprise AI can change people’s public information externalization, instead of simply decreasing their participation. The findings have implications for managers of online knowledge communities and organizations that adopt enterprise AI.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6876912`

- title: SCARA: A Semantics-Constrained Autonomous Remediation Agent for Opaque Industrial Software Vulnerabilities
- authors: Bowei ning, Xuejun Zong, Dong Li, Lian Lian, Kan He, Yifei Sun, Jinyang Liu, Plamen Vasilev
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6876912
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Under the EU NIS2 Directive and IEC~62443, critical-infrastructure operators must remediate vulnerabilities in deployed industrial software, yet much of it is delivered as opaque industrial software (OIS): stripped firmware, proprietary protocol handlers, and compiled control logic without source, debug symbols, build environments, or hardware interfaces. Every existing automated vulnerability repair system requires a prerequisite OIS cannot satisfy, leaving a gap between binary-level discovery and validated remediation. This paper presents SCARA, a Semantics-Constrained Autonomous Remediation Agent for OIS under a source-unavailable defender model, coordinating four agents: candidate acquisition (CACA), operational-state-aware verification (OSVA) over a nine-component industrial state model, tiered remediationsynthesis (RSA) spanning protocol mitigation, binary hardening, and SSCKG-constrained source patches, and correctness validation (CVA) by behavioural-coverage preservation, independent replay, and typed rejection-feedback. The novelty is the joint treatment of the operational-state envelope as a first-class correctness condition together with a tier selectorthat degrades gracefully as artifact opacity increases. On OIS-RemedBench, a 45-case benchmark balanced across binary, protocol, and ICS partitions over eight vendors and nine protocols, SCARA attains full verification precision, high post-rerun remediation success across all three partitions, and a 7.22x analyst-hour gain over PLCverif on OIS-ICS. Against the strongest LLM baselines under a common correctness oracle, it leads in conditional success at the lowest new-vulnerability rate, with every contrast significant under Benjamini--Hochberg control. SCARA is thus the first end-to-end framework connecting binary vulnerability candidates to conditionally validated remediation for opaque industrial software.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6876627`

- title: Governing Digital Innovation Ecosystems under Risk and Uncertainty: IP Circulation, Platform Dependency, and Policy Orchestration in Korean Content Industries
- authors: Taejun Lee
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6876627
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Digital innovation in cultural and creative industries unfolds under compounded risk and uncertainty, where, drawing on Knight’s (1921) distinction, firms face both calculable ”known unknowns” of platform competition, production cost volatility, and shifting audience attention, and structurally indeterminate ”unknown unknowns” generated by generative AI, platform restructuring, and contested intellectual property (IP) appropriation. Existing innovation ecosystem theories, grounded largely in manufacturing and technology-driven contexts, struggle to explain how value creation, coordination, and governance operate in industries centered on intangible and symbolic assets under platform dependency. Addressing this gap, this study develops a conceptual framework for digital innovation ecosystems in content industries under conditions of high risk and high uncertainty. Methodologically, it employs a qualitative, mechanism-oriented theory-building design combining integrative literature synthesis with abductive interpretive analysis of policy, institutional, and industry materials. The framework is empirically illustrated through the Korean content industry between 2023 and 2026, where IP, digital platforms, and policy institutions interact under intense platform dependency and AI-driven transformation. The study reconceptualizes content innovation ecosystems as systems structured around IP, coordinated through digital platforms, and aligned through policy orchestration, identifying three interdependent mechanisms: IP circulation, platform coordination, and network integration. The study extends innovation ecosystem theory and contributes to debates on managing unknowns in digital innovation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6878923`

- title: ”Let Humans Do Human Things, Let Algorithms Do Machine Things”: The impact of task orientation on algorithm aversion from an identity positioning Perspective
- authors: Siyuan Cheng, Zuhong Liu, Yubin Xie, Yu Zhu, Ruilin Wu, Ronggang Zhou
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6878923
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Even when algorithms outperform humans, individuals often continue to prefer human judgment—a phenomenon known as algorithm aversion. Prior research has documented both resistance to and appreciation of algorithmic advice, yet the basis of algorithm aversion remains contested. This study argues that algorithm aversion is rooted in oppositional identity positioning between humans and machines. Across four experiments with human participants and two experiments with Large Language Model (LLM)-simulated participants, we examined how task orientation, task objectivity, descriptive framing, and identity verification jointly shape responses to algorithmic advice. Results showed that human participants exhibited stronger algorithm aversion in human-centered tasks than in machine-centered tasks, and stronger algorithm aversion in objective tasks than in subjective tasks. In contrast, LLM-simulated participants consistently preferred algorithmic over human advice across conditions. Further analyses showed that identity verification amplified algorithm aversion in objective tasks by strengthening perceived human identity salience. In addition, cumulative probability distribution modeling revealed that task orientation and task objectivity function as structural determinants shaping trust distributions, whereas framing and identity verification act as modulatory factors influencing response intensity without altering the underlying distributional form. These findings suggest that algorithm aversion reflects identity-based judgment rather than a purely accuracy-based evaluation of algorithmic performance.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6879731`

- title: Human-LLM Automated Writing Evaluation: Scoring Reliability and Longitudinal Impact on L2 Writing Performance and Self-Efficacy
- authors: Jie Yang, Jiehui Hu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6879731
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

LLMs are increasingly integrated into writing assessment; yet scalable implementation workflows and conclusive evidence regarding their efficacy remain lacking. To address these gaps, this study: 1) developed a Human-LLM Automated Writing Evaluation (AWE) Workflow, featuring human-in-the-loop calibration and scalability within a classroom-based assessment context; 2) implemented a longitudinal mixed-methods intervention to examine its impact on lower-intermediate EFL learners’ argumentative writing performance and self-efficacy. Results showed comparable longitudinal growth in writing performance and self-efficacy across groups, indicating no incremental benefit from LLM intervention. Further analyses showed that the critical constraint lies in the affordance of LLM feedback, which failed to target learners’ ZPD and was perceived by learners as either too difficult or misaligned with their needs. Consequently, it did not enhance self-efficacy relative to the control group, which in turn undermined learner agency and precluded additional writing gains. These findings underscore that the pedagogical promise of LLMs may depend on a human-centric approach. Critically, such an approach is not merely about placing the learner at the center, but about creating LLM-integrated learning spaces informed by socio-cognitive principles where learner agency and teacher guidance are mutually scaffolded. Future research should integrate these psychological and pedagogical dimensions to enable productive Human-LLM negotiation.
