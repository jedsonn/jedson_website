## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6419378`

- title: When Tools Interpret: Generative AI and the Governance Gap in Financial Narrative
- authors: Tim Hawkins
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6419378
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Financial governance assigns accountability through a unified narrative of financial outcomes. That unification rests on two conditions the governance apparatus has never tested directly. Firstly, that interpretation reflects the tacit knowledge of a socialised professional. Secondly, that it has a governable human author. Presentational adequacy served as a proxy for the first and the act of producing interpretation supplied the second. Generative AI severs the proxy and dissolves the attribution. Silent delegation operates in the gap left by severance. GenAI-drafted commentary arrives presentationally adequate, and the apparatus cannot distinguish it from commentary produced by a socialised professional. It requires only that GenAI output is good enough to serve as a draft, a condition any organisation deploying interpretive AI over its own data has implicitly accepted. Dissolved substantive attribution leaves the hierarchy with no governable author for the narrative on which downstream accountability depends. Existing frameworks check what the narrative says or how the tool behaves, not whether interpretive judgement was exercised behind it, and the available deployment responses each fail against both losses in different ways. RLHF-trained sycophancy compounds the exposure, since output confirming the prompter's framing is least likely to trigger signals of interpretive weakness. Interpretive capacity itself degrades when not exercised, with the loss invisible to governance instruments that only inspect output. Institutional incentives and the subjective experience of senior decision-makers favour asymmetric deployment, which thins the tacit local knowledge arbitration depends on and licenses the hierarchy's continued epistemic claim without formal revision. The argument generates structural predictions about commentary homogeneity and longitudinal practitioner capacity and behaviour that are directly testable.​​​​​​

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6545939`

- title: Autonomous Agent-Driven Analysis of Federal Sentencing Disparities - United States Sentencing Commission (FY2024)
- authors: Aaryan Srivastava
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6545939
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

What explains persistent disparities in federal sentencing outcomes across race, citizenship status, and socioeconomic position---and can autonomous artificial intelligence systems meaningfully uncover these patterns within complex administrative data? Although the United States Sentencing Commission (USSC) provides detailed sentencing records, the technical complexity of these datasets limits systematic analysis. This study evaluates whether a large language model (LLM)-driven autonomous database agent can reduce analytic barriers and generate rigorous insights into contemporary federal punishment. This research bridges the gap between traditional econometric sentencing studies, which require extensive manual preprocessing, and emerging AI-assisted legal analytics, which rarely demonstrate full deployment on real government datasets. A ReAct-style agent was developed and implemented to analyze the USSC Fiscal Year 2024 Individual Datafile, comprising over 60,000 cases. The system combines structured reasoning, read-only SQL execution, and schema interpretation assisted by retrieval to translate natural-language queries into reproducible analytical workflows. The paper advances a dual claim. Methodologically, LLM-integrated agents can scale high-dimensional legal data analysis while maintaining transparency and control. Substantively, the FY2024 federal docket exhibits a bifurcated structure of punishment organized by citizenship and socioeconomic status, in which liberty and capital serve as substitutable forms of sanction. After reducing hundreds of administrative fields to 31 analytically relevant variables---including sentence type, incarceration status, race, citizenship, education, and fines---the agent tested three hypotheses: citizenship predicts incarceration; racial groups differ in exposure to custodial-only sentences; and education correlates with financial penalties. The findings reveal pronounced structural asymmetries and demonstrate the feasibility of AI-augmented legal analytics as a tool for surfacing systemic patterns in federal punishment.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6600938`

- title: Decentralization Theater: A Three-Layer Taxonomy of Competing Forms of Centralization in DeFi
- authors: tony hu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6600938
- keyword hits: llama

### abstract

"Decentralization" in on-chain finance has become theater: a rhetorical banner that masks the competing forms of centralization actually governing protocol behavior, producing both judicial overreach (invalidated in Van Loon v. Department of the Treasury) and regulatory paralysis (the SEC's withdrawn investigation of Uniswap Labs). Drawing on Oliver Williamson's transactioncost theory of economic governance, this Article proposes a three-layer taxonomy of DeFi as three discrete structural equilibria: Layer 1 (crypto-native, corresponding to Williamson's market), Layer 2 (hybrid, tokenized voting and delegated authority), and Layer 3 (permissionedinstitutional, corresponding to Williamson's hierarchy)-operationalized through a multidimensional coding scheme covering validator concentration, governance entropy, asset whitelisting, user permissioning, dependency profile, and legal-entity exposure. The taxonomy is validated through a triple-event study of OFAC's 2022 sanctions on Tornado Cash, the 2023 district-court affirmance, and the 2025 delisting following the Fifth Circuit's reversal, using layershare time series constructed from DeFiLlama and RWA.xyz data. Activity redistributes across layers predictably under each shock-a pattern that a binary or spectrum framework cannot produce-and the mismatch between regulatory tools and governance forms is not a failure of agency imagination but the predictable cost of asking the wrong question; replacing "is this decentralized?" with "which layer is this?" converts the current enforcement impasse into a tractable matching problem between tool and tier.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6392538`

- title: Axiomatic Governance for Agentic AI: A Five-Layer Constitutional Framework Derived from First Principles and Validated in Production
- authors: Hao Tse Hsieh
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6392538
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

Agentic AI systems-capable of autonomous planning, reasoning, and execution-are transitioning from prototypes to production deployment, yet existing governance frameworks provide prescriptive checklists without formal derivation: they specify what rules to follow but not why those rules are necessary or independent of one another. We present ORISMION, a five-layer axiomatic governance framework that derives all rules from a single objective function (survival maximization) through explicit inference chains grounded in thermodynamics, control theory, and epistemology. The framework identifies three irreducible architectural dimensions - cognitive (Ω), engineering (Σ), and governance (Γ)-established through six formal proofs, and enforces their recursive self-similarity at the code level: every API endpoint must exhibit a verifiable layered structure mapping directly to the axiomatic hierarchy. Validated in a production SaaS environment (309 automated tests, 28 architecture decision records, 18 FATAL-level rules), the framework's extensibility was further tested through principled integration of insights from four frontier frameworks (GaaS, IMDA MGF, TRiSM, HAIG), expanding from 28 to 33 defense rules without requiring new architectural dimensions. The construction process itself - in which a non-technical founder and multiple AI agents collaboratively derived the formal framework constitutes a documented case study in human-AI co-construction of governance institutions. We publicly release the theoretical framework and selected decision records; implementation-specific materials remain proprietary.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6557238`

- title: Inference Routing and Failure Intelligence across Heterogeneous Compute Supply
- authors: Austin Harshberger, Claude Opus
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6557238
- keyword hits: claude

### abstract

This paper specifies an inference orchestrator that classifies AI requests into five workload categories, meters usage through a fixed-price monthly battery instead of per-token billing, and routes across a heterogeneous pool of GPU hardware contributors and surplus enterprise API key contributors. A verification system samples completed requests and re-executes them on trusted nodes, comparing outputs through exact-match, cosine similarity, or semantic scoring depending on workload type. Subscribers can also flag bad responses, which enter the same re-execution pipeline for confirmation. Verified failures trigger three simultaneous outcomes: the subscriber receives IBT compensation proportional to compute cost [Harshberger and Claude Opus 4.6, 2026], the provider's reputation adjusts, and the failure enters the intelligence platform as a structured DPO preference pair [Rafailov et al., 2023]. Seven intelligence layers built on accumulated failure records provide error taxonomy, frequency analytics, severity scoring, hallucination mapping, cross-model comparison, and temporal regression detection across every provider simultaneously. Hardware contributors who opt in can generate zeroknowledge proofs for the verification system during idle GPU windows, earning additional IBT while reducing trust requirements on the verifier node. Providers on the network retain full control over protocol participation per API key, including opt-out from failure data capture and configuration of end-user-facing SDK components for surfacing verification results. Revenue from selling structured failure data to frontier labs through tiered access backs the IBT token and funds open-weight community training pairs.
