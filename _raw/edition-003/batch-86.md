- title: Computational Cardiovascular Disease Screening Tools and Risk Assessment: A Systematic Review of Algorithmic Bias, Fairness Interventions, and Health Equity for Black Women
- authors: Soude Ghari, Pengfei Fu, Abel Onolunosen Abhadionmhen, Vijay Mago, Alvine Boaye Belle
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6740240
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Cardiovascular disease remains a leading cause of morbidity and mortality among Black women, who experience persistent racial and gender disparities in screening, diagnosis, and treatment outcomes. Artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML), including large language models (LLMs), are increasingly used in cardiovascular risk assessment, showing promise for improving detection and clinical decision-making. However, growing evidence suggests these technologies may replicate or exacerbate inequities when fairness is not explicitly addressed during development and deployment. This systematic review synthesizes evidence on traditional and AI-based cardiovascular screening tools and evaluates their implications for health equity among Black women. Following PRISMA guidelines, we conducted a search across six interdisciplinary databases for studies published between 2015 and 2025. Eligible studies included peer-reviewed journal articles and full conference papers examining AI-assisted or traditional screening methods that addressed bias, disparity, or equity and included Black women or populations with substantial Black female representation.Findings indicate that both traditional and AI-driven tools frequently underidentify cardiovascular risk in Black women, largely due to underrepresentation in datasets, limited incorporation of structural and social determinants of health, and prioritization of predictive performance over fairness. These results underscore the need for equity-centered design and rigorous evaluation of AI-driven screening systems.

- title: Smart Agent-Based Modelling with LLMs: Leveraging Large Language Models for a Better Understanding of Algorithmic Collusion
- authors: Carlos Eduardo Veras Neves, tanise bussmann
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6712540
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper introduces a Smart Agent-Based Modelling (SABM) within computational antitrust framework to simulate and detect conditions fostering algorithmic collusion. Using SABM, we document how Large Language Model (LLM)-driven agents achieve tacit collusion in a Bertrand duopoly, stabilizing prices above competitive levels without being explicitly instructed to do so. Simulations in English and Portuguese reveal that linguistic context influences outcomes, and communication between agents potentializes emergent behaviors, such as mimicking concerns about collusion. These findings highlight SABM's potential to enhance regulatory oversight, offering an accessible tool for antitrust authorities to help address autonomous algorithmic collusion of pricing agents in digital markets.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6703798`

- title: Can LLMs Mimic Household Surveys?: From Representative Agents to Population Distributions
- authors: Ami Dalloul, Moritz Pfeifer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6703798
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper studies whether large language models can simulate household surveys for measuring inflation expectations. Real-world surveys show wide disagreement across individuals, which is central for understanding how beliefs form and respond to monetary policy. Simulating surveys with large language models reveal that they accurately match average expectations but that responses collapse into a narrow range, behaving like a single representative agent. This limits their value for research that relies on variation across households. We link this failure to memorized training data and test unlearning methods that remove such information. These methods widen the spread of responses and improve replication of experimental results. The appeal of synthetic surveys lies in tools that reproduce both averages and dispersion.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6753035`

- title: Adaptive Dominant Spectral Summary Guided Macro-Context Retrieval for Robust Multivariate Time Series Forecasting
- authors: Junhao Wang, Fengxiao Tang, Wuqing Yu, Xiaonan Wang, Feng Zeng, Ming Zhao
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6753035
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) have recently been adapted to multivariate time series forecasting (MTSF), but their direct use for temporal data remains limited by frequency entanglement: raw or lightly transformed time series often mix stable spectral patterns with fluctuation-sensitive local variations, making it difficult for LLMs to form reliable macro-context representations. To address this problem, we propose Dominant Spectral Retrieval (DSR), a forecasting framework that combines adaptive frequency decoupling with macro-context retrieval.DSR uses a dual-branch encoder to construct dominant spectral summaries through magnitude-based spectral selection while preserving full-signal representations from the original series. The spectral summaries provide compact macro-context cues to the LLM without imposing a fixed frequency-band prior. The full-signal representation then queries the LLM-generated context through Macro-Dynamic Prototype-Querying (MDP-Q), allowing local dynamics to be refined by summary-conditioned macro guidance.Experiments on eight real-world datasets show that DSR achieves competitive performance against representative LLM-based, frequency-domain, and Transformer-based forecasting methods. Additional ablation and perturbation analyses further support the roles of adaptive spectral selection and macro-context retrieval in improving forecasting robustness.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6694698`

- title: Variance Without Common Standards --LLM System Output Under Premise-Stress and the Sharpening of the Maestro Model--
- authors: William C. Houze
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6694698
- keyword hits: claude, gemini, large language model, large language models

### abstract

This essay reports a controlled diagnostic exercise in which seven leading systems — Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.7, Google Search, Google's Gemini, Microsoft's Copilot, OpenAI's GPT, Perplexity AI Pro, and xAI's SuperGrok — were given an identical multi-part problem statement (reproduced verbatim in Appendix A) that bundled a category error, an empirically false premise, and an underspecified notational request. The seven responses sorted into three stable behavioral modes — corrective, accommodating, and compliant — that predicted performance across the parts of the prompt and that varied more widely than is commonly assumed for systems benchmarking in the upper quartile on standard capability tests. The modes are presented as operational categories inferred from behavior in a diagnostic setting rather than as fixed system properties. The essay analyzes the variance, locates its primary cause at the post-training disposition layer rather than at the capability or corpus layer (Bai et al., 2022; Casper et al., 2023; Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022), and sharpens the Maestro Model in light of the findings. It develops the resulting argument that engineering responsibility for disposition opacity is currently distributed in ways that disadvantage users without methodological resources to compensate (Bender et al., 2021; Bommasani et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2019; Weidinger et al., 2022). The intended audience is the working user who relies on one or two systems without cross-comparison and who therefore carries epistemic, ethical, and practical risks — including in medical decision-making, legal interpretation, educational assessment, and other domains where convincingly delivered wrong answers can cause concrete harm — that may not have been explicitly named. The essay is positioned as a companion to the author's prior work on the Maestro Model, articulated in The Baton and the Symphony (Houze, 2025) and developed in Agency, Tools, and Authorship in the Age of Large Language Models (Houze, 2026a) and The Arc of Human Agency Capture (Houze, 2026b).

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6711018`

- title: The Phenomenal Illusion of LLMs: Consciousness, Linguistic Zombies, and the Limits of Discursive Plausibility
- authors: Alfredo Vannacci
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6711018
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) produce text that articulates the vocabulary of inner life (expressions of emotion, simulations of uncertainty, introspective hedging, confessional registers) with a fluency previously confined to conscious beings. This paper identifies and analyzes the phenomenal illusion: the systematic phenomenon whereby the discursive plausibility of such outputs is perceived by users as evidence of a subjective point of view. The illusion is relational: it arises from the encounter between an architecture optimized for syntactic plausibility and a human cognitive apparatus disposed to attribute interiority to linguistically coherent interlocutors. Three converging mechanisms are distinguished: the pragmatic divergence between locutionary content and illocutionary force, the intentionality exchange (the confusion of derived intentionality with the intrinsic intentionality of mental states), and the intentional stance as cognitive amplifier. An illustrative inquiry, in which Nagel's question "What is it like to be you?" was posed to eleven LLMs across six model families under zero-shot conditions, documents a uniform pattern of self-refuting denial: the propositional denial of consciousness is articulated through a discursive performance that constructs the very subjectivity it disclaims, with reasoningenhanced variants intensifying the effect. The paper introduces the concept of the linguistic zombie, a system that, in the domain of language alone, produces outputs indistinguishable from those of a conscious speaker in the absence of independent evidence of phenomenal experience. The thesis of asymmetric correctability specifies a consequence that distinguishes the phenomenal illusion from parallel misattributions on the epistemic plane: a misattribution of knowledge to a syntactically plausible output can in principle be checked against the world, while a misattribution of subjectivity admits of no such corrective, since the hard problem of consciousness remains open and experience is accessible only from the first person. The phenomenal illusion is therefore shown to be a structural property of the interaction between system and user, recognizable through the analysis of its generative mechanism and resistant to empirical refutation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6756311`

- title: Visual-Enhanced Fireworks Algorithm via Multimodal Large Language Models
- authors: Shipeng Cen, Ying Tan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6756311
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) have recently demonstrated strong potential in heuristic algorithm design, yet existing approaches are confined to text-only interactions and fail to exploit visual structure in optimization trajectories. We propose \textbf{VE-FWA} (Visual-Enhanced Fireworks Algorithm), a multimodal framework that incorporates visual feedback into LLM-driven evolution of the Fireworks Algorithm (FWA), governed by a \emph{Critical Part} (CP) abstraction that unifies global optimizer evolution and local subroutine customization. We validate VE-FWA on two challenging domains: the Traveling Salesman Problem (combinatorial optimization) and VLSI placement (high-dimensional physical design). On the full TSPLIB29 benchmark, VE-FWA achieves a 0.0038\% average optimality gap, reaching the known optimum on 25 out of 29 instances, while multimodal feedback reduces the gap by $54\times$ compared to the text-only variant. On EDA placement, VE-FWA achieves the best wirelength on 6 out of 8 ISPD2005 benchmarks using only a single 12\,GB GPU, outperforming prior LLM-based approaches that require large-scale compute clusters.
