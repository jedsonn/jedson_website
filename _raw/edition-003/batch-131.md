## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.4558719`

- title: How Generative AI Impacts Content Engagement: Evidence from an Online Learning Platform
- authors: Unnati Narang, Carl-Philip Ahlbom, Shrabastee Banerjee
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2023-10-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.4558719
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence is rapidly transforming user-generated content on online platforms. While AI can spur content creation, it has raised concerns about lower quality content or AI slop. Platforms have responded in different ways—from early bans to disclosure requirements and integrating AI tools. Yet, little is known about how encouraging the use of AI for content creation affects the quality of content and individual users’ engagement with it in active (i.e., commenting) and passive (i.e., reacting) ways. We study this question through a field experiment on Yellowdig.com, an online discussion platform, and through two follow- up online studies. Participants in our field experiment were randomly encouraged—akin to a platform policy promoting AI-assisted posting—to write posts using AI (i.e., AI-only condition), using AI with modifications (i.e., AI-assisted condition), or using only human inputs (i.e., Human condition). Our results show that neither AI treatment significantly increases active engagement but that posts in the AI-assisted condition generate significantly more passive engagement relative to those in the Human condition. We propose one key mechanism based on the nature of textual content. Specifically, because content becomes more informative but also increasingly complex with AI assistance, users are likely to reward seemingly higher quality AI content with passive reactions while not having to add much more through active commenting. Our follow-up online experiments on a custom AI platform we built replicate the main results and show that higher informativeness and greater complexity of AI content predominantly shape engagement, above and beyond alternative mechanisms (e.g., AI use disclosure and self-selection into AI use). Overall, the findings suggest that AI alters the nature of content in a way that increases passive engagement but may not meaningfully increase active engagement in online discussions.
