- authors: Chanwoo Park, Chanwoo Kim

- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6852773

### abstract

AI is moving fast, but we still don’t have a broadly accepted, systematic way to evaluate the ”cognitive” abilities of large language models (LLMs). In this study, we introduce the Language Model Mini-Mental State Examination (LMMSE), an early attempt to adapt the logic of clinical cognitive screening—originally built for assessing dementia and cognitive impairment—to the way LLMs actually behave. LMMSE measures multiple domains, including language, thinking, perception, and social cognition, and treats them as a profile rather than a single score. We run standardized experiments across several state-of-the-art models (including GPT-5.2 and Gemini 3) to map their strengths and weaknesses and to surface failure modes that show up reliably across repeated trials. What we find is that these ”cognitive” skills are not evenly distributed: models can excel in some areas while consistently struggling in others, especially on spatial perception tasks. We propose LMMSE as a practical evaluation framework for tracking progress toward more general AI systems, and as a tool developers and decision-makers can use to spot limitations early and guide future model improvements. To our best knowledge, this is the first study to integrate cognitive impairment testing with LLMs.

### abstract

Background: Large language models (LLMs) have transformed natural language processing, yet their enormous size creates a significant barrier to practical deployment. Running models like LLaMA or Mistral on everyday hardware demands substantial memory, computation, and energy—resources unavailable in many real-world settings. While post-training quantization (PTQ) offers a way to shrink these models without full retraining, existing approaches often treat compression and accuracy as separate problems, leading to disappointing trade-offs between model size and performance.Methods: We introduce Layer-Adaptive Self-Distilled Quantization (LASDQ), a unified framework combining sensitivity-aware mixed-precision allocation with layer-wise self-distillation. - link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6879759

### abstract

This study addresses the challenge of enabling generative artificial intelligence (AI) to perform structured reasoning in domain-specific engineering tasks. From an AI perspective, we propose a prompt-engineering framework that induces hypothetico-deductive reasoning in multimodal large language models (LLMs). The framework formulates prompt design as a combinatorial optimization problem and employs simulated annealing and Hoeffding-Upper Confidence Bound (Hoeffding-UCB) to identify optimal prompt structures based on multiple evaluation criteria, including semantic similarity, logical consistency, and hallucination suppression. From an engineering application perspective, the proposed method is applied to technical decision-making in road bridge maintenance, where expert-level reasoning is required under uncertain and time-constrained conditions, such as routine and post-earthquake inspections. The designed prompts enable LLMs to emulate the reasoning patterns of experienced bridge engineers without additional model training. Experimental results show that the optimized prompts significantly improve logical consistency scores (1.2–1.4 times compared to experienced engineers) and enhance the structural coherence of inspection findings, while maintaining practical usability in engineering workflows. However, hallucination suppression remains limited, indicating the need for integration with complementary techniques such as retrieval-augmented generation. These findings demonstrate that prompt engineering can systematically enhance reasoning capabilities of pre-trained LLMs and provide a practical pathway for integrating AI into real-world engineering decision-making processes.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.08853v1`

- title: AI-Assisted Variance Reduction in Randomized Experiments
- authors: David Arbour, Eli Ben-Michael, Avi Feller, Apoorva Lal, Lo-Hua Yuan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-07
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.08853v1
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Generative AI and large language models can produce realistic predictions of human behavior from rich, unstructured inputs with little to no task-specific training data. Recent work uses these ``digital twin'' predictions to supplement human responses in surveys and experiments. We study the special case of using AI-generated predictions to reduce variance in randomized experiments. We argue that doing so requires no new estimators and that researchers can simply include AI predictions as covariates in standard regression adjustment, analogous to adjusting for a prognostic score. A benefit of this approach is a ``do no harm'' property whereby the adjusted estimator reverts to the unadjusted difference in means when predictions are uninformative. Other methods, such as variants of prediction-powered inference, do not have this guarantee. We provide implementation guidance, including how to obtain continuous scores from discrete LLM outputs and how to use LLMs to featurize unstructured inputs as auxiliary covariates. We demonstrate these ideas in simulations and three empirical applications: a survey mega-study, an email marketing A/B test, and a large-scale technology platform experiment. Overall, efficiency gains are real if modest, with greater benefits in studies that contain substantial text and other unstructured data. We also confirm the do no harm property empirically. Given these gains and limited costs, we recommend adjusting for AI-generated predictions as a regular empirical practice.

### abstract

The intersection of Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) and Large Language Models (LLMs) represents a promising frontier in quantitative finance. However, current hybrid models frequently compress LLM outputs into scalar sentiment scores, discarding the high-dimensional reasoning traces necessary for complex market analysis. In this paper, we propose a novel Cross-Attention Latent Fusion architecture that directly integrates the internal hidden states (1536-D) of a reasoning LLM (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B) with a Selective State Space Model (Mamba) encoding 30-day temporal price momentum.
