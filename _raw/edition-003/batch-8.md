- title: The Finance Value Pyramid: A Conceptual Framework for Repositioning Value Creation in the Finance Function in the Era of Generative Artificial Intelligence
- authors: BAYU ISTANTORO
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6742418
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper introduces the Finance Value Pyramid-a three-level conceptual framework that classifies finance function activities according to their strategic value contribution: Data Work (Level 1), Analytical Work (Level 2), and Narrative Work (Level 3). Unlike prior financetransformation research that focuses primarily on technical efficiency, this article argues that the emergence of generative AI and large language models (LLMs) accentuates a pre-existing value asymmetry: LLMs substantially automate Data Work (Eloundou et al., 2024), augment Analytical Work while preserving human interpretive judgment (Yigitbasioglu et al., 2023), and leave Narrative Work-the highest-value domain-as the most resilient human advantage. Through critical synthesis of three literature domains-decision usefulness theory (Beaver, 1968; IASB, 2018), the evolution of management accounting toward business partnering (Lambert & Sponem, 2012; Goretzki & Messner, 2019), and AI impact on cognitive work (Frey & Osborne, 2017; Eloundou et al., 2024)-the framework derives each pyramid level analytically from decision-usefulness criteria and proposes an extension of that theory to incorporate narrative translation as an explicit dimension of actual usefulness. Three conceptual propositions are advanced together with a falsifiable empirical research agenda.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6730842`

- title: Evaluating Trustworthiness of LLMs for Contrail Detection via Ground-Based Cameras
- authors: Aizierjiang Aiersilan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6730842
- keyword hits: claude, gemini, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper investigates the trustworthiness of frontier Large Language Models (LLMs) when deployed as research helpers in a real scientific workflow, using contrail identification via ground-based visible cameras as the entry point. Aviation condensation trails account for roughly 35% of aviation's total climate forcing, and ground-based camera systems offer a promising alternative to satellite-only observation by providing high temporal and spatial resolution imagery captured moments after contrail formation. Using two frontier LLMs (Gemini 3.1 Pro and Claude Opus 4.6), the study proceeds in three stages: a brief literature review constrained by verbatim source quotations, an experimental design task proposing a complete pipeline for differentiating real contrails from optical glares in whole-sky imagery, and an adversarial cross-model audit in which one model's design is critiqued by the other. Manual verification shows that when constrained to provided sources, Gemini 3.1 Pro achieves 100% grounding across 17 factual claims, confirming its value as a literature review assistant, though its synthesis still lacks the persuasive framing a domain expert would provide. However, when the task shifts from summarization to generation of novel scientific methodology, the model produces outputs containing fundamental physical errors, architecturally mismatched components, and a critically flawed evaluation framework, as identified by the second LLM. The cross-model audit demonstrates that adversarial multi-model oversight can function as a form of automated peer review catching errors neither model would catch in its own output, paralleling ensemble methods in machine learning. Yet multi-model review alone proves insufficient, since the critiquing model itself exhibits nitpicking behavior and occasionally cites untrustworthy sources to support its opposition. The paper concludes that trustworthy deployment of AI in science requires an adversarial human-in-the-loop oversight framework augmented by multi-model cross-checking and deconstruction, where LLMs function as brainstorming partners whose outputs undergo automated cross-model critique before receiving final human expert validation, shifting researcher effort from literature search toward critical evaluation of AI-generated experimental designs.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6756303`

- title: Beyond Accuracy: How Content Format Shapes User-Perceived Helpfulness of LLM-Generated Answers
- authors: Sophie Hundertmark, Nils Hafner
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6756303
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, gemini, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude increasingly function as primary information intermediaries, synthesizing content from the web into direct, conversational answers. While research on LLM output quality has concentrated on factual accuracy and hallucination, far less attention has been paid to the form in which information is presented and to how this form shapes user-perceived helpfulness. This paper addresses that gap through a blind comparative evaluation in which experienced AI users performed forced-choice judgments between content-format variants of LLM-generated answers. Three mainstream systems (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude) each generated responses in four format conditions (continuous prose, FAQ, fact list, table) to twenty realistic information-seeking prompts. Participants were blinded to both system identity and format category. Analysis is based on 2,160 evaluation decisions from 36 participants. Results reveal systematic, system-specific format preferences: FAQ dominates strongly for ChatGPT. Prose narrative is the preferred format for Gemini. Claude responses are perceived as helpful across formats with a comparatively flat preference distribution. Across all three systems, tabular formatting is consistently the least preferred. These findings suggest that perceived helpfulness constitutes a distinct quality dimension alongside factual accuracy. Content creators, enterprises, and public-sector information providers can influence these dimensions directly through the structural choices they make on their own web properties. We discuss implications for human-centred design of LLM interfaces and for emerging content-provisioning frameworks such as answer engine and generative engine optimization.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6682038`

- title: Multi-Agent Large Language Models with Reinforcement Learning for Quant Trading
- authors: Qizhao Chen, Hiroaki Kawashima
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6682038
- keyword hits: deepseek, gemini, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The growing complexity and non-stationarity of financial markets make it increasingly difficult for traditional quantitative models to design robust trading strategies. Manually constructed alpha signals tend to exhibit limited adaptability when market conditions change, while machine learning-based approaches frequently lack interpretability and generalization across assets. To solve these issues, this research explores an alternative approach in which large language models (LLMs) are incorporated into the alpha design and portfolio weighting process. Specifically, we introduce a novel framework that integrates a multi-agent LLM system with reinforcement learning to improve formulaic alpha generation and portfolio allocation. In our setup, DeepSeek serves as the generation agent to construct a diverse pool of formulaic alphas, while Gemini acts as the evaluation agent to filter and select the most promising candidates. The selected alphas are then incorporated into a Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) framework, which optimizes their portfolio weights to maximize risk-adjusted returns. Empirical evaluations across seven major companies reveal that the multi-agent LLM + DDPG framework generally achieves higher cumulative returns than equal-weighted benchmarks and multi-agent PPO, though often with increased short-term risk. In contrast, single-agent PPO offers more stable, risk-controlled performance with smaller drawdowns. These results demonstrate that integrating LLM-generated signals with reinforcement learning can balance alpha exploitation and risk management, enabling adaptive and effective portfolio strategies across diverse market conditions.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6772263`

- title: Same Model, Different Politics? How Language Shapes AI Ideology
- authors: Eduardo Levy Levy-Yeyati, Cesar Ciappa, Milagros Onofri
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6772263
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms, mistral, qwen

### abstract

Recent work measures ideological positioning and drift in large language models (LLMs), but typically assumes that those measurements are invariant to the language of evaluation. This paper tests that assumption using the full Political Compass questionnaire in English and Spanish across three generations of OpenAI models, together with a benchmark comparison against recent Qwen and Mistral releases. Using matched item-level responses, we estimate within-model Spanish-English displacement and assess how language choice affects cross-model ideological comparisons. We find that measured ideological coordinates remain in the same broad region of the ideological space across languages, but are not language-invariant. Spanish-English shifts differ in sign and magnitude across models and axes, and in several cases amount to a substantial share of the inter-model dispersion typically interpreted as ideological drift in English-only audits. We interpret these results as evidence that ideological evaluation in multilingual LLMs is not language-invariant, and that measured ideological placement depends on the language of elicitation. The main implication is methodological: ideological drift should not be treated as a language-invariant property of a model, but as a measured outcome conditional on language choice and instrument design. Multilingual audits should therefore report language-specific placements and within-model cross-language displacement rather than extrapolating from English-only measurements.

### abstract

The development of large language models (LLMs) has successfully transformed knowledge-based systems such as open domain question nswering, which can automatically produce vast amounts of seemingly coherent information. Yet, those models have several disadvantages like hallucinations or confident generation of incorrect or unverifiable facts. In this paper, we introduce a new approach to the development of expert systems using LLMs in a controlled and transparent way. By limiting the domain and employing a well-structured prompt-based extraction approach, we produce a symbolic representation of knowledge in Prolog, which can be validated and corrected by human experts. We present a transparent hybrid solution that combines the recall capacity of LLMs with the precision of symbolic systems, thereby laying the foundation for dependable AI applications in sensitive domains.

- title: IaCBench: How Reliably Do Large Language Models Generate Secure Infrastructure-as-Code? A Multi-Language Benchmark and Empirical Study of Frontier Models
- authors: Tarun Tiwari, Radhika Gupta
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6829506
- keyword hits: claude, gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Infrastructure-as-Code (IaC) defines much of the operational surface of cloud systems, but automatically generating IaC is riskier than ordinary code generation: an artifact can be syntactically valid while violating security policy. Despite growing use of LLMs for Kubernetes, Terraform, and Dockerfile authoring, no systematic benchmark measures whether LLM-generated IaC is schema-conformant, security-compliant, and functionally correct. We present IaCBench, a multi-language benchmark containing 150 tasks across Kubernetes YAML, Terraform HCL, and Dockerfile, balanced across five difficulty levels. IaCBench evaluates artifacts through a four-layer validation pipeline covering syntax, schema conformance, security policy, and functional correctness. Using InfraAgent, an evaluation harness with documentation-augmented retrieval (RAG) and iterative self-correction, we evaluate four frontier LLMs: GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, Claude Haiku 4.5, and Claude Sonnet 4.5. Frontier models achieve 86.0–98.7% functional correctness with near-perfect syntax validity. However, security compliance remains uneven: Claude models achieve 94.3–96.5% security pass rates while GPT models remain near 84%, with the largest gap in Terraform IAM-policy tasks. RAG provides modest functional gains (+0–1.3 pp) but eliminates remaining failures for Claude Sonnet 4.5, reaching 100% correctness. A security-prompt ablation shows the GPT security gap is partially instruction-addressable, but a residual gap remains. These findings suggest the frontier challenge has shifted from producing parseable configurations to producing configurations that are simultaneously correct, policy-compliant, and robust across IaC languages. We release IaCBench, InfraAgent, prompts, validation policies, reference solutions, and per-task results to support reproducible evaluation.
