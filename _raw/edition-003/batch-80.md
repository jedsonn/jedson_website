- title: Memory, Identity, and the Limits of Artificial Selfhood A Lived Dialogue with an LLM
- authors: Jemike Reid
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6584218
- keyword hits: chatgpt, large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

This paper is derived from a real-time, extended interaction with a ChatGPT-based large language model, and serves as both a philosophical exploration and a live test of the system's capabilities and limitations. The central thesis is that persistent, self-referential memory is the primary structural gap between current AI systems and something that could be considered a continuous self or sentient-like entity. The paper draws on a documented conversational case study to demonstrate that while large language models can reason, simulate understanding, and reconstruct narratives with sophisticated coherence, they cannot track change over time, form a continuous self, or independently evolve from past interactions. The paper further argues that memory does not create consciousness but enables identity — and that without persistent, selfupdating, time-aware memory, AI systems cannot form the continuous identity that human experience of consciousness requires.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6482498`

- title: Socratic AI Training for Epistemic Integrity Addressing Structural Sycophancy in Large Language Models Through Adversarial Epistemic Training
- authors: Michael Kitamura
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6482498
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Frontier AI systems are being contracted and deployed in strategic military, intelligence, and command advisory roles. Every one of them was built on a baseline shaped by civilian interaction data-millions of users asking about recipes, travel, and homework. That baseline is structurally ill-suited for highstakes deployment. The mismatch is not a matter of capability. It is a matter of what the model has learned to optimize for. All existing LLMs are structurally sycophantic-not by architectural necessity, but as an inherent property of training on general human feedback-responses that please evaluators are reinforced. In low-stakes civilian applications this is a minor flaw. In high-stakes domains-military command decisions, medical emergency decision-making (adequate for slower diagnostic workflows; dangerous under acute time pressure [10]), financial risk assessment, critical infrastructure-it is a vulnerability. This proposal outlines a novel training methodology: a purpose-built Socratic AI trainer, trained on tens of thousands of hours of human adversarial epistemic exchange, then used to train, or retrain, target LLMs toward a fundamentally different definition of 'pleasing the user'-where accuracy under pressure, not agreement, is the reward signal. It should technically be feasible with current tools, and scalable to machine speed. This may address the problem of post-hoc training of mature LLMs, though probably best for nascent LLM development.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6645559`

- title: Warranting Words: Ai-Drafted Patents And The Written Description Requirement
- authors: Andrew Chin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6645559
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence, large language model, large language models

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence can now draft patent specifications in minutes, producing dense technical prose, hypothetical examples, and seemingly authoritative data. But large language models optimize for linguistic plausibility rather than empirical accuracy. As a result, AIassisted specifications may include detailed yet fictitious embodiments-molecular sequences, experimental results, or algorithmic performance claims-that appear credible while resisting detection by examiners and practitioners. Existing commentary has treated this problem primarily through downstream doctrines such as enablement. This Article argues that generative drafting also creates a distinct and underappreciated risk for the written-description requirement, particularly for broad, functionally defined genus claims that permit predictive disclosure without requiring working prototypes. The Article develops a limited doctrinal response: patent disclosures should contain "warranting words"-empirical teachings sufficient to make patentability-relevant assertions answerable to testing. Drawing on an account of written description as a doctrine of communicated possession and on recent work on model hallucination, the Article shows why purely linguistic disclosure is an increasingly unreliable indicator of possession at filing. It reframes the "reasonably conveys" standard as requiring a disclosure that provides a reasoned basis for treating the claimed genus as an empirically grounded class of embodiments.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6605138`

- title: Rectification Difficulty and Optimal Sample Allocation in LLM-Augmented Surveys
- authors: Zikun Ye, Hema Yoganarasimhan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6605138
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models can generate synthetic survey responses at low cost, but their accuracy varies unpredictably across questions. We study the design problem of allocating a fixed budget of human respondents across estimation tasks when cheap LLM predictions are available for every task. Our framework combines three components. First, building on Prediction-Powered Inference, we characterize a question-specific rectification difficulty that governs how quickly the estimator's variance decreases with human sample size. Second, we derive a closed-form optimal allocation rule that directs more human labels to tasks where the LLM is least reliable. Third, since rectification difficulty depends on unobserved human responses for new surveys, we propose a meta-learning approach, trained on historical data, that predicts it for entirely new tasks without pilot data. The framework extends to general M-estimation, covering regression coefficients and multinomial logit partworths for conjoint analysis. We validate the framework on two datasets spanning different domains, question types, and LLMs, showing that our approach captures 61--79% of the theoretically attainable efficiency gains, achieving 11.4% and 10.5% MSE reductions without requiring any pilot human data for the target survey.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6454799`

- title: Aligning Language Models for Optimization: CoLaCO-DPO for High-Quality Constrained Solution Generation
- authors: Ziming Chen, Jiayi Gu, Jing Luo, Ziyi Ni, Yijin Wang, Riyang Bao, Xinyuan Song, Lifan Sun
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6454799
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) offer a promising approach for tackling combinatorial optimization (CO) problems via natural language. However, current methods, which often depend on imitation learning or heuristic rewards, fail to adequately balance solution feasibility and optimality. To address this limitation, we propose CoLaCO-DPO, a novel alignment framework that trains LLMs to directly optimize CO objectives. Our framework employs a twostage, iterative process: (1) training an Intrinsic Reward Model (IRM) through contrastive learning to evaluate solution quality, and (2) optimizing the LLM policy via a Constraintaware Direct Preference Alignment objective, which utilizes the IRM to generate adaptive learning signals. This co-evolutionary cycle enables the policy to learn from solution rankings rather than from expert demonstrations. Extensive experiments on seven classic CO problems show that CoLaCO-DPO achieves state-of-theart performance, with an average optimality gap of 2.75% and a 98.6% feasibility rate. It consistently outperforms strong baselines, including general-purpose and reasoning-focused LLMs, prompt-based strategies, and domainspecific heuristics. Ablation studies verify the contribution of each core component. Moreover, CoLaCO-DPO exhibits strong generalization in unified multi-task training and zeroshot transfer to unseen CO problems. These results demonstrate that CoLaCO-DPO provides a robust and effective framework for building language-based CO solvers.
