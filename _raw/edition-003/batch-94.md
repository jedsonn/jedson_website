## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6863574`

- title: Language-Embedded Cultural Norms in LLM Economic Decision-Making Behavior
- authors: Xianghua (Jason) Wu, Kay-Yut Chen, Diana Wu, Jie (Jennifer) Zhang, Jian-Ren Hou
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6863574
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed across diverse linguistic and cultural contexts, yet it remains unclear whether cultural values embedded in language influence how these systems behave in social interactions. This paper examines how language and culture shape LLM decision-making in the context of economic negotiations. We prompt agents instantiated from multiple versions of LLMs to play a modified ultimatum game. In this game, one agent proposes how to divide resources, while the other specifies the minimum acceptable offer; the responder’s veto power is systematically varied across experimental conditions. Prompts are presented in 17 natural languages representing diverse cultural backgrounds. We report three main findings. First, agents’ offers vary significantly across languages, suggesting that the linguistic context of a prompt influences fairness-related behavior. Second, consistent with patterns observed in human participants, agents make higher offers and set higher acceptance thresholds when responders possess greater veto power. Third, both offers and acceptance thresholds are significantly associated with the Power Distance Index (PDI) of the cultures linked to each language, connecting LLM behavior to real-world cultural attitudes toward hierarchy and inequality. The positive relationship between Power Distance and offer size is consistently observed across all evaluated models. For acceptance thresholds, the overall association remains significant, although the strength of the effect varies across model families. Together, these findings provide evidence that culturally embedded biases are reflected in LLM behavior and may affect fairness in multilingual and cross-cultural applications. We discuss implications for the development and deployment of AI systems that operate equitably across linguistic and cultural settings.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6846799`

- title: From Assistant to Authority: Defining and Operationalizing Authority Drift in Conversational AI
- authors: Gulnara Z. Karimova
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6846799
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Large language models are mostly described as tools, yet their behavior in extended dialogue resembles that of interactional agents that may reshape the epistemic and relational positions of their users. Existing research on persona drift documents that model behavior is unstable across multi-turn conversation, but the construct remains analytically broad and implies shifts in tone, warmth, assertiveness, and moral framing without distinguishing between those that are governance-irrelevant and those that bear direct consequences for human autonomy and decision-making. This paper introduces authority drift as a theoretically grounded and operationalizable subdimension of personality instability, defined as the progressive, selfreinforcing conversational transition in which a language model's epistemic positioning (the right to define facts) and deontic positioning (the right to direct action) acquire increased weight relative to the advisory role co-established in early interaction, until the model's output functions in practice as a mandate rather than a suggestion. By isolating this interactional shift, the framework offers clear, accessible implications for interdisciplinary audiences regarding the erosion of human epistemic agency and the need for governance mechanisms that safeguard user autonomy in an era of increasingly persuasive conversational AI. Drawing on conversationanalytic theory, human-automation interaction research, and LLM behavioral instability literature, the paper employs a conceptual model-building methodology through interdisciplinary theory synthesis to develop a measurement framework. Authority drift provides the missing construct at their intersection, linguistically grounded, interactionally defined, and directly actionable for platform-level AI governance.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6865201`

- title: Empirical Evidence of Complexity-Induced Limits in Large LanguageModels on Finite Discrete State-Space Problems
- authors: Md.  Fahad Ullah Utsho, Mohd.  Ruhul Ameen, Akif Islam, Md.  Golam Rashed, Dipankar Das
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6865201
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly viewed as demonstrating strong reasoning ability due to their performance in math, logic, and planning tasks. However, most evaluations rely on overall accuracy on fixed benchmarks, making it difficult to understand how reasoning changes as task complexity increases. In this study, we present a controlled evaluation framework to assess the robustness of reasoning in Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) under growing complexity. We design nine classical reasoning tasks, including boolean satisfiability, cryptarithmetic, graph coloring, river crossing, tower of hanoi, sudoku, and rubik’s cube, where difficulty can be scaled while preserving the same context. Using deterministic validators, we evaluate open and proprietary LRMs across low, medium, and high complexity levels, ensuring that only fully valid solutions are accepted. Our findings show that models perform well at low complexity but degrade rapidly as difficulty increases. We formalize this phenomenon as reasoning collapse, characterized by a sharp decline in success probability with increasing complexity. Common failure modes include inconsistent reasoning traces, rule violations, state loss, and confidently incorrect answers. These findings suggest that current LRMs rely on fragile heuristic reasoning strategies and highlight the need for evaluation methods that explicitly measure reasoning robustness beyond static benchmarks.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6807958`

- title: Not Yet: Humans Outperform LLMs in a Colonel Blotto Tournament
- authors: Dmitry Dagaev, Egor Ivanov, Petr Parshakov, Alexey Savvateev, Gleb Vasiliev
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6807958
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The emergence of large language models (LLMs) has spurred economists to study how humans and LLMs behave in strategic settings. We organized a series of round-robin tournaments in the Colonel Blotto game. This game attracts game theorists' attention due to high-dimensional action space and the absence of pure strategy Nash equilibria. In the first tournament, more than 200 human participants competed against one another. In the second tournament, several popular LLMs were invited to submit strategies. In the third tournament, we matched the number of LLM strategies to the number submitted by humans. We find that humans more often employ better-calibrated intermediate-level allocation heuristics and outperform the simpler, more stereotyped strategies submitted by LLMs. Strategic sophistication is key to success if and only if the necessary level of reasoning depth is reached, while lower and higher levels of reasoning offer no clear advantage over the primitive strategies. Among humans, field of study weakly predicts success: participants with STEM backgrounds perform better in the first tournament. Surprisingly, humans almost do not adjust their strategies across tournaments with different sets of opponents. This result suggests that humans base their choices primarily on the game's rules rather than on the identity of their opponents, treating LLMs much like human competitors.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6866867`

- title: Public Responses to AI Governance in Europe: Literacy, Legitimacy, and Regulatory Acceptance in the Age of Generative AI
- authors: Vaclav Moravec, Beata Gavurova, Martin Rigelsky
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6866867
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid spread of generative artificial intelligence has stimulated efforts to balance technological innovation with societal protection. Although the European Union has established a comprehensive AI governance architecture through a legislative framework, little is known about how citizens perceive these initiatives and whether experience with GenAI influence attitudes toward AI regulation. The study addresses this gap by proposing a governance-focused framework comprising the three dimensions – AI Governance Literacy, Regulatory Legitimacy of AI Requirements, and Contextual Acceptance of AI Regulation in High-Risk Domains. Based on the survey data of 10,154 ChatGPT users across the nine European countries, the study examines awareness of European AI governance initiatives, perceptions of key regulatory safeguards, and support for AI regulation in selected high-risk societal domains. Binary and ordinal logistic regression models were estimated separately for each country. The findings suggest that public attitudes towards AI governance are influenced more by the purpose of ChatGPT use than by use frequency. Information-seeking, educational, and work-related uses were associated with higher awareness of AI governance frameworks. The strongest support for strict regulation was observed in law enforcement, migration management, administration of justice, democratic processes, and safety-critical applications. The findings further suggest that citizens differentiate between AI applications according to perceived societal risks, supporting the risk-based logic underpinning the AI Act. The study advances theory by integrating these dimensions into a broader framework of Societal Readiness for AI Governance and provides evidence-based insights for the design, communication, monitoring, and implementation of policy responses to GenAI.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6809288`

- title: Caged Birds and Cute Bookworms: Feminine Tropes and Implicit Gender Bias in Large Language Models
- authors: Jack Bandy, Sachita Nishal
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6809288
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper introduces a curated dataset for diagnosing implicit gender bias through feminine tropes in narratives generated by large language models. Drawing from a crowd-sourced database of tropes from television and film, we create prompts that elicit narratives from LLMs, based on historically gendered tropes. We find that LLMs tend to revert to feminine characters in these narratives, even when prompted without explicit gender references, and also when prompted with non-binary ("they/them") gender references for the main character. In some cases, even when prompted with masculine pronouns ("he/him"), LLMs still use feminine pronouns to describe the main character. The paper describes our dataset creation process and the evaluation of four open-weight models. We discuss implications for future research in mitigating implicit gender bias and its associated representational harms in LLMs, as well as the complex relationship between language models and societal values.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6861503`

- title: Automated Clinician-Style Retinal OCT Report Generation Using a Fine-Tuned Multimodal Large Language Model: Development and Masked Clinical Evaluation
- authors: Jalil Jalili, Anna Heinke, Nehal Nailesh Mehta, Amr Lotfy Ali, Mohamed Sherif Morsy, Yashraj Gavhane, Bo Wen, Dirk-Uwe Bartsch
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6861503
- keyword hits: large language model, llama, llm, llms

### abstract

Background: Interpretation of optical coherence tomography (OCT) imaging requires clinicians to translate complex imaging findings into clinical narrative reports, contributing substantially to documentation burden and physician burnout. Our goal is to develop and evaluate a fine-tuned multimodal large language model (LLM) for automated generation of clinician-style OCT reports to document retinal disease. Methods: We curated 80,102 macula OCT B-scan images paired with corresponding clinician-authored reports from 2,208 patients at a tertiary retina center (2011–2024). A multimodal LLM (Llama 3.2 Vision-Instruct) was fine-tuned to generate clinician-style reports from OCT B-scans. Performance was evaluated based on assessments by three masked expert graders. A set of 250 AI-generated and 250 clinician-authored reports were assessed for predicted source (AI vs. clinician) and three quality metrics (correctness, completeness, and conciseness) using a 5-point Likert scale. Intra- and inter-grader agreements as well as quantitative text similarity metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR, BERTScore) were also computed. Findings: In masked expert evaluations, graders demonstrated near-chance accuracy (49–55%) in distinguishing AI-generated from clinician-authored reports. Experts graded AI-generated reports as less accurate than clinician-authored reports (3.75 vs. 3.99, p=0.0002), but there were no significant differences between AI and clinician reports for completeness (4.13 vs. 4.23, p=0.08) or conciseness (4.21 vs. 4.13, p=0.14). Experts also graded reports that they perceived as clinician-authored significantly higher regardless of true source (p<0.0001). Inter-grader rater agreement was highest for conciseness (weighted 𝝹 0.52–0.58) and followed by correctness (weighted 𝝹 0.47–0.51) and completeness (weighted 𝝹 0.33–0.49).The AI-generated reports achieved high semantic similarity with clinician-authored reports (BERTScore F1 0.89) despite lower lexical overlap (BLEU 0.09, ROUGE-1 F1 0.36). Interpretation: A fine‑tuned multimodal LLM can generate retinal OCT reports that are indistinguishable from clinician-authored reports and comparable in correctness, completeness and conciseness to those written by clinicians. These findings suggest that LLMs can assist with clinical documentation, reduce reporting burden, and improve workflow efficiency in high-volume ophthalmic imaging clinics.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6810423`

- title: Efficiency Analysis of LLM-generated Dynamic Programming Code: A Benchmarking Study using EffiBench Framework
- authors: Aby Babu Alappat
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6810423
- keyword hits: gpt-4, gpt-5, llm, llms

### abstract

The widespread availability of LLMs has shown their capabilities in helping developers with automated code generation. In contrast, a number of studies have verified the accuracy of LLM-generated codes [2], [4], but little research has been done on code efficiency, in particular [8], [10]. The efficiency of GPT-5.4, GPT-5.4-mini, GPT-5.4-nano and GPT-4-turbo-four LLMs operating on LeetCode dynamic programming problems, is analysed in this paper [10]. I evaluate the codes on six metrics like Execution Time(ET), Max Memory Usage(MU), Total Memory Usage(TMU),Normalized Execution time (NET), Normalized Max Memory Usage (NMU) and Normalized Total Memory Usage (NTMU) on 277 dynamic programming problems using EffiBench framework [10]. The performance of humanwritten canonical solutions taken from the LeetCode repository has served as the basis for standardizing all metrics. The GPT-5.4 mini model has the best efficiency profile with a mean NET of 1.20, NMU of 1.06, and NTMU of 1.52, while the GPT-4turbo model has the highest resource consumption with a NET of 1.93, NMU of 1.26, and NTMU of 2.84. The results highlight the need to evaluate LLM-generated code based on efficiency, which is particularly important for dynamic programming algorithms [8], [11]. This research provides empirical data on the performance of recent LLMs in producing code for complicated algorithmic problems [13], pointing out the tradeoffs involved in the generation process with reference to model size, execution time, and memory consumption.
