## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7050283`

- title: Multimodal Depression Detection via Information Fusion: Methods, Benchmarks, and Emerging Directions
- authors: Xiangrui Meng, Peng Zhai, Xiping Hu, Xianhe Cheng, Yang Liu, Dingkang Yang, Zhen Huo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7050283
- keyword hits: foundation model, large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

Depression affects approximately 350 million people worldwide and represents a growing global health crisis, yet timely diagnosis remains hindered by severe shortages of mental health professionals. Automated depression detection through multimodal information fusion has emerged as a promising solution, with interview recordings and social media content providing complementary behavioral and linguistic signals that no single modality can fully capture. This review provides a comprehensive synthesis of multimodal depression detection research spanning 2013 to 2025, covering 157 studies on interview-based and social media-based approaches, alongside 19 studies on large language model (LLM)-driven methods. We systematically benchmark publicly available datasets, categorize fusion strategies into feature-level, decision-level, and model-level fusion, and analyze their respective strengths and limitations across diverse experimental settings. We further examine how LLMs are reshaping the field through zero-shot inference, data augmentation, and multimodal alignment. Finally, we identify critical open challenges including dataset standardization, cross-modal alignment, model interpretability, and real-world clinical deployment, and outline emerging research directions at the intersection of multimodal fusion and foundation models. This review aims to serve as a structured reference for researchers seeking to advance automated, scalable, and clinically grounded depression detection systems.

### abstract

The financial services industry is undergoing a paradigm shift as artificial intelligence evolves from passive automation to active agency. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6997818`

- title: A Pre-registered, Compute-Controlled Falsification of LLM-Derived Signals on Crypto Microstructure -and a Heterogeneous-Convergence Finding
- authors: Jasper Man Him Chan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6997818
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

We ask whether large language models (LLMs), reading a compressed order-book/trade-flow snapshot, produce signals that incrementally predict short-horizon crypto market dynamicsand, in particular, whether disagreement between heterogeneous base models predicts forward realized volatility (RV). We pre-register the hypotheses, the metric, the materiality thresholds, and the kill gates before computing any result, and we enforce a firewall that gates expensive live data collection behind cheap offline pilots. On real MEXC Spot BTC/USDT data (a 50,277snapshot corpus collected over two live runs), we report a four-way falsification: neither (i) single-model directional bias, (ii) single-model conviction magnitude, (iii) single-model sampling dispersion, nor (iv) inter-model disagreement clears its pre-registered incremental bar at 5/15/30minute horizons. The headline hypothesis-inter-model disagreement → forward RV-is killed decisively and triple-robustly at n ≈ 21,905 (15-minute partial Spearman +0.010, MBB 95% CI [-0.006, +0.027], well inside ±0.05). The mechanism is a positive, publishable finding in its own right: heterogeneous base models reading the same compressed snapshot converge on directional bias (in-sample rank agreement ≈ 0.49; best available cross-lineage decorrelation only |ρ| = 0.56), so the disagreement signal has little headroom and what little exists is orthogonal to volatility. The contribution is methodological: a pre-registered, provenance-fingerprinted, compute-controlled evaluation pipeline that distinguishes signal from the volatility-clustering confound (which inflates the marginal correlations roughly threefold), in a literature where over-claiming P&L backtests are the norm.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.05682v1`

- title: FirstResearch: Auditable Question Formation for LLM Scientific Discovery Agents
- authors: Yufeng Wang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-06
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05682v1
- keyword hits: deepseek, gemini, llm

### abstract

LLM systems for scientific discovery increasingly assist with ideation, literature synthesis, experiment planning, and report generation, but the first research question they propose can remain difficult to audit: it may sound plausible without exposing the mechanism, falsifier, or assumption that a scientist should inspect. We introduce FirstResearch, a first-principles research-question formation framework for scientific LLM agents whose core artifact is a structured Research Question Certificate. The certificate records primitive definitions, assumptions, a mechanism model, a tension or contradiction, a falsifiable hypothesis, a minimal decisive test, and a failure update rule, making the proposed question inspectable before downstream execution. On ten LLM-agent research topics, FirstResearch outperforms controlled prompt-level baselines inspired by AI co-scientist, Agent Laboratory, and AI Scientist-v2 under a primary DeepSeek-blind-judge protocol. A Gemini-2.5-Flash independent-judge rescore of the same 40 baseline packages preserves the system-level ranking, with FirstResearch scoring 4.86/5 versus 4.38/5 for the strongest baseline and Pearson agreement of 0.865 on average score. A one-repeat ablation checkpoint further suggests that the certificate-centered core is the strongest component: certificate-only scoring reaches 4.90/5 under DeepSeek and 4.88/5 under Gemini, while removing certificates drops below 1/5 under both judges. These results are preliminary and use LLM judges rather than human domain experts, but they support a narrow scientific-discovery claim: explicit derivation constraints are a promising mechanism for making LLM-generated scientific questions more auditable. Code, prompts, saved outputs, and reproduction scripts are available at https://github.com/louiswang524/FirstResearch.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.04640v1`

- title: Wrong Before Right: Late Rescue and Interface Failure in Aligned Language Models
- authors: Jiaqi Deng
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-06
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.04640v1
- keyword hits: llama, mistral, qwen

### abstract

We study how correctness is assembled inside aligned language models, not only whether the final answer is right. Using layer-wise difference-in-differences (DiD) trajectories over polarity-controlled minimal pairs, we identify the wrong-dip: in mid layers (25-90% depth), internal preference transiently commits to the incorrect answer and is rescued only by late-layer correction. We verify this causally with patchscope-style activation transplantation across 17 models, three families, and 64x scale (0.5B-32B). Four findings follow. (1) Alignment amplification of the causal wrong-dip is recipe-specific and emergent: it emerges at 3B in Qwen2.5, remains high, and peaks at 32B (paired t up to 9.7), reverses in Llama-3-8B (t=-2.31), and sits between for Mistral-7B. (2) The dip predicts real compression failures: high-dip items are 3-7x more likely to flip under late-layer low-rank compression, block dropping, or structured pruning, while quantization flips are dip-blind, a double dissociation confirmed by late-layer ablation. (3) The dip is trainable: a LoRA fine-tune with a mid-layer wrong-margin penalty matches output-only SFT accuracy while cutting the causal dip by 67-70% and improving compression robustness; output-only SFT worsens the causal dip by up to 2.8x at perfect surface accuracy. (4) With controlled readouts, the phenomenon survives natural-language I/O: dip stratification of structural-damage failures is significant on naturalistic vignettes, and free-form fragility separates into a dip-auditable late-rescue layer and a dip-blind interface layer. Together, output-level correctness can hide a late-rescue process that governs compression risk, post-training quality, and evaluation distortion.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7068678`

- title: Prediction-driven resource scheduling for QoS-aware edge–cloud LLM inference in pervasive mobile services
- authors: Weiyang Wu, Chi Zhang, Qiang Yao, ZhiWei Yang, ShuaiLiu Yuan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7068678
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, llms, qwen

### abstract

In pervasive mobile services such as smart transportation, mobile assistants and context-aware IoT applications, large language model (LLM) inference requests are increasingly served by edge–cloud infrastructures under strict time-to-first-token (TTFT) service-level objectives (SLOs). Edge inference of LLMs faces sharply fluctuating request loads, volatile resource utilization, and schedulers that lack foresight: reactive policies act only after congestion has formed, which inflates tail latency and timeout rates. This paper proposes a prediction-driven proactive resource scheduling method. Multivariate runtime states — request arrival rate, input token length, GPU utilization, KV-cache memory, queue length and bandwidth — are collected and fed to a PatchTST forecaster to anticipate near-future load and to derive three risk signals: congestion, memory overflow and SLO violation. A memory- and load-aware scheduler then jointly decides execution placement (edge / edge–cloud / cloud, where edge–cloud is fixed as edge-draft/cloud-verify speculative collaboration) and model tier (Qwen3 1.7B/4B/8B) by minimizing a cost over predicted TTFT, SLO-violation risk, energy and task-score loss, while a quantile-based robustness mechanism keeps scheduling stable under forecast error. Distinct from engine-level optimization and reactive offloading, the core of the method is a closed decision loop from multivariate forecasts to risk signals to joint placement-and-tier selection. Trace-driven simulation on workloads constructed from real load statistics and validation on a Jetson AGX Orin edge testbed show that, against static-threshold, reactive and DRL-based baselines, the method cuts the timeout rate from 4.79% (static threshold) to zero and reduces P99 TTFT by about 23% against the reactive baseline while keeping zero timeouts, with simultaneous gains in energy and normalized task score.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7074124`

- title: A Trustworthy Intent-Driven LLM-Agent Workflow with State Verification for Power System Simulation
- authors: Shanxiang Mao, Muyang Liu, Junru Chen, Dongliang Nan, Jian Ma
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7074124
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Modern power system simulation workflows, including model setup, parameter configuration, scenario construction, solver invocation, and result verification, remain heavily manual and procedurally intensive. Large language models (LLMs) offer a promising way to transform high-level engineering intents into structured and reviewable simulation workflows. Yet, as probabilistic models, LLMs may generate outputs that are incomplete, incorrectly ordered, or inconsistent with the active simulation state, posing risks to workflow reliability. This paper proposes a trustworthy intent-driven LLM-agent workflow with state verification for power system simulation. The proposed framework separates LLM-based workflow planning from solver-side execution. The LLM converts heterogeneous engineering instructions into structured and reviewable candidate workflows, while a state-verification mechanism checks workflow admissibility, state-dependency constraints, and execution evidence before solver-side results are accepted. Instead of evaluating only software executability, the proposed evaluation distinguishes planning validity from verified workflow completion, where a workflow is successful only when the executed workflow satisfies the required state conditions of the intended simulation task. A controlled benchmark on the New England 39-bus system evaluates robustness under different task-complexity levels and instruction-ambiguity variants, with ablation studies quantifying the roles of schema guidance, orchestration, and LLM backbone selection. A real-world 201-bus power system further assesses practical scalability in model preparation and N-1 contingency testing. Results show that the proposed workflow reduces low-level manual interaction, achieving approximately a 19-fold improvement in model-preparation workflow efficiency and a 4.5-fold speedup in N-1 contingency-test workflow execution.
