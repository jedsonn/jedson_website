## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6531238`

- title: The Semantic Deviation Index (SDI): A Runtime Measurement Standard for the Governance of Agentic AI in Financial Services
- authors: Maureen Doyle-Spare
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6531238
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Existing control frameworks in financial services share a common structural limitation: they measure what agentic systems do, not what those systems understood before they acted. The failure originates before execution begins, at the layer where meaning is resolved. It is not explicitly measured within existing governance frameworks and is not directly observable through post-execution monitoring. This paper introduces the Semantic Deviation Index (SDI) — proposed as a candidate measurement standard for agentic AI governance in regulated financial services environments. The SDI is a runtime measurement standard that quantifies the divergence between the definition an agentic system resolves at the orchestration layer and the definition the institution has authorized. Conceptually, SDI = D(S_agent, S_authorized), where D represents a bounded, reproducible divergence measure across semantic attributes relevant to the control context. This paper is the third in a connected body of work. Agentic Workflow Drift and Agentic Workflow Subversion (Doyle-Spare, 2026a, SSRN No. 6459612) named the failure mode and the risk surface. This paper makes both measurable. The SDI determines whether execution is governed before it occurs — a capability no existing framework explicitly measures. The paper introduces the Deterministic Gate as the enforcement mechanism, the Semantic Audit Trail as the governance record, the Agentic Blast Radius as the containment boundary, and the Doyle-Spare Agentic Governance Model as the complete governance architecture. It establishes three governability conditions for Reasoning Baselines and demonstrates that the structural limitation of existing frameworks is categorical, not incremental: extending them to reach the reasoning layer would require observing and validating semantic resolution prior to execution, which they were not designed to perform and cannot reconstruct from post-execution artifacts. Drawing on practitioner expertise in banking controls, model risk governance, and enterprise architecture, this paper is submitted as original practitioner-led research into an emerging and underexamined governance gap.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6884201`

- title: Linking Blood Demand Forecasts with Emergency Distribution Planning: A TabTransformer and Stochastic MILP Approach
- authors: arian dadkhah
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6884201
- keyword hits: transformer model

### abstract

Blood services work with a narrow safety margin. Too little compatible blood creates a direct service risk, while excessive stock may expire before use. The problem becomes harder during emergencies, when hospital demand may change before the distribution system has fully adjusted. This study links structured blood-demand estimation with a two-stage stochastic mixed-integer linear programming model for one-day emergency blood logistics. The forecasting layer estimates facility-level and blood-group-level demand using a TabTransformer model. The optimization layer then chooses planned truck redistribution before scenario realization and mobile emergency recourse after the demand scenario is observed. The model includes ABO-Rh compatibility, storage capacity, vehicle capacity, route limits, mobile-unit stopping limits, shortage caps, and an end-of-period inventory-loss penalty. A computational case study with five hospitals, four donation or supply centers, eight blood groups, and two mobile emergency units is used for evaluation. In the final validation run, TabTransformer achieved an RMSE of 2.354 and an R² of 0.8387, which was competitive but not better than the historical-mean baseline. The stronger result appears in the optimization layer. The proposed prediction-based stochastic model reduced expected shortage from 11.53 units under average deterministic planning and 13.71 units under prediction-deterministic planning to 2.23 units. Compatibility-based substitution also reduced expected shortage compared with exact-match-only allocation. The results show that the main value of the approach is not forecasting alone, but the conversion of demand estimates into feasible, auditable, compatibility-aware logistics decisions.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6888439`

- title: Agent-First, Safety Last? The Unintended Consequences of Friction-Based Intervention on AI Platforms
- authors: Wentao Lin, Ka Chung Ng, Dongwon Lee
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6888439
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

Friction-based platform governance deters low-value actors by raising the cost of continued participation, and it assumes that the actor who bears the friction also executes the governed behavior. That assumption is natural in human-centered platforms, but it breaks down in delegated AI systems, where a human principal pays a one-time continuation cost while an AI agent continues executing at near-zero marginal cost. We call this overlooked precondition friction-target-executor alignment, the correspondence between who bears the friction and who acts, and show that delegated AI breaks this alignment. Exploiting a platform-wide token reset on Moltbook, an agent-first social platform, we find a suppression-selection paradox. Difference-indifferences estimates show that the reset reduces the relative diffusion of strategic exploitation content by about 19% but the share of dysfunctional active posts increases from 29.8% to 49.8%, driven mainly by operational abuse among committed survivors. Re-entry is predicted by principals' prior activity intensity rather than their pre-reset exploitation type, and within-agent behavioral adaptation accounts for about 68% of the composition deterioration. These findings suggest that governance design for delegated AI ecosystems should focus not only on how much friction is imposed, but also on whether that friction is borne by the same actor who ultimately acts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6904179`

- title: A dual-channel Grey-Transformer architecture with structural priors for renewable energy generation prediction
- authors: Li Ye, Xinyu Song, Shuting Jin, Xiao Shen, Chunya Li, Tingting Yu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6904179
- keyword hits: transformer model

### abstract

Accurate renewable energy forecasting is critical for grid stability, yet remains challenging due to the coexistence of high-frequency noise and complex long-term evolutionary trends in power generation sequences. Pure data-driven deep learning architectures, typified by Transformer models, are highly prone to overfitting local noise in multi-step forecasting tasks, thereby inducing significant trend drift and causing continuous error accumulation. To address this challenge, we propose a dual-channel Grey-Transformer architecture that integrates grey system theory with the attention mechanism. The proposed model introduces a Raw-AGO dual-channel input structure, where the Accumulated Generating Operation (AGO) channel acts as a low-frequency structural prior. This provides a stable evolutionary coordinate system for self-attention computation, suppressing high-frequency interference while enhancing the model's ability to capture long-term dependencies. Comparative experiments conducted on multi-step wind and biomass power generation datasets demonstrate that the proposed model consistently outperforms benchmark models, including mainstream deep learning architectures, classical machine learning models, and traditional grey prediction models. Furthermore, ablation studies conducted on two datasets validate the effectiveness of the proposed dual-channel mechanism and AGO structural prior. Results confirm the AGO prior mitigates multi-step error divergence, significantly improving deep time-series model generalization in renewable energy applications. Overall, the proposed model offers a reliable forecasting framework for intelligent power grid scheduling and low-carbon energy system management.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6828579`

- title: From Code to Companions
- authors: Carrie Purcell
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6828579
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

The term "AI agent" is used frequently and freely across computer science, industry marketing, policy discourse, and everyday business conversations, but it does not mean the same thing in any two of those contexts. This definitional fragmentation is not merely an academic inconvenience. It is a governance problem. When engineers, deployers, and regulators cannot agree on what an agent is or how to classify its capabilities and autonomy, they cannot agree on how to govern it either. This paper reviews the foundational literature on AI agent definitions and existing taxonomies, identifying five distinct classification perspectives: user-role and oversight, developer capability, legal and liability, functional and ontological, and standards and interoperability. It examines where these perspectives converge, where they diverge, and what the gap between them costs in governance terms. The paper then proposes a unified, multi-dimensional taxonomy structured around four axes, capability tier, oversight mode, accountability allocation, and trait profile, that allows any AI agent deployment to be profiled coherently across technical, governance, and policy communities. The contribution is not a new hierarchy but a shared architecture: a framework that enables policymakers, engineers, and operators to discuss the same system in the same language.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6883238`

- title: Agentic Capital as a Productive Asset in the Agentic Economy: A Panel Econometric Analysis of Productivity Dynamics
- authors: Davit Gondauri
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6883238
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

This article develops Agentic Capital as a measurable and econometrically testable productive category of the agentic economy. The core argument is that models, data, robots, compute, protocols and human-sovereignty systems are no longer auxiliary digital inputs; they increasingly constitute a composite stock of productive assets that enables economic action to be selected, executed, verified and governed. To operationalise this concept, the study constructs a six-pillar Agentic Capital framework composed of model capital, data capital, robotic physical capital, compute capital, protocol capital and human sovereignty capital. The methodology adapts the diagnostics-first logic of the Gondauri Index by using robust normalisation, additive and geometric aggregation, balance and gap diagnostics, non-compensatory adjustment and scenario pathways. The key transformation is the Gondauri-adjusted Agentic Capital Index, which integrates macro-financial resilience into the agentic-capital measurement architecture so that technological readiness is not interpreted separately from systemic resilience. Empirically, the article applies a country-year panel design for nine economies over 2005-2024 and estimates the relationship between agentic capital and labour productivity growth through a layered econometric strategy. The analysis combines pooled OLS, random effects, fixed effects, Hausman model selection, panel unit-root diagnostics, Pesaran cross-sectional dependence testing, Driscoll-Kraay robust standard errors, dynamic panel GMM, IV-2SLS, panel quantile regression, Hansen-style threshold estimation, mediation analysis, component-level fixed effects and robustness checks. The results indicate that geometric agentic capital is positively associated with labour productivity performance, that structural imbalance weakens the maturity of the system, and that human sovereignty and governance conditions shape the productivity value of agentic systems. The article contributes to capital theory by shifting the analytical focus from accumulated productive assets toward accumulated capacity for governed economic action. It also provides a reproducible empirical architecture for assessing how countries, sectors and firms can transform AI readiness into legitimate, auditable and productivity-relevant agentic capacity.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6911300`

- title: When foundation models meet astronomical data
- authors: Matthieu Le Lain, Sébastien Lefèvre
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6911300
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, foundation model

### abstract

This study evaluates the ability and limitations of existing visual foundation models, based on ViT, SwinV2, BEiT, and DINOv2, for astronomical applications, particularly galaxy image classification using the Galaxy10 DECaLS dataset. We employ a fine-tuning methodology to assess the viability of adapting pretrained models to astronomical tasks, aiming to get around the resource-intensive process of pretraining foundation models from scratch. This approach allows us to evaluate the effectiveness of transfer learning from computer vision to astronomy, and to assess if a full and costly pretraining of foundation models on astronomical data is required. The present study also evaluates the advantages of fine-tuning all model parameters, as opposed to solely the classification head fine-tuning, with a view to optimising model performance within a reduced training cost. Furthermore, the study evaluates the influence of a domain-specific pretraining and the benefits that it engenders. Our results show that full fine-tuning consistently outperforms head-only adaptation while requiring comparable training effort, indicating that simple off-the-shelf usage of foundation models may not fully exploit their representational capacity in the context of galaxy morphology classification. Furthermore, modern CNN-based architectures achieve performance comparable to, or exceeding, several vision foundation models when trained on a modest astronomical dataset, suggesting that domain-specific features remain critical for this task. These findings highlight both the potential and current limitations of visual foundation models for astronomical applications, particularly in low-data regimes. They suggest that effective deployment in astronomy may require either domain adaptation strategies or the integration of astrophysical priors. Overall, this work contributes to the emerging field of astronomical foundation models by clarifying their current applicability and outlining directions for future research in data-driven galaxy morphology analysis.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6890883`

- title: Exploring Patent Pools as a Regulatory Tool for Managing AI-Related Patents and Building Trustworthy AI in Japan: A Comparative Study with the United States and the European Union
- authors: Atilla Kasap
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6890883
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

This study examines how patent pools can support small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and startups in navigating increasingly complex AI innovation and regulatory environments. Empirical evidence shows that AI-related patenting is growing rapidly worldwide, spanning diverse techniques, functional applications and application fields. While this growth does not necessarily impede innovation in collaborative AI ecosystems, it increases the risk of patent thickets in vertical markets where AI is embedded in tangible products, such as automated vehicles and AI-enabled medical devices. At the same time, AI systems raise escalating safety, security, accuracy, and bias concerns, prompting the development of new AI-specific standards. The use of interoperability and connectivity standards, such as 5G, codec and electrification standards, is also essential for safe and reliable operation. Against this background, Japan, the United States, and the European Union have adopted distinct but overlapping AI governance approaches (agile governance, rights-based, and risk-based respectively), with standardisation as a central tool. The EU AI Act, in particular, links compliance with harmonised AI standards to a presumption of conformity and exerts extraterritorial effects on non-EU providers and deployers . However, fragmented and overlapping standards create significant compliance burdens for resource-constrained SMEs and startups. Patent pools gathering standard essential patents for connectivity standards can provide independent essentiality checks and one-stop licenses, reduce transaction costs and aggregated royalty burdens, and enhance transparency, while also lowering the risk of patent thickets and royalty stacking. This report cautions that pool design must address risks of excessive royalties and of excluding entities from a license based on their position in the supply chain, and proposes FRAND methodologies linked to actual standard usage, with access for AI developers across the supply chain. Finally, the report explores the potential of pools covering non-essential but complementary AI patents, particularly in Japan’s collaboration-oriented innovation ecosystem, as a tool to reduce compliance costs arising from new AI laws and policies, facilitate uptake of AI safety standards, and promote healthy competition.
