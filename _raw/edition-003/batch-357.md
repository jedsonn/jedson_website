## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7031578`

- title: AI Penalty: Insights From the US Corporate Bond Market
- authors: Tianqi Luo, Mathieu Mercadier, Anh Vu, Lu Xu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7031578
- keyword hits: chatgpt

### abstract

This paper examines whether sectoral artificial intelligence (AI) intensity is reflected in corporate bond pricing at issuance. Using a sample of corporate bonds issued by U.S. firms between 2015 and 2025, we find an "AI penalty" that bonds issued by firms in more AI-intensive sectors exhibit higher yield spreads. The positive association is stronger in bonds with maturities above three years and becomes more pronounced after the launch of ChatGPT. Among the four dimensions of AI intensity defined by the OECD taxonomy, our results suggest that AI use is associated with the largest increase in issuance spreads, whereas AI human capital, AI innovation, and AI exposure show weaker or statistically insignificant associations.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7042398`

- title: The Chartered Agent: How the City of London Wrote the Legal Architecture of Agentic Commerce in 1555
- authors: Paul F. Accornero
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7042398
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

The rise of autonomous AI shopping and procurement agents has reopened the debate on legal personhood for artificial intelligence. This paper argues that, for the domain of commerce, the debate is asking the wrong question. Commerce does not need AI persons; it needs chartered agents, and the legal template already exists. The paper traces a single institutional arc: from the civic autonomy of the City of London (1067, 1215), through the guild system's invention of collective non-human legal actors, to the chartering of the Muscovy Company on February 6, 1555, the first major English chartered joint-stock company. That charter solved a problem structurally identical to the one autonomous AI agents pose today: how to let a non-human construct transact, hold assets, bear obligations, and absorb liability on behalf of human principals operating in an environment that exceeds individual human capacity. The paper then connects this history to the Shopper Schism, the author's framework describing the structural disaggregation of the consumer function (retained by humans) from the shopper function (delegated to algorithms), and shows that the joint-stock company and the algorithmic shopper are two instances of the same 1 delegation architecture, applied first to the supply side of commerce and now, for the first time, to the demand side. Reviewing the legal record, including Thaler v Comptroller-General [2023] UKSC 49, Moffatt v Air Canada (2024), the CFTC enforcement action against Ooki DAO, and the Wyoming, Tennessee, and Utah DAO statutes, the paper shows that courts consistently refuse to invent AI personhood while legislatures are quietly converging on the chartered-company architecture. It concludes with a Chartered Agent framework for agentic commerce and a research agenda spanning management, law, and economic history.

- title: Reimagining Assessment of Learning in the Era of Generative Artificial Intelligence
- authors: Aurelio Aguite Jr
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7044938
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Introduction-The rapid integration of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) renders traditional, artifact-based higher education assessments obsolete. This study explores how institutions must transition toward evaluating the holistic learning process rather than static end products. Methodology-A Narrative Literature Review was employed to synthesize interdisciplinary evidence, encompassing quantitative AI adoption trends, qualitative cognitive judgment evaluations, and multidimensional institutional readiness frameworks. Results-Findings indicate that the GenAI disruption constitutes a "wicked problem" requiring a shift to a Postplagiarism Framework, where human-AI co-authorship is standard practice. Traditional assessment formats are highly vulnerable, necessitating structural redesigns such as interactive oral vivas and process portfolio audits. Furthermore, standardized models like the AI Assessment Scale (AIAS) and the GENESIS framework are crucial for establishing explicit permissibility levels and executing systemic programmatic reform. Discussion-Higher education must abandon punitive detection strategies in favor of cultivating students' evaluative judgment and critical AI literacy. Implementing resilient, process-driven evaluations combined with synchronous checkpoints is essential to secure the validity of educational credentialing in an AI-saturated learning landscape.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7026958`

- title: Text And Data Mining Exceptions in the Generative AI Era: Preserving European Innovation and Competitiveness
- authors: Marco Scialdone
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7026958
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid ascension of generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) has sparked intense debate surrounding copyright frameworks and upstream data ingestion. This paper examines the critical role of Text and Data Mining (TDM) exceptions, specifically under Articles 3 and 4 of the EU Copyright in the Digital Single Market (CDSM) Directive, as the foundational infrastructure for European AI development. By analyzing historical technological shifts, recent European legislative implementations (including the Italian Copyright Law updates and the EU AI Act), and comparative international models like Japan's "non-enjoyment" framework, this brief argues against protectionist rollbacks. Restricting TDM exceptions introduces severe market consolidation risks, drives innovation out of the European Union, and exposes consumers to localized algorithmic biases from foreign-trained models. Instead, this paper advocates for a balanced approach that pairs robust TDM exceptions with standardized machine-readable opt-outs and statutory monetization mechanisms at the commercialization phase.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7158180`

- title: Technological Opportunity Identification via Multidimensional Technology Landscape: From Technology Element Expansion to Solution Generating
- authors: Jinfeng Wang, DUAN YAN, Yicheng Feng, Weiyu Zhao, Lijie Feng, SONG XIAOZHAO, WANG NINGTAO
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7158180
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

Technological innovation is recognized as a primary driver of manufacturing competitiveness. Among various information sources, patent documents contain rich and structured technical information and have therefore become a key basis for technological opportunity identification (TOI). However, the increasing complexity of manufacturing systems and the demand for production flexibility have exposed the limitations of the previous TOI methods, especially their limited semantic precision and technological homogeneity, which restricts their practical effectiveness and lead to innovation myopia. To address these issues, a method that integrates BERTopic topic modelling with the F-term classification system is proposed in this paper to achieve technology topic migration and technology element expansion. This approach enhances the granularity of technology elements and the depth of semantic analyses while introducing diverse technical concepts through cross-domain migration to facilitate technology fusion and reveal new technological opportunities. The approach consists of four phases: 1) collecting data from Derwent; 2) extracting technology topics on the basis of the BERTopic model; 3) screening using LLMs and NER based on the technical elements of the F system expansion.; and 4) constructing a multidimensional technology landscape and identifying technological opportunities. In this paper, the effectiveness of the proposed method is demonstrated by applying it to automatic aircraft riveting technology. The results indicate that the identified technological opportunities exhibit high granularity and strong practical applicability, facilitating the iterative development of automatic riveting processes and fostering technological innovations within aerospace manufacturing enterprises.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7163013`

- title: Tri-path DINO: Feature Complementary Learning for Remote Sensing Multi-Class Change Detection
- authors: Kai Zheng, Hang-Cheng Dong, Zhenkai Wu, Fupeng Wei, Qin Huang, Wei Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7163013
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

In remote sensing imagery, multi-class change detection (MCD) is crucial for fine-grained monitoring,yet it has long been constrained by complex scene variations and the scarcity of detailed annotations. To address this, we propose the Tri-path DINO architecture, which adopts a three-path complementary feature learning strategy to facilitate the rapid adaptation of pre-trained foundation models to complex vertical domains. Specifically, we employ the DINOv3 pre-trained model as the backbone feature extraction network to learn coarse-grained features. An auxiliary path also adopts a siamese structure, progressively aggregating intermediate features from the siamese encoder to enhance the learningof fine-grained features. Finally, a multi-scale attention mechanism is introduced to augment the decoder network, where parallel convolutions adaptively capture and enhance contextual information under different receptive fields. The proposed method achieves optimal performance on the MCD task on both the Gaza facility damage assessment dataset (Gaza-change) and the classic SECOND dataset. Grad-CAM visualizations further confirm that the main and auxiliary paths naturally focus on coarse-grained semantic changes and fine-grained structural details, respectively. This synergistic complementarity provides a robust and interpretable solution for advanced change detection tasks, offering a basis for rapid and accurate damage assessment.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7160963`

- title: Does Artificial Intelligence Displace Young Workers? Regional Evidence from the 2024 Generative-AI Diffusion in Europe
- authors: Claudio Costanzo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7160963
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Automation is widely expected to displace young, entry-level workers, and the 2024 arrival of generative AI raised that fear for cognitive work. This paper examines the youth labour-market effects of the 2024 generative-AI diffusion across European regions, and finds the opposite of displacement. I measure each NUTS-2 region's exposure to firm AI adoption with a shift-share index - its 2010 industry mix weighted by EU-wide, industry-level AI-adoption rates - and exploit the sharp 2024 break in adoption in a continuous difference-in-differences design. Over the diffusion's first two years, more-exposed regions saw their youth labour markets improve: the NEET rate (15-29 year-olds not in employment, education or training) fell about 1.4 percentage points per standard deviation of exposure, youth unemployment fell, and young-adult (25-34) employment rose, while a rise in young-adult (20-24) education participation is a weaker, secondary margin. The pattern is one of labour demand, consistent with early complementarity rather than displacement, and it loads on the 2024 break rather than the preceding decade of digital diffusion.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7049319`

- title: Agentic Banking Security: A Governance Framework for Authenticating and Controlling Autonomous AI Agents in Financial Services
- authors: Muhammad Hasan Syed
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7049319
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

The rise of autonomous artificial intelligence (AI) agents acting on behalf of retail and commercial banking customers represents a structural shift in how financial institutions must approach digital security. Industry data indicates that automated traffic now constitutes the majority of internet activity directed at financial services platforms, and a growing share of that traffic originates not from conventional bots but from AI agents capable of maintaining session context, executing multi-step transactions, and interacting with banking systems exclusively through application programming interfaces (APIs). This paper examines the security implications of agentic banking, contrasts the risk profile of agent-initiated transactions against traditional human-initiated journeys, and proposes a vendor-agnostic, four-pillar governance framework-Identify, Classify, Authenticate, Respondfor financial institutions seeking to enable agentic access without compromising fraud controls, regulatory compliance, or customer trust. The paper further outlines a phased twelve-month implementation roadmap and situates the framework within existing regulatory instruments, including the EU's PSD2 Open Banking regime, the Financial-grade API (FAPI) 2.0 standard, and the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA). The central argument is that blanket blocking of non-human traffic is neither technically sustainable nor commercially viable, and that financial institutions must instead adopt risk-proportionate, identity-aware controls purpose-built for a traffic landscape in which the majority of interactions are no longer humaninitiated.
