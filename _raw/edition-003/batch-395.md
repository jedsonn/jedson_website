## uid: `arxiv:2606.16326v2`

- title: Gaming-Resistant Insurance Contracts for Autonomous AI Agents: Strategy-Proof Toll Mechanism Design
- authors: Hao-Hsuan Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-15
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.16326v2
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

Paper A defines a time-consistent actuarial runtime that prices each side-effect-bearing action against a contractually fixed safe default and gates execution against a reserve budget. It treats the operator as passive. This paper makes the operator strategic. We characterise a five-attack space for autonomous AI-agent insurance contracts and prove when the actuarial runtime is gaming-resistant. Two attack surfaces -- post-toll safe-default selection and within-boundary action splitting -- are closed by Paper A's minimal-authority and no-splitting clauses. The remaining three require new contract clauses. First, common-control aggregation prevents cross-boundary re-routing from reducing toll below the boundary potential applied to total exposure. Second, interface failures such as invalid JSON are contract-relevant events, not safety wins: treating them as zero-toll safe defaults can reward unreliable models, while escalation fees reverse the incentive. We validate this interface-compliance theorem on committed cross-model traces from the companion empirical paper. Third, a model-identity menu with a componentwise-minimum penalty schedule makes truthful reporting of the deployed model weakly dominant. We then compose these clauses with Paper A's runtime guarantees to obtain joint incentive compatibility over the five-attack space. Finally, a two-parameter premium family discharges operator individual rationality and weak budget balance at the truthful equilibrium. The result is an incentive-compatibility layer for actuarial control of autonomous-agent side effects.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6949565`

- title: From Augmentation to Substitution: Delegation Habits, Skill Atrophy, and the Limits of Trust Calibration in Human-AI Collaboration
- authors: Samuele Dell&apos;Oca, Valentina Rotondi, Vincenzo Cutrona, Giuseppe Landolfi, Andrea Bettoni, Luca Canetta
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6949565
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

The accelerating deployment of agentic artificial intelligence in industrial work environments has exposed a gap between what the dominant theoretical lens, trust calibration, was built to explain and the behavioral reality of sustained human-AI interaction. Trust calibration theory accounts for episodic reliance decisions informed by an appraisal of system reliability; whereas in repeated, embedded AI use, reliance behavior progressively decouples from that appraisal. The result is automatized delegation routines that operate below the threshold of deliberate decision-making, and measurable degradation of the cognitive capabilities those routines replace. This critical review synthesizes four bodies of literature (human-AI trust research, habit psychology, automation complacency from aviation and Human-Robot Collaboration, and cognitive offloading theory) into the Delegation Habit Framework (DHF), a conceptual model that reconceptualizes human-AI reliance as a habit-formation process unfolding in four progressive stages: formation, crystallization, divergence, and skill atrophy. The framework repositions industrial knowledge work as the next frontier of a dynamic already documented in physical human-machine teaming, where automation complacency and capability degradation have long been recognized as safety-critical outcomes. We operationalize the DHF through adapted self-report measures of behavioral automaticity, experience sampling protocols, skill retention assessments, and behavioral indicators derivable from industrial information systems, and articulate a structured research agenda with eight prioritized research questions. Implications for system design, organizational AI governance, and regulatory compliance are discussed. The dividing line between augmentation and substitution, we argue, is behavioral: AI augments capability only while delegation remains a deliberate choice.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6951954`

- title: From Tragedy to Transition: Severe Accidents and the Recalibration of Urban Land Allocation in China
- authors: Xin Lin, Dong Zhang, Jianfu Shen, Eddie Hui
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6951954
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Do severe accidents trigger substantive responses in top-down regimes? Using Chinese industrial land transaction data and a staggered difference-in-differences approach, we find that accidents recalibrate local officials’ political risk, prompting substantive adjustments in land allocation. Specifically, local officials prioritize direct risk mitigation strategies by reducing high-risk land supply, rather than adaptation strategies by increasing low-risk supply to foster diversification. We identify three potential mechanisms behind the substantive responses: supervisory chilling effects, career-incentive reorientation and the routinization of risk management. Our findings highlight how top-down accountability enforces substantive, risk-reducing governance through strategic land market interventions.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6852042`

- title: Deployer Obligations under the EU AI Act: Article 26, Provider Boundaries, and Governance Architecture
- authors: Michael Clark
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6852042
- keyword hits: prompt engineering

### abstract

The EU AI Act establishes a substantive set of obligations for organisations that deploy AI systems supplied by third parties, a category that covers the vast majority of enterprise AI use. Article 26′s eleven paragraphs include use in accordance with provider instructions, human oversight assignment with competence and authority, input data quality where the deployer controls inputs, monitoring and incident reporting with a suspension obligation, log retention for at least six months, employer information of workers before workplace deployment, EU database registration by public authority deployers, use of Article 13 information for the GDPR Article 35 DPIA, law enforcement post-biometric identification rules, and transparency to natural persons affected by Annex III decisions. These obligations apply cumulatively with GDPR controller responsibilities, NIS2 cybersecurity requirements, DORA ICT risk management, and sector-specific model governance frameworks including EBA GL/2020/06, PRA SS1/23, and NHS DCB0160. Article 27 imposes a fundamental rights impact assessment obligation on a defined subset of deployers, not all of them. This paper analyses each Article 26 paragraph in depth, examines the circumstances under which deployers become subject to provider-equivalent obligations under Article 25 (covering the substantial modification, prompt engineering, and RAG boundary questions), prescribes the contractual architecture required at the provider-deployer interface, analyses the API access special case, and proposes a practical deployer governance programme with three worked examples covering NHS clinical AI, financial services credit AI, and multinational recruitment AI deployments.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6855560`

- title: The Token Economy: Compute Power, Automation, and the Future of Work in an Ecosystem of Human Labor, Capital, and Artificial Intelligence
- authors: Alejandro J. Guipe Salazar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6855560
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Compute power has become a first-order economic variable. The cost of processing a token-the elementary unit of information that artificial-intelligence (AI) models consume and produce-has fallen roughly 280-fold in two years, even as the compute used to train frontier models grows almost fivefold per year and the largest cloud providers invest more than half a trillion dollars annually in data centers. By May 2026 a single provider, Google, was processing 3.2 quadrillion tokens per month, a sevenfold annual increase. This paper integrates that emerging token economy with the formal economics of automation. Building on the task-based framework of Zeira (1998) and Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018, 2019), it treats compute as a new factor of production and derives a firm-level automate-or-hire rule in which a task is automated only when its token cost falls below its labor cost. The central contribution is to show that this boundary is non-monotonic: because agentic systems can consume up to a thousand times more tokens per task, the cost of automating a task can rise above the cost of human labor even as unit token prices fall-a mechanism consistent with 2026 reports that AI can cost more than the employees it replaces. The paper connects these results to macro estimates (Acemoglu, 2025), transition scenarios (Korinek & Suh, 2024), and 2025-2026 evidence on adoption, corporate spending, and provider economics, and discusses three scenarios for an ecosystem of human labor, capital, and AI.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6948577`

- title: Data analytics investment for delivery timeliness in instant retail: The role of consumer privacy concern in a two-period framework
- authors: Zonghuo Li, Lingchen Huang, Xiangfeng Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6948577
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Delivery timeliness plays a critical role in instant retail, prompting platforms to leverage consumer historical data to enhance delivery speed. Nevertheless, growing consumer privacy concerns complicate data application and may diminish its effectiveness. We develop a game-theoretic model to investigate an online platform’s data analytics investment strategy when collaborating with a physical retailer to provide instant service. Two cases regarding whether the platform invests in data analytics and four configurations concerning whether consumers have privacy concerns across two periods are modeled. Results show that the platform is motivated to invest in data analytics to facilitate delivery timeliness, even when consumers’ privacy concern degree and data handling cost are high. Yet, the investment may prove uneconomical when consumers’ privacy concern degree is low, owing to a high data leakage risk. Results also indicate that the platform conducts the strongest data analytics effort in the scenario where consumers transition from being privacy-unconcerned to privacy-concerned over the two periods. In this situation, the retailer can set the highest price because delivery timeliness is superior. Nonetheless, the platform and retailer derive the highest revenue in this scenario only if consumers’ privacy concern degree is sufficiently high. Otherwise, the most profitable scenario turns out to be the scenario where consumers are privacy-concerned in both periods. Interestingly, when consumers are privacy-concerned in both periods, data analytics investment consistently enhances both consumer surplus and social welfare. Extension discussions confirm the robustness of our model, including consumers being time-insensitive, platforms’ self-operated instant delivery, and multiple delivery time settings.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6950571`

- title: Evaluating Hybrid and Standalone Deep Learning Models for Crude Oil Price Forecasting under Volatile Market Conditions
- authors: Zahra Rezaei, Sara  Safi Samghabadi, Mohammad amin Amini, Yaser  Michael Banad
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6950571
- keyword hits: transformer model

### abstract

Accurate forecasting of crude oil prices is difficult because energy markets are nonlinear, non-stationary, and highly volatile. Reliable forecasts are important for market monitoring, operational planning, investment decisions, and risk assessment across the energy sector. This study evaluates the forecasting performance of deep learning architectures for West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil price prediction using daily data from June 2015 to June 2025. The dataset was enhanced with commonly used technical indicators, including the Relative Strength Index (RSI), Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD), 10-day Moving Average (MA10), and trading volume, to capture momentum, trend behavior, and market activity. A comprehensive statistical characterization was first conducted to examine the underlying structure of the WTI time series. The analysis confirmed key challenges associated with crude oil forecasting, including non-stationarity, autocorrelation, volatility clustering, and heterogeneous variance. To address these complex temporal dynamics, three deep learning models were developed and compared under a unified experimental framework: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), a Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) network, and a hybrid CNN-BiLSTM-Transformer model. Hyperparameters for all architectures were systematically optimized using Optuna to support a fair and reproducible comparison.The results show that all three models captured meaningful temporal patterns in crude oil price movements. The optimized standalone CNN achieved the best test performance, with R² = 0.9025 and RMSE = 0.0122, followed closely by the BiLSTM model, with R² = 0.8993 and RMSE = 0.0123. The hybrid CNN-BiLSTM-Transformer model also achieved competitive performance, with R² = 0.8483 and RMSE = 0.0151, demonstrating its ability to integrate local feature extraction, sequential dependency learning, and attention-based representation.Overall, the findings indicate that greater architectural complexity does not necessarily improve crude oil price forecasting accuracy. This study highlights the importance of rigorous model optimization and provides empirical guidance for AI-based energy market forecasting and decision support.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6957336`

- title: Video-based Abnormal Human Activity Recognition: A Systematic Evaluation and Adaptation of Vision-Language Models
- authors: Suhel Khan, Shiqi Zhou, Chao Fu, Yanwu Xu, Si Yong Yeo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6957336
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, qwen

### abstract

Health-related abnormal behavior recognition has important applications in public safety monitoring, elderly care, and healthcare assistance. However, behaviors such as falls, vomiting, and choking are often difficult to recognize because they usually occur infrequently, last for a short duration, and may visually resemble normal daily activities. Although recent vision language models (VLMs) have shown strong performance in general visual understanding tasks, their capability in health-related anomaly recognition remains insufficiently explored. In this work, a video dataset containing falls, vomiting, choking events, and normal activities was constructed using publicly available videos collected from online platforms. Based on this dataset, eight open-source VLMs were systematicallyevaluated under a unified zero-shot setting using the same prompt strategy. The results showthat existing models can recognize certain abnormal behaviors, but their performance decreases considerably when the task additionally requires judging whether immediate assistance is needed. According to the evaluation results, Qwen3-VL-2B-Instruct was selected for a two-stage fine-tuning process. The first stage used only real-world videos, while the second stage further incorporated generated videos produced by Wan2.2-TI2V-5B. After fine-tuning, the model achieved substantial improvements in both recognition accuracy and response consistency. Additional performance gains were observed for categories with limited real-world samples, particularly vomiting and choking. The results demonstrate that task-specific adaptation and generated video augmentation are effective for improving health-related abnormal behavior recognition using vision-language models.
