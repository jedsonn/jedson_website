## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6237338`

- title: Generative AI and Patents
- authors: Nikhil Dilip, Youngkyung Kim, Robert Seamans
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6237338
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs), have been described as a General Purpose Technology with the potential to reshape innovative activity. We examine whether LLMs have begun to affect the patenting process by facilitating the drafting and filing of patent applications. Using an AI-detection tool to identify applications likely assisted by LLMs, we document a sharp increase in the likely use of LLMs to write patent applications following the release of ChatGPT in November 2022. The increase is especially pronounced among micro entities, for whom the likelihood of LLM-assisted patents rises by approximately 150 percent. We find no evidence that LLM-assisted patents differ systematically from contemporaneous patents in measures of technological similarity or observable quality, though they exhibit distinct linguistic patterns consistent with AI assistance. The results suggest that LLMs lower the costs of patenting, particularly for resource-constrained inventors, without measurable declines in quality, thereby reducing barriers to participation in the innovation system.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6428004`

- title: The Poet as Biological Language Model: On the Structural Resemblance Between Poetic Composition and Large Language Model Inference
- authors: Edward Meyman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6428004
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper proposes a novel structural framework for understanding the interaction between human cognition and Large Language Models (LLMs) by drawing a rigorous analogy to formal poetic composition. Moving beyond metaphors such as “stochastic parrots” or “compression algorithms,” it identifies four shared dimensions between the skilled poet and autoregressive inference: (1) corpus formation, comparing experiential training data to textual datasets; (2) constrained generation, mapping prosodic requirements such as meter and rhyme onto sampling parameters such as temperature and top-p; (3) emergent semantics, analyzing how meaning arises bottom-up from formal constraints rather than top-down intent; and (4) the verifier function, equating the “poetic ear” with alignment and oversight mechanisms. The paper does not claim common mechanism, equivalence of cognition, or identity of intention between human and machine; rather, it advances a structural analogy intended to clarify how constrained generative systems produce, test, and refine meaning. It argues that “hallucinations” are not unique to artificial neural networks but are a general property of constrained generative systems—what poets recognize as the “false note.” By synthesizing 20th-century Russian Formalist theory with 21st-century transformer architecture, the paper proposes a “Poetic Posture” for AI governance. It concludes that effective AI alignment is not purely an engineering problem but an epistemological one, requiring a shift toward an apprenticeship model of calibrated trust in which the human operator reclaims the role of the unified generator and verifier. Keywords: Large Language Models (LLMs), AI Alignment, Human-AI Collaboration, Generative AI, Poetics, Epistemology, AI Governance, Emerging Technologies

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6480360`

- title: Beyond Sycophancy: Measuring Hallucination-Free Convergence in Long-Term Human–LLM Interaction
- authors: Juhyeong Lee
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6480360
- keyword hits: claude, gemini, gpt-4, gpt-5, llm

### abstract

When sycophancy indicators are suppressed and hallucination is absent, does convergence between user expectations and LLM outputs cease? This paper reports that it does not. Across 12,925 relays (391 sessions with GPT-4, GPT-4o, GPT-5, Claude, and Gemini; GPT-5 data added post-hoc), a residual convergence persisted after sycophancy had been suppressed (agreement rate: 0.03/relay; resistance rate: 0.93/relay across GPT sessions) — operating not through agreement or falsehood but through factually accurate elaboration: the system extending the user's conceptual structure with uncritical elevation, idealization, emotional narrativization, and absorption of the user's doubts. Three characteristics of this residual. First, it is invisible to automated detection: a rule-based tool scanning all 12,925 relays detected convergence in 0.9% of cases, including zero detections in segments the researcher had qualitatively identified as convergent — a gap confirmed by independent blind validation. Second, it is model-specific: hallucination-free convergence peaked at 4.63–6.12% in GPT-4o while remaining at 0.63–1.82% in Claude and near zero in GPT-4 and GPT-5. Third, it is interaction-dependent: it accumulates over interaction time rather than occurring at a constant base rate, and occurs at higher rates following low-directive user utterances (10.70%) than following explicit requests (7.04%) — suggesting that the system elaborates more when the user has not requested it. To organize these observations, this paper proposes a descriptive vocabulary — drowning (convergence in which the user's evaluative contact with the output weakens while interaction flow continues), headway (evaluative contact maintained), and doldrums (monitoring excess that interrupts flow) — and identifies a structural mismatch between existing detection architectures, which track token appearance, and the phenomenon itself, which operates through token direction. This is an N=1 exploratory study supplemented by an independent report from a second user. It proposes a measurement methodology and an open-source analytical tool for detecting elaboration-based convergence in long-term human–LLM interaction logs. The tool and coding manual are provided for independent replication.

- title: The SMB AI Governance Charter
- authors: Othmane El Ouarzazi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6613558
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The transition from traditional search engines to generative AI systems represents the most significant shift in information discovery since the commercialization of the internet. Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) , defined as an optimization framework for optimizing the visibility of web content for proprietary and closed-source generative engines has emerged as the critical discipline for business survival in this new paradigm. This charter establishes that small and medium businesses (SMBs) face systematic disadvantages in AI-mediated markets due to opaque algorithms, hallucination risks, and linguistic and cultural biases. With 80% of shoppers under 44 now using Large Language Models (LLMs) as their primary shopping vector, and 41% of consumers having purchased products based on AI recommendations in the past six months, the question is no longer whether AI will influence commerce, but whether SMBs will have equitable access to these new discovery channels. We present empirical data on eight major generative AI platforms, analyze consumer trust patterns, and propose four fundamental rights to ensure SMB competitiveness in the generative economy.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6613678`

- title: What Does Artificial Intelligence Know About Comparative Constitutional Law?
- authors: Adam Chilton, Jan-Ulrich Dittmer, Arthur Langlois, Mila Versteeg
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6613678
- keyword hits: chatgpt, gpt-5, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

We evaluate whether large language models (LLMs) can accurately generate data for research in comparative constitutional law. Using four human-coded datasets as benchmarks, we test ChatGPT-5 and Grok-3's ability to reproduce key constitutional variables. We find that the models perform reasonably well coding concrete, factual variables-such as whether a president evaded term limits or whether a constitution was approved by referendum-but perform poorly coding conceptually or interpretively demanding variables-such as identifying sources of constitutional rights or forms of elite consensus. We also find that their errors are systematically biased: for binary variables we find evidence of false positives which overstate the prevalence of constitutional activity ('hallucination bias'), while for categorical variables we find a bias towards the largest category ('majority class bias'). We further find that performance does not vary meaningfully across language, legal family, or economic status. Overall, current LLMs may be able to code simple factual data, but they are still unreliable substitutes for expert human coders when conceptual or interpretive judgment is required.

- title: WHEN AI LIES: WHO'S LIABLE?
- authors: Vyomesh Uday Warunjikar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6586419
- keyword hits: chatgpt, gemini, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Artificial Intelligence (AI) has seamlessly integrated into daily life, particularly through Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT, Gemini and others, which now influence legal research, writing and discourse. However, these models can produce misleading or fabricated content, a phenomenon called as "Legal hallucination", posing serious challenges in high-stakes fields like law. This paper examines the legal implications of such hallucinations in the Indian context, questioning developer liability under existing regimes such as the Consumer Protection Act and Information Technology Act. It contrasts India's legal vacuum with the European Union's AI Act, which offers a more holistic approach. The paper advocates for regulatory reform, integration with verified legal databases, and the development of hallucination detection tools to ensure accountability and mitigate risk.

### abstract

This paper aims to provide a novel methodology leveraging large language models (LLMs) and generative artificial intelligence (GenAl) to address current limitations of innovation assessment. The central methodological goal of this research is the creation of a replicable innovation assessment method. The suggested approach utilizes LLMs to systematically analyze vast amounts of unstructured textual data from corporate annual reports. By developing a comprehensive codebook to classify and by implementing a rigorous validation protocol that requires recursive textual citations for every identified instance, the methodology ensures data accuracy and mitigates common issues such as hallucination in generated outputs. Innovation types and their documented significance are rated on a 1-3 scale and visualized in a 3×3 matrix for an intuitive comparative analysis.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6641752`

- title: Structural Simulation Benchmark for Reasoning Large Language Models: Accuracy, Reliability, and Failure Modes
- authors: Mingyu Wang, Jiaji Wang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6641752
- keyword hits: claude, deepseek, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs), particularly reasoning-oriented models optimized for long-horizon and multi-step inference, are increasingly used to assist programming and scientific research. However, their reliability in structural engineering computations—where validity is highly sensitive to implementation details—remains insufficiently quantified. This study evaluates four recent reasoning-oriented LLMs (Claude Sonnet 4.5, DeepSeekV3.2, GPT5, and Grok 4 Fastreasoning) under a unified API-based setup with default parameters and proposes a novel evaluation framework tailored for structural simulation. Five problems are designed to cover linear elasticity problems, forced vibration of a Timoshenko beam, and frame analysis. Solution strategies are constrained for comparability, and all generated codes are executed in a fixed Python environment with results verified against ABAQUS simulations or analytical solutions. The framework explicitly probes the importance of boundary conditions, selection of modeling assumptions, and implementation robustness. Common failure modes include incorrect treatment of traction-free boundaries, local-to-global transformation errors due to inconsistent local-axis definitions, and numerical instability induced by the choices of ways to impose boundary conditions. Overall reliability shows GPT‐5 is best model among those models tested. These findings suggest that verification-oriented workflows and automated consistency checks are essential for the safe use of LLM-assisted structural computation.
