- title: A Survey on Agent Skills for LLMs: A Lifecycle Perspective from Construction to Ecosystems
- authors: Wang Yang, Chaoda Song, Xinpeng Li, Shouren Wang, Nengbo Wang, Yanyan Zhang, Chuang Ma, Debargha Ganguly
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6746498
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

Skills-modular, reusable units of agent capability ranging from prompt templates to executable artifacts such as SKILL.md have emerged as a central abstraction for LLMs to accumulate, compose, and refine experience. Yet research on skills remains fragmented, with rapid progress scattered across construction, usage, training, safety, and evaluation. We present a unified, lifecycle-oriented article of skill-based agent systems organized into six interconnected dimensions: Construction and Evolution, Access and Composition, Model Integration via Training, Safety, Evaluation and Feedback, and Skill-Native Systems and Ecosystems. A complementary taxonomy characterizes skills by representation, source, granularity, and role. For each dimension we surface structural challenges and forward-looking directions, including lifecycle management, compositional safety, dynamic utility evaluation, and ecosystem standardization. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6776642`

- title: Learning Contextualized Representations for Light-Weight Anomaly Detection:,An Unsupervised Threat Hunting Approach
- authors: Varol Kayhan, Shivendu Shivendu, Mohammadreza Ebrahimi, Manish Agrawal, Ryan Irving
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6776642
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

In the domain of unsupervised threat hunting, organizations analyze text-based process commands captured from endpoint devices to proactively detect cyber threats. However, analyzing massive volumes of commands require automated decision support tools. Existing anomaly detection approaches suffer from three critical limitations: they undermine sequential context, are susceptible to normalization bias by conflating repeating attacks with benign behavior, and function as opaque black boxes that fail to support human investigation. Generative large language models are also not well suited for this task. They raise privacy concerns, are expensive to deploy, and are prone to hallucinations when analyzing log data. To address these gaps, we apply the design science research paradigm to propose a novel context-based anomaly detection artifact conceptualized as a decision support system. The proposed artifact introduces three distinct contributions to literature. First, it performs semantic integrity verification by using a custom transformer to calculate the localized conditional probability of constituent terms of each command. Second, it implements frequency-pattern decoupling via a dynamically calculated threshold, preventing the model from learning and normalizing propagating attacks. Third, it utilizes intrinsic interpretability by pinpointing the specific terms that render a command anomalous. Rigorous experimental evaluation demonstrates that the artifact outperforms benchmark models, achieving the highest accuracy (94.82%), precision (3.23%), and F1-score (6.23%). A subsequent case study confirms the findings by translating raw probability scores into actionable, term-level evidence.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6773839`

- title: An Efficient Multimodal Transformer with Large Language Model-Inspired Components for Lithium-Ion Battery Remaining Useful Life Prediction under Limited Charging Cycles
- authors: Yunpeng Liu, Yaxin Sun, Changze Wu, Yuhang Liu, zhiyu mao, Zhongwei Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6773839
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Remaining useful life (RUL) prediction for lithium-ion batteries is commonly developed using long-horizon lifecycle data, whereas practical applications often provide only a few charging records, making degradation learning and model generalization highly challenging. To address this extreme low-data scenario, this study proposes an efficient multimodal Transformer framework with large language model (LLM)-inspired components for battery RUL prediction under limited charging cycles. The proposed framework jointly exploits 1D charging curves and their corresponding gramian angular field (GAF) image representations. Only five consecutive charging cycles are used as input, accounting for approximately 0.6% of the entire battery lifetime, where the 1D signals include voltage, current, temperature, and capacity. A dual-branch encoder is designed to extract modality-specific representations, with a 1D-CNN capturing local temporal patterns from charging curves and a 2D-CNN learning spatial features from GAF images. The resulting tokens are concatenated and fed into a stacked Transformer to model cross-modal interactions for RUL regression. To improve computational practicality for long-token multimodal fusion, the framework incorporates several LLM-inspired components, including FlashAttention for memory-efficient attention computation, rotary positional embedding for relative positional modeling, and a mixture-of-experts module for conditional capacity scaling. Experiments on two benchmark datasets demonstrate that the proposed method consistently achieves superior performance under the extremely limited-data regime, highlighting its strong accuracy, robustness, and cross-dataset generalization capability.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6719098`

- title: A Comparative Study of AI Code Generation Tools in Modern Front-End Development: Evaluating ChatGPT-4, GitHub Copilot, and v0.dev
- authors: Mostafa Mohamed Ismail, Mostafa Amer Abdalsamea, Jana Shawki Abdelkarim Zarad, Ghada Salama Abdelaleem, Fatma Mohamed Khal, Ahmed Nasser
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6719098
- keyword hits: chatgpt, gpt-4

### abstract

The proliferation of AI-powered code generation tools has introduced both opportunity and ambiguity for front-end developers: while multiple assistants promise productivity gains, practitioners lack empirical evidence to guide tool selection for specific workflow contexts. This paper presents a controlled comparative evaluation of three prominent AI code generation tools—OpenAI ChatGPT-4, GitHub Copilot, and Vercel v0.dev—across three representative front-end development tasks: React component generation, CSS Flexbox debugging, and legacy jQuery code explanation. Three professional developers participated in a within-subjects experiment measuring completion time, code correctness, cyclomatic complexity, and subjective developer satisfaction using a 5-point Likert scale. Results indicate that v0.dev achieved the fastest completion time on component generation tasks (mean 6.8 min), GitHub Copilot yielded the highest code correctness on debugging tasks (91.3%), and ChatGPT-4 produced the highest-rated explanations for legacy code comprehension (4.6/5). No single tool dominated across all metrics, confirming that optimal tool selection depends on task type. These findings provide actionable guidance for practitioners and a methodological baseline for future large-scale studies.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6788788`

- title: Faculty orientations toward generative AI in Higher Education: perceptual dimensions, attitudinal profiles, and implications for institutional governance
- authors: Xabier Gonzalez Laskibar, Beñat Landeta‐Manzano, Amaia Mendoza-Larrañaga, Naiara Uriarte-Gallastegi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6788788
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid expansion of generative artificial intelligence (AI) is driving organizational change in higher education, raising important questions about faculty perceptions, academic values, and institutional governance. This study examines how university teachers and researchers perceive generative AI, focusing on disciplinary and socioprofessional differences and their relationship with core academic values such as autonomy, collegiality, and integrity. A cross-sectional survey was conducted with 458 faculty members at a Spanish public university, measuring seven perceptual dimensions related to AI knowledge, training, benefits, opportunities, risks, sustainability, and ethics. Data were analyzed using descriptive statistics, proportional-odds ordinal regression models, and k-means cluster analysis. Results indicate high levels of perceived knowledge and benefits of AI, alongside low evaluations of institutional training. Socioprofessional and disciplinary factors showed differentiated associations across dimensions. Three faculty profiles emerged—Critical Enthusiasts, Pragmatists, and Skeptical—distinguished primarily by their configurations of perceived benefits, risks, and ethical concerns. These findings reveal persistent tensions between AI adoption and foundational academic values and highlight the inadequacy of uniform institutional responses. The study contributes empirical evidence on faculty heterogeneity and advances a profile-sensitive framework for participatory governance, emphasizing differentiated training strategies, inclusive decision-making, and integrity-oriented AI policies in higher education.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6790221`

- title: GCAL: Group-Centered Collaborative Advantage Learning for Policy Optimization in Multi-Agent Systems
- authors: Yiwu Hu, Si Shi, Zhen Tian, RunDong Yuan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6790221
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Large language model (LLM)-driven multi-agent systems are increasingly being used for complex reasoning and decision-making tasks, yet stable collaboration among agents remains difficult to achieve. A key difficulty is collaborative credit assignment: in multi-agent collaboration, the overall reward is often difficult to decompose and attribute to each agent’s specific contribution, which makes the optimization signals for individual behaviors unstable and hard to compare. To address this problem, we propose GCAL, a group-centered collaborative advantage learning framework for policy optimization in multi-agent systems. Through a three-round collaborative interaction process, GCAL evaluates how different auxiliary feedback messages affect the target agent’s final output, providing comparable utility signals for credit assignment in multi-agent collaboration. Building on this, GCAL further constructs a group-centered collaborative advantage function, which compares the relative utility of different auxiliary feedback messages under the same target context and provides more discriminative and stable training signals for collaborative policy optimization. Experimental results show that agents trained with GCAL achieve overall improvements over competitive baselines and exhibit robust performance across multiple collaborative reasoning settings.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6795404`

- title: Auditing what MLLMs see: A Rubric-to-Map Framework for Planning-Oriented Urban Perception Mapping from Street-View Imagery
- authors: Yuelin Yan, Liangcun Jiang, Zehong Huang, Yuan Guo, Jiejun Huang, Han Zhou
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6795404
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Evidence-based urban planning requires street-level evidence that is spatially explicit, reviewable, and comparable across locations. While recent advances in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have demonstrated the feasibility of perceptual scoring from street-view imagery (SVI), their direct use in urban perception mapping remains limited by insufficient auditability, unstable score comparability, and the ”black-box” opacity that hinders cartographic interpretation. To bridge this gap, this study proposes a ”Rubric-to-Map” framework for auditing, calibrating, and mapping MLLM-based urban perception from SVI. Using Wuhan Tiandi, China, as a case study, we apply a five-dimensional street-quality rubric covering character, enclosure, walkability, nature, and facade quality to 477 observation points, with human rating baselines as a reference. The framework treats rubric-constrained MLLM outputs as a raw observation layer rather than direct final map values. It audits their agreement, cross-run stability, within-point uncertainty, and systematic biases, and then applies a backend semantic calibration mechanism using grouped visual features as interpretable evidence. Empirical results show that the semantic evidence layer alone explains 40.1% of the variance in the human reference scores. When used for backend calibration of the MLLM scores, it substantially improves cross-point score comparability, raising the Pearson correlation for the overall score from 0.685 to 0.779. The findings also reveal dimension-specific biases, particularly for walkability, indicating that different perception dimensions vary significantly in their suitability for direct map use. Ultimately, the framework produces GIS-ready point-level perception, uncertainty, error, and calibration-effect layers that empower targeted spatial diagnosis, street-segment comparison, and planning review with robust, interpretable AI evidence.

- title: Prompt Engineering as Pre-writing: Not Writing with GenAI, Writing before GenAI
- authors: Haley Greer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6795098
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai, prompt engineering, prompting

### abstract

Since the launch of ChatGPT and other generative AI (genAI) models, the composition field has had varied responses ranging from writing with genAI, to genAI refusal. With this vast spectrum of responses, it may be unclear where the composition field currently stands with these new technologies, and what its trajectory will be. This paper positions previous pedagogical writing practices such as process writing and pre-writing as skills that not only withstand the current technological development but are even more pertinent for students’ writing processes. Drawing on process writing and rhetorical situation analysis, this paper reframes structured prompting as a pre-writing protocol that teaches students to analyze the rhetorical situation before technological engagement. It then uses the TRACI prompting framework as a worked example, demonstrating how structured prompting can operationalize rhetorical situation analysis as a pre-writing practice before genAI engagement.
