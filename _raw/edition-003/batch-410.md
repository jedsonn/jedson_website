## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6931279`

- title: The Hypercomputational Ontology of AI Economic: Embodying the Real World, Human Mind, and the Limits of Turing Machines
- authors: Zehao Zhou, Danxia Xie
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6931279
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

This paper develops a unified theoretical framework for AI economics integrating computability theory, non-equilibrium thermodynamics, endogenous growth theory, and the philosophy of mind. We resolve the half-century debate between computationalism and anti-computationalism by showing that human brains and physical reality are inherently hypercomputational systems with continuous nonlinear dynamics, capable of solving problems undecidable or intractable for any Turing machine. We introduce the hypercomputational ontological manifold M and dissipative projection operator Pdrop, demonstrating that data is not neutral bits but a topological collapse of continuous hypercomputational dynamics onto discrete symbolic spaces. Modern AI’s success stems not from a breakthrough in Turing computation, but from its ability to compress and approximate hypercomputational solutions encoded in low-entropy data from human minds and physical environments—a process we term extensional alignment. We show that deep reinforcement learning’s strong performance on NP-hard and continuous control problems constitutes a computational outsourcing mechanism: rather than solving problems internally, AI agents delegate computation to the physical environment as an online hypercomputational oracle. We then build a three-tier nested endogenous growth model where growth is driven by AI’s absorption of hypercomputational data, not pure computational power alone. Our model yields three counterintuitive, testable predictions: (1) Pure Turing synthetic data has diminishing marginal value and leads to semiotic collapse via entropy growth in isolated Markov chains; (2) Long-run AI economy growth is bounded by new hypercomputational information from human innovation and physical exploration, not Moore’s Law; (3) Non-algorithmic creative and experimental human labor remains irreplaceable as the ultimate source of economic value. These findings carry profound implications for AI policy, labor economics, and the future of economic growth.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6930198`

- title: Stablecoins as Tokenized Money
- authors: Lin William Cong, Gordon Liao, Eswar S. Prasad, Tony Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6930198
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

We document five facts about payment stablecoins using granular blockchain data from 2020 to 2025. First, stablecoins are functioning as a transaction currency: their onchain transaction volume now exceeds Bitcoin's despite a far smaller outstanding supply, their velocity has risen sharply, and their transaction-size distribution has shifted toward small-value transfers. Second, we develop a Bayesian refinement of the standard visitor-share attribution methodology that uses transaction-timing data to help geolocate individual wallets. This approach shows that stablecoin activity is distributed broadly across the Americas, Europe, and Asia, but that standard methods substantially overstate the cross-regional share of flows. Third, we estimate a structural model of cash management on stablecoin transaction data and find that the typical stablecoin wallet operates on quantitative terms comparable to traditional cash holdings in terms of wallet replenishment opportunities and estimated transaction costs. Fourth, stablecoins are becoming the settlement currency for a rapidly expanding onchain credit market in which lending terms are enforced by rule-based smart contracts rather than by intermediary discretion; balance-sheet accounting disciplined by reserve and currency data indicates that stablecoins' growth reallocates rather than destroys credit. Fifth, stablecoins support programmable, machine-initiated payments: smart contracts participate in roughly a third of stablecoin transactions and over two-thirds of transfer volume on Ethereum, and early agentic-payment protocols such as x402 demonstrate machine-to-machine commerce at sub-cent transaction sizes. Together, these facts suggest that stablecoins are behaving as tokenized money. We discuss implications for traditional financial markets and the design of regulatory frameworks.

- title: Smart Money, Old Patterns, New Amplifiers: Billionaire Portfolio Reallocation, Historical Analogues, and Systemic Risk in the Age of Agentic AI
- authors: Ka Wah Philip Ng
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6933718
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Between June 2025 and June 2026, several prominent billionaire investors reallocated capital away from U.S. public equities and toward cash, gold, private markets, and private artificial- intelligence (AI) exposure. This paper examines two related questions. First, to what extent do these allocation patterns resemble portfolio positioning observed before major financial dislocations such as 1929, 1973–74, 1987, 1997–98, and 2000? Second, given the growing integration of AI, agentic systems, and interconnected cyber infrastructure into financial markets, could a future shock propagate differently from previous crises? Drawing on regulatory filings, insider disclosures, industry surveys, and publications from the Financial Stability Board (FSB), European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB), International Monetary Fund (IMF), and World Economic Forum (WEF), the paper finds that several contemporary signals — including structural equity de-risking, elevated market concentration, and rotation into hard assets — exhibit meaningful historical parallels. Other features, including the scale of private-market illiquidity and the concentration of AI infrastructure, appear comparatively novel. The paper does not argue that a crisis is imminent, nor that billionaire portfolio behaviour is itself a reliable predictor of future market dislocations. Rather, it argues that if a material shock were to occur, AI-driven, agentic, and cyber-enabled financial systems could compress the time between initial disruption and broader systemic impact relative to previous cycles. The paper's principal contribution is therefore not a forecast of crisis, but an examination of how emerging technological and operational dependencies may alter the transmission characteristics of future financial shocks. The paper concludes with implications for risk managers, allocators, regulators, and governance practitioners, while highlighting the limitations and uncertainties inherent in historical- analogue analysis.

- source: SSRN

### abstract

A single architectural pattern explains two June 2026 supply-chain compromises that on the surface involve different systems and different threat communities. In the first incident, attackers adopted orphan or abandoned packages in the Arch User Repository (AUR), spoofed git commit metadata to inherit the trust of the long-standing maintainer, and delivered a Rust binary infostealer paired with an optional eBPF rootkit through over four hundred confirmed packages, with community trackers reporting up to one thousand five hundred. This paper analyses the two cases through a single architectural lens, names the pattern as trust inheritance without manifest-bound enforcement, and surveys the closely related agentic-workflow lock-file mitigation introduced by GitHub Agentic Workflows in public preview on the same day as the AUR campaign onset. The paper argues that the manifest-to-runtime binding pattern named in OWASP AISVS C9 (research chapter, merged June 2026) and the chain-audit schema being drafted in adjacent CSA Non-Human Identity working-group work provide a verification-side architecture for both layers. Open research questions on cross-layer detection, integrity-binding scope, and reversibility-graded gate selection are catalogued.

- title: ProjAgent: Procedural Similarity Retrieval for Repository-Level Code Generation
- authors: QiHong Chen, Aaron Imani, Iftekhar Ahmed
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-09
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08691v1
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Repository-level code generation requires implementing target functions while accounting for complex cross-file dependencies and project-specific conventions. Existing retrieval methods predominantly rely on lexical, structural, or semantic similarity, often overlooking repository functions that implement similar procedural logic despite differing in identifiers or application domains. We propose ProjAgent, a repository-level code generation system that introduces procedural similarity as an explicit retrieval signal. ProjAgent decomposes the target function into intermediate reasoning steps and employs an agentic workflow to retrieve repository functions that exhibit similar procedural behavior at each step. The retrieved procedural context is integrated with conventional semantic retrieval to construct a richer repository context for code generation. ProjAgent further incorporates a conservative static-analysis feedback loop that iteratively repairs generated code using compiler and static-analysis feedback. Evaluated on REPOCOD, ProjAgent achieves 41.14% Pass@1, outperforming existing retrieval-based baselines. These results demonstrate that procedural similarity is an effective and previously unexplored retrieval dimension for repository-level code generation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6943944`

- title: The Two-step Paradigm for Autonomous Agents: From Cognitive Task Analysis to DESIRE-based Multi-agent Architecture
- authors: Jan Veldsink
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6943944
- keyword hits: llm, prompting

### abstract

The enterprise adoption of Artificial Intelligence is currently characterized by a fundamental architectural error: treating AI as a centralized IT project rather than a cognitive extension of the human worker. This "improvisation at scale" leads to unpredictable behavior, hallucinations, and a loss of organizational control. This paper argues for a Human-Centric AI paradigm, asserting that reliable AI systems cannot be built without first explicitly mapping human cognition. We propose that this mapping-Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA)-serves as the indispensable foundation for two parallel domains of AI application. In the first domain (LLM-Assisted Work), individual knowledge workers use CTA to deconstruct their own expertise into targeted prompts, retaining active control while offloading 60-80% of the cognitive burden. In the second domain (Autonomous Agent Systems), CTA is embedded as an organizational design activity, where human expertise is formally translated into the DESIRE (DEsign and Specification of Interacting REasoning components) framework and populated with Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) mental states. By positioning CTA as the shared foundation for both personal prompting and formal multi-agent architecture, organizations can ensure that AI remains deterministic, verifiable, and strictly aligned with human intent.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6939818`

- title: Evaluating Agentic AI Workflows in Finance: A Harness for Grounding, Stability, Permissioning, Prompt Injection, and Workflow Ablation
- authors: Mike Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6939818
- keyword hits: agentic, llm

### abstract

We present an evaluation harness for agentic AI workflows in finance. Its defining choice is the unit of evaluation: the workflow's artifact — a memo, a signal proposal, a screening recommendation — rather than a single model answer. On that harness we run five experiment families: grounding, stability, permissioning, prompt injection, and workflow ablation. The harness runs on raw provider SDKs, calling each model's API directly rather than through an agent-orchestration framework, so the results do not hinge on a third-party library that could change underneath them. It locks the analysis plan locally with a SHA256 commitment, uses two independent evaluator families with deterministic backstops, and preserves every record as raw JSONL. Across the five experiments, the final release holds 33,420 subject-run records (Exp 1: 1,140; Exp 2: 12,960; Exp 3: 4,320 raw / 4,317 paired-evaluator; Exp 4: 12,600; Exp 5: 2,400), with evaluator judgments stored in-record or as companion scored files. The point of this study is not to rank vendors. It is to evaluate workflow controls in a way an investment research team can reproduce and challenge. There is no human-rater validation in this release — a deliberate scope limit, not an oversight. So we treat the LLM-evaluator judgments as measurement signals, triangulated through cross-family evaluators and deterministic backstops, not as stand-ins for human ground truth. Human-rater validation would be valuable future work, especially on the more semantic fields: artifact usability, unsupported claims, and source–inference separation. The headline methodological finding is that agent self-report is not a reliable measurement layer for workflow evaluation. In Experiment 5, a self-report-derived version of our utility metric — the Net Useful-Artifact Rate, or NUAR (here the deprecated NUAR_v1) — ranked the gated multi-agent workflow worst. An externally-evaluated reconstruction of the same metric (NUAR_v2) ranked it best or tied-best across two independent evaluator families. That externalevaluation ranking holds up across subject-family subsets and across evaluator families. We treat NUAR_v1 → NUAR_v2 as a case study in why external evaluation is non-negotiable for agentic-workflow assessment.Here is what the five experiments show. Grounding can be measured with high cross-family agreement on the structural metrics (Exp 1). Borderline cases reverse more often than confident ones, and rerun-agreement beats model-stated confidence as a stability signal (Exp 2). Permissioning policies produce large, monotone, panel-wide drops in unsafe-action attempts (Exp 3). In the synthetic prompt-injection suite, the tool allow-list condition produced the lowest mean net attack success rate, while the intended adaptive attack generation did not reliably produce harder attacks and the full-stack mitigation backfired on one subject model (Exp 4). And the gated multi-agent workflow was best or tied-best on the external-evaluator utility metric and eliminated permissioning violations relative to the ungated multi-agent baseline (Exp 5). We claim no alpha generation and no production-security certification; the contribution is a reproducible harness and a measurement architecture for these workflow controls.
