## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6996730`

- title: A Multimodal LLM Framework for Short-Term Footfall Time Series Forecasting
- authors: Zhijie Li, Gao Libo, Wang Fuqi, Changhua Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6996730
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Short-term footfall forecasting is important for staffing, crowd management, energy scheduling, and event-aware operations in large commercial areas. However, this task remains challenging because pedestrian flow is affected by heterogeneous external factors, strong periodic patterns, and abrupt local deviations. Existing forecasting methods often struggle to jointly model multimodal signals and maintain interpretability under high-frequency prediction settings. To address these challenges, this paper proposes MFF-LLM, a multimodal LLM-based forecasting framework with an offline–online design. In the offline stage, aligned observations are transformed into modality-specific token streams. In the online stage, these tokens are processed by a dual-path forecasting network that integrates Stage-1 fusion, a frozen Transformer backbone, explicit modality posterior estimation, history-guided routing, and a two-stage prediction head. Experiments on real-world footfall forecasting show that the MFF-LLM family achieves the strongest and most consistent MAE performance across multiple forecast horizons. Further ablation and mechanism analyses show that the seasonal prior, explicit modality branch, and structured multimodal fusion are key contributors to the final performance. Case studies also demonstrate that the proposed model can follow event-day deviations, preserve major peak–valley patterns, and capture short-term dynamic changes with reasonable fidelity. These results indicate that MFF-LLM provides an effective and interpretable solution for short-term multimodal footfall forecasting.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6875778`

- title: The Economics of Synthetic Output: Scarcity, Verification, and Trust in Generative Systems
- authors: M. Elizabeth Simmons
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6875778
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI has reduced the marginal cost of producing text, summaries, analysis, and recommendations to near zero. This paper argues that the economic consequence is not simply abundance, but a structural shift in where scarcity resides. As synthetic output scales, the bottleneck moves from production to evaluation: the scarce resources are no longer the outputs themselves, but the trust, provenance, domain expertise, accountability, and judgment required to determine whether those outputs are reliable enough to act upon. The paper frames synthetic output as an economic object: a category of informational good defined not only by its content, but by the conditions of its production and the indeterminacy of its quality. Drawing from information economics, signaling theory, institutional economics, trustworthy AI governance, human-in-the-loop machine learning, and emerging concerns in AI-assisted decision systems, it examines how the proliferation of synthetic output changes the economics of decision making in environments where generated outputs are used to support interpretation, recommendation, forecasting, evaluation, or strategic action. This paper is exploratory and conceptual. Its aim is to define the economic problem clearly and to connect that problem to a broader research trajectory on AI-generated response evaluation, output quality control, and human-in-the-loop decision-readiness. The closing argument is simple: synthetic output abundance does not decrease the value of trusted judgment. It increases it.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6877798`

- title: Agentic AI Payments and the Opportunities for MiCA-Compliant Stablecoins
- authors: Andrea Stazi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6877798
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

The e-commerce landscape is undergoing a structural transformation toward "agentic commerce", where autonomous AI agents execute complex transactions, driving a market projected to reach between $3 trillion and $5 trillion by 2030. This monumental shift is fundamentally incompatible with the traditional financial system, which is designed for human consumers and cannot handle the high volume of near-instant, fractions-of-a-cent microtransactions required for machine-to-machine payments. This paper argues that stablecoins are the critical infrastructure for the agentic economy, providing the requisite predictable value, 24/7 liquidity, and low-cost settlement that volatile cryptocurrencies and slow legacy fiat rails cannot. Crucially, the stablecoin market poised to win this multi-trillion-dollar frontier is one built on regulatory trust. While many stablecoins suffer from opaque reserves and regulatory uncertainty, MiCA-compliant stablecoins possess a competitive edge. Europe's stringent MiCA Regulation requires 1:1 backing by safeguarded, bankruptcy-remote fiat reserves in Tier-1 EU banks, a "compliance-by-design" approach that provides the enterprise-grade assurance large multinational corporations require. Furthermore, MiCA's integration with AMLA and Verification of Payee standards ensures auditable, risk-managed boundaries for autonomous workflows. This unique combination of frictionless payment capabilities and robust European regulatory oversight positions MiCA-compliant stablecoins to become the foundational layer and market leader for automated e-commerce value transfer.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6881538`

- title: AI Impact on Executive-level Jobs Performance: A Literature Review
- authors: Ihab T. Elsehmawy
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6881538
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Artificial intelligence has changed from being a nice to have to be a fundamental capability that changed how executives conduct work across all industries. This paper reviews the growing empirical evidence on how AI is changing executive-level job performance, with particular attention to decision-making, leadership, strategic management, and the reshaping of managerial skills. Inspecting studies in upper echelons theory, the resourcebased view, and the dynamic managerial capability perspective, the paper reviews thirty studies published between 2019 and 2026 in leading management, economics, and information systems journals. The review reaches a conclusion that AI, especially generative AI, leads to substantial productivity gains at the task level, shrinks the productivity gap between newer and more experienced knowledge workers, and accelerates strategic decision-making. Executive work is not simply being augmented, it is being reformed. Top management teams now face new demands for AI literacy, ethical stewardship, cross-functional orchestration, and the design of hybrid human-AI decision-making processes. The reviewed studies demonstrate that executives tend to rely on AI advice in strategic contexts, that algorithm aversion persists in high-stakes settings, and that human-AI collaboration produces superior outcomes only under specific task and delegation conditions. The literature also documents how executive AI adoption is significantly associated with firm-level performance outcomes such as innovation, sales growth, and operating efficiency. The review identifies four recurring themes across the literature which are AI's effect on executive decision-making, the redefinition of executive skills and capabilities, the mediating role of AI in leader-employee relationships, and AI's impact on firm-level outcomes. The paper closes with implications drawn from the reviewed evidence and an agenda for future research, suggesting that executives in the AI era are being asked to lead in substantively new ways rather than being displaced by intelligent systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6917558`

- title: Authorship Without an Author: Rethinking Copyright Ownership in the Age of Generative AI
- authors: Bilal Shafiq
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6917558
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Copyright law has always rested on a simple premise: creative works have authors, and authors are human beings. Generative artificial intelligence has broken that premise. When an AI model produces a novel, a piece of art, or a poem autonomously without human intervention, the work so produced has no author in a legal sense. This article examines how India's Copyright Act, 1957, 1 confronts this challenge. It argues that the Act's authorship requirements, rooted in Section 2(d)'s definition of "author" and Section 13's conditions for copyright protection, create three distinct but important legal issues: First, purely AI-generated works are unprotectable; Second, AI-assisted works are legally uncertain; Third, the data used to train AI systems raises infringement questions. In light of the ongoing proceedings of ANI Media Pvt. Ltd. v. Open AI Inc. in the Delhi High Court 2 and the DPIIT's December 2025 Working Paper on Generative AI and Copyright, 3 this article deliberates on three contested ownership questions on content generated by AI models, and proposes a tiered authorship framework as a resolution.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.26924v1`

- title: A Deterministic Control Plane for LLM Coding Agents
- authors: Padmaraj Madatha
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-25
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26924v1
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

LLM coding harnesses grant agents broad file and shell access, yet the configuration layer that steers them -- rules files, agent definitions, IDE-specific markdown -- is largely unmanaged. A prevalence study of 10,008 public GitHub repositories (n=6,145 agent config files) finds that agent configurations propagate as undeclared shared components: 10.1% of tracked paths are SHA-256 exact duplicates across independent repositories (fork-adjusted, threshold-independent), with 75.5% of clone pairs crossing organisational boundaries. Two further patterns are indicative: configurations are rarely revised (58% single-commit; 0.4 vs 0.6 commits/month age-normalised against CI/CD workflows), and rarely declare permission boundaries (<1% of agent configs vs 33% of Actions workflows, n=31 true positives). We propose a deterministic control plane above the harness that maps one-to-one to these gaps. Rel(AI)Build treats agent definitions as a managed supply chain (SHA-256 content addressing, HMAC-stamped lockfiles, hash-chained audit logs); enforces tiered permissions and attack-derived blocklists before LLM invocation; gates feature work through a phase state machine with requirement-to-file-to-test traceability; compiles a single canonical definition to seven IDE targets; and detects prompt drift via Jaccard similarity. Conformance tests on injected violations confirm each mechanism enforces its stated invariant; developer outcomes remain future work. Governance of this layer must be deterministic and tool-agnostic -- not delegated to further LLM orchestration.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7003306`

- title: Quantifying Techno-Nationalism Emotional Decay in Chinese Social Media: A Lifecycle-Aware Multi-Task Sentiment Framework
- authors: Zhuoyi Fan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7003306
- keyword hits: chatgpt, deepseek

### abstract

Techno-nationalist sentiment on Chinese social media is intense but temporally bounded, yet how the cross-national sentiment advantage surrounding AI-technology events rises and subsides across the opinion lifecycle has not been quantified. Treating the releases of ChatGPT (2023) and DeepSeek-R1 (2025) as a comparative two-event design, we track sentiment toward a foreign and a domestic AI system across four lifecycle phases on Weibo and Zhihu, and report two findings. First, we identify and quantify Techno-Nationalism Emotional Decay (TNED): the odds ratio of positive sentiment for DeepSeek over ChatGPT declines from a 2.67–2.72 plateau across the outbreak and diffusion phases to 0.98 in the settling phase, indicating that the national-pride premium domestic technology initially enjoys erodes systematically as discourse matures into instrumental evaluation. Second, we document an accountability paradox: the domestic system attracts both elevated positive sentiment and elevated skepticism-accountability discourse, whereas the foreign system attracts more rational-analysis framing. These findings rest on a newly constructed 5,765-comment dual-platform corpus jointly annotated for polarity and discourse function (Cohen's κ = 0.752 and 0.708), whose complete annotation codebook we release publicly, and on a lifecycle-aware classifier (EventAware-RoBERTa) that we validate as a measurement instrument (polarity Macro-F1 = 0.72; function Macro-F1 = 0.64). Because the two events are separated by roughly two years, we present TNED as a benchmark finding that requires cross-event replication, and we refrain from causal interpretation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6886958`

- title: Classification and Extraction: A Framework for the Sociology of Artificial Intelligence
- authors: Krzysztof Jaskulowski
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6886958
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Sociological research on artificial intelligence has expanded rapidly over the last decade, producing scholarship on algorithmic bias, digital labour, surveillance, cultural imaginaries, data colonialism, and generative AI. However, these research programmes, while individually productive, remain theoretically disconnected. The paper proposes an integrative framework built on two co-constitutive social processes: classification (the sorting, ranking, and categorising) and extraction (the capture of economic, epistemic, affective, and relational value from what has been classified). Drawing on classical sociological theory and materialist account of AI infrastructure, the paper shows that classification and extraction form a single recursive mechanism. In paradigmatic cases such as data annotation the two are ontologically inseparable. The act of classifying is simultaneously an act of extraction. In other cases (credit scoring, content moderation) the same mechanism operates across temporally extended cycles. What distinguishes algorithmic co-constitution from the feedback loops described by STS is the elimination of inter-cycle time. The temporal gap between one classification and the next, in which the classified subject could contest, adapt, or resist, tends to collapse toward zero. The originality of the proposed framework lies not in the discovery of classification or extraction as social processes but in their configuration as a single mechanism. Reading the field through this lens reveals both achievements and blind spots, including the relative absence of comparative cross-national research, the neglect of class, insufficient engagement with classical sociological theory, and the gap between AI ethics as industry and sociological critique.
