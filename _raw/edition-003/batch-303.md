## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6866171`

- title: The GUI-to-CUI Shift in Mobile Medication Management for Blind and Low-Vision Users: Effects on Usability, Workload, and Information-Seeking Behaviour
- authors: San Hong, Minjoon Sohn, Jaewon Choi, Jongmin Jung, Hyungjun Cho, Woojin Park
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6866171
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

This within-subject, counterbalanced study compared two mobile medication management services: a GUI-based mobile health platform and an LLM-powered prototype with a conversational user interface (CUI). Twenty-one blind and low-vision (BLV) adults participated. Cognitive workload was measured using NASA-TLX and usability using MARS. Think-aloud and semi-structured interview data were collected and analysed thematically using Wilson's Model of Information Behaviour as the analytical framework. Quantitative and qualitative results were integrated through a Joint Display. The CUI-based platform produced significantly lower workload and higher usability across all measured dimensions. Qualitative analysis identified five themes describing how the GUI to CUI paradigm shift reconfigured information seeking: navigation burden and search abandonment, trust and information credibility, autonomy and privacy trade off, technological competence and query formulation, and the shift in search mode and feedback cost. Under the GUI condition, multi-step visual navigation imposed a dominant environmental barrier that blocked search progression and suppressed re-activation through the feedback loop. The CUI-based platform resolved this barrier, shifting information seeking from passive attention to active, query driven retrieval with near-zero feedback cost. Notably, the CUI condition surfaced three previously latent psychological variables: query formulation ability concern, output uncertainty, and privacy-autonomy trade-off. The findings indicate that resolving environmental accessibility barriers is necessary but insufficient. They also show that the hierarchy among Wilson's intervening variables shifts with the interaction paradigm: under the GUI condition, environmental variables dominated and suppressed other variables; under the CUI condition, their removal allowed psychological and source-related variables to emerge as the primary constraints on information seeking.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6822138`

- title: Cross-Jurisdictional Compliance for Agentic AI Systems
- authors: Rizwan Tanveer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6822138
- keyword hits: agentic, fine-tuning, foundation model

### abstract

Purpose. This paper analyses how four anchor jurisdictions, namely the European Union, the United States, the United Kingdom, and the Gulf Cooperation Council, with particular attention to the United Arab Emirates, allocate compliance responsibility across the layered agentic AI stack, and where their allocations conflict, overlap, or leave gaps for an organisation operating across all four. It advances the argument that the central compliance problem is not the stringency of any single regime but the misalignment between regulatory responsibility axes and the technical architecture of agentic systems, a misalignment that compounds under cross-jurisdictional operation. Approach. The paper adopts a comparative legal and standards-review methodology, synthesising primary regulatory instruments, national-authority guidance, and the contemporary 2024 to 2026 practitioner and scholarly literature across the four jurisdictions. It maps each regime's allocation of obligations onto a three-layer model of the agentic stack derived from the AI Supply Chain Trust Boundary Model developed earlier in this series, and it analyses the provider-versus-deployer boundary as the principal fault line along which cross-jurisdictional divergence manifests. Findings. Three findings emerge. First, every regime examined organises obligations around a developer-or-provider versus deployer-or-user distinction, but each draws the boundary differently and none maps it cleanly onto the three-layer agentic stack, so the same orchestration platform may be a deployer in one jurisdiction and a provider in another. Second, the reclassification trigger, whereby substantial modification or fine-tuning of a foundation model converts a deployer into a provider with heavier obligations, is the single most consequential and least harmonised mechanism, and it bears directly on orchestration platforms that adapt upstream models. Third, the GCC layered model, far from being a regulatory gap, functions as a distinct compliance architecture in which free-zone regimes such as the Dubai International Financial Centre impose binding obligations on autonomous systems while federal instruments set a baseline, creating an intra-jurisdictional layering that mirrors the cross-jurisdictional problem in miniature. Implications. For practitioners operating multi-jurisdictional AI estates, compliance cannot be achieved by satisfying the single most stringent regime, because the regimes diverge on who bears which obligation rather than merely on how much. The paper proposes that organisations adopt an architecture-first compliance posture, mapping obligations onto the technical layer at which control actually resides and treat the provider-versus-deployer boundary as a governed decision rather than an incidental consequence of engineering choices. The analysis connects to the orchestration liability framework, which is the subject of the author's broader doctoral work, and a future extension will empirically validate these findings through a vignette-based practitioner survey, conducted with institutional ethics approval.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.07392v1`

- title: Online Pandora's Box for Contextual LLM Cascading
- authors: Alexandre Belloni, Yan Chen, Yehua Wei
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-05
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07392v1
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Motivated by Large Language Model (LLM) cascading, we propose an online contextual Pandora's Box model for adaptively querying and selecting LLM APIs. In each period, a decision-maker observes a request context and faces a two-phase decision problem. In the query phase, the decision-maker sequentially queries APIs, where each query reveals a generated output and the decision-maker incurs an (output-dependent) cost. In the selection phase, the decision-maker selects one of the generated outputs to deploy and observes only the downstream reward of the deployed output. This output-mediated feedback structure differs from classical online contextual Pandora's Box models, in which opening a box directly reveals its reward. Rather than estimating the full conditional output and cost distributions of each API, we directly model the reservation index and develop a learning approach for the query phase. Specifically, we impose a parametric structure on the contextual reservation index functions induced by the classical Weitzman's policy. Our policy combines generalized method of moments (GMM) type estimation of these reservation indices with UCB-style confidence bounds for both these indices and the shared output-level reward evaluator. Under regularity conditions, we prove that the resulting policy achieves dimension-dependent $\widetilde O(\sqrt T)$ cumulative regret over a horizon of $T$ periods.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6875449`

- title: Measuring the AI Hook: Psychometric Validation of the MOHA Scale and its Association with Cyberchondria among Medical Students
- authors: Sami Monjed, Lobna Ghanoom, Amr Hadaki, Layla Mousa, Zakia Alshehet, Ebrahem Rade, Bayan Alsaid, Yousef Latifeh
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6875449
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Cyberchondria is a multi-dimensional condition characterized by compulsive online health searches, now potentially exacerbated by the rapid integration of generative Artificial Intelligence (AI). However, few psychometric instruments effectively capture the distinct facets of AI engagement: usage, dependence, and trust. This study validated the Arabic MOHA Scale and examined the relationship between AI engagement and cyberchondria among 446 medical students. We employed a cross-sectional design using the Cyberchondria Severity Scale (CSS-12) and the novel 12-item MOHA scale. Psychometric properties were evaluated via a split-sample approach involving Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) and Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA). Psychometric evaluation of the MOHA Scale confirmed a robust three-factor structure (AI usage, AI trust, and AI dependence) with strong internal consistency (α = .86) with acceptable model fit. Second-order Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) revealed that AI engagement is significantly and positively associated with cyberchondria severity. Path decomposition identified AI dependence as the predominant associator, whereas frequency of usage and trust were secondary contributors. While male students reported significantly higher AI usage and trust, no gender differences were found regarding AI dependence. Notably, academic seniority emerged as a significant negative predictor, while higher academic achievement was negatively correlated with AI dependence. The MOHA scale demonstrates that AI dependence, rather than mere interaction frequency, is a significant amplifier of AI-related cyberchondria. These findings suggest that clinical seniority provides only a modest protective effect, highlighting the need for educational interventions that prioritize metacognitive autonomy and digital health literacy over simple usage restrictions.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6822960`

- title: No Grounding, No Belief: The Structural Roots of Epistemic Failure in Large Language Models
- authors: Alfredo Vannacci
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6822960
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

The capacity to represent another agent's belief as distinct from the way the world is sits at the core of clinical, legal, and educational practice, and is among the central acquisitions of human social cognition. A growing body of empirical work shows that large language models, while attributing beliefs to third parties with near-ceiling accuracy, collapse when asked to acknowledge a first-person false belief, defaulting to factual correction in place of belief recognition. This failure has been read as a contingent training artefact. We propose a structural reading: a system whose operative universe is exhausted by token co-occurrence statistics has no independent access to either how things stand in the world or how a subject represents them, and cannot maintain the separation between truth and belief in a non-derivative way. The firstperson/third-person asymmetry is one clear instance of a broader class of epistemic illusions produced by ungrounded architectures. The limitation is architectural; deployment frameworks in epistemically sensitive domains should be designed around it.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6826378`

- title: The World as Oracle: Adversarial-Resistant AI Probability Estimation for Event-Driven Financial Instruments
- authors: V Guruprasad
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6826378
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

The world leaks probability before it admits reality. A shipping company rerouting one hundred vessels at one million dollars each is not making a statement-it is expressing a probability estimate in the language of capital. An insurance market repricing Red Sea coverage is not reporting news-it is encoding adversarial intelligence about conflict risk. A state petroleum ministry confirming it will buy sanctioned crude regardless of waivers is not issuing a press release-it is revealing a utility function that no amount of diplomacy will change. These are not signals that appear in news articles. They leave structural traces in shipping registries, actuarial databases, commodity flows, and procurement records. The oracle that reads these traces is more adversarially resistant than any oracle that reads articles, because structural behavioural traces cannot be manufactured as cheaply as narratives. Existing approaches to event-linked derivative pricing rely on dealer bank quotation, which is information-asymmetric, or prediction market crowd consensus, which is susceptible to thin-market manipulation and offers only binary resolution at event maturity. Neither provides a continuous, adversarial-cost-raising probability stream suitable as a floating rate reference in bilateral OTC swap contracts. This paper introduces a probability oracle-a new infrastructure category-that prices events through epistemic filtering: distinguishing genuine signal persistence from manufactured information amplification. The core mechanism, temporal persistence filtering, caps fast shock contributions at twenty percent of total probability composition and requires sustained independent multi-source confirmation before persistent signal accumulates. This design addresses citation amplification-a structural problem in which a single primary source generates cascading secondary coverage that appears statistically independent, creating false signal density in any volume-based probability system. The same structural flaw appears in academic citation networks, analyst consensus loops, social media cascades, and AI training data contamination. We demonstrate the architecture through a 40-month, four-episode retrospective analysis of the Adani Group case (January 2023 to May 2026), validated against a Federal Reserve rate decision consistency check versus Kalshi market-implied probabilities, and a real-time validation through the Maersk and Strait of Hormuz disruption case (2023-2026), in which the oracle's 47% probability estimate preceded and correctly anticipated the YES resolution of 28 February 2026. The architecture introduces the CONTESTED state-an explicit epistemic conflict output that surfaces information warfare risk rather than suppressing it behind a forced probability estimate-and is designed as infrastructure for event probability swaps, bilateral contracts in which the oracle's continuous probability stream serves as the floating rate, accessible via Model Context Protocol to LLM-driven financial agents.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6886878`

- title: Outsourcing Flexibility in Uncertain Times: A Real Options Decision Framework for Strategic Governance
- authors: Koushik Das Sarma, Chandrima Roy
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6886878
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Outsourcing governance has become a front-line strategic challenge. The convergence of generative artificial intelligence (AI), geopolitical fragmentation, and post-pandemic supply-chain reconfiguration has rendered static governance frameworks inadequate: the central question is no longer which governance mode best fits today's transaction characteristics, but which architecture preserves the most valuable future options at acceptable cost. This paper develops a real options–based decision framework anchored on real options theory as the integrating logic of outsourcing governance under uncertainty. We position environmental uncertainty as the antecedent that activates the commitment–flexibility trade-off, and introduce two problem-structuring instruments: a two-stage option map delineating the strategic sourcing choices and lifecycle interdependencies available to the firm, and a recursive 2×2 matrix locating four governance archetypes along the dimensions of environmental uncertainty and strategic importance. Rather than treating transaction cost economics, the resource-based view, and dynamic capabilities as competing explanations, the framework nests them within this option-theoretic architecture, where each supplies an input the option calculus requires but does not itself determine, respectively, the efficiency cost of commitment, the strategic cost of externalisation, and the feasibility of exercise, with prescriptive weight varying in proportion to quadrant conditions. It further introduces quadrant migration as an explicit governance discipline and demonstrates why AI-driven VRIN reclassification makes periodic reassessment essential, not optional. For senior managers, the framework provides decision rules for configuring outsourcing governance across all uncertainty–importance configurations and for executing principled transitions as conditions evolve.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6822759`

- title: Non-Human Identity and Access Management for Agentic AI
- authors: Rizwan Tanveer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6822759
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

Background. Identity and access management was designed for human users, and a modest population of long-lived service accounts, yet agentic artificial intelligence has inverted the population it must govern. Non-human identities, comprising service accounts, OAuth tokens, API keys, certificates, and now autonomous AI agents, already outnumber human identities in the enterprise by a factor commonly estimated at twenty-five to fifty, and the ratio is accelerating as agentic deployment scales through 2026. Contemporary breach data confirms the consequence: most security incidents now involve machine identities; the 2026 Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report singles out service and machine accounts as the likely locus of compromise in an agentic future; and high-profile token-based intrusions have propagated across hundreds of downstream environments via compromised non-human credentials. Purpose. This paper argues that the central security problem of agentic AI identity is an attribution gap: existing identity infrastructure cannot reliably establish which identity, at which layer of the agentic stack, authorised a given action, because agents create ephemeral, delegated, machine-speed identities whose chains of authority traditional IAM was never built to represent. It develops the claim that resolving this attribution gap is the necessary precondition for any framework that would allocate responsibility across the layered agentic stack, and it positions the gap as the identity-layer foundation of the orchestration liability framework that is the subject of the author's doctoral work. Approach. The paper adopts a conceptual and standards-review methodology, synthesising the contemporary 2025 to 2026 practitioner and standards literature, including emerging workload-identity primitives (SPIFFE/SPIRE), delegation-preserving authorisation patterns (OAuth 2.0 Token Exchange and the act claim), the OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications 2026, the NIST AI Agent Standards Initiative, and zero-trust guidance for non-person entities. It maps the attribution gap onto the three-layer agentic stack used elsewhere in this series and analyses how identity-based access control, least privilege, and the emerging principle of least agency bear on each layer. Findings. Three findings emerge. First, the attribution gap is structural rather than incidental: the dominant failure mode, shared credentials across multiple agents, collapses attribution by design, and even well-architected deployments struggle to preserve a verifiable chain of authority across dynamic multi-agent invocation. Second, the technical primitives needed to close the gap, cryptographic workload identity, short-lived and identity-bound credentials, and delegation-preserving tokens, now exist and are converging, but their adoption lags the pace of agentic deployment, producing a widening governance deficit. Third, identity is emerging across the standards landscape as the control plane for agentic security, with OWASP, NIST, and zero-trust guidance converging on identity-centric, least-agency controls, making the identity layer the natural anchor for accountability.
