- source: NBER

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are trained on a prodigious corpus of human writing and may reveal human preferences over characteristics of life courses, such as income, longevity, and working conditions.We present OpenAI's GPT-5.4 and a broadly representative sample of Americans with pairs of life stories and ask them to choose the life they would prefer for themselves.A person's choice is better predicted by the LLM's choice than by another person's choice over the same stories, and LLM valuations of several life attributes are similar to those derived from human responses.Our results suggest that LLM responses offer a scalable and cost-effective complement to existing methods for studying human preferences.

- title: Designing AI Policy for Child and Youth Flourishing
- authors: Ronald Ivey, Henry Shevlin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6712518
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, gemini, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This paper addresses a central policy challenge: how to advance child and youth well-being in a world shaped by generative artificial intelligence (Gen AI). We focus on conversational, human-like, and socially interactive systems, including general-purpose chatbots such as ChatGPT , Claude , and Gemini , and examine their potential impacts on children and youth from early childhood through adolescence. Drawing on the principle of human flourishing, the paper analyzes both risks and potential benefits across developmental domains. We identify significant gaps in current AI governance, which often fail to account for the developmental needs and rights of children and youth. Building on lessons from social media, where policy responses lagged behind technological adoption, we argue that key harmful mechanisms were insufficiently anticipated. As Amy Orben has described, this reflects a recurring “sisyphean cycle” of reactive governance (Orben, 2020). To avoid repeating this pattern, we propose a proactive framework for aligning Gen AI systems with child and youth well-being. This includes a design paradigm for developers, a model policy framework adaptable across jurisdictions, and a set of whole-of-society recommendations. The paper aims to inform product design, policy development, and public debate, emphasizing the need to act on existing evidence rather than waiting for important, long-term longitudinal data.

- title: Predicting the Spanish IBEX-35 Banking Sector Stock Prices with LLM-Based News Sentiment Analysis and LSTM
- authors: ELENA CONDERANA-MEDEM, Maria Coronado, Eduardo C. Garrido-Merchan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6758459

### abstract

Stock price prediction remains a foundational and complex task in analytical finance, with the inherent volatility of markets and the limitations of technical indicators spurring the integration of sentiment analysis. Large Language Models (LLMs), such as encoder-only FinBERT, are garnering increased attention for their potential to develop more accurate and adaptive forecasting models in synergy with machine learning (ML) algorithms. This study investigates the impact of integrating FinBERT-derived sentiment from Reuters financial headlines alongside technical indicators on the predictive performance of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models for IBEX-35 banking sector stock prices. LSTM models were trained on an extensive set of technical indicators from May 2020 to May 2025, where closing price served as target variable of the research. Through extensive experimentation across various lookback windows and meticulous hyperparameter tuning, the findings consistently demonstrate that FinBERT-derived sentiment acts as a differential factor, capturing underlying market dynamics unobservable through traditional indicators alone, significantly improving LSTM model performance across metrics R2 , RMSE, and MAE. The distinct contribution of this research lies in the application of LSTM models, Reuters news collection, and FinBERT sentiment extraction to the largely underexplored Spanish index, the IBEX- 35, as well as solely including sentiment from company-specific news. Overall results underscore sentiment’s significant potential for enhancing stock price prediction and warrant further exploration of its impact and interplay with other models within the IBEX-35.

### abstract

Sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) is widely recognized as a key pathway for aviation decarbonization, yet its evaluation remains difficult because pathway suitability depends on interacting environmental, regulatory, market, and resource conditions. This study proposes a multi-model LLM-RAG framework for systemic evaluation of SAF pathways by integrating a curated multi-source evidence corpus, a shared retrieval layer, and comparative reasoning across ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude. ### abstract

This working paper develops a complete philosophical programme around asymmetric eliminativism, a defeasible diagnostic for adjudicating debates over whether contested concepts apply to particular target classes. The programme has five parts. Part I articulates the diagnostic itself: a concept is reverse-defined when it inherits its extension from pre-theoretical folk vocabulary rather than from an observed phenomenon, and asymmetric eliminativism is the structurally appropriate response to a reverse-defined concept that has failed to admit operationalisation on a contested target class while continuing to function as a coordination device elsewhere. Three jointly sufficient diagnostic conditions (E1-E3) operationalise the profile, with a (b)-style licensing structure permitting elimination ahead of replacement-programme maturity. - title: Comparative Analysis of the Energy, Water and Carbon Costs of AI Hardware
- authors: Syed Awab
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6580964
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper examines the energy, water and carbon costs of AI hardware, expanding from current AI discourse to include AI hardware beyond GPUs: in-production ASICs, NPUs, FPGAs and emerging neuromorphic, biological and brain organoid, analogue, photonic/optical, probabilistic, thermodynamic and quantum. This paper finds that Nvidia GPUs, the dominant AI hardware of choice, particularly for generative AI and large language models (LLMs), have vastly higher energy, water and carbon costs despite more efficient AI hardware existing in the realms of production and R&D that have demonstrated better energy efficiencies and lower water footprint and carbon footprint. AI ASICs are upto 21 times faster, 10 times more energy efficient with 19 times lower carbon footprints than GPUs. Neuromorphic computing is upto 72.7 times faster and 100 times more energy efficient; photonic/optical computing upto 100 times faster and 200 times more energy efficient; and thermodynamic computing 10,000 times faster. Brain organoids are 1,000,000 times more energy efficient than a 1 exaFLOP supercomputer but are the least invested into despite showing the most promise. This paper proposes judiciously redirecting investment and resources into more efficient AI hardware and computing, particularly into brain organoids, biological computing and organoid intelligence. Such hardware, combined into an efficient AI stack, could consume vastly lower energy, water and resources, accompanied by a lower environmental and carbon footprint.

### abstract

Online reviews have become a primary source of information that shapes consumer decisions and corporate reputation in the digital economy. The proliferation of fake reviews poses serious threats to market fairness, consumer trust, and platform governance. The emergence of large language models (LLMs) has fundamentally transformed both the generation and detection of fake reviews. On the generation side, LLMs enable highly fluent and context-aware deceptive content. On the detection side, pre-trained language models (PLMs) and LLMs have shifted the paradigm from feature engineering to deep semantic reasoning. This survey systematically reviews fake review detection from traditional methods to PLM- and LLM-based approaches, analyzes their strengths and limitations, and identifies key challenges such as adversarial generation, cross-domain generalization, and interpretability. Finally, we outline promising future directions, including knowledge-enhanced detection and robust learning strategies.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6756301`

- title: RPA-Check: A Multi-Stage Automated Framework for Evaluating Dynamic LLM-based Role-Playing Agents
- authors: Riccardo Rosati, Edoardo Colucci, Massimiliano Bolognini, Adriano Mancini, Paolo Sernani
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6756301
- keyword hits: chain-of-thought, instruction-tuned, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The rapid adoption of Large Language Models (LLMs) in interactive systems has enabled the creation of dynamic, open-ended Role-Playing Agents (RPAs). However, evaluating these agents remains a significant challenge, as standard NLP metrics fail to capture the nuances of role adherence, logical consistency, and long-term narrative stability. This paper introduces RPA-Check, a multi-stage automated evaluation framework designed to objectively assess the performance of LLM-based RPAs in complex, constraints-heavy environments. Our methodology is based on a four-step pipeline: (1) Dimension Definition, establishing high-level qualitative behavioral criteria; (2) Augmentation, where these requirements are expanded into granular boolean checklist indicators; (3) Semantic Filtering, to ensure indicator objectivity, no redundancy and agent isolation; and (4) LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation, which employs chain-of-thought verification to score agent fidelity. We validate this framework by applying it to \textit{LLM Court}, a serious game for forensic training involving several quantized local models. Experimental results across five distinct legal scenarios demonstrate the framework’s ability to identify subtle trade-offs between model size, reasoning depth, and operational stability. Notably, the findings reveal an inverse relationship between parametric scale and procedural consistency, showing that smaller, adequately instruction-tuned models (8-9B) can outperform larger architectures prone to user-alignment bias or sycophancy. RPA-Check thus provides a standardized and reproducible metric for future research in generative agent evaluation within specialized domains.
