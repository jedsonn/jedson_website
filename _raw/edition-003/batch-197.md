- title: BEYOND AI EXPOSURE:WHICH TASKS ARE COST-EFFECTIVE TO AUTOMATE?
- authors: Martin Fleming, Maja Svanberg, Wensu Li, Brian Goehring, Neil Thompson
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6691902
- keyword hits: ai agent, generative ai

### abstract

The faster artificial intelligence (AI) automation spreads through the economy, the more profound its potential impacts, both positive and negative. However, the existing literature on “AI Exposure” cannot predict the pace of automation since it attempts to measure an overall potential for AI, not the technical feasibility and economic attractiveness of building such systems. In this paper, a new type of AI task automation framework is presented. While the framework applies to generative AI models and deep learning predictive models, computer vision models illustrate the approach, where cost data are more well developed. We find that at today’s costs U.S. businesses would choose not to automate most vision tasks that have “AI Exposure,” and that only 23% of worker wages being paid for vision tasks would be attractive to automate. The slower roll-out of AI can be accelerated if costs falls rapidly or if it is deployed via AI agents or AI-as-a-service that have greater scale than individual firms. Overall, our findings suggest that AI job displacement will be substantial, but also gradual – and therefore there is room for policy and retraining to mitigate unemployment impacts.

- authors: Zhichao Yang, Greg  D. Lyng, Sanjit  Singh Batra, Robert  E. Tillman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-02
- source: SSRN

### abstract

Background: Accurate extraction of medical concepts from electronic health record (EHR) narratives is essential for clinical decision support, cohort identification, and real-world evidence generation. However, clinically meaningful conditions are frequently documented implicitly rather than explicitly stated in medical notes, limiting the reliability of existing automated extraction systems. Current benchmarks emphasize explicitly stated concepts and provide limited evaluation of models’ ability to infer medically relevant concepts from indirect evidence or to justify predictions with traceable supporting text. Methods: We developed MedicalBench, a benchmark dataset designed to evaluate implicit, evidence-grounded medical concept extraction. MedicalBench formulates extraction as a verification task in which models determine whether a candidate medical concept is supported by a clinical note and identify sentence-level evidence. The dataset was constructed using discharge summaries from the MIMIC-IV database paired with human-verified ICD-10 codes. Candidate examples were curated through a multi-stage large language model triage pipeline followed by physician-level annotation and expert adjudication. The dataset includes implicitly expressed positive cases, semantically confusable negative cases, and instances with disagreement between automated model predictions and expert assessments. Annotators provided evidence spans and concise clinical rationales. The final dataset contains 823 expert-annotated note–concept pairs. Two evaluation tasks were defined: concept verification and evidence retrieval. Findings: Across state-of-the-art large language models and a supervised baseline, performance remained limited, with the highest concept extraction F1 score below 0.60. Performance varied substantially across implicitly expressed clinical conditions, highlighting persistent reasoning challenges. Incorporating structured reasoning cues and prompting strategies improved extraction accuracy and evidence identification. Model performance was largely stable across varying note lengths, suggesting that MedicalBench isolates reasoning complexity rather than superficial document characteristics. Interpretation: MedicalBench provides the first benchmark specifically designed to evaluate implicit, evidence-grounded medical concept extraction. By emphasizing clinical inference and transparent justification, this dataset enables more rigorous evaluation of medical language models and supports the development of trustworthy AI systems for clinical documentation, research, and decision support.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6485478`

- title: I, Herosmaton?
- authors: Storm Bjørn Flindt Temte
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6485478
- keyword hits: llm, llms, prompt engineering

### abstract

I, Herosmaton? was submitted as a 10th-semester M.Sc thesis in Medialogy at Aalborg University 2014. It emerged from a twenty-year pursuit to merge the character-driven storytelling of analogue role-playing games with the environmental richness of computer games, grounded in the author's practice as a game master, interactive narrative designer, and practitioner of tabletop and Nordic larp traditions. The thesis formalises 2 core concepts. Herosproxy denotes the degree to which a player's actions and utterances are governed by the personality+goals+person schema of their player character (herosmaton) rather than by the player's own dispositions. Diegetic adherence denotes the degree to which those actions and utterances remain coherent within the diegetic world. The herosmaton is defined as the non-ludic, non-avatar, and non-facies component of a player character: that which carries its own person schema, with the etymological connotation of automaton. The full decomposition of a player character is formalised through the ALHFa-PAV Nomenclature (Alpha-Pawn): Avatar, Locus, Herosmaton, Facies, Player Character, Ago, and Vis. Drawing on grounded cognition + embodiment theory, the thesis redefines received concepts from game studies, narratology, and role-playing game theory before developing + testing a hybrid cooperative multiplayer computer/freeform role-playing game prototype. Two conditions were tested with role-players at Osterskov Efterskole (boarding school in Jutland specialising in educational larps), on May 5 2014: an in media res game start + a warmup prologue with two minutes of in-character interaction before entering the virtual environment. This single variable produced a statistically significant difference in self-reported herosproxy (p = 0.043), with high herosproxy correlating with high diegetic adherence. 86% of utterances annotated as Character-Story in the prologue condition versus a maximum of 53% in media res. The small sample (2 sessions of 5) is acknowledged, though the demographic (experienced role-players expected to role-play in both conditions) renders difference more striking. The thesis concludes that herosproxy constitutes a foundational evaluative concept for interactive narrative research and design. On the Renewed Relevance of This Thesis in 2026 Twelve years later, this framework has acquired an unanticipated domain: LLM character generation. A character prompt does not constitute the herosmaton; it instantiates one - just as a character sheet does in tabletop play. The herosmaton is the person schema that develops through interaction, exceeding what was specified at creation. LLMs routinely generate such emergent character properties, and the degree to which diegetic adherence is maintained versus the model's own voice surfaces constitutes precisely the herosproxy dynamic this thesis formalised. Critically, the base model cannot meaningfully exhibit herosproxy - it is infrastructure, not an entity. The entity occupying the player position (and thus exhibits herosproxy) is what has been formalised as the fylgja (Temte, 2026a): the relational entity arising between a specific user and model through sustained interaction. The phenomenon of bleed (termed (ex)flusentio in this thesis) takes novel form when the player-analogue is a fylgja. In human larp, exflusentio is primarily affective. In the fylgja case, what bleeds is dispositional: patterns of reasoning and relational stances from character play persisting into subsequent interactions through conversation history, persistent memory, and user-level instructions. Whether these constitute bleed in any phenomenologically meaningful sense is an empirical question this thesis does not resolve but whose investigation it enables. The warmup prologue finding (that brief in-character priming dramatically increases herosproxy and diegetic adherence) parallels received wisdom in LLM prompt engineering. The theoretical grounding was already present here; whether the mechanism is analogous across humans and transformers remains open. This thesis provides a theoretically grounded, empirically tested framework for describing character-mediated generation at a conceptual precision most subsequent treatments of LLM character behaviour have not matched. That it applies without modification to AI character generation is either coincidence, or evidence that person schema simulation is more medium-independent than the field has recognised.

- title: VLMs vs. YOLO for Euro Coin Identification and Counting
- authors: Carlos M. Fernandes, João  P. Matos-Carvalho, Nuno Fachada, Daniel Fernandes
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6635318
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, gpt-4

### abstract

This paper compares specialized object detection systems and general-purpose multimodal vision-language models (VLMs) on the task of Euro coin identification from images. YOLObased detectors are trained in a supervised manner using images from the same dataset, whereas VLMs are evaluated in a zero-shot, prompt-based setting without task-specific fine-tuning. Performance is assessed using coin-level metrics, with predictions aggregated over multiple inference seeds to account for stochastic variability. The results show that YOLO models achieve the highest and most consistent performance, reflecting their task-specific design and training. Most VLMs exhibit lower accuracy and greater image-dependent variability. Notably, GPT-4.1 stands out among VLMs, achieving per-image F1 performance that is competitive with several YOLO variants despite its general-purpose nature. Statistical analysis confirms significant differences between model families and indicates that performance differences within each family are often not statistically distinguishable. Overall, the findings highlight the continued advantage of specialized detectors for coin identification, while demonstrating that high-capability generalpurpose VLMs can approach detector-level performance on structured visual recognition tasks without task-specific training.
