- title: Assessing the quality of AI-generated task descriptions for spontaneous volunteers in disaster management
- authors: Michael Klafft, Ricardo Gacitua
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6642478
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Since about 2013, the first prototypical crowdtasking applications for disaster management have become available that integrate generative AI into the process of message design. It has been proposed that generative AI could assist the operator in the Emergency Operations Center during the production of task descriptions, e.g. for the coordination of spontaneous volunteers in disaster situations. However, one key factor that will determine the acceptance of these solutions by practitioners is the quality of the messages proposed. This paper presents an evaluation scheme that helps to determine message quality of task descriptions for spontaneous volunteers in a structured way. It shows how the evaluation scheme can be used in practice, for example to assess and compare the task description quality of (two) different generative AI tools for a given use case. Furthermore, it discusses one notable limitation of the proposed evaluation process, and how this limitation can be addressed in the future.

- title: The 425× Premium and the 85:1 Discount Are the Same Trade: A Partition Framework for Pricing Chinese AI Equity Across Private and Public Markets
- authors: Wilson L Yang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6621378
- keyword hits: deepseek

### abstract

This paper documents and explains a simultaneous pricing anomaly in the 2026 global market for Chinese frontier artificial intelligence (AI) equity. In private markets, DeepSeek is raising its first external funding round at approximately $10 billion, against OpenAI’s post-money valuation of $852 billion — an 85-to-1 ratio applied to two companies the 2026 Stanford AI Index places 2.7 percentage points apart on capability benchmarks. In public markets, MiniMax trades on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange at a price-to-sales (P/S) multiple of approximately 425× on its $79 million 2025 revenue against a $33.6 billion market capitalisation, while OpenAI’s private round implies roughly 35× sales, Anthropic’s 13×, and Nvidia’s public-market multiple is approximately 26×. The same asset class — Chinese frontier AI — is priced at a 91 percent discount in one venue and an order-of-magnitude premium in another, at the same moment in time. I argue that neither market is in error: they are pricing different objects, because they are populated by different buyers operating under non-overlapping mandates. I develop a framework — the Partition Map — that sorts the global AI equity space into four quadrants by asset class and access venue, each with a distinct dominant pricing pool. I isolate the mechanism producing the global-private quadrant’s discount, which I term the Mandate Discount, and document its construction through 2025 via systematic 13F-visible withdrawal of U.S. institutional capital (Appaloosa, Bridgewater, Scion). I argue that the public-market premium is produced by a closed pricing loop in which scarcity dynamics dominate. The paper specifies convergence conditions, testable predictions, and three realistic bridging mechanisms — Middle Eastern sovereign capital, Stock Connect expansion, and privatisation followed by A-share relisting. I acknowledge a bidirectional causality: the partition is not only an effect of capital withdrawal but also a cause of further withdrawal, producing a self-fulfilling equilibrium.

### abstract

Agricultural stubble burning across the Indo-Gangetic Plain and its eastward extension into West Bengal constitutes one of the most consequential land-management failures in contemporary South Asian agriculture. VIIRS satellite telemetry documents a 200% inter-annual increase in localised fire events in West Bengal over the study period (2012–2023), as the region transitions from a passive smoke sink into an active producer of its own hydrological destruction. Existing enforcement paradigms — satellite detection followed by administrative fines — are structurally incoherent when applied to smallholder farmers for whom the cost of field clearance by burning (Rs. 1 per matchstick) is offset by a Rs. 600–1,200 per bigha invoice for mechanised alternatives within a 10–14 day harvest window that admits no delay. This thesis presents the complete technical architecture of Project SATARK (Sanskrit: satark, meaning ‘Alert’ or ‘Vigilant’) — an autonomous, multi-layer detection and pre-emptive dispatch system that reframes the problem from post-hoc fire detection to logistics-first intervention. The architecture integrates three mutually validating intelligence layers. The first is a multi-constellation space-to-soil telemetry pipeline fusing NASA FIRMS VIIRS 375m near-real-time fire products with 15-minute geostationary coverage from INSAT-3DR and GK-2A, bypassing the 4-hour state reporting latency to achieve a target orbit-to-intervention loop of approximately 30 minutes. The second is a Cascaded SwinIR Vision Transformer that ingests NDVI time-series from Sentinel-2 and MODIS 16-day composites to map the ‘green-to-brown’ phenological transition and predict the 14-day harvest vulnerability window at individual field parcel resolution, generating a per-parcel Burn Risk Index (BRI) that drives pre-emptive machinery dispatch. The third is a distributed ESP-NOW mesh swarm of ESP8266 nodes deployed in the field as in-situ soil-hydraulics sentinels, providing ground-truth thermal and micro-climatic validation that extends detection capability through cloud-obscured satellite coverage windows and validates satellite signals against industrial false-positive sources. The domain adaptation strategy — pre-training the SwinIR model on 32,000+ images from the California 2020 August Complex wildfire before domain transfer to West Bengal agricultural conditions — is detailed in full, alongside the cascaded super-resolution protocol (INSAT 4km → MODIS 1km → Landsat-8 100m → Sentinel-2 guided 20m) that eliminates the AI hallucination failure mode identified at direct 4km-to-200m upscaling. The Llama-3 70B semantic false-positive filter, operating on OpenStreetMap LULC overlays, achieves target-zero false dispatch rates in the mixed-use Kolaghat deployment zone. Phase 1 field validation results from the Kolaghat Development Block, Purba Medinipur — including the first systematic micro-climatic dataset for the region — are presented alongside the institutional integration pathway secured through BDO and ADA mandates. The ESP-NOW IoT hardware stack is OSHWA-certified IN000084. The Phase 1 ground-truth dataset is published at Harvard Dataverse DOI: 10.7910/DVN/QG8W63

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6644222`

- title: Are Survey-Based Rate Expectations Informative? Evidence from less liquid markets
- authors: Marcin Dec
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6644222
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

We evaluate four forecasts of the central-bank policy rate on one of the most important less-liquid sovereign bond markets in the world, using Poland as a representative example of a less-liquid market (LLM): macroeconomically non-trivial, marked by episodic liquidity, frequent monetary-regime shifts and a relatively short modern-regime sample. Such markets sit between the deep OECD curves for which term-structure tools were originally designed and the very thin frontier curves for which surveys dominate by default, and they are the setting where the trade-off between model-based and survey-based policy-rate expectations is sharpest. The four forecasts compared are the Nelson-Siegel-Svensson (NSS) market forward fitted with liquidity-sensitive weights, the Adrian-Crump-Moench (ACM) affine risk-free forward, its Bauer-Rudebusch-Wu (BRW) bias-corrected variant, and the implied path from the NBP Survey of Professional Forecasters (PF). Evaluation uses mean errors, Diebold-Mariano and Clark-West tests, and Chong--Hendry forecast-encompassing regressions with Newey-West HAC standard errors. Three findings emerge. First, the BRW-corrected forward is the most accurate at every horizon of twelve months or longer, while the survey is worst at every short horizon. Second, the encompassing regressions attach a negative long-horizon coefficient to the survey, so mechanically combining it with the affine forward would hurt accuracy rather than help. Third, a real-time exercise that re-estimates the affine models on a truly expanding window reverses the long-horizon result for raw ACM but restores it under BRW, confirming that the Nickell-Kilian bias correction is substantive. Robustness checks varying curve weighting, factor count, tenor grid and estimation window leave the ranking unchanged. Practically, any user of policy-rateforecasts on a less-liquid sovereign curve should take the BRW-corrected affine forward as the default and treat the survey as a soft-information overlay at the two to three-year horizon only.

External guardrails for LLM safety add latency and compute overhead while remaining blind to internal model reasoning. We ask: does the model already know when content is harmful? - title: Fiduciary Architecture for AI Systems: Power Needs Duties
- authors: Samantha King
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6646178
- keyword hits: chatgpt

### abstract

Most governance frameworks for artificial intelligence ask what AI systems do: how they perform, where they fail, what they disclose. These are necessary questions. They are not sufficient ones. This paper advances a different frame. As AI systems increasingly mediate access, shape judgment, and function as dependency infrastructure for individuals and institutions, a foundational governance question has emerged: what do these systems owe the people who rely on them? That is a fiduciary question. We have barely begun to ask it seriously. Fiduciary Architecture is a governance design model that imposes duty structures on AI deployments proportionate to the asymmetries of power, dependency, and influence they create. This paper introduces eight core duties, a tiered application framework scaled to consequence level, a failure-mode taxonomy, and a structural mapping to the GICD Framework as the operational assurance layer. Its argument is that without enforceable duty structures, AI governance produces compliance theater rather than institutional accountability. AI safety asks what systems do. Fiduciary Architecture asks what they owe. Those are different questions. They lead to different designs. A note on methodology: this paper was developed in active collaboration with ChatGPT (OpenAI). The framework, analysis, and conclusions are the author's. The collaboration was used for structural development and drafting. No undisclosed optimization shaped its outputs. This disclosure is consistent with the Duty of Cognitive Integrity the paper itself proposes.
