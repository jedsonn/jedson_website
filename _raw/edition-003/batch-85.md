## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6740799`

- title: AI AS A THIRD-PARTY CONSULTANT? THE CORPORATE ATTORNEY-CLIENT PRIVILEGE & WORK PRODUCT DOCTRINE APPLIED TO GENERATIVE AI
- authors: Michele DeStefano
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6740799
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, generative ai

### abstract

Lawyers routinely rely on generative AI platforms--both general-purpose tools such as Claude and ChatGPT and legally focused platforms such as Harvey, Legora, and CoCounsel--to analyze transactions, assess risk, draft documents, and structure legal advice for corporate clients. Currently, however, there is no agreement among federal courts on whether the attorney-client privilege or work product doctrine protects these communications. Four landmark 2026 decisions frame the debate. United States v. Heppner (S.D.N.Y. Feb. 17, 2026) held that documents a criminal defendant generated using a consumer AI platform were not privileged or protected as work product. Warner v. Gilbarco, Inc. (E.D. Mich. Feb. 10, 2026) reached the opposite conclusion, holding that AI-assisted litigation materials constitute protected work product and that treating AI tools as third parties for waiver purposes would "nullify work-product protection in nearly every modern drafting environment." Morgan v. V2X, Inc. (D. Colo. Mar. 30, 2026) substantially extended Warner, analogizing AI to email, phones, and smart devices, and introduced AI-specific protective order language. And the Sixth Circuit in United States v. Farris (Apr. 3, 2026) issued ethical guidance emphasizing safeguarding privilege when attorneys use AI. The critical unresolved question is how courts will treat attorney-directed use of closed, proprietary AI systems that contractually prohibit vendor access to client inputs. This Article makes three main arguments. First, the work product doctrine, although more easily applied to AI, is insufficient because it applies only in anticipation of litigation. Much of the most consequential AI-assisted legal work--M&A due diligence, regulatory compliance, contract drafting--occurs in transactional contexts where work product offers no protection. This limitation makes essential the privilege recommendations this Article proposes. Second, it argues that waiver analysis related to use of AI should be similar to that applied to other third-party vendors like e-discovery vendors, or contract analytics tools used by lawyers. As long as there is a reasonable expectation of confidentiality, Federal courts have never held that uploading privileged materials to contract analytics software, e-discovery platforms, or legal research tools constitutes a third-party disclosure that waives privilege or work product protection. Third, this Article argues that AI platforms should be analyzed under the same doctrine applied to human third-party consultants. AI platforms are functionally the most capable consultants in the history of the legal profession. The question is not whether they are "real" third parties in a philosophical sense, but whether they perform functions that, when performed by humans, courts have protected or declined to protect. However, applying that framework reveals that the emerging doctrine is at once potentially too narrow and too broad. Under the narrowest interpretation, use of AI would be categorically excluded from the privilege framework even when the substantive conditions for privilege—attorney direction, legal purpose, maintained confidentiality— are fully satisfied. Under the broadest application, courts might protect all inputs to, outputs from, and communications with a closed AI platform. This is because when AI is involved, the “legal purpose” inquiry becomes so easy to satisfy on the attorney's side and extremely hard to successfully challenge on the opponent's side, particularly in circuits applying the significant purpose test. This creates a de facto zone of secrecy even though the doctrine formally requires legal purpose, a problem amplified by the unresolved circuit split between the primary purpose and significant purpose tests left intact by the Supreme Court's dismissal of In re Grand Jury as improvidently granted. This Article proposes analysis under a single, uniform agency-based standard requiring a strong nexus between the AI's function and the legal advice ultimately provided, assessed through four factors: (1) who directed the AI use; (2) the nature of the platform--enterprise and closed, or consumer and open; (3) what is being sought to be protected--an output, input, prompt, or communication exchange; and (4) the purpose of the use, specifically whether providing legal advice was a primary or significant purpose. Unlike a categorical rule, this multi-factored nexus test embraces the essential role AI plays in the provision of high-quality legal advice while preventing corporations from using AI-laundered communications as a discovery shield.

- title: AI- versus human-generated titles of master’s dissertations and research articles in linguistics: a comparative genre-based study
- authors: Fatma Benelhadj
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6737104
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Crafting effective academic titles is a challenging task that requires balancing informativeness, conciseness, and reader engagement. This paper investigates titles produced by master’s students of Linguistics at the Faculty of Letters and Humanities of Sfax (FLSHS) and compares them with research article titles written by expert authors and with AI-generated alternatives. The study aims to evaluate students’ titles in relation to expert norms and to explore the potential of Large Language Models (LLMs) in academic title generation. A corpus of 659 titles, including master’s dissertations, research articles, and AI-generated titles, was analysed quantitatively and qualitatively using a synthesised model based on Ken Hyland and Zou (2022) and Swales and Feak (2012). The findings show that students adhere more closely to academic title conventions by producing informative and lexically dense titles, whereas expert authors prioritise reader engagement. AI-generated titles, although capable of producing useful lexical content, rely heavily on formulaic expressions and often fail to recognise the genre-specific conventions of academic discourse. They tend to be longer, more clausal, and more question-based than human-crafted titles. The study suggests that AI tools can serve as valuable brainstorming resources in academic writing pedagogy when their output is critically evaluated and adapted by users.

### abstract

This study examines whether Large Language Models (LLMs) can support the development of sentiment-based indicators for technological hype cycles, characterized by optimistic media language during hype phases and negative sentiment during periods of disillusionment. The rapid growth of digital news makes manual tracking of sentiment trends impractical. Traditional computational approaches relying on basic natural language processing often fail to capture context in long-form texts. LLMs offer a scalable alternative by enabling context-aware sentiment analysis across large collections of complex news articles. The study introduces an LLM-driven methodology to analyze temporal sentiment patterns in English-language news coverage over a 16-year period, focusing on carbon removal technologies. - title: Generative AI Fuels Solo Entrepreneurship, but Teams Still Lead at the Top
- authors: Hyunso Kim, Hyo Kang, Jaeyong Song
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6747440
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai, generative artificial intelligence, gpt-3

### abstract

Recent advances in generative artificial intelligence (AI) are reshaping who enters entrepreneurship, but not who reaches the top of the quality distribution. Using data on over 160,000 product launches on Product Hunt, we find that entrepreneurial entry increased sharply following the public release of ChatGPT-3.5, driven disproportionately by solo entrepreneurs. This shift toward solo entry is particularly pronounced in categories that historically favored team-based ventures. However, much of this growth reflects low-commitment, experimental entry and does not translate into greater representation among the highest-quality outcomes. Team-based ventures are increasingly dominant in the top tiers of platform rankings. These findings suggest that generative AI lowers barriers to solo entrepreneurship while reinforcing team-based advantages.

- title: Evolutionary Alpha Miner: Family-Aware Symbolic Alpha Discovery with LLM-Guided Hybridization A Feedback-Driven Framework for Correlation-Aware Formulaic Alpha Search
- authors: Su Gao
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6746480
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

This paper proposes Evolutionary Alpha Miner, a family-aware evolutionary framework for formulaic alpha discovery. The framework represents alpha expressions as symbolic programs, groups nearduplicate expressions into normalized seed families, and uses ordered A-B parent pairs to generate new candidates. In each pair, the A parent acts as the primary signal backbone, while the B parent is restricted to a weak role such as a gate, regime condition, data-domain modifier, or correlation breaker. Large language models are used as constrained symbolic program synthesizers rather than as unconstrained formula generators. The framework is designed as a closed-loop research system. It records parent-family usage, tag-pair outcomes, mutation modes, simulation results, audit labels, and failure reasons. These records form a feedback memory that guides later exploration and repair. This design separates two tasks that are often mixed together in alpha research: broad candidate exploration and targeted failure-aware refinement. Across 45 search rounds, the system selected 4,620 parent pairs and generated 3,857 valid symbolic candidates. Of these, 3,214 obtained simulation identifiers, corresponding to an executable-candidate rate of 83.33%. The process produced 65 confirmed candidates and 862 strong-but-not-landed candidates. The latter group consists of candidates with promising signal characteristics that fail one or more final constraints, and therefore serves as a structured repair pool. Empirical summaries show that heterogeneous tag pairs such as fundamental+volume, volume+price, and option+price are associated with different success profiles. Mutation modes such as mild correlation breaking, regime hybridization, and weak gating also exhibit distinct outcome patterns. The results suggest that family-aware symbolic hybridization can produce a diverse and diagnostically useful candidate population for formulaic alpha research. The paper does not claim to identify deployable trading strategies, nor does it disclose private alpha expressions, proprietary data, credentials, or confidential implementation details. Its contribution is methodological: it presents a structured, feedbackdriven approach to correlation-aware symbolic candidate generation.

- title: A Survey of Agentic GraphRAG: From Retrieval-augmented Generation to Graph-native Agents
- authors: Zihan Chen, Lei Zheng, Di Zhu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6713979
- keyword hits: agentic, large language model, large language models, llm, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

This survey provides a comprehensive analysis of Agentic GraphRAG, an emerging paradigm that integrates Graph-based Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) with Agentic RAG. While large language models know a lot, their knowledge is frozen at training time and they often suffer from hallucinations. RAG is the standard workaround: before the model answers, fetch relevant text from an external corpus and generate response based on the it. Two lines of work have pushed RAG in different directions: (1) GraphRAG changes what the model retrieves from: instead of a pile of text chunks indexed by similarity, it builds a knowledge graph of entities and their relations, summarizes densely connected regions, and retrieves over that. (2) Agentic RAG changes how retrieval happens: moving beyond one shot generation based on user query, an agent plans, acts, critiques its own work, keeps memory across steps, and then retrieves multiple times as needed. Recent work combines the two, with Karpathy's LLM Wiki as a salient example of non-tech-savvy solutions. The agent's planning, acting, critiquing, and remembering all operate over graph operations. The graph stops being a passive lookup table and becomes the space the agent moves through. This survey treats such combination as a research area in its own right. We organize recent primary papers on a 4x3 grid (four agent roles vs. three graph operations) grounded in a central observation: pure GraphRAG and standalone Agentic RAG each possess recurring failure modes, and the combination of the two paradigms can effectively mitigate these. The body of the survey covers seven method families that populate the grid: single-agent iterative traversal, self-evolving GraphRAG, RL-trained graph walkers, multi-agent graph systems, agentic graph construction, persistent graph memory, and hybrid structured–unstructured retrieval. Three application areas ground the discussion: medicine, legal reasoning, and cyber threat intelligence. Furthermore, recent papers reach directly opposing conclusions about whether graphs are still needed once an agent is in the loop. We trace where the disagreement comes from, and discuss evaluation protocols that jointly score agent trajectories and retrievals, alongside the adversarial robustness of agent–graph systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6694918`

- title: Incentive Issues in Developing Factual LLMs
- authors: Xiang Cheng, Manmohan Aseri
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6694918
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The advent of large language models (LLMs) has drastically lowered the cost of producing misinformation, leading to an explosion in its volume and making fact-fiction distinctions increasingly difficult. Paradoxically, the same LLMs can also be powerful tools for fact-checking and factual content generation, provided they are trained on high-quality factual data. Such factual data are primarily produced by traditional news organizations and content providers, for whom fact collection is a costly process, and they may not have enough incentive to share these facts with LLMs. We study the incentives for fact sharing between LLMs and content providers using a game-theoretic model. We show that content providers with low monetizability may naturally partner with LLMs without any compensation. Interestingly, such partnerships reduce the LLM's factual quality. This is because partnership reduces the competition between the LLM and the content provider, which leads to less effort from the content provider in fact collection. The factual quality of LLMs improves only when they partner with providers with highly monetizable content. However, such content providers require financial compensation for partnering with LLMs. We analyze several compensation schemes and find, counterintuitively, that compensation can further reduce the LLM's factual quality compared to no compensation, when the content provider's monetizability is low or moderate. This is because the compensation further reduces competition between the LLM and the content provider, thereby reducing the content provider's incentive to exert effort in fact collection. Our results highlight nuances in the partnership between LLMs and content providers and show that such partnerships do not always yield a more factual LLM.
