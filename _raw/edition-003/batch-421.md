## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7122520`

- title: Substitution, Not Speculation: Why the Artificial Intelligence Investment Cycle Need Not Be a Bubble, and Why Its Robustness Accelerates the Commoditization of the Build
- authors: Arthur de Miranda Neto
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7122520
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

The 2025-2026 debate over an "AI bubble" has generated much heat and little classificatory clarity. This paper does not argue that valuations are modest, that froth is absent, or that no capital will be destroyed. It argues something narrower: the prevailing analogy to the dot-com episode of 1999-2001 misclassifies the object being repriced, and once that error is corrected the cycle need not be read as a bubble at all. Dot-com was the speculative repricing of a medium: the internet transports, stores, and recombines value created elsewhere but performs no productive work itself. The present cycle reprices a production factor: a synthetic, scalable, increasingly capable substitute for qualified human labor. The distinction is not rhetorical. A medium is valued on the expectation that others will build productive activity upon it, a long, contingent link, easily over-extrapolated, which is why media bubbles are common; a factor is valued on the marginal product it delivers directly, and the link between an AI system and the cognitive task it performs (drafting, translating, coding, reviewing) is immediate and measurable. Building on the author's Commoditization Stack and Data-Driven Platform of Platforms (DPoP) frameworks, the paper advances four propositions. First, the concentration of venture capital in frontier laboratories is a rational repricing of a labor-substituting factor, not mania. Second, weakness in SaaS valuations is not a symptom of an AI bubble but a confirmation of the commoditization gradient: layer-4 application software is being substituted by agentic AI as the Stack predicts. Third, AI's dual-use character and centrality to state strategy place it in a different risk class than commercial speculative assets. Fourth, the cycle's genuine pathologies (circular vendor financing, the enterprise deployment gap, energy constraints, the contested economic life of AI hardware) are best read as the friction of a mispriced-but-real factor market, not evidence of a content-free bubble. An illustrative, data-calibrated demonstration for Brazil's services economy makes the mechanism concrete: as capability commoditizes, a measurable share of a national wage bill is converted into payments for an imported factor that migrate toward the bloc hosting the frontier laboratories, surfacing as revenue and valuation, while a substantial premium is retained domestically. Modelling one economy and only the labor-substitution channel, it is a conservative lower bound; benchmarked against the official Brazil-United States trade balance, that flow would become a first-order driver of the bilateral imbalance. A network-effects reading converges: durable effects accrue not to commoditizing capability access but to the defensible upper layers, where revenue compounds. A falsifiable diagnostic closes the argument, and the double thread stands: the robustness that makes AI a real factor is the same force that accelerates the commoditization of the build.

### abstract

We audit whether candle-based machine-learning models can turn predictions of cryptocurrency extrema or short-horizon outcomes into positive Binance Spot paper policies after assumed costs. Numerical results come from scripted fixed-seed model runs and deterministic simulators; human-supervised AI agents supported the July 20 evidence-integrity revision through literature retrieval, separately tasked critique, artifact reconciliation, documentation, and source packaging, not trading decisions. ## uid: `arxiv:2607.18912v1`

- title: From a Multilingual Streaming ASR Backbone to Kenyan-Language Systems: Data-Centric Adaptation of Nemotron 3.5 for Kikuyu, Dholuo, and Kalenjin
- authors: Mark Gatere
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18912v1
- keyword hits: fine-tuning

### abstract

Automatic speech recognition (ASR) for African languages is constrained by orthographic inconsistency, annotation artifacts, missing audio, speaker and domain imbalance, and evaluation procedures that differ from deployment. We present an end-to-end engineering study adapting NVIDIA Nemotron 3.5 ASR Streaming 0.6B to Kikuyu, Dholuo, and Kalenjin. Starting from a Kenyan Swahili-adapted checkpoint, we retain its cache-aware FastConformer RNN-T, prompt conditioning, and streaming decoder during full-parameter fine-tuning. The study covers corpus auditing, Unicode normalization, split checks, duration filtering, low-rate continuation, validation-based checkpoint selection, true-streaming evaluation, artifact preservation, and isolated serving. On internal, adaptively consulted evaluation sets excluded from gradient updates at context [56,13], selected Kikuyu and Dholuo models achieve 42.97% and 33.98% WER, respectively. Dholuo records 9.59% CER and 8.13% no-space CER under its frozen historical label policy; Kikuyu records 7.79% no-space CER. Kalenjin remains a work in progress: v1-v reaches 68.74% WER on a 2,411-row clean-v3 diagnostic subset excluding long-pause annotations, digit-bearing references, and targets shorter than three tokens. Its checkpoint selection used a mixed-source validation manifest containing test-origin rows, so the score is not an independent generalization estimate. We also report negative findings involving non-speech labels, short-utterance over-generation, boundary-sensitive WER, and cloud job-lifecycle failures. We make no state-of-the-art claim because the internal sets, repeated consultation, and normalization differ from public benchmarks. This work provides an auditable account of adapting a multilingual streaming model into language-specific systems without discarding streaming constraints.

## uid: `doi:10.5281/zenodo.19060285`

- title: Building Trust in Autonomous Commerce: A Verifiable Global Event Timeline and AI-Ready Fraud Intelligence Layer
- authors: Rajat Srivastava
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19436v1
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Agentic commerce protocols such as AP2 and ACP define mechanisms for secure agent-initiated transactions but do not provide interoperable, tamper-evident auditability or verifiable temporal ordering of events across heterogeneous domains. This paper addresses these gaps by proposing a verifiable global event timeline for agentic commerce, constructed from four core components: canonical event schemas that enforce deterministic serialization, deterministic batch formation ensuring reproducible ordering without reliance on synchronized clocks, Merkle-based append-only commitments providing logarithmic-cost inclusion proofs, and blockchain anchoring establishing a tamper-evident temporal backbone. Building on this infrastructure, we introduce a cryptographically signed fraud marker that binds risk labels to anchored evidence through an unforgeable provenance chain, and a dataset lineage model enabling reproducible, tamper-evident AI training pipelines. Empirical results from a prototype implementation demonstrate: Merkle tree construction processes 50,000 events in 47 milliseconds; end-to-end verification completes in under 0.013 milliseconds regardless of batch size; inclusion proof sizes grow logarithmically from 320 bytes at 1,000 events to 512 bytes at 50,000 events; and Merkle-based verification outperforms linear scan by 14.4x at 50,000 events.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7042538`

- title: Attending to the Supply Chain: Science-based Climate Commitments
- authors: William Diebel, Robert D. Klassen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7042538
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

For many large firms, the vast majority of carbon emissions originate outside their internal operations, across their supply chains. The Science-Based Targets initiative is among the few frameworks that require firms to confront these emissions through externally defined, independently validated reduction targets. Yet most publicly listed firms have not committed to the initiative, prompting the question of what distinguishes those that do. Drawing on the attention-based view of the firm, we argue that supply chain operations create attentional structures, namely organizational routines and information channels that direct senior managers' focus and shape their priorities, influencing whether firms commit credibly to reduce supply chain carbon emissions (i.e., Scope 3). We propose two pathways: proactive engagement (the top-down mechanism), tested using CDP Supply Chain Program membership, and reactive exposure (the bottom-up mechanism), tested using cumulative environmental supply chain incidents reported in the media. Using a panel of 13,663 firms across 113 countries (2013-2022), we find that both pathways are positively and independently associated with commitment, with magnitudes that are substantial relative to the annual base rate. Among firms that commit, prior proactive engagement is further associated with more stringent Scope 3 reduction targets. Thus, our findings extend the attention-based view beyond the firm's boundary, showing that supply chain operations act as interorganizational attentional structures and that credible climate commitments can be understood as attentional outcomes. Because supply chains account for such a large share of corporate emissions, programs and transparency mandates that specifically target supply chain operations may accelerate credible commitments more effectively than environmental initiatives focused on firms' own operations.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7032178`

- title: Agentic Coding, Developer Exploration, and Software Security
- authors: Ao Huang, Nina Huang, Kevin Hong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7032178
- keyword hits: agentic, claude

### abstract

Agentic coding has shifted artificial intelligence (AI) from an assistant augmenting local changes to delegated agents executing tasks end-to-end. We argue that agentic coding collapses the cost of exploration but not the judgment that sustains it: delegated construction becomes cheaper, but ongoing maintenance may require judgment agents do not supply and novice developers, whose exploration expands most, least possess. Leveraging Claude Code’s launch and parsing its commit-message trailers in public commit histories, we observe 61,056 treated developers before and after treatment and compare them with 61,056 controls matched on pre-treatment activity, using monthly activity from January 2025 through March 2026. Combining rolling-entry matching with matrix-completion counterfactual estimation, we find agentic coding increases activity: repository initiation rises by 7.4% and push activity by 93.1%. The increase is concentrated in exploration: pushes to original repositories rise by 72.6%, versus 4.5% for pushes to forks, with sharper contrasts for novices. We follow repositories developers create to test whether exploratory expansion compromises security. Using OpenSSF Scorecard measures, we separate security practice (construction-bundled setup including security policies or static analysis) from security state, a repository’s absence of known code and dependency vulnerabilities, which ongoing maintenance sustains. Repositories show modest practice gains, similar across experience levels, but sizable state declines, steepest among novices. The findings shift evidence on AI coding from task-level productivity to the extensive margin of software production. Agentic coding broadens exploration, especially among novices, while underproviding the sustained judgment needed to keep artifacts secure: the margin on which those developers are most exposed.

- title: Energy-Sensing Integrated Gas Sensors with Mg-MOF-74 Catalyst for Highly Sensitive and Selective NO2 Sensing
- authors: Xinyu Li, Ce Guo, Zhaokun Sun, Mengqiu Wang, Jilei Yang, Xiao Wang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7163029
- keyword hits: transformer model

### abstract

Monitoring harmful nitrogen dioxide (NO2) gas is crucial for atmospheric pollution control, health monitoring and agricultural production. However, traditional semiconductor NO2 gas sensors typically face challenges in reliance on external power source, high operating temperatures and poor selectivity. Herein, an energy-sensing integrated NO2 gas sensor based on zinc-air batteries is constructed, in which the battery’s cathode also serves as the sensor’s gas-sensing layer. This necessitates cathode materials with both high NO2 reduction activity and gas adsorption capacity. Four X-MOF-74 materials (X = Mg, Ni, Zn and Co) featuring abundant single-metal active sites and tunable pore sizes were therefore prepared as catalysts. The experimental findings demonstrate that, at room temperature (RT, 25℃), the sensor with Mg-MOF-74 catalyst shows the largest NO2 sensing response (25 ppm @ 0.23 V) and lower theoretical limit of detection of 10.1 ppb. Theoretical calculations indicate that Mg-MOF-74 possesses a higher isothermal adsorption heat (3.22 kJ/mol) to NO2 gas toward that of other sensors. Moreover, the O atom in NO2 forms a coordinate bond with the Mg atom and gains 0.98 |e| as confirmed by Bader charge analysis, significantly facilitating the adsorption of NO2 gas molecule. After that, a Transformer model is introduced to enhance the NO2 gas identification capability in complex gas environments. Finally, by integrating the gas sensor, transmission module and intelligent control system, a smart sensing device is constructed for real-time and remote NO2 monitoring.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7162285`

- title: Adaptive Strategy Generation for Boundary Value Exploration Beyond Numeric Inputs
- authors: Sabinakhon Akbarova, Felix Dobslaw, Robert Feldt
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7162285
- keyword hits: agentic, llm

### abstract

Software behavior often changes abruptly at boundaries between input regions, and these transitions are known to be fault-prone. Boundary Value Exploration (BVE) automates boundary discovery by searching for pairs of similar inputs that nevertheless trigger different program behaviors. Existing automated BVE techniques rely on mutation operators hand-engineered for each input type, or even for each function under test, which has confined their use to numeric inputs. We present ABEX, an agentic LLM-based framework that replaces operator engineering with adaptive strategy generation: specialized LLM agents propose, select, and execute boundary-exploration strategies, guided by execution feedback and a quality-diversity (QD) archive. Because strategies are expressed in natural language, they can encode both type-level and function-specific knowledge, and effective strategies can even be stored and reused. We evaluate ABEX in a black-box setting on 20 functions with numeric, string, array, and mixed inputs. On numeric functions, ABEX outperforms a state-of-the-art QD method on 10 of 11 functions, with average QD-scores 11.7x higher. On non-numeric functions, addressed here for the first time in automated black-box BVE, ABEX discovers domain-aligned boundary behaviors for all subjects. Mutation testing shows the discovered boundaries are fault-revealing: with equally sized test suites, ABEX reaches an average mutation score of 86.2% versus 61.9% for the QD baseline, and kills nine times as many hard-to-detect stubborn mutants. An ablation study identifies adaptive strategy generation as the primary driver of these gains.
