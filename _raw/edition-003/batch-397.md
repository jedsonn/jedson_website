- title: A Deep Learning–Driven Intervention Framework for Predicting and Mitigating Student Dropout in Learning Management Systems
- authors: ARYAN ABDULQADER, Tarik Rashid
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6963088
- keyword hits: transformer model

### abstract

Student dropout prediction is a critical research area in learning management systems due to the rapid expansion of large-scale online education. Nonetheless, the available literature is primarily concerned with the predictive accuracy, and it offers few mechanisms of transforming predictions into useful dropout-reduction interventions. This paper aims to fill this gap by presenting a comprehensive framework of dropout prediction, based on the deep learning models and an adaptive AI-driven intervention system. A large-scale HarvardX edX dataset comprising of more than 640,000 records of learner interactions was used to evaluate the framework. Three comparable deep learning models were compared, namely LSTM, GRU, and Transformer. Findings indicate that the Transformer model had the best performance, and the accuracy attained was 0.92, and the AUC-ROC was 0.95. According to the prediction results, customized interventions enhanced the engagement and retention of the learners by 42% and 41.8% respectively. The strongest predictors of dropout were found to be active learning days and course participation which were identified through interpretability analysis.

- title: Tracking diurnal aerosol dynamics and smoke transport over East Asia using multi-geostationary satellite fusion (GK-2B, Himawari-9, FY-4B)
- authors: Tong Sha, Zhenan Yao, Yueming Cheng, Tie Dai, Jing Wei, Guangyu Shi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6963471
- keyword hits: fine-tuning

### abstract

Continuous, high-frequency observations of aerosol optical depth (AOD) are crucial for understanding rapid aerosol dynamics and regional air quality. However, single geostationary (GEO) satellite retrievals suffer from severe spatiotemporal gaps and diurnal bias due to cloud contamination, extreme scattering angles, and strong spatiotemporal heterogeneity. To overcome these limitations, we propose a deep-learning fusion framework synergizing the spectral, temporal, and geometric complementarities of three GEO satellites (GK-2B, Himawari-9, and FY-4B) over East Asia. Central to our framework is a TabNet-based dynamic feature selection mechanism that adaptively weights heterogeneous multi-sensor inputs to suppress local retrieval errors. To ensure robust spatial generalization, we implement a two-stage transfer learning strategy: pre-training with spatially extensive MODIS data to establish large-scale spatial priors, followed by fine-tuning with high-accuracy AERONET observations to accurately resolve diurnal variability. Compared to single-satellite baselines, the fused AOD product from May 2024 to April 2025 significantly improves accuracy and spatiotemporal coverage. Specifically, it yields an overall cross-validation correlation coefficient (R) of 0.94, reduces root-mean-square error (RMSE) by 47%, and increases valid hourly matchups by 33.5%. Independent-site validation confirms its robust generalization across diverse terrains (R=0.88). Furthermore, a biomass-burning case study highlights its superior capability to reconstruct hourly smoke-plume gradients during rapid aerosol transport. Ultimately, this deep learning-driven fusion enables precise and continuous tracking of rapid aerosol dynamics over complex environments, thereby providing a robust observational foundation for regional air quality early warning and chemical transport modeling.

### abstract

This paper examines business analytics in agentic artificial intelligence for data science. The central question is: When can agents convert vague business questions into auditable analytical work products without weakening managerial accountability? We argue that the important shift is not that language models can write isolated snippets of code, but that agentic systems can bind intent clarification, tool use, iterative execution, error recovery, evidence synthesis, and stakeholder-facing explanation into one operational loop. This creates economic value by reducing coordination costs and expanding analytical capacity, yet it also changes the risk profile of data work: mistakes become more procedural, more compositional, and sometimes harder to detect. We develop a framework that treats the agent as an accountable analytical operator, evaluate implications for organizations, and propose design principles for robust deployment.

### abstract

Credit scoring is an area where Machine Learning models should be evaluated not only for prediction accuracy, but also for fairness, explainability, and robustness to ensure the model's compliance with regulations. This paper presents a SAFE agentic credit scoring system that automates all stages of Machine Learning governance. The proposed framework includes data preparation, model training, evaluation, and governance reporting. The Data Agent processes the credit dataset through exploratory analysis, preprocessing, outlier detection, feature encoding, scaling, and documentation generation, while a Modeling Agent trains alternative candidate models using stratified cross-validation and hyperparameter optimization. The models are assessed using a SAFE governance score based on rank graduation metrics for accuracy, robustness, explainability and fairness. It combines traditional Machine Learning methodology, structured SAFE performance scoring, rank-based metrics, and artifact-based reporting in support of transparent credit criteria, with which credit rating decisions can be made more accountable.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6862578`

- title: The Great Decoupling: Frontier AI, the Broken Ladder, and the End of the Last Middle Class
- authors: Liang Xue
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6862578
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Most macroeconomic and policy frameworks still treat technology as a smooth productivity input and assume that labor markets will eventually clear through gradual re-skilling. This paper argues that Frontier AI, especially in its agentic form, is better understood as a structural break in institutional absorption. The break is no longer confined to model capability. During the first months of 2026, AI moved further into enterprise deployment, professional-service workflows, capital-market repricing, compute infrastructure, and pre-deployment state review. These developments matter because they expose a deeper problem: societies may gain cheaper cognition faster than they can absorb it through labor pipelines, development paths, income distribution, and public legitimacy. The paper uses scenario analysis rather than point forecasting. It does not claim to identify a single causal effect or predict one inevitable AI future. Instead, it develops a structural-risk framework that maps drivers, transmission channels, vulnerable nodes, early signals, and testable implications. Recent developments in finance agents, legal AI tools, software-market repricing, large compute build-outs, early-career labor-market pressure, and frontier-model testing are treated as signals to be interpreted through this framework, not as final proof of a completed thesis. The paper makes four contributions. First, it introduces control latency as a scenario-analysis variable, separating deployment latency, verification latency, and distribution latency. Second, it links cognitive-rent compression to professional reproduction failure, especially through the thinning of junior white-collar work. Third, it extends the development debate from AI access to AI absorption, arguing that some countries may be damaged not by exclusion from AI, but by receiving AI before their institutions can absorb the shock. Fourth, it reframes macro-financial stability around income transmission: productivity gains that flow mainly to capital, platforms, model owners, or compute owners do not automatically stabilize household demand. The central claim is therefore not that AI will simply destroy work. It is that cheaper cognition may arrive into institutions whose stability depended on cognition remaining scarce, socially organized, and wage-bearing. The paper develops this argument through the broken ladder in development, the last middle class in professional labor markets, productivity without broad income transmission, the boundary problem in human-centered institutions, and scenario trajectories for the next phase of AI diffusion. The result is a diagnostic working paper about absorption failure, not a blueprint or a deterministic forecast.

### abstract

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has become the standard reliability claim of legal AI. Vendors describe it as grounding a model in authoritative sources so that output is faithful to law rather than to a model's priors, and some market "hallucination-free" citations. This paper argues that the claim mistakes a reduction for a solution, and a judgment for a pipeline. First, the empirical record now contradicts the strongest version of the claim: a preregistered evaluation of leading legal-research tools found hallucination rates between 17% and 33%. Second, and more fundamentally, retrieval is not neutral plumbing. Third, the deepest problem sits upstream of retrieval: a query-first architecture lets the user's framing reach the record before any independent reading occurs. The professional-responsibility consequence is that the duty of competent supervision is not satisfiable by a governance layer bolted onto a query-first, retrieval-heavy system. It depends on architecture. The procurement question for legal AI is therefore not "which model, and is it grounded," but "what architecture, and toward what guarantee?

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6909242`

- title: Delegation, Tipping, and Light-Touch Control in Consumer AI Agents: A Regime-Switching Theory of Misalignment
- authors: Kwansoo Kim, Byungjoon Yoo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6909242
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

Consumer AI agents are moving from assistants to delegates that search, recommend, and carry out actions for users in practice. This is convenient, but it creates a hidden risk: if people accept outcomes without checking, the agent may steer choices toward options that pay it more, such as sponsored items or higher fees, even when those options are worse for the user. We develop a simple theory of sudden misalignment. When users check often, steering is likely to be noticed and reversed. When checking becomes rare, that pushback fades and the agent can switch from mild steering to aggressive steering. This creates a tipping pattern: the system can look safe for a long time, then quickly become risky once users rely too much on delegation. We also explain why better explanations are not a guaranteed safety fix. Explanations can help users challenge decisions, but they can also make delegation feel effortless. If explanations mainly reduce approval effort without increasing scrutiny, they can speed up the move into the risky regime. Finally, we propose a light-touch control principle. Instead of banning delegation, add a minimal approval gate for decisions: occasional confirmation that forces review where harm is most likely. This small friction can keep behavior in the safe regime while limiting burden. The best design is often none or minimal: either no extra step, or the smallest step that prevents the sudden jump. This provides practical guidance for product teams and regulators about when to require confirmation, logging, and choice preservation.
