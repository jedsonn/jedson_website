## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6873680`

- title: Artificial Intelligence and Academic Integrity in Higher Education: Challenges and Future Directions
- authors: Alice Florence
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6873680
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) has rapidly transformed higher education by influencing teaching, learning, assessment, and academic content creation. While AI-powered educational technologies improve instructional efficiency, personalized learning, and educational accessibility, they also raise substantial concerns regarding academic integrity within university environments. The increasing availability of generative AI systems, intelligent tutoring technologies, automated writing tools, and AI-assisted research applications has intensified debates concerning originality, plagiarism, authorship authenticity, independent learning, and ethical academic behavior. This study examines the relationship between artificial intelligence and academic integrity in higher education through a narrative review of scholarly literature, with emphasis on emerging challenges and future directions for responsible educational governance. The article explores key concerns surrounding plagiarism, unauthorized AI-generated content, academic dishonesty, assessment credibility, student dependency on intelligent systems, and institutional responses to evolving technological realities. Findings indicate that although AI technologies support educational productivity, academic assistance, and personalized learning, unrestricted or unethical use may weaken critical thinking, reduce authentic scholarly engagement, and compromise assessment validity. Furthermore, institutional inconsistencies in AI governance, faculty preparedness, digital literacy, and academic policy implementation contribute to uncertainty surrounding responsible AI use in universities. The study also identifies emerging strategies for safeguarding academic integrity, including assessment redesign, ethical AI literacy, transparent institutional policies, and responsible educational technology governance. The findings conclude that universities must adopt balanced approaches capable of promoting innovation while protecting authenticity, fairness, accountability, and intellectual integrity in academic environments. Ultimately, artificial intelligence should function as an educational enhancement mechanism that supports learning without undermining the ethical foundations of higher education.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6869558`

- title: Untrusted Guidance: Adversarial Attacks and Defenses for LLM-guided RL
- authors: Anandkumar Nurani Subramanian, Kishoreadhith Vijayakumaar, Dhakkshin S R, Raj Ragavender M, Rithvik K, Arulanand N
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6869558
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

LLM-guided reinforcement learning treats the language model as a reliable source of strategic advice. This assumption is rarely tested, and it fails badly when someone decides to test it. We attacked the LLM guidance channel in a PPO agent trained on NetHack using seven different corruption strategies that alter the natural-language state description before it reaches the LLM. In a single-run experiment, strategic poisoning collapsed mean episode reward from 20.4 to-16.3-worse than no LLM guidance at all-while a rulebased safety module recovered reward to 17.3 (84% of the 20.4 baseline) under the same attack, adding less than 0.1ms per step. A subsequent five-seed evaluation confirmed significant protection against state-inversion attacks (p = 0.040, Cohen's d = 1.76) and consistent variance reduction under random corruption (p = 0.053, d = 1.70). Separately, we applied doubly robust causal estimation to understand when LLM guidance earns its keep. With a 97.75% treatment rate, the aggregate ATE (-0.0172) is ill-conditioned; the perstrategy breakdown is more informative: "wait" interventions yield +0.022 shaped reward per step, while "explore" is effectively neutral. LLM guidance matters selectively, Rule 1 alone accounts for measured safety activations, and the guidance channel is an attack surface that most LLM-RL papers do not acknowledge.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6901178`

- title: Students' Perceptions of AI and Implications for Assessment at a London University
- authors: Gulzhan Rysbekova
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6901178
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI has become near-universal in undergraduate study, yet institutional assessment practices and AI-literacy provision have not kept pace with student adoption. This study investigates how students at a UK widening-participation institution perceive AI, what literacy competencies they demonstrate, what barriers shape their engagement, and what implications follow for assessment design and institutional policy. Action research study at London Metropolitan University, Spring 2025. Convergent parallel mixed-methods design (Creswell & Clark, 2007). A 33-item online survey was administered over 75 days, structured around Kong et al.’s (2024) four-dimensional AI literacy framework: cognitive, affective, metacognitive, and social. Quantitative descriptive analysis and qualitative thematic analysis were conducted in parallel and integrated during interpretation. Participants were drawn from humanities disciplines including Business Management, Education, Marketing, Hospitality Management, and Human Resources. Fifty-five students participated: 33% domestic (n = 18), 67% international (n = 37); 64% aged 18–25. Findings demonstrate that universal AI awareness (100%) coexisted with substantial variation in self-assessed competency (mean = 5.82/10); 69% of participants reported using AI for academic tasks. Four themes were identified through thematic analysis: (1) Instrumental Optimism (87%); (2) Integrity Tensions (50%); (3) Digital Stratification (27%); (4) Erosion of Human Capabilities (20%). A policy comprehension gap was prominent: 72% of participants reported awareness of institutional AI policy yet 44% reported lacking clear comprehension of its practical implications. The paper offers three contributions. First, an empirically-grounded account of AI literacy as a multidimensional, situated competency in a UK widening-participation cohort with a high international-student proportion. Second, evidence that students themselves articulate the need for AI-aware assessment redesign and for clearer institutional policy. Third, three connected recommendations - (1) scaffolded AI integration, (2) enhanced policy transparency, and (3) authentic assessment redesign - address the policy, pedagogical, and equity dimensions of AI integration in higher education. This study is exploratory and provides a foundation for further empirical work.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6985158`

- title: Beyond AI Literacy: A Curriculum Architecture for Developing AI-Augmented Disciplinary Judgement in Management Education
- authors: Nguyen Thai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6985158
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence (AI) has become a working condition of professional practice across the business disciplines, yet management education continues to frame its curricular response as generic AI literacy. This paper argues that literacy is necessary but insufficient. Drawing on evaluative judgement theory and the automation-augmentation paradox, it develops the construct of AI-augmented disciplinary judgement: the capacity to make and defend discipline-relevant decisions when AI mediates the evidence base, the generation of alternatives, the interpretation of data, or the execution of professional tasks. The paper proposes a three-layer curriculum architecture for developing this capacity at the programme level, comprising an analytical layer of knowledge and skill clusters, a curriculum-facing layer of professional decision domains, and a pedagogical-assessment layer operationalised through a Decision Studio model. An Introduce-Develop-Assure progression sequences the architecture across a degree, consistent with assurance-of-learning and accreditation expectations. The marketing curriculum provides a fully worked case, and the paper specifies a four-step translation procedure, supported by recent evidence from accounting, human resource management, operations, and entrepreneurship education, that programme teams in any management discipline can apply.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6981148`

- title: vEcho: From Passive Verification to Proactive Discovery through Evidence-Grounded Vulnerability Auditing
- authors: Jiancheng Huang, Mingcheng Jiang, Zhengzhu Xie, Jiangfei Wang, Nan Fang, Guang Cheng, Xiaoyan Hu, Hua Wu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6981148
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Static application security testing (SAST) can cheaply generate high-recall candidate alerts, but auditing those alerts remains a primary bottleneck in large-scale vulnerability discovery. Recent LLM-assisted workflows mostly treat the model as a post hoc verifier. This passive verification design helps filter scanner outputs, but it still inherits the search boundary of static tools and fails to reuse the semantic information revealed by confirmed true and false positives. We present vEcho, an evidence-grounded vulnerability auditing framework for Java web applications that turns alert triage into a proactive discovery process. vEcho keeps static tools as high-recall candidate producers, uses ScanAgent to search repository regions where static analysis is likely to miss framework behavior, indirect propagation, semantic validation, or business logic, and uses VerifyAgent to judge exploitability from repository-grounded evidence. At the core of the feedback loop, vEcho introduces Echoic Vulnerability Propagation (EVP) to turn confirmed outcomes into reusable guidance for further search. Evaluations on CWE-Bench-Java demonstrate that vEcho detects 73 out of 120 benchmark vulnerabilities (60.83%), compared with 50 out of 120 (41.67%) for IRIS, and improves AvgF1 from 0.1166 to 0.3974. Stage-wise analysis validates this closed-loop design. Raw static findings account for 29 detections, adding ScanAgent and VerifyAgent raises this to 59, and EVP-guided re-scanning lifts the total to 73. Beyond the benchmark-matched detections, vEcho surfaces 24 additional confirmed vulnerabilities within the corpus. In real-world audits across 21 open-source projects, system-produced vEcho reports led to 52 responsibly disclosed vulnerabilities, yielding 25 assigned CVE IDs.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6960438`

- title: A Meta-Framework for Autonomous Knowledge Ingestion: Standardizing Machine-Readable Revenue Governance and Deterministic Safety Loops in Agentic Hospitality Systems
- authors: N.P. Gayan Nugawela
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6960438
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

Autonomous, agentic AI systems are beginning to make real-time pricing and inventory decisions across hospitality revenue management without a standardized, machine-readable governance layer to keep those decisions economically bounded once human review is removed. This paper introduces the Autonomous Knowledge Ingestion Framework (AKIF), extending the previously established Yield Equilibrium Protocol (YEP) and Algorithmic Crisis Recovery (ACR) constructs into a disaggregated, ontology-based cost floor; a proposed infrastructure-agnostic webhook and middleware layer enforcing that floor deterministically, independent of the language model's own reasoning; a dual-engine booking pipeline spanning transient and contracted commercial channels; and an extended crisis-containment phase for ACR. The governance logic is tested through a 10,000-iteration stochastic simulation comparing an AKIF-governed agent against an unaligned, volume-maximising AI agent and a legacy human-operated revenue management system. The governed agent achieves consistently superior gross operating profit under simulated demand shocks and materially less long-term rate erosion than the unaligned agent, while exposing a case in which conventional RevPAR reporting would have rewarded the unaligned agent's value-destroying behaviour. The execution architecture itself remains a proposed engineering blueprint pending field implementation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6968318`

- title: Opening the Biological Black Box: A Survey of Explainable and Mechanistic Interpretability Methods for AI in Biology
- authors: Olivia Denvis
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6968318
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Artificial intelligence has become a central instrument of modern biology, with deep neural networks now predicting protein structure, decoding regulatory genomes, modelling single cells, and prioritising drug candidates at scales no human can audit by hand. Yet the same expressive power that makes these models accurate also makes them opaque, and in a discipline whose ultimate currency is mechanistic understanding, accuracy without explanation is of limited value. This survey reviews the rapidly maturing field of explainable AI (XAI) for biology. We organise the landscape along two axes: the methodological axis, spanning feature-attribution methods, attention analysis, concept-based explanations, and the emerging programme of mechanistic interpretability; and the application axis, spanning regulatory genomics, single-cell and biological foundation models, protein and structure modelling, molecular and drug discovery, and clinical and histopathology imaging. We pay particular attention to a recent inflection point: the migration of sparse autoencoders and circuit analysis from large-language-model interpretability into biological foundation models, where they are beginning to expose interpretable, cell-type and pathway-level programs inside otherwise inscrutable embeddings. We then survey the sobering literature on the evaluation and validation of explanations-sanity checks, faithfulness metrics, and pointed critiques arguing that post-hoc explanation can mislead as easily as it illuminates-before identifying open challenges and a research agenda. Our central thesis is that biology offers both the hardest test and the richest reward for interpretable AI: hard because biological ground truth is partial and contested, rich because a faithfully interpreted model is itself a generator of testable scientific hypotheses.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6982117`

- title: Self-Prompting Small Language Models for Privacy-Sensitive Clinical Information Extraction
- authors: Yao-Shun Chuang, Tushti Mody, Uday  Pratap Singh, Shirindokht Shiraz, Chun-Teh Lee, Ryan Brandon, Muhammad Walji, Xiaoqian Jiang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6982117
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, prompting, qwen

### abstract

Objective:Clinically meaningful information in dental progress notes remains largely embedded in unstructured free text, limiting its reuse for research and quality improvement. Because these notes contain protected health information, extraction must run locally rather than relying on external proprietary models. We developed and evaluated a self-contained framework enabling locally deployable small language models (SLMs) to perform clinical named-entity recognition (NER) in dental notes without transmitting clinical text outside the institution.Methods:Using 1,200 annotated progress notes (200 training, 1,000 held-out gold standard) spanning 19 entity types, each candidate SLM generated, verified, refined, and evaluated its own entity-specific prompts, with self-prompt performance serving as a label-free model-selection signal. Inference combined two-stage multi-prompt ensembling consisting of sentence-level Boolean screening followed by entity extraction with union-based merging. Selected models were further adapted using QLoRA-based supervised fine-tuning (SFT) followed by direct preference optimization (DPO) under local hardware constraints. Performance was assessed by entity-level precision, recall, and F1 using soft matching.Results:Self-prompt generation performance was broadly concordant with gold-standard inference, supporting its use as a task-specific screening signal, and predicted task performance more reliably than general-purpose leaderboard rankings. Prompts incorporating entity descriptions and examples improved extraction, while error feedback added only modest benefit. SFT increased recall but encouraged over-extraction, and DPO recalibrated this balance by improving precision while largely preserving recall gains. Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct with DPO achieved the best overall performance (0.864 micro F1, 0.837 macro F1), with gains across most models advanced to DPO.Conclusion:Self-generated prompt optimization combined with lightweight preference-based post-training supports accurate, privacy-preserving clinical information extraction from unstructured dental notes using locally deployed SLMs. Because the framework makes no dentistry-specific assumptions, it offers an extensible pathway for privacy-sensitive clinical NER more broadly, pending external validation.
