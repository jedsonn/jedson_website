## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6738816`

- title: Can Multimodal Large Language Models Extract Investment Signals from Financial Charts? Evidence from Portfolio Backtests
- authors: Sungsoo Kim, Ha Young Kim
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6738816
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

This study examines whether a pretrained multimodal large language model (MLLM) can extract investment-relevant signals from financial chart images. Using stocks from the Dow Jones Industrial Average and Euro Stoxx 50, we evaluate signals derived from a pretrained MLLM across directional prediction, cross-sectional ranking, and portfolio back testing. To analyze the role of visual information, we construct a tiered chart representation that progressively incorporates technical indicators. The results show that chart-based visual information alone provides useful signals for cross-sectional asset ranking and portfolio construction, while directional prediction remains close to random. Signals derived from the pretrained MLLM improve ranking performance relative to price-only charts and conventional rule-based strategies, and these improvements are reflected in portfolio outcomes. Increasing chart complexity does not consistently improve performance. These findings suggest that pretrained MLLMs exhibit useful capabilities for financial chart interpretation and highlight the importance of information selection in chart-based analysis.

- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-10

### abstract

Problem/Motivation: The proliferation of national AI strategies, sovereign cloud programmes, and government foundation model initiatives has created a critical measurement gap in comparative AI governance research: no existing instrument quantifies the structural misalignment between what a nation builds in AI infrastructure and what it governs through regulatory frameworks. Oxford Insights measures government readiness but not infrastructure. Tortoise Media measures capability but conflates commercial and sovereign dimensions. The OECD AI Principles provide normative guidance but not a measurement instrument. This gap makes the Sovereignty Paradox — the condition in which infrastructure investment systematically outpaces governance framework maturity — invisible to comparative analysis and untrackable over time. Approach/Method: This paper formally introduces the National Sovereign AI Readiness Index (NSARI) as a replicable, citable methodology for measuring infrastructure-governance alignment at the national level. Findings: The EU calibration validation confirms the NSARI's discriminant validity across three critical tests. All three EU member states (Germany, France, Estonia) produce negative IS-RM gaps, consistent with the EU AI Act's statutory framework driving Regulatory Maturity above Infrastructure Sovereignty scores. France's stronger Algorithmic Sovereignty from Mistral AI correctly produces a higher AS score than Germany. Estonia's extreme negative IS-RM gap (−2.8) correctly captures the paradox of the world's most e-governance-advanced state having among the lowest sovereign compute capacity in the EU. All three EU states score materially above the GCC average on Regulatory Maturity (8.6–8.8 vs. GCC average 4.3), confirming the NSARI correctly discriminates between statutory and non-statutory governance regimes across regional contexts. Practical Implications: The NSARI replication protocol enables any researcher to apply the framework to any national or regional context using publicly available primary sources, producing NSARI-compliant scores comparable to published applications. Annual rescoring produces a longitudinal governance-infrastructure alignment trajectory. Cross-regional extension to ASEAN, the African Union, and Latin America is identified as a priority research direction, to test whether the GCC Sovereignty Paradox documented in Pillai (2026c) is region-specific or globally structural. NSARI version 2.0, incorporating empirically derived dimension weights following primary validation, is proposed as the immediate successor instrument.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6746900`

- title: Generative AI and Firm-Candidate Dynamics in the Labor Market
- authors: Miaozhe Han, Xianghua Lu, Shuang Wen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6746900
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid proliferation of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) is significantly reshaping the labor market. While existing literature primarily focuses on GenAI's isolated effects on either labor demand (e.g., task automation) or labor supply (e.g., skill development), research exploring the combined, interactive effects on firm-labor dynamics remains limited. This study addresses this gap by examining how the interplay between firms and job candidates has changed since the advent of GenAI. Leveraging data from a leading bi-directional job matching platform in China, we investigate key pipeline metrics, from initial communication (measured by the volume of chats, resumes, and contact information exchanged) to final conversion (operationalized as the successful scheduling of an interview), across job categories differentially exposed to GenAI. Our findings demonstrate that positions highly exposed to AI experience a significant increase in overall communication volume, yet no corresponding change in interview conversions. Further analyses reveal that these observations stem from asymmetric shifts in communication and conversion patterns between firms and candidates. Specifically, among highly AI-exposed positions, candidates initiate significantly more communication but achieve fewer interview conversions, while firms initiate less communication yet secure more conversions. A dynamic regression analysis confirms the persistence of these divergent trends throughout our observation period. We provide strong mechanistic evidence for this pattern: GenAI devalues domain-specific knowledge while elevating skill transferability, thereby lowering entry barriers for ex-ante ineligible candidates and expanding the applicant pool with AI-augmented generalists. We discuss implications for both firms and individuals navigating this evolving job market landscape.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6750021`

- title: When Does Generative AI Improve Innovation Efficiency? Organisational Culture as a Complementary Capability
- authors: Huanjia Ma, Di Xiao
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6750021
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

The release of ChatGPT represents one of the most significant recent shocks to the way organisations and individuals work, as generative AI became rapidly accessible beyond specialised technical settings. This shift may have substantial implications for productivity and innovation, particularly for how firms convert R&D investment into revenue and growth. However, these effects are unlikely to be uniform across firms. This paper examines whether organisational culture acts as a complementary capability that moderates the effect of generative AI exposure on firm-level innovation efficiency. Using the public release of ChatGPT in November 2022 as a technological shock, we document a decline in innovation efficiency among US research-intensive public firms after the release. We further show that this decline is less pronounced among firms with stronger innovation-and adaptability-oriented cultures. These findings suggest that the innovation effects of generative AI depend not only on technological exposure, but also on firms' organisational capacity to adapt, experiment and integrate new tools into existing R&D routines.

- title: Beyond Multi-Stage Pipelines: End-to-End Analog Gauge Reading via Specialized Vision-Language Models
- authors: Abdul Mueez, Aaditya Baranwala, Junior Chaj-Mejia, Jason T. Voelker, Guneet Bhatia, Shruti Vyas
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6749578
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, foundation model, in-context learning, qwen

### abstract

While industrial automation is accelerating, the persistence of legacy analog gauges necessitates robust, automated monitoring solutions to ensure operational safety and data continuity. This paper presents an end-to-end framework for robust analog gauge reading by specializing the Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct vision-language model (VLM) for high-precision industrial metrology. We systematically benchmark the transition from general-purpose foundation model to specialized industrial tool across zero-shot, in-context learning (ICL), and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) strategies. Evaluations are conducted on three diverse datasets: a public synthetic collection, a video-derived benchmark, and a proprietary real-world industrial dataset featuring complex environmental conditions. Our findings demonstrate that while foundational VLMs lack the inherent zero-shot reliability required for industrial standards, PEFT using Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation (QLoRA) transforms the model into a high-precision regressor. The specialized model achieves a Mean Percentage Error (MPE) between 1.93% and 3.4%, significantly outperforming traditional multi-stage computer vision pipelines and smaller VLM architectures. We further analyze the model's sensitivity to explicit scale parameters and identify a critical ‘perceptual-cognitive disconnect’ where the model exhibits high-confidence failure modes despite correct visual attention. These results provide a roadmap for integrating large-scale foundation models into autonomous industrial inspection frameworks while highlighting the necessity for external uncertainty quantification in safety-critical deployments.
