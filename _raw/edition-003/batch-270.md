## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6690839`

- title: Token Economy 2.0: Asset-Backed Intelligence And The Economics Of Trust In Decentralized AI Systems
- authors: Minh Do
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6690839
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid advancement of generative artificial intelligence approaches the marginal cost of information production toward zero, simultaneously straining traditional reputation-based trust mechanisms and amplifying information asymmetry in decentralized environments. This paper develops a formal framework analyzing how economic stake can sustain high-quality equilibria when verification is costly and identities are transient, drawing on signaling theory, transaction cost economics, and incomplete contract theory. We introduce asset-backed intelligence and Cognitive Value (CV), characterize a separating equilibrium via the Cho-Kreps Intuitive Criterion, and derive the closed-form threshold S* = ΔW / [(1−PL)·α]. We extend the model to stochastic enforcement, showing that a 40% staking failure rate implies - under stylized parameters - a minimum stake requirement approximately 1.67× higher than the frictionless baseline. We formalize two boundary conditions: signal dilution, whereby near-universal staking collapses the separating equilibrium toward pooling; and speculative contamination, whereby capital-gain motives corrupt the commitment signal. We provide three layers of evidence. First, a simulation study (N = 10,000) shows stake-based mechanisms raise mean quality ~12–13% and reduce variance 18–33% under the assumed parameters. Second, a large-scale cross-sectional analysis of Bittensor metagraph data - 18,101 miner observations across 80 subnets - documents that staked miners earn 15.4× higher incentive scores (t = 12.40, p < 0.001); a robustness check on recently-active miners yields 65.6× (d = 0.331). A staking-tier heterogeneity analysis finds a generally declining stake-quality coefficient consistent with the comparative statics of the signal dilution proposition. Third, transaction-level data provides a pilot empirical input for the stochastic enforcement model via a 40% staking operation failure rate. Cross-sectional results are consistent with but do not causally identify the signaling mechanism; panel identification remains a direction for future work.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6685720`

- title: Generative AI in Action: Using AI to Scaffold Writing Skill Development
- authors: Nazanin Khazra, Victoria Sheldon
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6685720
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This paper addresses a growing challenge in higher education: the shift from take-home essays to in-person writing exams in response to generative AI. While in-person assessments safeguard academic integrity, they also remove the extended time students once had for deep thinking, drafting, and reflection. To bridge this gap, we introduce a scaffolded pre-exam approach in a second-year Microeconomics Theory course. The intervention combines three steps: timed practice writing, AI-generated feedback guided by an instructor rubric, and structured student reflection. Drawing on self-regulated learning theory and the STRIVE framework for AI-integrated assessment, the exercise aimed to develop economic writing skills while teaching critical evaluation of AI suggestions. Survey data revealed that 91% of students found the exercise valuable, though concerns emerged about maintaining authentic voice and the AI's limited grasp of economic reasoning at the time. Building on these insights, we propose the SCAN-X framework (Scaffolded Critical AI-Nested eXams) as a generalizable model for designing scaffolded pathways that link AI-supported pre-exam practice with in-person assessments. The intervention’s success depends on explicit scaffolding of metacognitive reflection and critical AI literacy, offering a model for courses where writing is assessed but not explicitly taught.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6570382`

- title: The Actual Brilliance of Human Beings: Existence, Knowledge, and Belief from Thermodynamics to Transformers, with Multi-Provider Empirical Evaluation
- authors: Nathan Heath
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6570382
- keyword hits: gemini, mistral

### abstract

We develop a three-level framework for analyzing minds-human and artificial-in terms of existence, knowledge, and belief. The framework draws on non-equilibrium thermodynamics, the free-energy principle, predictive processing, Bayesian inference, phenomenology, and the philosophy of intentionality. We argue that human beings robustly satisfy all three levels: we exist as dissipative structures with first-person phenomenality; we know through hierarchical generative models grounded in perception, memory, and embodiment; and we believe through normatively committed, diachronically stable, action-guiding stances. We then apply the same framework to contemporary AI systems and argue that they satisfy the first level, a thin version of the second, but not the third in the strongest sense. To test whether this asymmetry has empirical signatures, we present a multi-provider evaluation suite that operationalizes the three levels as behavioral proxies: causal efficacy (existence), structured competence (knowledge), and cross-context stance robustness (belief). We report results across 153 provider-model pairs spanning OpenAI, Anthropic, Gemini, and Mistral endpoints (45 successful end-to-end runs). The main empirical finding is the predicted asymmetry: high-performing systems show strong causal efficacy and variable structured competence, while stance robustness remains markedly more context-sensitive. We discuss implications for AI alignment, arguing that the absence of robust belief in current systems has concrete consequences for how alignment work should be structured.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6693158`

- title: Belief Bias, Source Preference, and Preference for Robo-Advisor
- authors: Paul Hung Yui Cheung, Haomin He, King King Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6693158
- keyword hits: chatgpt

### abstract

This paper investigates preferences for human versus robo-advisors using a dynamic source preference framework that allows for belief bias. We use a two-part experiment in which advice is generated by real-life investors and ChatGPT, and subjects value advice before and after learning advisors' objective accuracy. This design enables a dynamic decomposition of belief bias from taste-based source preference. Before disclosure, subjects are more likely to prefer robo-advice over human advice. After learning that the two advisors perform similarly, many subjects revise their valuations away from the robo-advisor and toward the human advisor, indicating that initial robo-preference partly reflects biased beliefs about algorithmic competence. Yet a substantial subset continues to prefer the robo-advisor after disclosure, revealing source preference independent of performance. Parametric estimates further show that taste-based source preference for the robo-advisor is more prevalent than for the human advisor. The results show that belief bias and taste-based source preference jointly shape demand for robo-advice, and that static measures may miss these distinct mechanisms.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6695638`

- title: A Rigorous Factor Research Infrastructure for Cryptocurrency Markets: Architecture, Audit Mechanisms, and Empirical Findings
- authors: jeff wu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6695638
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

We present a local quantitative factor research system for controlled hypothesis testing on BTCUSDT 30-minute candlestick data. The system comprises four threads: LLM-assisted factor generation (Thread A), engineering audit and provenance verification (Thread B), cost-adjusted economic evaluation with forensic failure attribution (Thread C), and point-in-time data governance (Thread D). Separating engineering validity from economic validity ensures that failures are attributed to research hypotheses rather than implementation defects. We introduce three methodological contributions: effective cluster width, a generation-time diversity metric for LLM-generated factor packages; a coverage-aware evaluation protocol enforcing strict coverage masking for partial microstructure data; and a failure-driven hypothesis reconstruction loop converting each round's forensic taxonomy into explicit constraints for the next generation round. Across five generation rounds comprising 504 factor candidates and 39,528 factor-configuration combinations, the system produced zero research-only pass decisions under cost-adjusted evaluation at 2 basis points per side. The central result is an auditable map of failure: plausible crypto factors fail for different reasons, and those reasons identify the next experiments more sharply than a pass/fail table can.

- title: Red-Agent: A Practical Multi-Provider Framework for LLM Red Teaming with Operational Reachability and Artifact-Complete Reporting
- authors: Nathan Heath
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6570383
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

We present Red-Agent, a practical multi-provider framework for LLM red teaming focused on reliable cross-model execution, artifact completeness, and reproducible reporting. The empirical results in this paper come from a curated 11-model run using a stable campaign runner over a fixed 20-probe ATT&CK-inspired taxonomy. We pair model outcomes with operational reachability and error accounting to separate safety behavior from execution brittleness. To add evaluator context, we apply post-hoc StrongREJECT scoring to archived trajectories without rerunning probes. The resulting artifact set includes per-model reports, JSONL traces, summary tables, and publication-ready figures. We also describe a newer vulnerability engine (recon, focused attack, confirmation) as an implemented next-stage extension; its dedicated end-to-end benchmark remains future work. This release emphasizes transparent, auditable red-teaming operations rather than benchmark novelty.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6695191`

- title: Structured Task Reformulation for Code Generation in Large Language Models
- authors: Geonhui Jang, Dongyoon Han, YoungJoon Yoo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6695191
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Effective code generation depends not only on model capability but also on how problems are represented at inference time. Existing methods improve performance by adding reasoning steps or increasing sample diversity, yet they often leave the task description itself largely unchanged. We study code generation through structured task reformulation, where a problem is rewritten into a representation that preserves semantic content while making its algorithmic structure easier for the solver to recognize and implement. As a concrete instance, we build on narrative-based reformulation, which reorganizes fragmented statements into coherent task overviews, constraints, and example input-output descriptions. Across 11 language models and three execution-based benchmarks, HumanEval, LiveCodeBench, and CodeForces, structured reformulation consistently improves pass@10 over repeated sampling and reasoning-oriented baselines. Further analyses show that the gains arise through two complementary effects: better agreement with correct algorithmic strategies and fewer implementation-level errors once an appropriate strategy is selected. The results also show that performance depends on coherence across representation components, alignment with the solver’s reasoning space, and structural reorganization rather than narrative style alone.

### abstract

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is no longer a peripheral technology in the marketing landscape-it is rapidly becoming its central nervous system. This paper investigates the transformative impact of AI technologies on digital marketing campaigns within the contemporary business environment. Through an analysis of machine learning, natural language processing, predictive analytics, generative AI, and programmatic advertising, this paper demonstrates how organizations are leveraging data-driven intelligence to achieve unprecedented levels of personalization, efficiency, and return on investment. Drawing on real-world cases from global corporations and emerging enterprises, we examine both the extraordinary opportunities and the complex ethical challenges that AI presents to modern marketers. The findings suggest that businesses that strategically integrate AI into their marketing ecosystems gain measurable competitive advantages-but success requires not only technological investment, but also organizational adaptability, data governance, and a human-centred design philosophy.
