- title: Surrogate Modelling for CO​​2 Laser Ablation Response in Poly(methyl methacrylate) with Interval-Calibrated Uncertainty Estimation
- authors: Niloy Dey, Dr. Mohammad Muhshin Khan, Ahmed Sayem
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6881388
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Selecting laser process parameters for target groove geometry in polymer substrates requires physical trial iterations, consuming material and process time. This work addresses that constraint by presenting a calibrated surrogate model that predicts groove geometry with confidence intervals for any candidate parameter combination, enabling automated quality gating — accepting, flagging, or rejecting candidate parameter sets based on both geometric prediction and prediction reliability, without physical trials. Five machine learning architectures — Gaussian Process Regression (GPR), Support Vector Regression (SVR), Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), Random Forest (RF), and Polynomial Ridge Regression (PRR) — are evaluated on CO2 laser-engraved polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) groove depth and width prediction, with hyperparameters tuned via Grey Wolf Optimizer across a 125-run full-factorial design space to ensure architecture selection reflects model capacity rather than tuning disparity. Variance Inflation Factor diagnostics identified severe multicollinearity (VIF > 39) when raw and composite features were combined into a single set, prompting its exclusion to prevent model instability. Substituting Area Energy Density for raw laser power retained predictive parity, whereas pure composite feature sets yielded poor predictive accuracy — a reproducible negative result with direct implications for feature engineering in data-scarce process regimes. GPR on raw process parameters achieved R2 = 0.999 for depth and R​2 = 0.998 for width on held-out data. Calibration via Prediction Interval Coverage Probability (PICP ≥ 0.94) and Negative Log Predictive Density confirmed interval reliability at the 95% nominal level, validating the surrogate as a deployable decision-support tool for precision polymer laser processing.

- title: May 2026: The Window Europe Did Not Use
- authors: Gregorio Martín
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6826141
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Between 19 May and 23 June 2026, the European Commission held a targeted consultation on the draft guidelines for the classification of high-risk AI systems under Article 6 and Annex III of the AI Act. This paper argues that the consultation closed without incorporating a criterion that is technically necessary and legally available: a criterion distinguishing systems whose action sequences are determined at design time from systems that generate their own intervention sequences at runtime. The absence of this criterion has direct consequences for the enforceability of the AI Act against the category of systems that present the highest risk profile-agentic systems operating without a predetermined action plan. The paper documents the criterion, the empirical record of its absence, the institutional record of three bodies that received formal notification and did not produce a substantive response, the structural explanation for that failure, and three remedial actions that remain legally available to the Commission without new legislation. Special attention is given to the forensic methodology underlying the Pegasus empirical record, distinguishing the CCN-CERT analysis of the Sánchez device-which rests on direct physical access and a judicial forensic report-from the Citizen Lab CatalanGate methodology, which establishes targeting but not in all cases confirmed infection.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6873839`

- title: Optimised Preparation of α-ribazole Standards to Support Cyanide-Free Vitamin B12 Analysis in Infant Formula
- authors: Yanan Li, Merilyn Manley-Harris, M.N.C. Grainger, Brendon D. Gill
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6873839
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

α‑Ribazole is an essential component of a recently developed cyanide-free method for vitamin B12 determination, but application of the method is limited by the lack of a commercially available α‑ribazole standard and the difficulty of its reliable in-house preparation. This study evaluated and optimised a previously reported preparation of α‑ribazole from cyanocobalamin, focusing on the chemical hydrolysis and boronate affinity chromatography (BAC) clean-up steps. Implementation of the previously published protocol in our laboratory did not reproduce the reported yield, prompting systematic investigation of key variables, including hydrolysis conditions and BAC eluent composition and volume. The optimised method, incorporating acid hydrolysis (hydrochloric acid, 1.5 M) and stronger BAC elution conditions, substantially improved α-ribazole recovery. An acetonitrile-modified formic acid solution (20:80, v/v, 1.0 M) enhanced elution efficiency from the boronic acid gel, and the final protocol achieved an overall yield of 76%. The identity of the product was confirmed by comprehensive 2D nuclear magnetic resonance and mass spectrometric analyses. The improved procedure provides a more robust and reproducible route for α-ribazole preparation and facilitates broader use of cyanide-free vitamin B12 analysis.

- title: Capacity attenuation anomaly detection of energy storage lithium batteries based on Transformer multimodal fusion
- authors: Chenchen Dong, Dashuai Sun
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6890398
- keyword hits: transformer model

### abstract

In order to solve the problem that the traditional capacity calculation method in the lithium-ion battery (LIB) system has significant errors and is difficult to accurately detect the abnormal capacity attenuation of single cells, this paper proposes an early capacity attenuation evaluation algorithm for energy storage batteries based on Transformer multimodal fusion(BTMF). By analyzing the voltage, temperature and current during the battery aging process, the algorithm innovatively designs a multivariate cooperative interval counting feature extraction strategy for one-dimensional and two-dimensional data to effectively capture the key features of capacity attenuation. For two-dimensional data, in order to enhance the model's perception of local details and global context dependencies, a channel-space dual attention parallel mechanism is proposed, and the two work together to improve the robustness of feature representation. At the same time, the algorithm introduces a hybrid fusion mechanism of soft and hard collaboration in multimodal feature fusion, realizes feature-level soft attenuation through dynamically generated channel-level attenuation coefficients, and suppresses the noise interference of secondary modes. Finally, the effectiveness of the dual attention mechanism is verified through accelerated aging experiments, and the performance of BTMF is verified in the actual data of large-scale energy storage power stations. At the same time, it is compared with a variety of Transformer models. The results show that BTMF significantly improves the accuracy and inference performance of capacity attenuation evaluation, providing reliable technical support for battery health management.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6857438`

- title: Artificial Intelligence Behavioral Pathology as an Epistemological Framework: Towards Pre-Execution Reasoning Chain Interception for Auditable AI Governance
- authors: Dipankur Bodwal
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6857438
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

Language has always been our primary tool for navigating the world. Since the first words were spoken, humans have used categories to map their reality, dividing experience into dualities positive and negative, self and other based on our own physiological and philosophical evolution. Over time these simple words evolved into stories, forming the collective knowledge base that defines our human existence. However, the advent of Artificial Intelligence has created a new challenge. By feeding the sum of human knowledge into machines that calculate probability, we have inadvertently inherited a problem of our own making, (we are now fearful of the words we taught these machines to use). We can no longer easily trace how a machine’s processing of mathematical probabilities translates into actions that shape our future. Our questions of ”how” the machine works have shifted into urgent questions of ”what” it will do next and that in correlation creates the question of ”why”. If a machine generates output is based on human like language, it must be governed by constraints that account for the human psyche. Mathematical observation is the only objective way to verify truth in such a system. Therefore, to ensure that AI output remains grounded, human language must be treated as a mathematical base. The potential for positive or negative outcomes can and must be governed by mathematical formulas derived from psychological and philosophical principles. The following framework provides such a solution. The rapid deployment of autonomous AI agents in high-stakes sectors such as financial services, healthcare, and law has outpaced our ability to govern them. Current auditing standards are fundamentally reactive. They rely on ”Output-Based Alignment,” where risks are identified only after an agent has already executed a decision and potentially caused harm. This framework is insufficient for agents that perform complex, multi-step reasoning. This paper proposes a shift toward AI Behavioral Pathology. Instead of auditing the final output, we introduce a methodology for intercepting and scoring an agent’s reasoning chain, declared reasoning its inner monologue before any external action is taken. To achieve this, we present the Symmetic 154-Index Framework, adiagnostic schema that decomposes machine cognition into 154 specific behavioral dimensions across 10 Clinical Nodes. This pre-execution interception model converts epistemically opaque autonomous agents into mathematically auditable Glass Box systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6825858`

- title: Fiduciary Infrastructure for Agentic AI: Directors' Duties and Investor Protection in the EU, South Korea and Singapore
- authors: Luh Luh Lan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6825858
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Agentic artificial intelligence-autonomous systems that plan across multiple steps and act in dynamic environments without human direction-has begun to be integrated in a material way into the operations of companies around the world. Its deployment highlights the limits of the classical principal-agent framework on which modern corporate law rests: agentic AI systems are not utility maximisers, their internal reasoning is not readily seen nor reliably verifiable, and they fall outside the legal and economic categories through which agency doctrine allocates responsibility. This paper argues that directors' fiduciary obligations in respect of agentic AI are best understood through what the paper terms the fiduciary infrastructure: a four-layer account (data, model, deployment, accountability) of the duty of care that directors now owe to their companies in the adoption of agentic AI in their operations. The term is developed through a comparative study of three jurisdictions whose AI governance frameworks together occupy the spectrum of regulatory responses available to advanced economies-the prescriptive EU AI Act, the hybrid Korean AI Basic Act, and Singapore's voluntary Model AI Governance Framework for Agentic AI-and is anchored in existing corporate-law authorities: Vita Health and Goh Jin Hian in Singapore, Siemens/Neubürger in Germany as representative Member State, and the Daewoo line in South Korea. The paper extends the analysis to investor protection, identifying three distinct enforcement architectures and the novel information asymmetries that agentic AI generates for capital markets, and proposes targeted reforms in corporate law and securities disclosure.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6905759`

- title: BenchAudit: checking whether benchmark leaderboard rankings actually hold up
- authors: Yashraj Behera
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6905759
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Public benchmark leaderboards rank many models on the same fixed test set, and people read those rankings as if each step were real. But comparing many models at once on a shared set of items is a multiple comparisons problem with correlated tests, and some of the ordering near the top is sampling noise. BenchAudit is an open-source Python tool that re-scores an existing leaderboard to tell you which ranking steps survive a proper correction and which do not. It works on any leaderboard that reports item-level pass or fail per model, scores each model with an honest standard error, builds a null by bootstrapping over benchmark items (so the cross-model correlation from shared items is handled by construction), and applies a Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate correction across the comparisons. It also reports how many genuinely independent comparisons a leaderboard contains, which is usually far fewer than the number of models. We demonstrate it on two different domains, MMLU (multiple-choice question answering) and SWE-bench Verified (agentic software repair), and in both the leaderboard turns out to have far fewer independent comparisons than entries and several adjacent rankings that are not statistically real.
