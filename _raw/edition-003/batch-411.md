## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6959799`

- title: Suptech-Powered Consumer And Investor Protection Supervision. Architectural Pathway From Reactive Oversight To Outcome-Driven Institutional Intelligence.
- authors: Simone Di Castri, Matt Grasser
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6959799
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Consumer and investor protection supervision (CIPS) is undergoing a structural transition. While financial authorities have made substantial progress digitizing complaint handling, case management, and supervisory workflows, the analytical capabilities required to transform these data assets into anticipatory, outcome-driven supervision remain unevenly developed. This report argues that the principal constraint is no longer the availability of technology, but the absence of integrated supervisory data architectures capable of converting fragmented signals into actionable intelligence. Authorities frequently deploy individual components—portals, databases, dashboards, chatbots, and analytics tools—without the architectural integration required to create a coherent supervisory pipeline. Drawing on four years of State of SupTech survey data, a longitudinal dataset covering hundreds of financial authorities, and the GovSpace corpus of 102 documented CIPS suptech implementations across 73 countries, the report develops the concept of the “foundation-to-intelligence transition.” It shows that adoption has concentrated in complaint and conduct signal capture, while more advanced capabilities—including early warning systems, predictive analytics, algorithmic auditing, vulnerable-consumer profiling, and other anticipatory functions—remain limited or absent in most jurisdictions. The challenge is no longer moving from no data to data, but from signals to intelligence. To address this gap, the report applies two complementary frameworks: the GovTech Generations 3.0 maturity model and the DataStack Blueprint. Together, they provide a diagnostic and design methodology for assessing supervisory capability across data collection, processing, storage, analytics, data products, interoperability, and governance, while identifying the sequencing required to advance from reactive oversight toward AI-augmented and agentic supervision. The report concludes with a practical decision framework that links architectural development to six outcome categories—Access, Deterrence, Welfare, Coverage, Speed, and Intelligence Quality—and offers a structured pathway for financial authorities seeking to build supervisory systems that not only process information, but measurably improve consumer and investor outcomes.

- title: AI and Quantum Trading Agents: Cybersecurity, Governance, and Regulatory Challenges in Financial Decision-Making
- authors: Audrey Rah
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7074360
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Artificial intelligence agents are increasingly being deployed as autonomous decision-makers capable of executing financial strategies, managing portfolios, and interacting with digital markets with limited human intervention. Simultaneously, advances in quantum computing and quantum machine learning are transforming the computational foundations of financial optimization, risk assessment, and algorithmic trading. Despite these developments, existing cybersecurity, governance, and regulatory frameworks continue to address artificial intelligence, quantum technologies, and financial systems as largely independent domains, creating a significant governance gap for the emerging generation of AI-and quantum-enabled trading agents. This study presents a systematic mapping review of the interdisciplinary landscape connecting agentic AI, quantum finance, cybersecurity governance, post-quantum security, and financial regulation. Following the PRISMA 2020 methodology, 198 records were identified from academic databases, regulatory repositories, standards organizations, and governmental sources, resulting in a final corpus of 96 studies for detailed analysis. The review synthesizes evidence across five research dimensions: quantum finance and quantum machine learning, autonomous financial agents, cybersecurity and post-quantum security governance, AI governance and regulatory oversight, and integrated governance frameworks for next-generation financial systems. The findings reveal a growing body of research on quantum finance and agentic AI, yet identify significant gaps in governance mechanisms for addressing autonomous financial decision-making, post-quantum resilience, auditability, accountability, and supervisory control. Regulatory guidance remains fragmented across jurisdictions, while existing frameworks provide limited guidance for managing the systemic risks associated with autonomous trading agents operating within quantum-enhanced financial environments. By integrating insights from cybersecurity, artificial intelligence, quantum computing, and financial governance, this review develops a comprehensive research agenda and identifies key priorities for regulators, financial institutions, and researchers seeking to establish trustworthy governance foundations for future autonomous financial systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7072740`

- title: Efficiency Inflation and the Repricing of Knowledge Work: A Conceptual Model of Judgment Density in AI-Augmented Organizations
- authors: Jia-Liang Yeh
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7072740
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Generative and agentic AI have sharply lowered the marginal cost of producing knowledge-work outputs such as reports, presentations, code, copy, research summaries, and operational plans. Yet the diffusion of AIenabled speed has not automatically produced proportional organizational performance gains. This paper develops the concept of efficiency inflation to explain this gap. Efficiency inflation occurs when AI expands the volume and apparent completeness of professional output faster than organizations can increase the human judgment, contextual understanding, verification discipline, and accountability embedded in that output. Under such conditions, ordinary output becomes abundant and loses its premium, while high-density judgment becomes more scarce and more valuable. The paper advances a conceptual model in which the perceived value of a unit of professional output depends on the relationship between judgment density and output abundance. It integrates public enterprise surveys, labor-market signals, and organizational theory to identify five mechanisms: output commoditization, hidden supervisory labor, verification failure, cognitive-load transfer, and apprenticeship collapse. The paper further argues that AI is not simply replacing work, but repricing work by separating low-judgment execution from high-judgment responsibility-bearing. This is a theory-building working paper rather than a causal empirical study. Its contribution is to translate fragmented evidence on AI productivity, worker exhaustion, skill shifts, and organizational redesign into a coherent management theory for AI-augmented organizations. The proposed implication is that firms should not manage AI adoption primarily by output volume or task-speed metrics. They should redesign work systems to measure decision quality, evidence discipline, trust formation, and the preservation of human learning pathways.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7073098`

- title: Before the Law takes Effect: Provider Anticipatory Behavior
- authors: Sreyaa Guha, Katia Meggiorin, Rodrigo Belo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7073098
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

What if the market impacts of platform regulation begin before the law actually takes effect? While traditional hierarchical firms manage regulatory compliance through top-down mandates, digital platforms face a distinct vulnerability: autonomous supply-side users can preemptively alter their operations, causing immediate platform revenue losses before a law is even enforced. This paper explores how Airbnb hosts respond during the critical approval-to-enforcement window of short-term rental regulation. Using listing-level data from five U.S. cities, combined with ordinance requirements, we show that hosts reduce supply immediately after regulatory approval, well before formal enforcement begins. The decline is gradual, grows as enforcement approaches, and reduces platform revenue and realized bookings. We argue that regulation approval itself makes future operating constraints credible, prompting hosts to reassess whether their current routines can continue under the incoming rules. To test this mechanism, we construct a Regulatory Risk Index that compares each listing's pre-approval activity to the requirements of the regulation. Listings with higher regulatory risk reduce availability more strongly, especially along the margins directly targeted by regulation, such as multi-listing operation and high prior booking intensity. These early reductions also foretell post-enforcement sorting: listings that scale back more before enforcement are less likely to apply for a license and more likely to exit. Our findings show that anticipatory behavior is not merely a threat to identification or a pre-trend to be controlled away. It is a meaningful market response through which credible future regulation begins reshaping platform supply before enforcement formally starts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7075038`

- title: Proportionate Governance in Practice An IDEA-CRUS-T Framework for Risk-Tiered, Runtime-Governed AI Systems in Financial Services
- authors: Satyaki Ghosh Dastidar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7075038
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Financial institutions increasingly deploy generative and agentic AI across use cases that range from internal drafting assistance to autonomous transaction execution. Applying uniform governance controls across this range produces two simultaneous failure modes: high‑reach, low‑authority systems are over‑governed into shadow deployment, while high‑authority systems inherit lightweight controls unsuited to their consequence. We argue that proportionate governance is a design property of the control architecture rather than a discretionary adjustment applied at review time, and we operationalize this claim through two linked instruments. The first, IDEA (Identification, Development/Deployment, Explainability/Execution, Accountability), is a lifecycle spine common to every AI system regardless of risk. The second is a five‑dimension, capability‑level risk‑tiering method (Authority, Impact, Agency, Exposure, Recoverability) that assigns a discrete control tier at design time, and CRUS‑T , a normalized, programmatically computable runtime reliability score across four dimensions—Consistency, Robustness, Uncertainty awareness, and Safety—gated against tier‑specific thresholds and evaluated on a conservative statistical bound rather than a point estimate. Each dimension decomposes into standard, auditable sub‑metrics (self‑consistency agreement, performance‑retention ratios, calibration error, selective‑prediction quality, claim‑level groundedness, and policy‑conformance rate) aggregated by a collapse‑sensitive geometric mean, with an asymmetric hard gate on critical safety violations. We illustrate the framework with two contrasting, hypothetical‑but‑representative case studies drawn from retail banking and anti‑money‑laundering operations, showing how capability‑level CRUS‑T scoring identifies the specific reliability dimension responsible for under‑governance in one case and the single authority‑bearing action responsible for over‑governance in the other. We situate the contribution relative to existing model risk management guidance, agentic‑risk taxonomies, runtime governance architectures, and agent reliability science, and discuss limitations—principally evaluation‑set representativeness, parameter governance, and the dependency of learned verification components on their own validation—together with directions for future work.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6940278`

- title: How Creative is AI writing? Generative and Collaborative AI in Japanese Fiction
- authors: Yuki Asano, Marco T. Bastos
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6940278
- keyword hits: generative ai, prompting

### abstract

This study examines readers’ perception of quality and originality in AI-generated and human-AI collaborated Japanese literary fiction. Fifty-eight participants with backgrounds in creative writing ranked the endings of 25 stories across four conditions. In all cases, the first half of each story was authored by professional or amateur writers; the second half varied by condition: original human-authored text, zero-shot AI generation, AI generation with detailed prompt specification, and human-AI collaboration combining multiple-shot prompting with human editing. Focus group sessions followed to explore the criteria underlying the ranking provided by participants. Human-AI collaboration received the highest mean quality rankings, followed by original human writing, detailed prompt specification, and zero-shot generation. While the differences between conditions were small relative to within-condition variance, the contrast between human-AI collaboration and zero-shot generation was statistically significant. In addition to that, collaboration was rated highest in perceived quality but lowest in originality, a dissociation that previous research had not clearly identified: AI-assisted editing produces polished, coherent prose that readers find accessible, but at the cost of the stylistic expression that defines literary fiction. These findings suggest that the contribution of Generative AI to creative writing is better characterized as a reduction of variance than a tangible elevation of quality.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7091055`

- title: Modeling quality responses of cultivated nori (Pyropia yezoensis) to a regional nutrient-management plan in the Seto Inland Sea, Japan
- authors: Rinka Fujita, Chihiro Kashima, Yusuke Nakatani
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7091055
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Oligotrophication in parts of the Seto Inland Sea has increased the risk of discoloration of cultivated nori (Pyropia yezoensis), prompting regional measures to increase nitrogen discharge while maintaining water-quality targets. This study developed a cultivated-nori module that represents tissue nitrogen status and coloration through the nitrogen-to-carbon (N/C) ratio and coupled it with a three-dimensional hydrodynamic–ecosystem model. Field observations showed that the tissue N/C ratio increased with ambient dissolved inorganic nitrogen (DIN) concentration following a saturating relationship and was positively related to SPAD values, an indicator of nori coloration. The coupled model reproduced the broad spatial distributions of DIN, chlorophyll a, and total nitrogen and qualitatively represented observed nori-color categories at several cultivation sites, although short-term DIN increases were underestimated at some locations. The model was then applied to the nitrogen-load changes specified in the Hyogo Prefecture Nutrient Management Plan. Relative to the current case, the future case produced localized increases in DIN and total nitrogen mainly along the northeastern coast of Harima nada and a small increase in chlorophyll a along the coast of Osaka Bay. Improvements in the modeled tissue N/C ratio were also concentrated in northeastern Harima nada, whereas changes were small in most other cultivation grounds. These results show that the responses to the official management scenario were spatially heterogeneous and largely confined to areas near the targeted nitrogen sources. The model provides a regional framework for evaluating nutrient-management plans in terms of both coastal water quality and the quality response of an economically important aquaculture species.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7090430`

- title: Automatic seismic phase picking benchmark on a local network in Central Asia: DT-SFM, PhaseNet, and EQTransformer versus a tuned STA/LTA
- authors: Suratbek Khalbayev, Javokhir Kodirov, Ming Zhao, Shi Chen, Timur Mamarozikov, Bakhodir Alimov, Timur Kurbanov, Diyorbek Yusupov
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7090430
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, foundation model

### abstract

Automatic earthquake detection and phase picking are increasingly performed with deep-learning models, yet how they behave under adverse, out-of-distribution conditions remains poorly constrained, particularly in regions absent from existing training datasets. We assemble a deliberately challenging benchmark of 846 manually reviewed events recorded by a compact local network in Central Asia, dominated by low-magnitude seismicity on noisy stations across local to regional distances, and use it to evaluate four detectors under a single protocol: the DiTing seismic foundation model (DT-SFM), the open-source PhaseNet and EQTransformer pickers, and a tuned STA/LTA detector. The deep-learning models are applied with their default configurations and without regional fine-tuning, while the STA/LTA detector is tuned to provide a strong baseline, and all are scored against the same manual reference picks. Ranked by P-phase F1, the order is DiTing (0.307), tuned STA/LTA (0.161), PhaseNet (0.127), and EQTransformer (0.077): only DiTing surpasses the classical detector, while both open-source pickers fall below it as their recall declines sharply with epicentral distance. A threshold analysis shows that the missed arrivals of PhaseNet are largely recoverable by lowering its probability threshold, so its position below the classical baseline is specific to the default operating point; those of EQTransformer are not recoverable in the same way. Timing accuracy is comparable across all methods, so detection rather than timing is the discriminating factor. The results delineate the limits of current pickers under demanding conditions and provide guidance for their use where no local training data exist.
