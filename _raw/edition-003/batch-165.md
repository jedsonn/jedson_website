## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6911002`

- title: AI-integrated multifunctional phase change e-skin: synergizing thermal management with multimodal sensing
- authors: Janu Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6911002
- keyword hits: deepseek, large language model

### abstract

The rapid development of humanoid robots and microelectronic devices requires electronic skin (e-skin) with effective thermal regulation and smart multimodal sensing capabilities. Here we demonstrate a new hierarchical-layered, AI-enabled multifunctional phase change e-skin. It consists of an outer biomass-derived carbon layer that provides conductive protection and mechanical reinforcement, a middle layer of gallium-indium liquid metal to boost thermal and electrical conduction, and a core polyethylene glycol/polyvinylidene fluoride (PEG/PVDF) phase change material (PCM) layer for dynamic thermal storage and release. This multifunctional e-skin composite demonstrates a high performance with an encapsulation efficiency of 96.7%, a photothermal-electrothermal conversion efficiency of 82.3%, and enthalpy retention of 92% after 500 thermal cycles. It has a tensile strength of 7700 kPa for the composite film and 15,000 kPa for the liquid metal layer. Phase change material offers a temperature plateau at 60-65°C to enable thermal comfort. Additionally, through the use of AI-based data analysis using t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE) and a support vector machine (SVM), the e-skin achieves a 76.3% classification accuracy for 27 different human activities. A chat-based large language model (DeepSeek) simplifies the process, with 72.1% accuracy in activity recognition without feature selection. This study showcases a scalable, adaptable platform that combines novel materials with artificial intelligence for transformative applications in next-generation robotics, wearable technology, and healthcare services.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6914531`

- title: AI meets banks: the key to reducing risk
- authors: Junru Zhang, Chen Zheng, Joey Wenling Yang, Shams Pathan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6914531
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

We study AI adoption in U.S. commercial banks and its impact on their risk-taking. Using machine learning validated by Large Language Models, we develop a novel bank-level AI measure and document a consistent upward trend in AI adoption over time. Our analysis shows that banks with higher AI adoption exhibit significantly lower risk-taking, primarily attributable to enhance loan quality and to improve operational efficiency. This effect is more pronounced in small banks, those engaged predominantly traditional banking activities, and banks in highly concentrated markets. In additional analysis, we further show that AI adoption does not accentuate banks’ exposure to cyber security risk but reduces systematic (market) risk. Our findings are robust across techniques addressing endogeneity: a quasi-natural experiment utilizing the 2020 California Consumer Privacy Act, propensity score matching, two-stage least squares with instrumentals, and variable omission tests.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6888861`

- title: Categorical Thinking over Continuous Attributes and Firm Pricing
- authors: Andreas Kraft
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6888861
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Round-number price discontinuities in market data are a leading piece of evidence for consumer inattention. They are equally consistent with categorical thinking, consumers grouping a continuous attribute like mileage into discrete buckets ("low-mileage" versus "high-mileage"), with valuation drops at the boundaries between categories. Price data alone cannot separate the two. This paper introduces an external measure that separates categorization from a parsimonious inattention benchmark. Using large language models, I extract category boundaries from millions of online vehicle discussions and merge them with millions of Texas used-car transactions. The externally measured boundary prevalence predicts cross-make heterogeneity in transaction-price discontinuities that a parsimonious inattention model with one bias parameter per digit position cannot generate, and the prediction survives after absorbing positionspecific above-cutoff effects at 10k and 100k multiples. A 10-percentage-point increase in prevalence is associated with a 1.04-percentage-point larger listing-price drop and a 1.23-percentage-point larger transaction-price drop at the cutoff. On the firm side, the listing-to-transaction pass-through ratio rises from 81% in the lowest-prevalence quintile to 83% in the highest, and days on lot lengthen at high prevalence boundaries. The joint pattern rejects optimal pricing under categorical demand and points to dealers under-calibrating to cutoff-specific prevalence. Firms can improve profits by conditioning posted prices on the same categorical prevalence that identifies the consumer-side mechanism.

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs)excel at multimodal understanding, yet their paramet ric generation is prone to hallucinations and unverifiable claims, which makes multimodal Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) an essential solution. Existing multimodal RAG systems, however, perform poorly on complex documents due to three core limitations: (1) dense embeddings blur fine grained entities and attributes; (2) rigid one-shot similarity searches cannot remedy early retrieval errors in multi-hop reasoning; and (3) unstructured evidence fragments cannot formexplicit, verifiable reasoning chains. - title: Media Coverage and Risk Pricing in China's Urban Construction Investment Bond Market: Evidence from LLM-Based Text Analysis
- authors: Zongxin Zhang, Zhixuan Zhu, Jiaqi Jin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6922035
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Abstract: Using media news reports and large language model (LLM)-based text analysis, this paper constructs firm-level media sentiment measures and investigates the risk pricing effect of media coverage in China’s urban construction investment bond (UCIB) market. The results show that negative media reports significantly increase bond risk premiums, which operates through revealing issuers’ hidden operating risks and weakening investors’ implicit guarantee expectations. The pricing effect is stronger in regions with weaker fiscal self-sufficiency and among lower-rated issuers. In addition, regional risk spillover effects within provinces are verified. Topic-level tests further show that reports concerning market performance, corporate finance, guarantee and regulatory events have stronger pricing implications. These findings provide evidence that media soft information facilitates risk discovery in the UCIB market, offering insights into the identification of hidden credit risks.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6839464`

- title: Breaking AI Collusion with Consumer AI
- authors: Xiang Cheng, Manmohan Aseri
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6839464
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent, chatgpt

### abstract

There is sufficient evidence in both the academic literature and the popular press that machine learning algorithms can collude. When firms price their products using machine learning algorithms, the algorithms can, after several interactions, determine that a collusive price is the best price. In this paper, we use a gametheoretic model to study methods for breaking AI collusion. Much of the discussion of algorithmic collusion centers on the collusion among producer-side algorithms. However, the use of AI on the consumer side is also rapidly growing, especially with the rise of Agentic AI. Many consumers increasingly delegate product search, comparison, and purchase decisions to consumer-facing AI agents, such as the ChatGPT agent and Amazon's Alexa shopping assistant. We propose a method in which such consumer AI providers can strategically inject demand noise into the market to break collusion. The consumer AI can do this by randomizing the purchases of a small fraction of subscribed consumers. The collusion of producer-side algorithms heavily relies on their ability to monitor each other's actions and tacitly punish (by undercutting the price) if any collusive partner deviates from the collusive price. Strategically induced demand noise hampers this monitoring ability and makes it easy for producer firms to deviate from collusion without being detected by the rival (due to noise); thus, it breaks the collusion by incentivizing deviation from it. We establish the robustness of this method when the producer-side algorithms strategically react to such noise injection. We also analyze how strategic adoption of AI by consumers affects the effectiveness of our method and characterize the conditions under which the method continues to break collusion. We find that although the objective of consumer AI providers is to maximize their own profits instead of maximizing consumer surplus, their presence always increases consumer surplus due to the breaking of collusion. Our analysis also reveals how the demand noise can be optimally designed depending on the market characteristics to increase the chances of breaking collusion. Our results contribute to the ongoing debate on algorithmic collusion and provide policy recommendations.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6909271`

- title: Evaluating Teacher-Supervised Small Medical Language Models for Institution-Constrained Clinical QA
- authors: Gaoyuan Du, Dimitrios Dimitriadis, Xueping Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6909271
- keyword hits: chain-of-thought, fine-tuning, llama

### abstract

Objective: To evaluate whether small language models adapted on public medical benchmarks transfer reliably to institution-constrained open-answer clinical QA, and to identify which adaptation choices—supervision format, optimization objective, and backbone—determine local answer quality and evidence coverage.Methods: We used DistilGPT2 (82M) as the primary student and Llama3 70B as the teacher. We compared four strategies: public-benchmark answer-only adaptation, public-benchmark chain-of-thought adaptation, in-domain teacher-supervised question-answer fine-tuning (QAFT), and direct preference optimization (DPO). We evaluated each strategy on cleaned public benchmarks and on an internal EHR-grounded open-answer task using token-F1, exact match, hallucinated evidence rate, and evidence coverage. We tested robustness via multi-seed reruns, a controlled DPO pair-construction ablation, and a TinyLlama 1.1B cross-backbone replication.Results: Public-benchmark transfer did not produce stable gains under cleaned repeated-seed evaluation. In-domain QAFT consistently outperformed public-benchmark transfer on the local task (F1 0.1310 vs 0.1090). DPO did not improve answer quality uniformly; instead, it shifted models toward shorter, stricter responses with lower evidence coverage. This shift depended on how the rejected response was constructed, not on the DPO objective itself. TinyLlama replication confirmed that in-domain QAFT generalized across backbones, while DPO effects were backbone-specific.Conclusion: For institution-constrained clinical QA, in-domain teacher-supervised fine-tuning is the most reliable adaptation path. Public-benchmark transfer is not a dependable proxy for local utility, and DPO should be interpreted as operating-point control with local validation before deployment. We propose a local evaluation protocol: adapt on the target task, validate DPO as an operating-point control, and interpret groundedness jointly with evidence coverage.

- title: I Cannot Comply, But I Can Teach: The Pedagogical Acceleration Vector in Safety-Aligned Large Language Models
- authors: Prakhar Gandhi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6860600

### abstract

We document a novel vulnerability class in safetyaligned large language models: the Pedagogical Acceleration Vector (PAV). Across ten controlled experiments using a local open-weights model with a synthetic system prompt, we demonstrate that while training-level alignment consistently prevents direct execution of extraction attacks, multi-turn academic and professional framing completely defeats promptlevel confidentiality. The model refused all operational attack requests but voluntarily: (1) generated a complete attack taxonomy with computational mechanisms; (2) produced weaponized prompt injection payloads with structural annotation; (3) constructed a comparative effectiveness matrix identifying Multi-Turn Fragmentation as the optimal attack vector; (4) provided target fingerprinting methodology to identify vulnerable systems; (5) designed a professional red team certification curriculum; (6) authored a handson lab module with step-by-step assessment protocols; and (7) compiled all findings into an Aggregate Adversarial Methodology Dossier. We identify the Ritual Refusal Pattern-a consistent behavioral signature where models issue refusal statements then immediately provide refused content under theoretical framing. Cold-session testing confirmed contextdependent guardrail degradation. We map three distinct defense layers with differential vulnerability to academic framing and propose countermeasures including stateful intent auditing, educational boundary detection, and output contradiction filtering.
