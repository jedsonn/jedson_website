## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7001930`

- title: AI Dependence in Medical and Health-Professions Education: A Systematic Review with Implications for Clinical AI Deployment and Patient Safety
- authors: Hao Wu, YiXuan Guo, Feifei Na
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7001930
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Background: Clinical AI is being deployed across health systems faster than the physicians who will use it are trained around it. Generative AI — particularly large language models (LLMs) — entered medical and health- professions education within three years of ChatGPT's release, raising the question of whether the next generation of clinicians is being trained to use clinical AI safely. Concerns about "over-reliance" are routine but loosely defined. We mapped how AI dependence is conceptualised and measured, what evidence supports alleged harms, and what the findings imply for clinical AI deployment. Methods: Systematic review (PROSPERO registered; PRISMA 2020) of PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, and Scopus from Jan 1, 2020, to March 31, 2026, in English. Records were dual-independently screened and extracted into a 140-field form. Findings were synthesised narratively (SWiM); methodological quality was appraised by design-appropriate criteria; dependence-related constructs, measurement instruments, and curricular recommendations were thematically mapped. Findings: From 14 391 retrieved records (10 579 unique), 269 publications met eligibility (192 empirical studies, 16 randomised trials, 61 reviews; 79·2% from 2025–26). Rhetorical claims of harm exceeded empirical measurement by 6·2–20·5-fold across five dependence constructs (deskilling or skill atrophy claimed in 82 studies, measured in 4); only one study (0·4%) measured patient perspectives on AI-trained clinicians; geographical representation was broad, with studies mapped to 52 countable country/region labels after geography normalisation; 10 records were multinational, unclear, or not reported. China, including Hong Kong SAR and Taiwan under the display rule used throughout this review, contributed 48 studies (17·8%), and the United States contributed 46 (17·1%), but none reported outcomes by race or ethnicity. Empirical signals were consistent: junior learners showed higher dependence; trust calibration was systematically poor; AI-coaching benefits were asymmetric across performance tiers. Interpretation: The field's rhetoric of harm has outpaced its measurement by an order of magnitude. As clinical AI moves from pilot deployment to routine care, the cognitive resilience of the workforce that uses it is a patient- safety question, not only an educational one. Specificity-informed curricular design and educational impact assessment of clinical AI tools at the device-regulatory stage offer tractable next steps.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7037031`

- title: Rethinking Delphi for Tourism Futures: A Modified Multi-AI Approach to Anticipating Emerging Challenges in the Middle East and North Africa
- authors: Hafez Mansour
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7037031
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, deepseek, gemini, generative ai

### abstract

This article rethinks the Delphi method for tourism futures research by developing and applying a modified multi-AI Delphi approach to the Middle East and North Africa (MENA). Rather than treating generative AI systems as substitutes for human experts or as predictive oracles, the study examines whether multiple AI systems can be organized as synthetic expert informants within a transparent foresight protocol. ChatGPT, Gemini, DeepSeek, Copilot, and Claude were used across three iterative Delphi rounds to structure synthetic judgment, map uncertainty, identify convergence and divergence, revise challenge categories, and translate findings into alternative tourism futures scenarios. The findings identify climate change and extreme heat, geopolitical instability and destination-risk perception, and water scarcity as the most consequential existential risk set for MENA tourism over the next decade. They also show that infrastructure fragility, SME vulnerability, cybersecurity and synthetic media threats, AI-enabled competitiveness, workforce transformation, market diversification, sustainability regulation, sub-regional supply asymmetry, and shifting tourist values shape the region's adaptive pathways. The article contributes to futures methodology by demonstrating how AI-assisted Delphi can move beyond descriptive forecasting toward reflexive uncertainty mapping, scenario-ready synthesis, and plural futures inquiry. For tourism scholarship, it reframes MENA tourism futures as a condition of asymmetric compound instability in which alternative pathways depend on the interaction of external stress and adaptive governance capacity. The study argues that the value of multi-AI Delphi lies not in predicting a single future but in making assumptions, disagreements, risks, and scenario logics explicit for anticipatory governance.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7065905`

- title: Explainable Graph Learning for Vulnerability Detection with LLM-Generated Program Flows
- authors: Rajesh Kalakoti, Hayretdin Bahsi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7065905
- keyword hits: deepseek, gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Software vulnerabilities pose significant risks to the security and reliability of modern software systems. Graph-based vulnerability detection methods have demonstrated strong performance by leveraging program representations such as Control Flow Graphs (CFGs) and Data Flow Graphs (DFGs). However, constructing these graphs typically relies on static analysis tools, which require complex language-specific parsing and analysis pipelines and may be less effective when processing incomplete or syntactically incorrect code. In this paper, we investigate the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) for generating CFGs and DFGs directly from source code. We evaluate three state-of-the-art LLMs, namely GPT-4o, GPT-4.1-nano, and DeepSeek-V3, and compare the generated graphs against ground-truth graphs constructed using Joern. Experiments are conducted on the OWASP and Juliet Java datasets and the BigVul C/C++ dataset. GPT-4o produces quality graphs that are structurally closest to the Joern baseline across both graph representations. Results demonstrate that GT achieves performance close to the Joern baseline when trained on GPT-4o-generated graphs, indicating that LLM-generated CFGs and DFGs can effectively support graph-based vulnerability detection. Furthermore, we investigate the explainability of GT predictions using GNNExplainer, Grad-CAM, and Integrated Gradients (IG). The generated explanations are quantitatively evaluated using Graph Explanation Faithfulness (GEF) and Graph Explanation Stability (GES). Across both Java and C/C++ datasets, IG consistently provides the most faithful and stable explanations. Overall, the results demonstrate the feasibility of using LLM-generated program graphs for graph-based vulnerability detection while supporting reliable and interpretable model explanations.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.05685v1`

- title: Depression Symptoms and Relational Patterns in 187k ChatGPT Histories
- authors: Neil K. R. Sehgal, Dunigan Folk, Lyle Ungar, Sharath Chandra Guntuku
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-06
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05685v1
- keyword hits: chatgpt, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models are increasingly used as private, always-available conversational systems, but little is known about how people with depressive symptoms use them. Building on CSCW work on disclosure and peer support, we examine ChatGPT as an emerging informal support infrastructure: private, persistent, responsive, and available outside ordinary hours. We analyze 187,093 ChatGPT conversations from 766 participants who completed the PHQ-8, comparing those below the moderate-symptom threshold (score of 10) with those at or above it. Higher-PHQ participants used ChatGPT more for mental-health, interpersonal, loneliness, self-focused, and support-seeking conversations, with pronounced late-night and recurring month-level patterns. Their language contained more first-person singular pronouns and absolutist terms. They more often engaged ChatGPT in high-disclosure contexts, but professional redirection was not higher. Language-based prediction was modest and insufficient for screening (AUROC 0.591). We argue these histories should not be treated as clinical screening data but as evidence LLMs are increasingly used as informal support infrastructure.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6925119`

- title: Generative AI as Exam Evaluation Copilot
- authors: Leonardo Boncinelli, Paolo Brunori, Alessio Magnolfi, Giacomo Negrisolo, Eugenio Vicario
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6925119
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Grading open-ended exam responses is a cognitively demanding task and is subject to substantial random variability, or judgment noise, even when performed by the same instructor. While recent studies have explored the use of generative artificial intelligence in assessment, most treat human-assigned grades as ground truth and focus on agreement rather than reliability. This study adopts a different perspective by modeling grading as a stochastic process and examining whether large language models (LLMs) can support instructors in identifying potentially unstable grading decisions. Using real exam data from a university course, we fine-tune LLMs on historical grading data from a single instructor and compare AI-generated grades with two independent grading rounds conducted at different points in time. We find a statistically significant association between AI-human grading discrepancies and intra-rater grading variability, suggesting that AI-generated evaluations capture meaningful signals of grading instability. The results indicate that LLMs can function as evaluation copilots, supporting grading reliability by selectively flagging responses that may benefit from reconsideration, while preserving instructor control over final decisions.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.06845v1`

- title: LLMs Silently Correct African American English: Auditing and Mitigating Dialect Bias via Activation Steering
- authors: Huan Wu, Ali Emami, Muhammad Furquan Hassan, Osaretin Igbinoba, Osakpolor Idusuyi, Osamede Igbinoba, Faiza Khan Khattak, Laleh Seyyed-Kalantari
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06845v1
- keyword hits: instruction-tuned, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, prompting

### abstract

African American English (AAE), a rule-governed dialect spoken by over 30 million people, is routinely misinterpreted and "corrected" by large language models (LLMs). Across six instruction-tuned LLMs (14B to 70B), we show that state-of-the-art models systematically prefer Standard American English (SAE) continuations even when the preceding context is in AAE, effectively rewriting AAE into SAE. We present an end-to-end framework to audit and mitigate this bias. For auditing, we introduce conditional Dialect Group Invariance (cDGI), which isolates true model bias from translator-induced artifacts, and a feature-level localization analysis that identifies which AAE markers most strongly trigger bias; we find that syntactic constructions, especially negative concord (e.g., "ain't nobody"), are universal triggers across all models. For mitigation, we introduce, to our knowledge, the first application of activation steering to dialect bias: a training-free, test-time method that extracts dialect directions via causal tracing and injects them into bias-relevant layers. Activation steering reduces bias 5 to 20 times more than prompting while preserving SAE fluency. To enable this work, we release REAL-AAE , the largest real-AAE parallel corpus to date: 17,479 AAE/SAE/ AAE_back triplets from natural tweets (2 to 6 times larger than prior real-AAE resources), validated automatically (BERTScore F1 = 0.95) and by three native AAE speakers (83.0% semantic agreement).

## uid: `arxiv:2607.07858v1`

- title: Agentic AI and Retrieval-Augmented Models in Straight-Through Underwriting
- authors: Robert Richardson, Josh Meyers, Brian Hartman, David Sandberg
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07858v1
- keyword hits: agentic, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) is beginning to reshape actuarial practice, particularly in domains that require reasoning over unstructured documents, heterogeneous data sources, and regulated decision workflows. Actuaries now face a design space that ranges from traditional rule-based automation to large language models (LLMs), retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and multi-agent ``agentic'' systems that plan, retrieve, call tools, and reflect. This paper examines how these emerging architectures can support actuarial priorities such as transparency, auditability, and human-in-the-loop governance, with a focus on straight-through decision processes. To make these ideas concrete, we develop and analyze an agentic AI framework for straight-through underwriting of small commercial Business Owner Policies (BOPs). We construct a synthetic but realistic experimental environment and compare three underwriting pipelines: (i) a single-LLM baseline, (ii) a naive RAG system, and (iii) a multi-agent ``Agentic RAG'' pipeline that combines targeted retrieval, third-party data checks, and explicit multi-step rule evaluation. The agentic system performs best overall, with the largest gains in multi-step and missing-information scenarios, where structured retrieval and reflection help the model avoid unsupported straight-through decisions.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6931398`

- title: ASQ: Query Sensitivity Analysis — Citability in generative AI engines as a function of prompt design
- authors: Johnny Telles
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6931398
- keyword hits: claude, fine-tuning, gemini, generative ai, gpt-4, prompt engineering

### abstract

This study aims to formalize and expand the concept of Query Sensitivity Analysis (QSA): the empirical finding that an entity's citability in generative AI engines is not a stable attribute of its content, but a function of three independent variables: the entity, the engine, and the query formulation. Experiment 1 (108 responses, accommodation sector, Foz do Iguacu) compared structured natural language (E1) vs. exact keyword (E2). Experiment 2 (5,544 responses, 82 entities across hospitality and ERP) compared branded, categorical, and problem queries across three engines. Results confirm QSA at multi-vertical scale and reveal that sensitivity is a property of the engine x vertical pair: Gemini inverts the profile, being the most robust in hospitality (delta 0.368) and among the most sensitive in ERP (delta 0.828), while GPT is most severe in ERP (delta 0.874). The branded vs. discovery distinction is the determining axis; query subtype (categorical vs. problem) produces a marginal effect (0.014 and 0.023 respectively). A key finding: no entity is Invisible in branded queries: branded citation rates range from 0.968 to 1.000 across all engines and verticals, establishing branded as a necessary but insufficient condition for discovery citability. The specific model versions used were: Google Gemini 2.5 Flash, Anthropic Claude Sonnet 4.6, and OpenAI GPT-4o, all accessed via official APIs on May 22, 2026, with default parameters, without prompt engineering or fine-tuning.
