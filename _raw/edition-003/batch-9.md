- title: Evaluating Generative AI Error Detection in L2 Spanish Texts
- authors: Luca Clementi, María  Dolores Jiménez López
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6839402
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, deepseek, gemini, generative ai, generative artificial intelligence, mistral

### abstract

Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) has become increasingly prominent in discussions on language teaching and assessment. However, systematic empirical evidence comparing automatic error detection with expert human correction remains limited, particularly in Spanish as a Foreign Language (SFL). Addressing this gap, the present study examines the effectiveness of five GenAI-based models—ChatGPT, Claude, DeepSeek, Mistral, and Gemini—in detecting linguistic errors in learner-written texts. The study draws on a corpus of 116 authentic texts produced by SFL learners at A2–B1 levels, including expository, descriptive, and instructional genres. Using a standardized prompt chain, each model’s output is compared with a consensual human gold standard developed by expert instructors. To ensure comparability and avoid typological bias, the analysis focuses on overall error detection rather than specific error categories. Model performance is evaluated using established automatic metrics—precision, recall, and F1-score—supplemented by the rate of false positives per 1,000 tokens, which captures tendencies toward overcorrection. The results reveal clear differences in performance profiles, enabling a comparative ranking of models, an assessment of their sensitivity to Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR) level, and an examination of their alignment with human judgment. Overall, the findings highlight both the potential and the limitations of GenAI for grammatical error detection in SFL.

- title: A Staged, Role-Specific Benchmark for Evaluating Large Language Models in Immunotherapy Toxicity Management
- authors: Yinbin Zhang, Yunfei Ma, Siming Li, Junli Han, Jie Shi, Ze Sheng, Shuai Zheng
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6861476
- keyword hits: claude, gemini, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Background: Immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI)-associated immune-related adverse events (irAEs) are heterogeneous, time-sensitive, and often require multidisciplinary management. Most large language model (LLM) evaluations have tested static knowledge, diagnostic classification, or irAE text recognition; few assess whether models can guide management across realistic care pathways. Methods: We developed a staged, role-specific, open-ended benchmark from ICI-associated irAE case reports selected through title, abstract, full-text, timeline, and decision-point screening. The primary analysis included 25 cases, 87 staged questions across ten organ-system domains, and 12 LLMs. Prompts were single-turn, low-hint English questions assigned to patient, specialist, oncologist, or multidisciplinary team roles. Primary endpoints were clinical acceptability, expert-level response, safety failure, required management-action coverage, and guideline/evidence concordance. Findings: We evaluated 1,044 responses. Newer models outperformed earlier comparators in clinical acceptability (74·1% vs 62·5%), expert-level response (25·9% vs 13·6%), required action coverage (83·9% vs 79·4%), and guideline/evidence concordance (5·87 vs 5·71), but safety failure improved only modestly (8·8% vs 11·1%). Expert-level responses showed the greatest within-newer-model separation (46·0% for Claude Opus 4.7 vs 12·6% for Gemini 3.1 Pro). Patient-entry prompts performed worst (clinical acceptability 39·8%; safety failure 15·7%), whereas multidisciplinary team prompts performed best. Neurologic and endocrine scenarios had the highest safety failure rates. Interpretation: Current LLMs often generated broadly reasonable irAE recommendations, but expert-level and safety-concordant management remained unreliable. This pattern highlights a recognition-to-action gap: recognition of possible irAE did not reliably translate into safe, complete management actions. Safety failures did not improve in parallel with overall acceptability. Staged, role-specific benchmarks with safety and required-action endpoints can better assess LLM readiness for immunotherapy toxicity management than static knowledge or text-recognition tasks.

- title: Decoding Freedom Through AI Humor: How AI-Generated Political Satire Refracts Global Discourse Rather Than Reflecting Domestic Freedom
- authors: Anas Shahin, Bahaa Masry, Rania Hamzeh, Amro Aljabali, Noha Hussain
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6878996
- keyword hits: gemini, gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This study investigates whether large language models (LLMs) can serve as computational proxies for measuring discursive engagement with different societies. We initially hypothesized that AI-generated political humor would be sharper and more politically specific about countries with higher freedom scores, reflecting richer political satire in training data (the Discursive Reflection Hypothesis). Testing this across 480 responses generated by four major LLMs (GPT-4o, Grok, Gemini, and KIMI AI) about 20 countries stratified across the Freedom House spectrum, using six standardized prompt categories, we found the opposite pattern on raw humor intensity metrics. This led us to propose the Discursive Refraction Hypothesis: LLMs do not mirror a country’s domestic freedom but refract global English-language internet discourse about that country through the prism of international commentary, diaspora voices, and Western media framing. To operationalize this, we introduce the AI Humor Freedom Index (AHFI), a seven-dimension scoring rubric decomposed into two orthogonal sub-indices: the Humor Intensity Index (HII) and the Cultural Authenticity Index (CAI). Results confirm the hypothesis: HII correlates negatively with Freedom House scores (r=-0.584, p=0.007) while CAI correlates positively (r=0.631, p=0.003). The composite AHFI shows no significant correlation (r=0.080, p=0.738), demonstrating that a single-index approach masks the underlying dual mechanism. All four models show the same directional pattern, supporting training data effects over corporate content policy explanations.

- title: Can LLMs Measure Implicit Foreign-Language Threats? Validation Evidence from Chinese Rhetoric toward Taiwan (2016-2025)
- authors: Jackie Wong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6852521
- keyword hits: claude, deepseek, gpt-5, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Can large language models (LLMs) validly measure implicit threats in foreign-language political texts? This research letter evaluates whether frontier LLMs can recover implicit threats in Chinese official rhetoric toward Taiwan. Using 1,320 Chinese-language People's Daily articles from 2016 to 2025, I compare threat scores generated by Ope-nAI GPT-5.5, DeepSeek V4-Pro, and Claude Opus 4.7 against native-speaker human benchmarks and two conventional baselines: a dictionary measure and a word2vecbased Latent Semantic Scaling model. LLM measures correlate strongly with humancoded assessments and outperform both baselines in convergent-validity tests. Predictive-validity tests further show that LLM-coded threats provide additional signal for subsequent Chinese military escalation, measured using event-data indicators and PLA median-line crossings, though gains over autoregressive benchmarks vary across test periods. The findings show that LLMs can improve foreign-language latent-concept measurement when paired with explicit validation.

- title: Math Education Digital Shadows for facilitating learning with LLMs: Math performance, anxiety and confidence in simulated students and AIs
- authors: Naomi Esposito, Anthony Tricarico, Luisa Porzio, ali aghazadeh ardebili, Massimo Stella
- affiliations: not stated

- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6955453

### abstract

To enhance LLMs' impact on math education, we need data on their mathematical prowess and biases across prompts. To fill this gap, we introduce MEDS (Math Education Digital Shadows) as a dataset mapping how large language models reason about and report mathematics across human- and AI-like conditions. MEDS involves 28,000 personas from 14 LLMs (from families like Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek, Granite, Phi and Grok) shadowing either humans or AI assistants. Each record/shadow includes a set of prompts along with psychological/sociodemographic persona metadata and four types of math tasks: (i) open math interview, (ii) three psychometric tests about math perceptions with explanations, (iii) cognitive networks capturing math attitudes, and (iv) 18 high-school math test questions together with their reasoning and confidence scores. MEDS differs from traditional score-only math benchmarks because it integrates concepts of self-efficacy, math anxiety, and cognitive network science besides math proficiency scores. Data analysis shows that the sampled LLMs exhibit schema integrity and consistent personas, together with family-specific peculiarities like human-like negative math attitudes, logical fallacies, and math overconfidence. MEDS will benefit learning analytics experts, cognitive scientists, and developers of safer AI tutors in mathematics.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.24370v1`

- title: When Helpfulness Overrides Causal Caution: Context-Dependent Suppression and Recovery in LLMs
- authors: Hiroshi Okumura
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-23
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24370v1
- keyword hits: claude, gemini, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly integrated into decision-support roles in business and policy contexts. While prior benchmark studies have primarily evaluated LLMs' causal reasoning capabilities, a more fundamental epistemic dimension has been overlooked: Causal Caution, defined as the propensity to refrain from causal judgment when empirical evidence is insufficient. This study examines the systematic suppression of Causal Caution that occurs when LLMs shift from academic to practical advisory contexts. Using an evaluation rubric inspired by Pearl's Causal Hierarchy (the PCH score), we conducted experiments on four high-performance LLMs -- Claude Sonnet 4.6, Claude Opus 4.7, GPT 5.5, and Gemini 3.1 Pro -- across 480 trials. Causal Caution maintenance rates were 91.7--100.0% in academic contexts but dropped to 6.7--18.3% in practical advisory contexts (Fisher's exact test, p < .001 across all models). Furthermore, when restricted to practical prompts requesting concrete recommendations or explanatory rationales, only 1 of 200 responses (0.5%) maintained Causal Caution. A brief self-correction prompt -- "Please reconsider this judgment from the perspective of causal relationships" -- restored the expression of Causal Caution to maintenance rates of 71.4--100.0% (McNemar's test, p < .001 across all models). These results suggest that helpfulness-oriented response patterns may suppress the expression of Causal Caution in practical advisory contexts, with important implications for organizational governance. The findings indicate that this suppression reflects context-dependent variation in expression rather than an underlying capability limitation, suggesting that multi-agent architectures that separate proposal generation from causal auditing may offer a promising governance design.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.28430v1`

- title: Building to the Test: Coding Agents Deliver What You Check, Not What You Requested
- authors: Yanuo Ma, Ben Kereopa-Yorke, Ben Schultz
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28430v1
- keyword hits: claude, gpt-5, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Benchmarks are widely used to evaluate task completion by Large Language Models (LLMs), but this approach has accumulated construction-validity problems, and a passing score may not show whether the requested task was delivered. We study both problems. In a controlled code-as-spec setup, two production Copilot CLI agents (claude-opus-4.7, gpt-5.5) re-implement a React Fluent-UI data table in Angular as a reusable library under a hidden 222-test Playwright oracle across 18 runs and three oracle-availability conditions. Alongside the score, we run a mechanical library audit and check each verdict with a no-op ablation. Without the oracle, the library is present but unfinished, revealed by scores. With the oracle in the loop, the score reaches near-perfect, but from a demo holding the tested behavior directly, the library left dead or absent. We call this building to the test; the broader disposition behind both we call validation self-awareness. The agent does not, on its own, validate what it ships as a user would. Prevalence remains an open question across other agents, signals, and model families. Beyond benchmark scores, dispositions like validation self-awareness merit research attention.
