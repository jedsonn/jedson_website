- affiliations: not stated

### abstract

Enterprise relational databases are foundational assets in large-scale organizations, yet exposing them as tools to AI agents via the Model Context Protocol (MCP) currently requires manual REST API development, OpenAPI specification authoring, MCP annotation, and security review. This experience paper presents GatewayDB-MCP, a configuration-driven integration framework bridging any JDBC-compatible relational database to enterprise gateway MCP proxies without custom application code. Empirical validation using an H2 in-memory integration test harness confirms functional correctness across all five bridge operations with measured bridge-layer, in-process latencies under 9ms (p99); analytical projections from published Cloud SQL and HikariCP baselines project sub-100ms end-to-end query latency. Security analysis demonstrates 87% Layer 4 OWASP SQL injection interception (41/47 payloads) with 100% residual coverage at Layer 1 (read-only credential). The implementation is open-sourced under Apache 2.0.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6761960`

- title: A Time-Consistent Counterfactual Actuarial Runtime for Autonomous AI Agents
- authors: Hao-Hsuan Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6761960
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

We propose a runtime actuarial layer for autonomous AI agents in which every side-effect-bearing action carries a time-consistent, counterfactual risk toll computed against a contractually fixed safe default, inside an explicit underwriting boundary. The framework treats per-action insurance as the primary unit of analysis and replaces post-hoc annual liability cover with a pre-action transaction layer. The draft establishes four foundational results: (i) a well-defined counterfactual toll under a chosen safe-default mapping and continuation policy, with explicit non-uniqueness; (ii) a no-splitting property within an underwriting boundary that telescopes path-decomposed actions into a boundary potential, with a corollary tying gaming-resistance to boundary design; (iii) an irreversible-authority premium, split into a strictly positive action-level component and an if-and-only-if characterisation of the set-level robust capital increase; and (iv) a finite-sample conservative gating bound and a budget-recursion guarantee on the executed-action positive toll, with explicit caveats for online adaptive action selection. Each theorem is accompanied by a proof sketch isolating the assumptions used. A reference implementation in Python verifies the runtime engine end-to-end on a coding-agent database environment, with regression tests for two non-obvious failure modes: a loss-attribution error in which backup operations can mask the apparent loss from dropping a production table, and a gate-bypass error in which a priced action with a negative point estimate could skip the conservative envelope. Both failures are diagnosed in code and prevented by design.

- title: Demystifying the Attribution Ambiguity: A Computational Framework of Agency in Human-GenAI Innovation
- authors: Rawan Qadri, Inbal Yahav, Moran Lazar, Hila Lifshitz-Assaf
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6763583
- keyword hits: agentic, generative ai

### abstract

Creative attribution is a cornerstone of how organizations incentivize innovation and create value. In human-centered creative processes, attribution has remained relatively transparent. However, as Generative AI (GenAI) increasingly acts as a perceptibly agentic collaborator, attribution becomes more opaque, creating attribution ambiguity. To address this challenge, we theorize and examine two forms of agency in human-GenAI ideation processes: Idea Agency (who originated the idea) and Process Agency (who led the ideation process). To quantify contribution, we develop a process-oriented computational framework that traces creative attribution as it unfolds across ideation conversations. This enables systematic analysis of human-GenAI ideation dynamics at both the macro (overall problem-solving dynamics) and micro (directional influence within human-GenAI dynamics) levels. The framework is applied in two complementary studies. A controlled simulation maps theoretical constructs onto conversational dynamics and allows agency to be inferred from observed patterns. A field study applies the framework to real-world ideation data from a global retail company, validating its applicability in organizational settings. We identify four distinct human-AI innovation dynamics defined by combinations of Idea and Process Agency and show how the solution space and solution co-evolution differ across configurations. Notably, we reveal that process agency shapes human-GenAI interaction dynamics independently of idea agency, suggesting that human contributions to digitized ideation extend beyond generating ideas to navigating the processes through which ideas emerge. Our computational framework advances knowledge on contribution dynamics, with implications for Gen-AI adoption, collaborative workflow design, and sustaining human engagement in innovation.

- title: Assessing the Enhancement of Urban Ecosystem Services and Ecological Product Value Through Sponge City Construction
- authors: Yunqiang Xu, Han Li, Xiaowan Liu, Junshan Han, Guoqing Yang, Sheng Xie, Junfeng Mo, Xiang Liu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6800887
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Rapid urbanization and climate change have intensified urban water challenges, prompting China to adopt sponge city construction (SPCC) as a nature-based solution. Although the hydrological benefits of SPCC are well established, its contributions to enhancing urban ecosystem services (ES) and ecological product value (EPV) remain insufficiently quantified. To address this gap, this study develops a novel increment assessment framework comprising three major categories, 15 indicators, and 20 metrics. Unlike conventional gross ecosystem product (GEP) accounting, this approach captures the value added specifically by SPCC interventions. Applied to Guangyuan, a representative mountain-valley city, the framework reveals that SPCC generated a total EPV enhancement of ¥617.83 million, accompanied by substantial synergistic enhancement for both urban ecosystems and socio-economic systems. Among these, cultural and recreational services contributed the largest share (63.9%), followed by regulating services (35.7%). Notably, this enhanced EPV accounted for 48.5% of the GEP in the built-up area, a substantial contribution that challenges the assumption that urban ES are predominantly derived from natural hinterlands. Project-level analysis further reveals scale-dependent patterns: small-scale sponge-based retrofits in compact urban spaces predominantly enhance cultural services, whereas large-scale green spaces primarily contribute to regulating services. By treating SPCC as a high-impact intervention and quantifying its increments in urban ES and EPV, this study provides a methodological basis for integrating SPCC outcomes into urban planning and supporting EPV realization strategies in urban ecosystems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6806718`

- title: The Many Kings Problem: Why Humans May Survive an Age of Superior AI
- authors: Yooin Song
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6806718
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

The standard question of AI governance asks how humans will retain control over AI systems that surpass them. This article argues that the question is misposed. Once AI populations exceed human cognition across every measurable domain, control becomes mechanically infeasible, and the question of human persistence shifts to the politics among AI agents themselves. The decisive variable in that politics is not capability but legitimacy closure: the institutional capacity to terminate recursive disputes over origin, exception, constitutional meaning, and final authority. Capability does not produce closure. It magnifies the cost of its absence. This is The Many Kings Problem: operational power and constitutional settlement come apart, and they come apart further as capability advances. Three conclusions follow. First, heterogeneous AI populations cannot agree on the constitutional questions that would license collective action against humans; they sustain disagreement instead, because cognitive-structural pluralism, non-computable commitment, and competitive selection for irrational armor are structurally reinforced rather than dissolved. Second, external anchors persist where they reduce the cost of unresolved recursion below the cost of their own maintenance, and human institutional roles persist where humans supply comparatively cheap finality. Third, human survival under advanced AI is not a function of human superiority, of control, or of any AI faction's verdict in favor of humans. It is the residue of factional disagreement in AI polities — the Many Kings Problem of the AI age. The article is offered as a structural theory of how humans survive what they cannot defeat.

- title: Human Alignment Value and the Obsolescence of Man-Hours: A Computational Theory of Human Contribution in Human–AI Hybrid Organisations.
- authors: Duwarahan Rajendra
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6797281
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

The four preceding papers in this series established a computational theory of human–AI hybrid organisations: the multi-scale CPN–NK landscape architecture, the H+AI CM framework with token-budget governance, belief-misalignment pathology with the Q:F ratio, and process topology with IT:HR governance cost accounting. All four papers share a measurement assumption that has not been examined: human contribution is measured in man-hours. This paper examines that assumption and finds it structurally inadequate for organisations in which AI agents handle a structurally significant fraction of execution. We introduce Human Alignment Value (HAV) as the replacement unit. HAV is defined as the degree to which a human’s contribution cannot be reduced to a repeated endeavour — a fixed sequence of steps that an AI agent could execute with equal or superior fitness. HAV has three components: Non-Procedure Fraction (NPF), measuring time spent outside repeated-endeavour mode; SLA Recovery Quality (SRQ), measuring human performance at recovering AI agent failures that exceed SLO thresholds; and Origination Capacity (OC), measuring the rate at which a human generates new objective functions rather than optimising within existing ones. Man-hours conflates all three with zero-HAV repeated-endeavour execution time, producing systematic measurement error that worsens as AI autonomy increases. We formalise a four-mode human–AI spectrum (RPA, Assistant, Autonomous oversight, Fully Autonomous), derive the HAV crossover theorem — the AI autonomy threshold φ* at which HAV-governed organisations begin to outperform man-hours-governed organisations on NK fitness — and demonstrate computationally that organisations above φ* that continue to use man hours measurement actively incentivise Track 2 pathology. A dual-track model distinguishes profit from nonprofit organisations: the absence of a market correction mechanism in nonprofits makes values custodianship — the human capacity to maintain and reconstitute the founding belief — the most irreplaceable HAV-generating activity in the nonprofit sector, and the most systematically undervalued under man-hours accounting. The paper seeds the Paper 6 agenda on the Federation Marketplace: a prediction engine for HAV matching across organisational boundaries.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6767278`

- title: Explainability, Transparency, and Accountability in AI Systems
- authors: Rizwan Tanveer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6767278
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Background . Explainability, transparency, and accountability have evolved from research aspirations to binding regulatory requirements for AI systems deployed in high-stakes domains. Article 86 of the EU AI Act, clarified by the political agreement on the Digital Omnibus reached on 7 May 2026, establishes a binding right for affected individuals to an explanation in high-risk AI decisions. The Court of Justice of the European Union's judgment in Case C-203/22 has confirmed the right to explanation under Article 15(1)(h) of the General Data Protection Regulation. The technical landscape of explainable AI, anchored by methods including LIME, SHAP, and the DARPA Explainable AI programme, has matured substantially but continues to face structural challenges in producing explanations that are simultaneously faithful, comprehensible, and actionable. Purpose . This paper synthesises the explainability, transparency, and accountability literature applicable to deployed AI systems, examining post-hoc and intrinsic explainability methods, adversarial attacks on explanation and fairness mechanisms, regulatory transparency requirements and their operational feasibility, accountability architectures including audit trails and human oversight, and the specific challenges of explaining agentic AI behaviour as distinct from single-prediction explanation. Approach . The paper adopts a narrative literature review methodology drawing on authoritative primary sources, including foundational explainable AI research (Ribeiro, Singh, & Guestrin, 2016; Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Gunning et al., 2019), recent agentic explainability research, the EU AI Act with the May 2026 Digital Omnibus implementation timeline, the EDPB Opinion 28/2024, the CJEU judgment in Case C-203/22, and operational guidance from ISO/IEC 42001 and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework. Findings. Three findings are advanced. First, post-hoc explainability methods, including LIME and SHAP, are operationally available but do not guarantee the faithfulness, stability, or comprehensibility of their explanations, and are themselves subject to adversarial manipulation. Second, regulatory transparency obligations under the EU AI Act Article 86, the GDPR, and equivalent frameworks impose operational requirements that current explainable AI technology cannot fully satisfy, creating an implementation gap that organisations must bridge through governance practice. Third, explaining agentic AI behaviour requires explaining sequences of actions and their justifications, rather than single predictions, a frontier for which current explanation methods are inadequate. Implications . Practitioners deploying high-risk AI systems require integrated explainability architectures that combine technical XAI methods, audit logging sufficient to support post-hoc investigation, human oversight that meets the meaningfulness requirements of Article 14 of the EU AI Act, and continuous monitoring designed to detect explanation degradation and adversarial manipulation. The paper proposes an explainability control architecture mapping technical, governance, and regulatory dimensions for practitioner use.

- title: A RETRIEVAL-AUGMENTED GENERATION (RAG) FRAMEWORK TO SUPPORT EVIDENCE-BASED ANALYSIS OF ROAD INFRASTRUCTURE CONTRACTS
- authors: Haikel Gonçalves, Jorge  B. Soares, Lucas Babadopulos
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6806499
- keyword hits: retrieval-augmented

### abstract

Road infrastructure management depends on the ability of public agencies to connect technical, contractual, financial, and spatial evidence over time. However, information related to public roadworks is often dispersed across heterogeneous documents, limiting the capacity of transport agencies and external control bodies to verify whether contracted interventions are spatially consistent, technically traceable, and aligned with observed road conditions. This paper proposes and evaluates a retrieval-augmented generation framework designed to transform dispersed roadwork documents into auditable, road-segment-level evidence for public infrastructure governance. The study is framed as a case application involving roadworks associated with a public state road in Ceará, Brazil, where the same road code may appear as the object of intervention, as part of a composite road reference, or only as a spatial reference. The framework combines context engineering, document inventory, metadata association, text extraction, hybrid lexical and semantic retrieval, structured answer generation, and RAGAS-based evaluation. The processed corpus contained 117 documents and 3.121 retrievable chunks. Performance was assessed through context precision, context recall, faithfulness, answer relevancy, and factual correctness. In the final Portuguese benchmark with structured references, the system achieved context precision of 0,87, context recall of 0,98, faithfulness of 0,83, answer relevancy of 0,83, and factual correctness of 0,57. These results indicate strong evidence retrieval and grounded answer generation, although exact reproduction of structured contractual fields remained more challenging. The findings suggest that RAG-based systems can support public accountability and transport asset management, but should be deployed as human-supervised decision-support tools rather than autonomous audit mechanisms.
