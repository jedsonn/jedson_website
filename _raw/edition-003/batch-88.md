- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6758025

### abstract

This paper argues that the next stage of AI architecture should not be conceptualized primarily as larger language models, but as systems optimized for contextual comprehension, explicit knowledge integration, inconsistency handling, consequence anticipation, and auditable explanation. Drawing on foundation-model research, critiques of meaning-through-form alone, causal inference, neuro-symbolic AI, retrieval-augmented generation, and agentic systems, it proposes an operational definition of understanding as the capacity to maintain a task-relevant semantic state, ground claims in evidence, reason over constraints, evaluate action consequences, and produce explanations that remain traceably coupled to decision processes. On this basis, the paper introduces understanding-centric, consequence-aware architecture patterns, together with evaluation metrics and experimental protocols spanning language, reasoning, retrieval, contradiction handling, and action tasks. It also situates the broader LUM research stream as a historical research-program label inside the text while deliberately removing scale from the title and from the core definition of understanding. The resulting agenda is intended as a rigorous bridge between LLMs, SLMs, neuro-symbolic systems, and enterprise decision intelligence.

- authors: Ragul Shanmugam, Sean Kirk
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6718858
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Property condition assessment is a critical step in residential real estate investment underwriting, directly influencing afterrepair value (ARV) estimates and rehabilitation cost projections. Traditional approaches rely on in-person inspections or manual photo review by experienced investors-processes that are timeconsuming, subjective, and do not scale. Prior computer vision work on building analysis has focused on structural defect detection using convolutional neural networks, but has not addressed the holistic, room-level condition assessment needed for residential investment decision-making. This paper presents a per-room analysis framework that leverages multimodal large language models (MLLMs) to assess residential property condition from photographs. Unlike singleimage defect detection, our framework analyzes each photo independently at the room level—detecting room type, condition category, condition score, material features, and visible issues— then aggregates results into a property-level assessment. We evaluate the framework on two complementary datasets: (i) a primary condition evaluation on 57 photographs from 14 real off-market properties in the Memphis, TN metropolitan area, spanning three condition tiers (Fixer, Outdated, Standard), with independent condition labels from two experienced real estate investors; and (ii) a secondary room-classification evaluation on 39 listing photographs from the public REI Dataset. Our framework achieves 82.5% room classification accuracy on the condition dataset (100% on curated listing photos), and Cohen’s κ = 0.773 for condition agreement with one labeler, with weighted κ = 0.853 and Pearson r = 0.787 for the 1–10 condition score. Notably, the agent’s agreement with each human labeler (κ = 0.773 and κ = 0.502) bracketed the human-human inter-rater reliability (κ = 0.590), indicating the MLLM operates within the range of expert disagreement rather than below it. We identify systematic failure modes concentrated at the Outdated–Standard boundary and discuss implications for deploying MLLM-based condition assessment in production underwriting pipelines.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6786518`

- title: Empathy on Demand: How Empathic AI Can Scale Emotional Support for Verbal Harassment
- authors: Anouk Bergner, Philipp Winder, Christian Hildebrand
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6786518
- keyword hits: chatgpt, llm, llms

### abstract

Verbal harassment is a growing source of psychological stress for people around the world. It occurs both online and offline and relies on language to demean, threaten, or discredit its targets. Unlike other stressors such as loss or uncertainty, verbal harassment aims at silencing its targets by eroding their sense of being heard and weakening their perceived ability to respond. Many individuals lack access to adequate and timely support, however, when they experience such harassment. People increasingly turn to conversational artificial intelligence (AI) such as ChatGPT or dedicated AI companions for emotional support, raising questions about whether it can facilitate the same psychological benefits as actual human empathy. We focus on online contexts as a prevalent application of verbal harassment. We develop and test a psychological framework identifying three key linguistic signals of empathic listening (perspective-taking, emotional validation, and action orientation), that together restore a sense of feeling heard and enhance coping in the context of verbal harassment. We find that LLMs consistently produce language exhibiting stronger empathic-listening markers than human non-experts and trained mental health professionals, promoting more approach-oriented (vs. avoidance-oriented) coping strategies. A subsequent behavioral study shows that these linguistic signals boost recipients' sense of feeling heard and increase their coping self-efficacy. These findings reveal how specific linguistic features create empathic connections between humans and advanced conversational AI and can enhance people's psychological resilience. Our results highlight the potential for AI to serve as a scalable source of emotional support, especially when human support is unavailable or insufficient.

- authors: Andrzej Borkowski, Gabriela Buniewicz
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6805069
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Despite the widespread adoption of Building Information Modeling (BIM), no unified approach exists for automating quantity takeoff (QTO) and cost estimation using interoperable IFC files, which remain challenging due to semantic complexity and data inconsistency. Meanwhile, large language models (LLMs) are increasingly applied in construction. This article presents a workflow evaluating LLM capabilities in analyzing IFC files for QTO and cost estimation. The study comprised two phases: data extraction, cost calculation from a manufacturer's price list, interactive 3D model generation, and semantic IFC enrichment; followed by validation including the enriched IFC file and a BIM AI Assistant prototype. Results demonstrated that the LLM could interpret IFC schema relationships, map elements to catalog items, and generate enriched files preserving the original structure, and interactive 3D visualizations with a conversational interface. Potential training levels for LLMs were identified, impacting construction data interpretation quality. However, expert monitoring remains essential to mitigate erroneous mapping.

- authors: Min Liu, Xiyi Li, Yushuai Wang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6805808
- keyword hits: deepseek, large language model, large language models

### abstract

With the rapid development of digital finance, online public opinion has become an important factor influencing the systemic risks of small and medium-sized financial institutions. This study integrates the DeepSeek and FinBERT large language models to construct a multi-dimensional public opinion index system covering investor attention, emotional disposition, and opinion divergence. It measures risk exposure using ARMA-GARCH-Copula-CoVaR and conducts early warning analysis through deep learning models. The research findings show that the introduction of public opinion indicators can significantly improve the accuracy of early warnings. Among them, the BiGRU-Attention model has the best performance and can effectively capture the dynamic risks. Further analysis reveals that investor attention is the most sensitive leading indicator, and its predictive ability is significantly better than that of the emotion and divergence indicators. The research results provide a new perspective for financial risk prevention and control in the digital era, and offer important inspirations for building an intelligent monitoring system and implementing differentiated supervision.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6804404`

- title: PSEVGuard: Cloud–Edge Cyber–Physical LLM Security for Intelligent Connected Vehicles
- authors: Peixuan Yu, Haining Yu, Bo Yin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6804404
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Integrating large language models (LLMs) into intelligent connected vehicles (ICVs) introduces cyber–physical security risks: semantically plausible instructions may still trigger unsafe, safety-critical actions. Existing defenses are limited because they either lack physical grounding or rely on centralized filtering that cannot meet real-time constraints. We propose PSEVGuard, a cloud–edge security framework based on physical-semantic entanglement validation (PSEV). PSEV validates cross-modal consistency between semantic intent and physical context at the edge within strict latency budgets, while the cloud performs asynchronous adversarial analysis and policy refinement without blocking execution. In experiments under realistic threat models, PSEVGuard achieves a 98.7% attack interception rate with 0.8% false rejection, and maintains 99.2% task completion for benign inputs. The results indicate that enforcing physical–semantic consistency effectively constrains the attack surface of LLM-enabled cyber–physical systems.
