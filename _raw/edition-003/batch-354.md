- title: Agentic Attribution: Toward Forensic Investigation Standards for AI Agent Incidents
- authors: Maxwell Bosiako Antwi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7019578
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

As AI agents are given growing latitude to act autonomously across financial, technical, and communication systems, they will increasingly be implicated in incidents that cause real harm: unauthorized transactions, data exfiltration, corrupted records, or harmful real-world actions taken on a user's behalf. When such incidents occur, someone must investigate them. Yet the methods built for investigating human-caused digital incidents, and even the AI governance field's own emerging proposals for agent oversight, were not designed for a class of actor that reasons non-deterministically, holds delegated authority of uncertain scope, and frequently acts in concert with other agents. This paper examines what it would take to make AI agents forensically investigable: that is, capable of being examined after the fact with enough rigor that an investigator can reconstruct what happened, attribute responsibility with a defensible degree of confidence, and support a corrective or accountability response. Building on a published work on agent infrastructure (Chan et al., 2025), incident analysis (Ezell, Roberts-Gaal, and Chan, 2025), and authenticated delegation (South et al., 2025), the paper argues that these frameworks have made real progress on what information should be collected, but have not yet engaged with how that information would actually be evaluated as evidence: what standard of proof an investigator should apply, how confidence should be expressed under irreducible uncertainty, and how attribution should be apportioned across a chain of human principals, agent instances, and intervening third parties. The paper decomposes the attribution problem into three distinct sub-problems, evaluates existing proposals against the long-established evidentiary requirements of digital forensics, and proposes a tiered evidentiary confidence model and a set of minimum logging and retention standards designed to make agent incidents investigable without requiring deterministic reproducibility. The paper closes with concrete implications for regulators, AI developers, and incident-reporting bodies, and a short agenda for further research.

### abstract

Generative Artificial Intelligence, as one of the most important emerging technologies, has created a vast transformation in digital marketing processes and has provided new opportunities for businesses through the automation of content production, personalization of interactions with customers, increased productivity, and reduced operational costs. Despite the rapid expansion of this technology, understanding the factors affecting its adoption, especially in developing countries and in situations where there are infrastructural, institutional, and trust-based limitations, still requires further investigation. - title: GROVE: Grounded Violation Evaluator for Visual Inspection
- authors: Abhijeet Gupta, Shruti Singh, Vijayan Asari, Tam  V. Nguyen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7151338

### abstract

Automated safety inspection asks two things from a model: spot the hazard, and point to where it is. Vision–language models (VLMs) increasingly do both, but the common single-pass design collapse perception, reasoning, and localization into one forward pass. When such models fail, it is impossible to determine whether the system failed to perceive the hazard, misjudged the risk, or misaligned the coordinates. To address this opacity, we investigate whether decomposing a single VLM into distinct, inspectable phases incorporating scene captioning, structured OSHA-aligned hazard reasoning, visual grounding, multi-modal verification, and conflict reconciliation can improve detection accuracy, localization precision, and error traceability. We introduce GROVE (Grounded Violation Evaluator), a pipeline where a single Qwen 3.5 9B backbone executes all textual reasoning phases, paired with a grounding module and a specialized fallback mechanism to localize abstract or relational hazards. We evaluated GROVE against eleven single-pass baselines using a real-world construction image benchmark. While the detection scores of single-pass models cluster near 0.55, GROVE achieves an identification F1 of 0.745. In addition, GROVE significantly enhances localization, achieving a ground-truth coverage score of 0.802 compared to just 0.389 for the baseline Qwen model deployed in a single-pass configuration. GROVE also ensures near-complete visual accountability by generating bounding boxes for 99.7% of flagged hazards, reducing the missing-box rate to just 0.3% relative to the 11.1% to 73.6% rates observed in baseline models. Crucially, this modular architecture allows us to trace the majority of identification errors back to the hazard-reasoning stage. These results are established on a construction safety dataset, and demonstrate a single domain use case. GROVE is therefore best positioned as a transparent, auditable decision-support tool for offline inspection and its per-stage design offers a path toward a reliable, and transparent AI deployment.

- title: Can the "Answer Independence" Claim in Conversational AI Advertising Be Verified? Construct Decomposition and the Derivation of Audit Requirements
- authors: Sangok Kim
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7133422
- keyword hits: chatgpt

### abstract

When OpenAI introduced advertising into ChatGPT in 2026, it declared "Answer independence"-ads do not influence answers-as an official principle. This study takes that claim itself as the object of analysis, asking what must be measured to verify it and whether external observers can do so. Drawing on construct validity in measurement theory, we decompose "answer independence" into five sub-constructs-content invariance (C1), mention neutrality (C2), tone neutrality (C3), omission neutrality (C4), and longitudinal stability (C5)-and assess the verification conditions and external verifiability of each, building on the algorithm auditing literature. Verifiability proves asymmetric across constructs: C1 and C3 are partially verifiable, C2 is verifiable only conditional on sponsor disclosure, C4 is unverifiable in principle absent a stipulated completeness criterion, and C5 is practically unverifiable without access to training pipelines. We further observe a mismatch between the scope of the claim and the scope of its grounds: while the claim itself is broad enough to cover all five constructs, the grounds offered for it (system separation and labeling) address only the easily verifiable layer. From this matrix we derive three audit requirements-sponsor disclosure, counterfactual access, and third-party pipeline auditing-and connect them to existing institutional precedents including advertising substantiation doctrines in U.S. and Korean law, third-party measurement accreditation, and researcher data access under the EU DSA. As, to our knowledge, the first framework to decompose a platform's independence claim through a measurement-theoretic lens and systematically grade its external verifiability, this study argues that the debate over conversational AI advertising should turn not on the truth of the claim but on its verifiability.
