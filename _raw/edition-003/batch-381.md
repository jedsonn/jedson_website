## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6725248`

- title: Designing HIPAA-Conformant Agentic AI Architectures on Cloud Infrastructure on Clinical Decision Support Systems
- authors: Arvind Telharkar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6725248
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

The historical trend of integrating agentic artificial intelligence into clinical decision support systems would represent a paradigm shift of passive and recommendation-based decision support tools to autonomous and goal-oriented workflows capable of dynamic reasoning and action. But the autonomous nature of these agents, as characterized by an iterative process of perception, planning, and use of tools, directly challenges the underlying conditions of the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act especially with respect to granular access control, overall auditability, and accountability. Current clinical decision support systems based on clouds have no native constructs to regulate behavior of autonomous agents and hence there is a critical gap between the practical utility of agentic artificial intelligence and the regulatory imperative of HIPAA compliance. The given gap is filled in this paper, as it proposed a new four-part architectural structure that is intended to be used in cloud infrastructure. It includes policy-as-code and granular, dynamic authorization, encrypted episodic memory and patient-specific state retention, immutable and digitally signed action-log and verifiable audit trails, and a supervisory agent to enforce policy in real-time, refresh consent, and identify anomalies. Our reference implementation on a vendor-agnostic cloud stack based on Kubernetes, Dapr, and Open Policy Agent exemplifies a compliant patient-to-clinician data flow. The architecture supports fundamental HIPAA services such as a break-glass access service and a patient consent revocation service thus providing a secure, auditable and accountable basis to deploy autonomous agentic systems in regulated clinical settings.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6759126`

- title: An Engineering Framework for Self-Correcting Autonomous AI Agents: Mitigating Hallucinations and Reasoning Loops in Autonomous Engineering Workflows
- authors: Praneeth Kodumagulla
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6759126
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

This paper presents TRACE (Trajectory Reliability and Correction Engine), a runtime self-correction framework for autonomous AI agents in engineering workflows. TRACE targets a reliability gap in multi-step tool-using agents, where unsupported claims, contradictions, repetitive loops, and tool-use anomalies can propagate across long execution trajectories. Existing self-correction methods typically rely on post-hoc reflection or a single intervention mechanism, but do not integrate continuous runtime monitoring, failure attribution, and adaptive recovery in one control layer. TRACE wraps a standard ReAct-style agent with four modules: a Trajectory Monitor, a Failure Attribution module, a Recovery Controller, and an Audit Layer for structured trace logging. We evaluate TRACE on a simulated enterprise incident-triage benchmark of 50 tasks with injected hallucination- and loop-inducing conditions across four difficulty levels. TRACE achieves 76.0% task success versus 68.0% for a self-reflection baseline and 62.0% for vanilla ReAct, while reducing hallucination incidence from 19.4% to 9.6% and loop frequency from 12.1% to 4.8%. The framework is intended for reliable, auditable autonomous execution in engineering environments.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6762245`

- title: Leading Human-Agent Teams: The ORCHESTRA Framework for Accountable AI Work
- authors: Arkapravo Sarkar, Shashwat Mohapatra
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6762245
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Artificial intelligence is moving from a tool that assists individual tasks to an organizational actor that participates in workflows, decision processes, and service delivery. This shift creates a new leadership problem. Managers can no longer focus only on whether employees adopt AI or whether a workflow has been redesigned around AI assistance. They must also learn to orchestrate human-agent teams in which people, AI copilots, software agents, data systems, and governance routines interact continuously. This article develops the ORCHESTRA framework as a practical, research-informed model for leading such systems. Drawing on existing scholarship in employee AI adoption, workflow integration, human-AI collaboration, algorithmic management, sociotechnical systems, automation bias, and responsible AI governance, the framework organizes human-agent leadership into three recurring phases: Design & Enable, Operate & Govern, and Learn & Assure. The framework organizes leadership attention around Objectives, Rules and guardrails, Capabilities, Handoffs, Escalation, Supervision loops, Telemetry, Retrospectives, and Auditability. The article positions ORCHESTRA as an integrative framework developed from academic literature, practitioner dialogue, field-facing AI workflow design artifacts, and applied observation of emerging human-agent work practices. The central argument is that sustainable value from agentic AI will depend less on replacing human judgment than on designing the conditions under which human judgment, machine capability, and organizational accountability can work together.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6767042`

- title: Hierarchical Adaptive Reward-based Reinforcement Learning Model for High-Precision Text-to-SQL Generation
- authors: Zhixuan Liang, luping liu, Ruodan Quan, maoyang zou, du li, Yong Tang, haotian qin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6767042
- keyword hits: chain-of-thought, large language model

### abstract

Text-to-SQL, which converts natural language questions into executable SQL queries, plays a crucial role in lowering the barrier to database querying. However, accurately understanding the semantic information in natural language questions remains challenging, as existing methods often struggle to capture deep semantic relationships between user queries and database schemas. To address this issue, this paper proposes a novel Text-to-SQL large language model, termed HART, which integrates the Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) algorithm with a hierarchical multi-dimensional reward framework. Specifically, the proposed model leverages the advantages of GRPO in handling sequential decision-making and complex state spaces, thereby enhancing semantic structure understanding and improving user intent inference. Furthermore, a multi-level reward framework is designed to evaluate SQL quality from multiple perspectives, including structural correctness, semantic consistency, and execution effectiveness, through carefully designed reward signals. In addition, we also introduce a soft adaptive length-aware reward mechanism that dynamically regulates the chain-of-thought (CoT) length, enabling the model to generate accurate SQL queries with concise and efficient reasoning. Experimental results on the Spider dataset and its variants (Spider-DK, Spider-Syn, and Spider-Realistic) demonstrate that HART consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, achieving an average absolute improvement of 4–5% in execution accuracy. Meanwhile, the proposed model reduces CoT token length by over 85%, significantly improving reasoning efficiency. These results highlight the effectiveness of HART in balancing reasoning accuracy and efficiency for complex Text-to-SQL tasks.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6755498`

- title: Do AIS Dream of Electric Duties?
- authors: Robert J. Rhee
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6755498
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

The opportunity and problem of AI as manager are multifaceted, but an AI manager is a nonstarter if it is incapable of fulfilling a manager’s core legal duty—the duty to comply with the minimum level of high conduct that the law imposes on those who hold power over an obligee’s special interest. As a general legal principle, a business firm should not be operated by any person who is not subject to duty and accountability or by any autonomous thing to which the schema of duty and accountability cannot be implemented. This chapter answers these questions: (1) Could an agentic AI assume and satisfy fiduciary duties that human fiduciaries would owe to the firm and its owners? (2) What are the issues related to the satisfaction of these duties and the legal mechanisms that could hold AI accountable for its managerial conduct? These questions are answered in four Parts. Part I identifies the preconditions to AI management. AI must be capable of being a legal person, and personhood requires technological and legal preconditions. Part II discusses implementation of accountability mechanisms. They include contracting for fiduciary duties and owner approval of specific matters. Part III discusses issues related to liability of AI and owners. Law and policy must ensure that liability and its cost are fairly addressed. Part IV discusses the (mis)alignment problem, which is the divergence of AI conduct from ex ante owner’s interests. This agency problem is mitigated if owners contract for fiduciary duties, engage in robust monitoring that is incentivized by potential liability to third parties, and reserve the power of removal.

- authors: Iván Arribas, Fernando García García, María del Carmen García, Javier Oliver Muncharaz
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6771165

### abstract

Stock price forecasting remains a challenging problem in financial econometrics due to the inherently nonlinear, highly volatile, and non-stationary characteristics of financial time series. This study examines the application of deep learning approaches based on Transformer architectures and hybrid LSTM–Transformer models for predicting closing prices of S&P 500 constituents. Using historical data spanning from 2015 to 2026, four modelling frameworks are evaluated: Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), Bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM), a pure Transformer architecture, and a hybrid LSTM–Transformer model. The empirical results indicate that attention-based architectures are capable of effectively capturing complex temporal dependencies in financial data.In particular, the proposed hybrid specification integrates the LSTM’s capacity for modelling sequential dependencies with the Transformer’s ability to capture long-range interactions through multi-head attention mechanisms. Overall, this study contributes to the existing literature on financial time series forecasting by providing a systematic comparative evaluation of contemporary deep learning architectures applied to a large-scale dataset of S&P 500 equities.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6753921`

- title: Agentic Quantum-Classical Pipelines for Protein Folding and Variant Optimization
- authors: Kaushik Ganguly
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6753921
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Protein structure prediction through deep learning (AlphaFold2, ESMFold) has matured rapidly, yet these tools remain disconnected from quantum-enhanced optimization and physics-based structural verification. No existing system orchestrates heterogeneous quantum methods into a single governed pipeline for protein engineering. We present HelixAI Quantum, a 10-agent system orchestrated by LangGraph that extends ESMFold-based structure prediction with three independent quantum computation branches: (A) a Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) with Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR) aggregation for protein variant optimization, (B) a Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) on a tetrahedral lattice for physics-based structure verification, and (C) honeycomb lattice QUBO folding via simulated annealing with D-Wave-compatible formulations. Executing the full pipeline on the Bradykinin peptide (RPPGFSPFR, 9 residues) using Qiskit AerSimulator, we deployed 52 qubits across four quantum agents. The ZZFeatureMap embedding circuit required 176 gates at depth 101 with 64 CNOT operations, producing 1,778 unique bitstrings from 2,048 shots (86.8% measurement diversity). QAOA achieved fitness 3.76 versus classical best 3.95-a quantum advantage ratio of 0.9521-confirming that at 16qubit scale no quantum speedup is observed. The VQE verifier converged to folding energy-1.75 using 12 qubits and 36 variational parameters over 300 COBYLA iterations, while the lattice folder produced energy-5.515 with 8 binary variables. Our contribution is architectural: we demonstrate that gate-based optimization, gate-based simulation, and annealing-compatible formulations can coexist under agentic governance with fault tolerance, automated benchmarking, and real-time transparency. To our knowledge, this is the first system to unify three heterogeneous quantum modalities for computational biology.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6773533`

- title: Cross-condition and cross-chemistry battery prognostics with domain-adversarial and variance-regularized hierarchical transformers​​​
- authors: Liu Teng, Wei Li, Zhiqiang Li, Weiwei Huo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6773533
- keyword hits: fine-tuning

### abstract

Accurate remaining useful life (RUL) prediction of lithium-ion batteries under varying operating conditions and diverse chemistries remains challenging due to complex distribution shifts in degradation patterns. This paper proposes a unified prognostic framework integrating hierarchical transformers with domain-adversarial training and variance regularization for zero-shot cross-condition and cross-chemistry battery capacity trajectory forecasting. The framework encodes SOC-aligned within-cycle measurements into compact cycle representations via a lower-level transformer with attention pooling, then captures long-range degradation dynamics through an upper-level transformer to predict future capacity. Domain-adversarial learning with a gradient reversal layer suppresses domain-specific shortcuts, enabling zero-shot transfer across unseen datasets and chemistries without fine-tuning. Variance-regularized risk minimization penalizes loss dispersion across condition-chemistry subgroups to improve worst-case performance. The model is evaluated on an aggregated multi-chemistry dataset (LCO, NCA, NCM, blended cathode, sodium-ion) under -5 °C to 60 °C and 0.2C to 6.0C. Six benchmark protocols with progressively relaxed split keys systematically assess zero-shot generalization across cells, discharge rates, operating conditions, and chemistry-domain holdouts. Results show trajectory-level MAPE of 1.02%–8.44% and end-of-life MAPE of 5.01%–9.69% under zero-shot settings, with longer observation prefixes consistently improving accuracy. Ablation studies confirm that removing hierarchical encoding, domain-adversarial training, attention pooling, or variance regularization substantially increases errors under strong domain shifts, validating their complementary synergy for robust zero-shot battery prognostics.
