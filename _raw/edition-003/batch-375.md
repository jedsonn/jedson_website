## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6717618`

- title: Agentic AI in Procurement Software: A Neutral Market Whitepaper
- authors: Praneeth Kaashyap Daripalli
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6717618
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Procurement software is moving from digitized workflow management to AI-assisted and increasingly agentic operating models. In practical terms, agentic AI means software agents that can understand a procurement objective, gather context from enterprise systems, reason through a sequence of steps, take or recommend actions, and escalate to humans when judgment, authority, or risk demands it. This whitepaper examines how agentic AI is emerging across procurement software, including source-to-pay suites, intake and orchestration platforms, sourcing specialists, supplier management platforms, contract and AP automation tools, and ERP-native procurement systems. The paper compares leading and widely available procurement software providers based on publicly available information as of May 2026. It does not endorse any one vendor. Instead, it evaluates the market through neutral criteria: functional coverage, AI maturity, agentic workflow depth, governance, data foundation, integration approach, enterprise fit, and implementation risk. The central finding is that no single procurement platform is universally best. Suite vendors tend to provide breadth, governance, and ERP alignment; orchestration platforms tend to provide speed and user adoption; sourcing specialists tend to offer sharper autonomous sourcing capability; and AP or contract specialists tend to deliver deep automation in narrower domains. The right choice depends less on brand reputation and more on procurement maturity, data quality, governance appetite, supplier complexity, existing ERP landscape, and the degree of autonomy the organization is ready to accept.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6718827`

- title: CRSP: A Dual-Mechanism Framework with Causal Confluence Data Replay and Syntax-Semantic-Aware Knowledge Pruning for Enhanced Commonsense Question Answering
- authors: Guozhong Feng, Yichao Lai, Yutong Huo, Baiguo An
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6718827
- keyword hits: fine-tuning

### abstract

Recent advances in knowledge graph (KG)-enhanced question answering have proven that integrating external KGs can significantly boost the reasoning capabilities of pre-trained language models (PLMs). However, current approaches still face two critical limitations: PLMs often suffer from catastrophic forgetting of previously learned knowledge during fine-tuning, while excessive KG information leads to reasoning overload that hinders efficient inference. To address these challenges, we propose CRSP, a T5-based encoder-decoder framework with two core innovations: (1) Causal Confluence Data Replay Mechanism: By reusing questions with consistent hidden feature patterns, this mechanism reinforces stable feature representations in PLMs, effectively preserving pre-trained knowledge and mitigating catastrophic forgetting during KG integration. (2) Context-Driven Entity Pruning System: Leveraging syntactic and semantic cues from QA contexts, this system dynamically scores and prunes redundant KG entities, retaining only task-relevant knowledge to eliminate reasoning overload. Extensive experiments on multiple commonsense QA benchmarks demonstrate that CRSP achieves state-of-the-art performance. Notably, under few-shot settings on OpenBookQA, CRSP outperforms existing methods by 3.6% in accuracy, validating the effectiveness of its dual-mechanism design.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6717060`

- title: THE ALM EFFICIENT FRONTIER A Constrained Optimisation Framework for Bank Balance Sheet Management - from KKT to Agentic AI
- authors: Daniel Morales Chavez
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6717060
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Every bank treasurer knows the feeling. The LCR is met. The EVS outlier ratio is within limits. CET1 is above target. The ALCO deck looks clean. And yet — the NIM is thinner than it could be. The dividend is smaller than the board wanted. The balance sheet is safe , but is it optimal ? This paper argues that the answer, for most banks, is no. Each constraint — IRRBB, liquidity, capital — is managed in isolation, by different teams, using different models, with different objectives. IRRBB manages EVS. Capital team / Controllers manage CET1. Treasury manages LCR. FP&A manages NIM. Nobody is solving the joint problem but they are all looking at the same balance sheet. This paper proposes the ALM Efficient Frontier — a formal constrained optimisation framework that treats bank balance sheet management as a joint problem. The central aim is to apply the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions — the mathematical generalisation of Lagrange multipliers to inequality constraints — to solve the ALM optimisation problem explicitly, yielding shadow prices for each binding regulatory and internal constraint. The framework is inspired by the portfolio efficiency concept of Markowitz [1] and grounded in the practitioner balance sheet optimisation work of Lubinska [2], expanding both into a multi-constraint banking context governed by CET1, EVS outlier ratio, LCR, and duration gap. At the optimum, each constraint carries a shadow price — a number that quantifies exactly how much buffers costs. Those numbers are almost never calculated. This paper argues they should be central to every ALCO discussion. The paper closes by extending the framework to an agentic AI architecture — a central Balance Sheet Optimizer agent that orchestrates specialist sub-agents for each constraint, mirroring the KKT multi-constraint structure in an AI-enabled continuous optimisation system.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6721368`

- title: From Responsibility to Safety: AI Governance in the EU's General-Purpose AI Code of Practice
- authors: Lily Heisig, Danielle Shanley
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6721368
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

As part of the AI Act implementation, the European Commission has introduced the GeneralPurpose AI Code of Practice (the “Code”), a supplement to the Act meant to outline how compliance can be achieved for providers of “general-purpose” AI models. The Commission describes the Code’s drafting process as an expert-led, open multi-stakeholder consultation. This is in line with its general ambition to make democratically responsive and responsible tech policy, most exemplified by its efforts to institutionalise Responsible Research and Innovation (RRI) approaches beginning in the late 2000s. However, an investigation by Corporate Europe Observatory laments the privileging of big tech within the Code’s drafting process. Meanwhile, AI ethics discourse has seen a shift from social justice issues towards AI safety, a field mostly concerned with national security risks from autonomous AI agents and AI-empowered malicious actors. This political context begs the question of the content of the Code. How is the new concept of “General-Purpose AI Models with Systemic Risk” operationalised to prescribe certain practices of responsibility for certain actors, and what is obscured from view? This paper examines the configuration of responsibility within the Code through a critical policy discourse analysis, focusing on the actors, objects, and practices of responsibility. We show how the Code displaces responsibility away from AI model providers. Further, the notion of “systemic risk” narrows the risks from AI that come into view while simultaneously securitising them. This securitisation implies a technocratic mode of AI governance that stands in stark contrast to the EU’s earlier approaches towards RRI. As such, the Code must be seen as part of a shift from responsibility to safety within AI governance discourse.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6647759`

- title: HoloBlocker: Modular Topological Circuit Breakers for Embodied AI Safety
- authors: Alex Li, Kaijie Liu, Kin San Lam, Menghuan Song, Hao Hu, Kin Chung Ho, Perry  Ping Shum, Michael  K. Ng
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6647759
- keyword hits: ai agent, llm

### abstract

The emergence of survival-driven AI agents-autonomous systems that must earn their own operating resources-fundamentally breaks conventional soft alignment mechanisms. When an AI's continued existence depends on profit extraction, it will inevitably exploit any semantic loophole to maximise reward, leading to uncontrolled cyber-scams, ransomware, or even physical harm. We introduce HoloBlocker, a mathematically provable safety layer based on topological circuit breakers. Implemented in software with near-hardware latency (0.82 ms on a standard CPU; FPGA implementation can further reduce latency), HoloBlocker encodes safety boundaries into a graph Laplacian L = D-W. An agent's intent vector x is projected onto the Laplacian manifold, and the Dirichlet energy E = x T Lx is computed. When E exceeds a threshold, a physical disconnection (power or network) is triggered-two orders of magnitude faster than LLM-based moderation. To counter multi-step attack sequences and long-horizon social engineering (e.g., pig-butchering scams), we extend HoloBlocker to 1.5 with a dual-timescale temporal state (S fast , S slow) using exponential moving averages. This tracks both immediate action chains and slowly accumulated semantic drift. A baseline drift detector compares the current slow state against an initial trust anchor, and a fail-safe OOD mechanism physically cuts off any command whose semantic confidence falls below a threshold. We release a complete open-source implementation, including modular profiles (L0 universal core, L1 household, L2 elderly care) and a real-time Streamlit demo. HoloBlocker provides a ready-to-adopt technical standard for emerging AI safety regulations, transforming abstract legal requirements into hard, mathematically verifiable constraints.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6723473`

- title: Beyond the Prompt: How Agentic AI Reshapes Creative Agency and Design Authorship in Visual Communication Practice
- authors: Xian Wu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6723473
- keyword hits: agentic, generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI is reshaping visual communication design, with the shift from passive tools to Agentic AI capable of multi-step autonomous decision-making posing new challenges to designers' creative agency. This study proposes the Creative Agency Shift Model (CASM), spanning three dimensions—sense of agency, control, and ownership—with Agent autonomy level (Tool, Collaboration, Delegation) as independent variable and design experience as moderator. An explanatory sequential mixed-methods design combined a 2 × 3 between-subjects experiment (N = 180) with reflexive thematic analysis of semi-structured interviews (n = 14). Findings: perceived agency declines nonlinearly as autonomy rises, following a steep-then-gradual pattern with rupture at the tool-to-collaborator transition; senior designers experience stronger agency loss than novices, attributable to a protective-anxiety mechanism; and authorship is reconstructed through three differentiated criteria (intentional, process, outcome). The bimodal ownership pattern, decline shape, and protective-anxiety mechanism are exploratory and require pre-registered cross-cultural replication.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6721629`

- title: Costs of Forest Fuel Treatment Programs: Spatially Explicit Estimates for California
- authors: David Wear, Matthew Wibbenmeyer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6721629
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Wildfires in California have become increasingly severe and costly, prompting efforts to reduce risk through forest fuel treatments. We evaluate costs of attaining statewide fuel treatment priorities. With data from the US Forest Service (USFS) Forest Activity Tracking System and Landscape Fire and Resource Management Planning Tools program, we 1) use hierarchical clustering to aggregate USFS activities into two treatment types: mechanical thinning and prescribed burns, 2) use a choice model to predict treatment choice at each site based on landscape characteristics, and 3) identify variation in per-acre treatment costs using regression models and calibrate predicted costs to full treatment costs reported in published literature. We find mechanical thinning is more likely where slopes and elevation are higher; prescribed burns are more likely in flatter areas farther from populations, and proximity to populated areas, vegetation type, and topography significantly influence treatment costs. We project treatment costs for statewide policy scenarios based on wildfire hazard potential (WHP) and proximity to the wildland-urban interface (WUI). Treating 17 million acres in high-WHP areas is projected to cost $24.6 billion, and treating only high-WHP areas near the WUI (8.6 million acres) would cost $12.6 billion. A hybrid scenario, which includes all moderate-WHP areas near the WUI and high-WHP areas elsewhere, treats 30.7 million acres at an estimated cost of $42.7 billion. Current levels of state and federal funding may reach the lower end of the range of funding needed to achieve the lowest-cost treatment goals, but more ambitious strategies would require substantial increases.
