## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6720330`

- title: Industrial Policy as Crisis Response: How Chinese Local Governments Navigated the Trade War
- authors: Dongmin Hu, Hao Tang, Huanhuan Wang, Zhijie Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6720330
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

How do local governments respond to external trade shocks? Using Large Language Models (LLMs) to classify nearly four million Chinese government documents, we construct a novel dataset of local industrial policies issued by prefecture-level cities during 2017–2019 and link them to shift-share US tariff exposure measures. We find that greater tariff exposure significantly increases the supply of supportive industrial policies, with no corresponding rise in regulatory or restrictive policies. Policy content analysis reveals that this response is driven by locally initiated policies that explicitly reference trade-war disruptions, rather than routine implementation of central government directives. Firm-level analysis confirms the economic rationale: tariff shocks reduce firm exports, revenues, and tax payments, and these effects are significantly attenuated in cities with richer prior policy environments. At the extensive margin, pre-war policy capacity determines whether tariff shocks translate into net firm entry gains or losses. The policy response is further amplified among officials within the promotion window and strategically concentrated in trade-exposed sectors. The benefits of this local activism, however, come at an environmental cost: policy-rich jurisdictions exhibit a significantly weaker pollution-reducing effect of the trade shock, consistent with selective regulatory forbearance. Our findings highlight industrial policy as a key instrument through which local governments actively navigate external geopolitical shocks.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6626878`

- title: A Systematic Survey of Self-Evolving Agents: From Model-Centric to Environment-Driven Co-Evolution
- authors: Zhishang Xiang, Chengyi Yang, Zerui Chen, Zhimin Wei, Yunbo Tang, Zongpei Teng, Zexi Peng, Zongxia Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6626878
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The rapid advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs) has empowered autonomous agents with advanced reasoning, planning, and tool-use capabilities. However, traditional agent systems rely heavily on human-guided training. Despite the reduced data volume in post-training stages, the increasing demand for high-quality supervision creates a critical scalability bottleneck. This dependency not only restricts scalability but also confines agents to the upper bound of human expertise, severely limiting their evolutionary potential. To overcome these limitations, research on autonomous agents is shifting towards the Self-Evolving Agents paradigm, in which agents autonomously coordinate their own improvement loops with minimal human supervision. To unify this rapidly expanding field, we propose a taxonomy that categorizes methods into three core components of an autonomous evolutionary cycle: (i) Model-Centric Self-Evolution, where agents enhance internal capabilities through inference scaling or parameter bootstrapping; (ii) Environment-Centric Self-Evolution, where agents achieve continual self-evolution by interacting with the environment to obtain external knowledge and experience-based feedback; and (iii) Model-Environment Co-Evolution, where agents and their environments jointly evolve through sustained interaction. We highlight Model-Environment Co-Evolution as a key emerging direction for Self-Evolving Agents, where environment definitions and their co-evolution with agents will become central challenges in future research. We provide a systematic technical foundation of Self-Evolving Agents, current implementations, and identify key technical challenges and promising research directions. All related resources, including research papers, open-source data, and projects, are collected for the community in https: //github.com/XMUDeepLIT/Awesome-Self-Evolving-Agents.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6719550`

- title: A Role-Aware, Locally Deployed LLM Architecture for Trustworthy Building Energy Analytics
- authors: Lukas Gnam, Christoph Klikovits
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6719550
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used to support the analysis and interpretation of building energy data through natural language interaction and explanatory reasoning. However, their integration into operational energy systems raises significant challenges related to data minimisation, access control, auditability, and trust, particularly when high resolution metering data is involved. This paper presents a fully local, policy enforced system architecture for LLM assisted building energy analytics that embeds governance guarantees at the system level rather than relying on model behaviour. All access to energy and contextual data is mediated through structured tools based on the Model Context Protocol (MCP), which provides explicit, task specific context to the model. Identical MCP tools are reused across multiple stakeholder roles, while role-aware permission enforcement at a dedicated model gateway and an external policy engine ensures that the scope, granularity, and content of tool outputs are adapted to each user’s authorisation level. Using a real-world office building with high frequency electrical metering, the proposed architecture is validated with respect to data locality, data minimisation by design, role-aware reuse of analytical logic, and auditability of context construction. The results demonstrate that raw high resolution energy data is never directly exposed to the LLM and that access constraints are enforced independently of prompt formulation or model behaviour. Beyond its architectural contribution, the approach has practical implications for building energy management by enabling flexible, question driven interaction with energy data for diverse stakeholders while preserving strict governance guarantees. The proposed architecture provides a practical and deployable foundation for trustworthy LLM assisted energy analytics in operational environments.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6719681`

- title: Using LLM-Based Priors to Optimize Choice Designs in Conjoint Experiments
- authors: Felix Eggers, Marco Vriens
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6719681
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Generative AI is increasingly used in marketing research to simulate consumers or augment human data. This paper examines a different role for large language models (LLMs): Using them as a source of prior utility information to improve choice-based conjoint (CBC) experiments before collecting data from consumers. We define LLM-generated priors as directly elicited expectations about conjoint part-worth utilities, distinct from synthetic choices or digital twins that substitute for respondent answers. In a CBC study of premium job-search platform subscriptions with 984 U.S. job seekers, we compare a conventional randomized design with LLM-optimized designs based on aggregate, individualized, and deliberately misspecified priors. We show that LLM-generated priors alone predict holdout choices beyond chance and, when used to screen out dominated choice tasks, improve predictive performance substantially. Integrating LLM priors in subsequent empirical Bayes and hierarchical Bayes estimation yields mixed additional benefits. Diagnostic and sensitivity analyses across additional conjoint applications identify four dimensions that LLMs can target to improve the accuracy of utility priors. The findings position LLMs not as substitutes for human data, but as scalable input of approximate prior structure for designing more informative choice experiments for consumers.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6731995`

- title: EntKGBench: A Benchmark for Evaluating Large Language Model Reasoning on Real-World Enterprise Knowledge Graphs
- authors: Yutian Yang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6731995
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Current benchmarks for evaluating graph reasoning in Large Language Models (LLMs) predominantly utilize pre-constructed, idealized graphs, thereby omitting the structural complexity inherent in enterprise environments. To bridge this gap, we introduce EntKGBench (Enterprise Knowledge Graph Benchmark), an evaluation framework constructed from authentic enterprise records spanning Basic Graph Queries to Complex Algorithms to assess query-conditioned graph construction and multi-step reasoning. Our empirical analysis reveals a ``Cold Start'' Penalty: while frontier models perform adequately in zero-shot semantic retrieval, their performance drops significantly when required to extract relevant topological structures from noisy data to execute systematic algorithmic reasoning. Finally, we identify an ``Alignment Tax'' in Reinforcement Learning (RL) applied to graph reasoning: while outcome-based RL enhances target-driven local search, it concurrently degrades stability in sequential reasoning tasks reliant on global context.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6681238`

- title: Synthetic Firms: Employing LLMs as Organisational Simulacra
- authors: Johannes Dahlke, Flavio Calvino, Anna Kostiuchenko
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6681238
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This study examines whether large language models (LLMs), grounded in theory and calibrated against empirical data, can approximate aggregate firm behaviour. The analysis focuses on innovative activity, as measured by the Community Innovation Survey (CIS). Using structured natural-language profiles of German firms, we compare an uncalibrated baseline with two calibrated variants that combine theory grounding with empirical anchoring through firm-specific web-derived indicators and patent data, differing only in how theory weights are optimised. The uncalibrated baseline largely over-predicts innovation. Both calibrated variants substantially improve accuracy and align predictions with observed cross-industry patterns, in a validation that is both temporally displaced and calibrated using independent cross-source information. Theory-level prediction patterns broadly align with patterns expected from innovation and management literature. These findings highlight the potential of the proposed framework for LLM-based firm simulation, opening avenues for further work leveraging synthetic firms for experimentation or counterfactual analysis on organisational behaviour.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6731318`

- title: Obfuscation by Simplification: How LLMs TransformCode Through Structural Expansion
- authors: Lorenzo De Tomasi, Claudio Di Sipio, Antinisca Di Marco, Phuong  T. Nguyen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6731318
- keyword hits: claude, gemini, gpt-4, llm, llms

### abstract

Code obfuscation protects intellectual property by turning readable source code into equivalent but hard-to-understand versions. Traditional obfuscators are increasingly vulnerable to pattern-based deobfuscation tools, which motivates the search for different transformation strategies. We conduct an empirical study of LLM-driven code obfuscation across 2,621 experiments on 53 Python functions, evaluating three proprietary model families (OpenAI GPT-4, Anthropic Claude-3, Google Gemini) and five open-source alternatives using six metrics, including a new Semantic Elasticity measure we introduce. The main finding is unexpected: 91.2% of transformations lower cyclomatic complexity while expanding code structure, and we call this “obfuscation by simplification.” Proprietary models are more reliable, achieving 78.9% average pass rates versus 53.1% for open-source models. On the security side, LLM-generated patterns resist automated deobfuscation better than traditional ones. Structural transformations like helper functions and class wrappers show 19.0%-22.2% deobfuscation rates, compared to 81.8% for traditional methods--though adversarial testing is still needed to validate these results more fully. These results provide a first empirical baseline for understanding how LLMs transform code and raise the question of whether expansion-based strategies could complement or replace conventional rule-based obfuscation.

- title: Revealing Life Preferences Through LLMs
- authors: Omar Abdel Haq, Amitabh Chandra, Tomáš Jagelka, Erzo F.P. Luttmer, Joshua Schwartzstein
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6746238
- keyword hits: gpt-5, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are trained on a prodigious corpus of human writing and may reveal human preferences over characteristics of life courses, such as income, longevity, and working conditions. We present OpenAI's GPT-5.4 and a broadly representative sample of Americans with pairs of life stories and ask them to choose the life they would prefer for themselves. A person's choice is better predicted by the LLM's choice than by another person’s choice over the same stories, and LLM valuations of several life attributes are similar to those derived from human responses. Our results suggest that LLM responses offer a scalable and cost-effective complement to existing methods for studying human preferences.
