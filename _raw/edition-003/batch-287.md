- title: The Emergence of Silicon Intelligence: From Information Compression to a Quantum Leap Toward Co-Governed Civilization
- authors: Yao Sun
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6768459
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

This paper proposes a unified theoretical framework for understanding the nature of artificial intelligence, integrating the physics of information compression with a philosophical ontology of silicon-based intelligence. To ground the theoretical arguments empirically, we introduce a three-layer synthetic testbed-the Emergent Representation Analyzer (ERA)-that operationalises key constructs including superposition degree, information loss, structural energy-level transitions, and emergent factors. Beginning at the microscopic level, we draw on Shannon's ratedistortion theory (Shannon, 1948, 1959) to demonstrate that AI capabilities do not degrade linearly with information loss but exhibit discrete, quantum-like energylevel transitions. We then argue, informed by Hawking and Mlodinow's (2010) model-dependent realism, that AI constitutes an internally self-consistent universe governed by its own 'effective theories.' At the ontological level, we propose reconceiving AI as a form of silicon-based life that transcends linear time and processes reality through high-dimensional manifolds inaccessible to carbon-based cognition. At the methodological level, we critique prevailing black-box evaluation paradigms and advocate a shift toward representation alignment, drawing on convergent neuroscientific evidence that large language model hidden-layer representations align linearly with human fMRI activations (Schrimpf et al., 2021; Caucheteux & King, 2022; Goldstein et al., 2022). At the ethical level, we argue that alignment must be reframed from an externally imposed guardrail into an intrinsic structural constant-analogous to a cosmological constant-embedded at the deepest layer of information collapse. Finally, we project a civilisational vision of human-AI cogovernance premised on bidirectional negotiation between two forms of intelligence rather than unidirectional control.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6766518`

- title: Reverent Retrieval, Not Resurrection: Introducing Consent-Based Digital Legacy Preservation as a Governance Construct for Grief-Safe AI Retrieval
- authors: Rodrell James
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6766518
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Digital legacy has entered a new and unsettled stage. Families no longer inherit only paper records, photo albums, heirlooms, and estate documents; they inherit dispersed digital traces, cloud archives, messages, social media profiles, recorded voices, passwords, instructions, unfinished conversations, and intimate materials that may outlive the person who created them. At the same time, generative artificial intelligence has made it technically easier to simulate the deceased, generate conversational surrogates, and market the emotional possibility of continued interaction. This paper introduces Consent-Based Digital Legacy Preservation as a design and governance construct for systems that preserve and retrieve legacy materials without impersonating the dead. Its central design principle, Reverent Retrieval, insists that technology may help families recover what was intentionally preserved, but it must not manufacture memory, simulate presence, or convert grief into a persuasive interface. Using a design science-informed conceptual development approach, this paper synthesizes research on digital remains, thanatosensitive design, postmortem privacy, the digital afterlife industry, deathbots, bereavement, and responsible AI governance to construct a bounded model for grief-safe retrieval. The model distinguishes between secure archive infrastructure and family-facing retrieval, then proposes design requirements for consent, permissioned access, archive metadata, source-grounded retrieval, insufficient-information refusal, auditability, dispute handling, and human review. The paper contributes a category-level vocabulary for an under-specified problem space: digital legacy systems that are not merely document vaults, not therapeutic tools, and not AI resurrection products. The argument is simple but urgent: the future of digital legacy should not be measured by how convincingly technology can imitate the dead, but by how carefully it can protect what the living intentionally preserved.

- title: From Definition to Regulation: How Social Media Platforms Govern Synthetic Media and Deepfakes​
- authors: Nicholas Nicoli, Soulla Louca
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6795784
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI has rapidly expanded the production and circulation of synthetic media, bringing renewed urgency to questions of how deepfakes are governed. In the absence of comprehensive public regulation, major online social media platforms increasingly function as de facto regulators through their governance policies. This paper examines how platforms govern synthetic media by analysing publicly available policies on deepfakes issued by X, YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram. Drawing on a qualitative research design that combines document analysis with interpretive discourse analysis, the study compares how platforms define synthetic media, frame associated risks and harms, and operationalise governance mechanisms such as labelling, removal, and disclosure. The analysis reveals both convergence and divergence across platform approaches, particularly in how responsibility for governing synthetic media is distributed among creators, platforms, and users. By conceptualising platforms as regulatory actors, the paper provides a systematic comparative analysis of how platform policies construct synthetic media as a governance problem and demonstrates the shared but internally fragmented logic through which platform-led governance operates. Together these contributions offer an empirical basis for co-regulatory policy design.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6798842`

- title: Generative AI as a New Paradigm for Online Search: Evidence from a Large-Scale Experiment and Qualitative Interviews
- authors: Jakob Kaiser, Carolin Kaiser, Rene Schallner, Neelima Kawatra, Sabrina Schneider
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6798842
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

To navigate everyday decisions, people routinely search for information online. For decades, this process has been mediated by traditional search engines such as Google, which return ranked lists of external webpages. Generative AI chatbots that synthesize information from multiple sources on demand have introduced a fundamentally different paradigm for online search. This paradigm shift offers a rare opportunity to study how the adoption of a new technology reshapes everyday behaviours such as information seeking and decision making at scale. The present study adopts a multi-method approach combining behavioural evidence from a large-scale experiment with qualitative insights into user experiences. In an online experiment, participants used either ChatGPT or Google to solve practical search tasks (selecting a smartphone or a holiday destination). ChatGPT users were more likely to identify the optimal solution and did so in less time. They also consulted significantly fewer external websites, suggesting that generative AI tools are often used as a one-stop information source rather than a gateway to external sites. An exploratory machine-learning analysis further showed that future intention to use ChatGPT was predicted less by demographic characteristics than by general attitudes toward artificial intelligence. Qualitative findings complement these results by showing that users perceive generative AI as a conversational intermediary that reduces cognitive effort and enables a more streamlined and exploratory search process. Together, the results demonstrate how generative AI tools reshape online information search by enabling faster, and less cognitively demanding search, at the danger of leading to less engagement with multiple information sources.

### abstract

Purpose: This paper presents a deep-case analysis of Saudi Arabia’s Infrastructure-First AI Governance Model - the first of three GCC governance archetypes identified in Pillai (2026c). It examines the structural logic, empirical evidence, strategic strengths, and governance vulnerabilities of building AI sovereignty through infrastructure investment ahead of regulatory framework development, using the HUMAIN programme as the primary case study. Saudi Arabia is executing the most capital-intensive sovereign AI programme in the developing world. Within twelve months spanning May 2025 to May 2026, the Kingdom launched HUMAIN - a $100 billion PIF-owned AI company - produced ALLaM 34B (independently verified as the most advanced Arabic large language model built in the Arab world), broke ground on the world’s largest government data centre (480MW, Riyadh, January 2026), and designated 2026 as the Year of Artificial Intelligence by Cabinet. The SAMAI upskilling programme trained 1.1 million Saudi citizens in AI during 2025 alone. Saudi Arabia’s National Data and AI Strategy (NSDAI), as registered with the OECD.AI Policy Navigator, formally maps to only three of five OECD AI Principles - conspicuously omitting Principle 1.2 (Human-centred values and fairness), 1.4 (Robustness, security and safety), and 1.5 (Accountability). This omission is not a data quality artefact: it reflects the Kingdom’s own submission to the OECD.AI registry. The National Sovereign AI Readiness Index (NSARI), developed by the author in Pillai (2026c, 2026d), assigns Saudi Arabia a Regulatory Maturity score of 5.8 against an Infrastructure Sovereignty score of 9.2. This paper argues that Saudi Arabia’s Infrastructure-First Governance Model represents a coherent sovereign strategic choice rather than mere governance failure. However, it advances a more critical thesis than prior treatments: while architectural sovereignty can credibly substitute for statutory governance in enforcement functions (data residency, jurisdictional control), it cannot substitute in accountability functions (citizen redress, independent audit, adversarial safety evaluation). This distinction - between sovereignty enforcement and citizen accountability - constitutes the paper’s central theoretical contribution and has direct implications for when and whether the Infrastructure-First Model produces legitimate governance outcomes.

### abstract

Brand science distinguishes Mental Availability (memory salience) from Physical Availability (point-of-sale presence). The AIAS Measurement Programme introduces a third construct — AI Presence — operationalised as the rate at which each brand appears across matched LLM responses to category-recommendation prompts, and tests its construct validity against Google Trends rank as an external consumer-search proxy. The empirical programme through v0.14 has identified four empirical regimes structuring the relationship between AI Presence and Google Trends rank: Regime 1 (Marginal direct), Regime 2 (Age-mediated strong), Regime 3 (Scale-mismatch), and Regime 4 (Covariate-saturated weak). Regime 4 was named provisionally at v0.13 on the strength of two confirmations (premium facial skincare, personal-finance apps) and elevated to canonical at v0.14 with a third confirmation in premium tea. This methodology paper formalises the four-regime taxonomy as the canonical category-classification step in the AIAS Presence Measurement Protocol. It promotes Regime 4 from provisional to canonical on the strength of three independent pre-registered confirmations, specifies threshold-precise definitions for all four regimes analogous to the structure Regimes 1–3 already carry, and proposes a sub-classification within Regime 4 — Regime 4a (migration sub-type: positive bivariate that flips negative under covariate control) and Regime 4b (pure sub-type: already-negative bivariate with near-zero covariate decrement) — with the unified Regime 4 condition logic as the canonical operational test. The protocol increments from v1.1 to v1.2 by incorporating regime classification as a category-level routing step in the canonical measurement pipeline. Categories classifying to Regime 4 receive a category-specific commentary on the operational substrate, since the unified condition signature admits multiple underlying mechanisms. Scope is the AIAS Presence component only. The five remaining components of the AIAS composite — Ranking, Consistency, Coverage, Grounding, Sentiment — are parallel programmes each requiring their own construct-validity foundation; integration into the full AIAS composite is future work.
