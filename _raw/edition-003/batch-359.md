## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7049599`

- title: The Two-Tier Copyright System: How Licensing Deals and Automated Enforcement Shape Online Copyright Governance
- authors: Giorgos Vrakas
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7049599
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This paper argues that contemporary platform-based copyright enforcement has produced a structurally unequal, "two-tier" copyright system. Responding to large-scale online infringement, regulatory regimes such as the Copyright in the Digital Single Market Directive have encouraged platforms to adopt proactive measures, incentivising private licensing agreements and automated detection technologies operating at scale and reinforced by intermediary-liability rules. The paper first outlines the legal framework governing platform liability and the shift from safe-harbour regimes to "best efforts" obligations, comparing the US DMCA and the EU e-Commerce and CDSM frameworks. It then examines how this framework operates in practice through private licensing and automated enforcement, demonstrating how these mechanisms together create a quasi-regulatory enforcement environment on major platforms. The author shows that, while legal rights remain formally identical, their practical enforcement varies according to institutional power and access to platform infrastructures: major rightsholders enjoy privileged access to licensing arrangements and detection tools, whereas smaller creators and independent rightsholders rely on less effective mechanisms. The paper further argues that early developments in the governance of generative AI and synthetic media replicate these hierarchical patterns. It concludes that future copyright policy must look beyond the formal definition of rights to the infrastructures through which they are exercised, if copyright is to remain transparent, accountable, and equitable online.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7166814`

- title: Leveraging Open-Source Technologies for a Retrieval-Augmented Generation Chatbot in Greek Newsrooms
- authors: Nikolaos Armenakis, Panagiotis Germanakos, Christos Makris, Konstantinos Mourlas
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7166814
- keyword hits: instruction-tuned, llm, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

This paper presents a Retrieval-Augmented Generation chatbot application tailored for Greek newsrooms. The system leverages the open-source Greek LLM KriKri along with open-source embedding models, emphasizing newsroom autonomy by enabling the use of AI without reliance on external providers. The chatbot is designed to assist journalists in their investigations by delivering fact-based answers accompanied by cited sources from reputable Greek news outlets. The app can be run locally, making it an accessible and privacy-preserving AI solution for newsrooms. The dataset consists of articles sourced from four different Greek news websites. The system’s architecture is described, encompassing the construction of a vector database for source retrieval, the integration of an instruction-tuned model, and the implementation of a chatbot interface that displays linked sources. An initial evaluation with senior Journalism students indicated positive perceived usefulness, ease of use, satisfaction, and willingness to adopt the tool, highlighting the importance of independent, localized, transparent, and trustworthy AI solutions for journalism.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7162118`

- title: From Production to Discernment: Evaluation-Centered Organization Design Under AI-Enabled Production Abundance
- authors: Hancheng Cao, Ruihao Zhu, Hong Shen, Qing Xiao
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7162118
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI lowers the cost of producing plausible symbolic artifacts, including memos, analyses, code, designs, proposals, plans, evaluations, and prototypes. This shift is especially consequential for knowledge work, where organizational output often takes the form of artifacts that must be interpreted, compared, revised, and acted upon. Yet many organizations still evaluate work through practices built for a lower-volume production regime: episodic review, expert gatekeeping, static portfolios, and ad hoc escalation. The cost of judging whether AI-generated artifacts are correct, distinctive, strategically relevant, or worth pursuing has not fallen at the same rate as the cost of producing them. The result is a discernment gap: a mismatch between the rate at which organizations generate plausible work and the rate at which they can coherently evaluate, prioritize, integrate, and learn from it. This Point of View article argues that closing this gap requires evaluation-centered organization design. We identify current evaluation practices that become strained under AI-enabled production abundance and propose four linked design shifts: from episodic review to continuous curation, from expert gatekeeping to distributed calibration, from static portfolios to evaluative memory, and from production efficiency to attention governance. We conclude with a research agenda for studying organization design when production is abundant but discernment remains scarce.

- title: From Data to Decisions: Real-Time Risk Management using Multi-agent Systems
- authors: Annika Bonrath, Marc Eulerich
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7046618
- keyword hits: chatgpt

### abstract

In increasingly complex risk environments, traditional sequential and manual risk identification approaches are reaching their limits. Multi-agent systems offer an innovative and scalable architecture in which autonomous, specialized agents analyze distinct risk domains, such as financial stability, operational disruptions, and regulatory compliance, and synthesize their findings into a consolidated risk assessment. Drawing on a case study from a global logistics enterprise, complemented by practitioner insights from a workshop and a cross-organizational survey, we demonstrate how a multi-agent system enables holistic and near-instantaneous risk evaluations. Grounded in sociotechnical systems theory, the approach integrates technical capabilities with human, structural, and task factors to jointly optimize risk management outcomes. The system achieved comprehensive risk assessments in under 62 seconds and still offered domain-specific analytical depth. While multi-agent systems require careful orchestration of agent communication and knowledge integration, the approach enhances efficiency, consistency, and the ability to detect interdependencies between risk categories. During the preparation of this work, the authors used OpenAI ChatGPT in order to support language refinement, improve readability, and assist with the structuring and editing of selected text passages. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7039538`

- title: The Anticipatory Economy and Ambient Commerce AI, Geospatial Intelligence, and the Architecture of Presence-Triggered Trade
- authors: Abimbola Olufore
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7039538
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

A new coordination logic is forming across retail, logistics, and consumer services: economic activity organized around predicted demand rather than confirmed orders. This paper names and traces that logic as the anticipatory economy and introduces ambient commerce as its consumerfacing expression. In ambient commerce, the act of signalling intent to go somewhere, entering a destination into a navigation application, triggers the entire service chain ahead of arrival. A grocery store sees the customer coming, confirms inventory, stages the order, and reserves a curbside slot before the car leaves the driveway. A restaurant receives arrival time, requests a menu decision, and has the order placed before the customer walks in. Every transaction is just in time. Every service is pre-positioned. The physical visit becomes the final step in a transaction already substantially complete. We trace the verified evidence base for this emerging system. Walmart's hurricane-stocking program, running continuously since 2004, established that demand correlation can drive supply decisions without causal explanation. The more consequential development is the agentic commerce infrastructure built between September 2025 and early 2026, when OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Stripe, PayPal, Visa, and Mastercard (Mastercard, 2026) launched competing transaction protocols allowing AI agents to discover, negotiate, and pay on a consumer's behalf in real time. McKinsey projects this channel will redirect 3 to 5 trillion dollars in global retail spending by 2030 (Roberts, 2025). We connect this evidence to classical economic theory (Hayek, Coase, North, Granovetter) and examine the implications for competition policy, data governance, and the participation of developing economies in standards-setting processes now underway.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7053898`

- title: AI, Evolution and Technocapitalism
- authors: Howard Covington
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7053898
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

This essay brings together ideas on the changes that artificial intelligence may lead to. It makes observations on how intelligent biological life evolved and how large language models could lead to the evolution of silicon-based life. We think of evolution in terms of the descent with modification of biological species that are subject to natural selection. Although this is the context in which evolution was discovered, it is only the most spectacular example of the operation of a more general process that might be called an evolutionary algorithm. RNA strands, scientific knowledge, technology and capitalist economies all evolve by this algorithm. The processes of generating scientific knowledge and creating wealth by capitalism have come together to create a novel evolutionary process called technocapitalism. In turn, technocapitalism may be leading to the creation of a new kind of life called silicon life. The evolutionary algorithm may just use blind chance, as with biological evolution, or it may combine blind chance with conscious direction, as in the generation of scientific knowledge. The emergence of silicon life, if it happens, will be consciously directed. But blind chance will play a significant part in how silicon life subsequently evolves and how it relates to humans. European scholars, and especially prominent Austrian mathematical and social scientists, played an important part in developing the ideas that led to technocapitalism in Silicon Valley in the 1950s. Despite this major contribution, Europe has failed to adapt to technocapitalism and this failure has made it strategically vulnerable.

- title: A Methodology for Aggregating Published Performance Trends and Architectural Characteristics Across the Literature to Support Feasibility Assessment of Emerging Designs
- authors: Devin  Michael Atkin, Orly Yadid-Pecht
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7166812
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

A search for CMOS Image Sensors on the IEEE Xplore website returns well over 1,000 unique results, more papers than any individual can realistically read and retain. Many researchers have spent years pursuing ideas only to discover they had already been explored, either unsuccessfully or sometimes more disappointingly with major publications which were missed. To address this dashing of hopes, we present the Imaging Intellect website and the associated Python scripts which were used to build it. The website provides a high-level overview of CMOS image sensor literature. The site was constructed using various LLMs to help collate a large amount of data into a more manageable format. There is a trendy research topic of AI-enhanced search offering the potential for faster insights from large datasets; however, trust in AI-generated outputs remains a significant concern in practical usage. The software and scripts presented here aim to bridge that gap through a workflow which minimizes opportunities for LLM hallucination. The resulting platform explored here in some detail acts as a useful utility for evaluating additions to the broader image sensor literature. It serves to contextualize prototype sensor architectures within the previous decades of literature. By making this comparison researchers can better assess the plausibility of projected improvements before investing the efforts to scale designs to larger implementations.
