## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6888539`

- title: Measuring Behavioural Stability in Adaptive Agent Systems
- authors: Brad Long
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6888539
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Adaptive LLM agents increasingly modify their behaviour through memory updates, planning, tool acquisition, and self-modification. While governance frameworks such as the Invariant-Bounded Agent Alignment Model (IBAAM) help control these changes, it is still difficult to know if these controls keep agents stable. There remains a challenge in determining whether these controls effectively maintain stability over time. This paper integrates Agent Identity Evaluation (AIE) metrics with IBAAM, introducing a measurement layer for continuous assessment of behavioural stability. Rather than treating identity as an intrinsic property, the proposed approach uses identity metrics as observable indicators of invariant preservation. The resulting framework enables quantitative monitoring of behavioural drift, recovery, persistence, and continuity within adaptive agent systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6962668`

- title: Brief of Amicus Curiae Prof. Grossi in Anthropic v. Department of War Case No.: 3:26-cv-01996-RFLof
- authors: Simona Grossi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6962668
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

This amicus brief, filed in support of Anthropic's motion for summary judgment in Anthropic PBC v. U.S. Department of War (N.D. Cal.), situates the government's designation of Anthropic as a "supply-chain risk to national security" within a broader constitutional phenomenon the author terms "expressive governance": the Executive's use of routine administrative tools — procurement and contracting authority, funding decisions, regulatory designations, and personnel actions — to penalize disfavored viewpoints while preserving the appearance of ordinary governance. After Anthropic publicly declined to allow its AI models to be used for autonomous lethal weapons or mass surveillance of Americans, the Executive branded the company a national-security risk — a label historically reserved for foreign adversaries — and directed agencies and contractors to sever ties. The brief argues that these actions constitute First Amendment retaliation and viewpoint discrimination under settled doctrine, and that they follow a recurring template deployed against law firms, universities, media organizations, and federal employees who voiced dissenting views. Because expressive governance operates through domains of Executive discretion that ordinarily receive judicial deference, it can evade conventional First Amendment safeguards and produce a systemic chilling effect reaching well beyond any single target. To meet that problem, the brief proposes a principled analytical framework built on three interlocking tools: (1) a clear-statement requirement of specific congressional authorization before discretionary authority may be used in ways that burden expression; (2) burden-shifting once a plaintiff demonstrates a viewpoint-linked pattern and a dependency relationship; and (3) heightened scrutiny through an evidentiary presumption of systemic distortion where the Executive targets "expressive intermediaries" — entities, including large language model providers, that aggregate, filter, or generate speech. The framework recalibrates existing doctrine for the distinctive mechanisms of modern Executive power without creating a new tier of scrutiny, offering courts a workable means of distinguishing legitimate administration from viewpoint retaliation cloaked in discretionary action. The framework developed here draws on the author's broader scholarship on expressive governance and its constitutional implications, including the forthcoming article First Amendment and the Executive Power (forthcoming U.C. Law Journal*), available on SSRN at https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6190198 , and the forthcoming book* First Amendment and Executive Power (Cambridge University Press, forthcoming June 2027), which examine the phenomenon at greater length.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6890100`

- title: The Steeper Slope: Generative AI, the Junior Talent Pipeline, and the Self-Defeating Logic of Seniority-Biased Hiring
- authors: Habib Fathallah
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6890100
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

A claim has moved, in under three years, from individual practitioners to industry-wide policy: that generative AI has eliminated the need to hire and train junior knowledge workers, because a senior equipped with AI can absorb the work juniors once did. Firms are now acting on it. Large technology employers have cut new-graduate hiring by roughly half relative to pre-pandemic levels, and rm-level evidence documents a seniority-biased pattern in which junior employment falls at AI-adopting rms while senior employment holds steady. This paper argues that the inference behind these cuts is locally defensible but globally self-defeating, in three analytical steps. First, it synthesizes the experimental evidence showing that AI's productivity uplift is decreasing in prior skill and that AI compresses the time novices need to reach competencethe same mechanism, viewed two ways. Second, it formalizes these facts in an individual value-curve model in which the worker's learning rate is the decisive parameter. Thirdthe paper's main contributionit scales that model to the rm and then the labor market, representing the workforce as a stock-ow pipeline in which seniors are produced only by juniors who mature. The model shows that a no-junior policy raises capability briey and then erodes it; that the current stability of senior employment is a transient funded by the pre-2023 junior cohort; and that if many rms adopt the policy at once, the external senior market on which each is implicitly relying collapsesa coordination failure on a shared human-capital commons. I close with a decision framework specifying the three conditions under which hiring a junior dominates an AI-only conguration, and with falsiable predictions. The central result is that the current wave of junior-hiring cuts, insofar as it is rationalized by AI substitution, is a privately rational and collectively dangerous strategy.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7001392`

- title: CSIRT-Sim: An LLM-augmented crisis-decision simulator for cybersecurity incident response in regulated organizations
- authors: Bogdan Ksiezopolski
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7001392
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

CSIRT-Sim is an open-source, LLM-augmented simulator for cyber-crisis decision-making in regulated organizations. A small interdisciplinary team — Board (management), CISO (security/IT), and Legal — steers an organization through a time-compressed incident, coordinating board-level decisions (disclosure, market, and business continuity), technical containment and forensics framed by the NIST Cybersecurity Framework and ISO/IEC 27035, and legal notifications on a regulation-accurate EU clock (DORA, NIS2, GDPR, AI Act art. 73, MDR, MAR). Scenarios are declarative JSON; a deterministic weighted-KPI scorer yields an auditable 0–100 result, while an LLM evaluates free-text actions, advises in-exercise, and generates the debrief. It spans banking and healthcare across four incident families including an AI-supply-chain attack (OWASP LLM03+LLM04), in English and Polish, with three difficulty levels and a batch mode for cohorts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7002154`

- title: Evaluating AI-Generated Advice: Source Cues, Argument Quality, and Individual Differences in Credibility and Adherence
- authors: Elodie Andrieu, Aurelie Pistono, Franck Amadieu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7002154
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

As generative AI systems are increasingly used to provide advice in complex situations, understanding how recipients evaluate such messages has become essential. The present study examined how perceived credibility and adherence depend on message source (AI vs. human), message quality (well-written vs. poorly written messages), and individual dispositions toward AI and intellectual humility.A sample of 175 participants evaluated ten scenarios describing challenging student situations, each followed by a response attributed either to a human expert or to ChatGPT and manipulated to display either high or low content quality. Data were analyzed using cumulative link mixed models (CLMM) to account for the ordinal nature of the dependent variables and the repeated-measures design.The results show no main effect of message source on credibility or adherence: responses attributed to AI are evaluated similarly to those attributed to a human expert. Message quality increases perceived credibility, with well-written messages judged as more credible, while no main effect of quality emerges for adherence. In contrast, attitudes toward AI modulate these evaluations: more favorable attitudes heighten sensitivity to message quality. Intellectual humility does not emerge as a significant main predictor, although exploratory analyses suggest a secondary role.Overall, these results indicate that the evaluation of advice relies primarily on message content and individual expectations rather than solely on source attribution. They suggest that fostering critical engagement with these systems is a key lever for their effective integration in advisory contexts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7003668`

- title: Digital Divide or Digital Equaliser? Economic Status and the Access to ChatGPT Among Higher Education Students Worldwide
- authors: Sajjad Mahdavivand Fard
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7003668
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid uptake of generative artificial intelligence tools in higher education has reignited debate about whether emerging technologies narrow or widen socioeconomic inequalities. This study examines whether a student's economic background shapes how they access and benefit from ChatGPT and draws on data from 22,963 higher education students across 155 countries and territories. Three dimensions of AI-related inequality including version access (free vs. paid), usage intensity, and perceived educational benefit are examined. Self-reported economic status, financial aid receipt, and residential setting serve as the primary socioeconomic indicators. Findings from chi-square tests, One-way ANOVA, hierarchical regression, and bootstrapped mediation analysis show that economically advantaged students are more likely to hold paid subscriptions, use ChatGPT more intensively, and report greater perceived educational benefit. Version access partially mediates the relationship between economic status and perceived benefit. The interaction between economic status and residential setting on usage intensity was not statistically significant, indicating that the economic gradient in AI use operates consistently across geographic contexts. The results challenge the assumption that low-cost AI tools automatically democratise educational opportunity and point to version-tiered access as a structural mechanism of inequality that policy interventions in higher education have yet to address.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6888640`

- title: Distributed Epistemic Displacement: A New Philosophical Framework for AI-Mediated Epistemic Agency
- authors: Sajid Hussain
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6888640
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

What happens to human knowledge when an algorithm does the reasoning? That question sits at the heart of this paper. Over the past decade, Large Language Models have moved from peripheral tools to everyday epistemic companions, answering questions, summarizing debates, generating arguments, and producing justifications with a fluency that most people find difficult to interrogate. Philosophy has not yet caught up. The existing frameworks, cognitive offloading, extended mind theory, classical accounts of testimonial knowledge, each capture something real, but none of them was built for a world in which the epistemic mediator actively synthesises, reasons, and sometimes confabulates. This paper introduces the concept of Distributed Epistemic Displacement (DED) to name what is genuinely new about this situation. DED describes a structural condition in which the locus of epistemic agency, the site of judging, weighing, and taking responsibility for belief, migrates out of the individual human subject and into a human-AI assemblage, in ways that quietly erode the normative frameworks we rely on to distinguish knowledge from mere acceptance. The paper maps the dimensions of DED, shows why prior concepts fail to capture it, proposes a theoretical framework adequate to it, and sketches the normative architecture needed to govern it. It also argues that DED is the founding concept of a broader sub-discipline, the Philosophy of Algorithmic Agency, whose time has come.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6885542`

- title: The Direction of Value Flow: A Theory of Platform Fragility under AI Substitution
- authors: Babak Heydari
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6885542
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

Digital platforms built on user-generated content face a new kind of shock: generative AI substitutes directly for the interactions that give them value. Some have collapsed in the two years since ChatGPT's release while others in adjacent domains have remained stable. This heterogeneity-across platforms, and across user tiers within a platform-is not explained by accounts organized around AI's capability in different task domains. We develop an equilibrium theory in which fragility is governed by a structural feature of design: the degree to which value flows directionally, from higher-to lower-skilled users, rather than laterally among peers. The cascade threshold separating clean truncation from full collapse is strictly decreasing in this directionality, so more directional platforms collapse at lower AI reach. A second and more distinctive prediction concerns survivors: when the shock truncates a platform from below without collapsing it, the share of value carried by the peer channel rises for every remaining high-skill participant-a compositional reorganization that follows from the geometry of the matching technology rather than the model's asymmetric weights, and that a capability-only account cannot generate. A transient-overestimation mechanism produces hysteresis: a platform pushed into collapse by inflated early beliefs about AI does not recover when beliefs correct, because the empty platform is itself absorbing, and recovery requires coordinated re-entry of the whole surviving cohort. The theory yields falsifiable predictions that separate the directionality channel from the capability channel through within-platform tier variation, off-diagonal cross-platform comparisons, and compositional shifts in engagement.
