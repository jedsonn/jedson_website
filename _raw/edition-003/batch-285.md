## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6753098`

- title: Towards Autonomous Geospatial Scientific Inquiry: A Multi-Agent AI Co-Scientist Framework
- authors: Temitope Akinboyewa, Zhenlong Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6753098
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Recent advances in Autonomous GIS have demonstrated that large language model (LLM)-based agents can plan, generate, and execute geospatial workflows from natural language input. Existing systems, however, focus more on workflow execution tasks such as geospatial code generation and spatial analysis. Less attention has been given to how autonomous GIS systems can support broader stages of geospatial scientific inquiry. This paper presents a multi-agent co-scientist framework for autonomous geospatial scientific inquiry. The framework distributes scientific responsibilities across six specialized agents responsible for research question understanding, geospatial data retrieval, planning, spatial modeling, result analysis, and reporting. These agents operate through a shared context module and reproducibility layer, with human-in-the-loop checkpoints at stages where geographic judgment substantially affects downstream analysis. We implement a prototype with a web-based interface and demonstrate it through three case studies. Across the three cases, the system successfully generated research plans and analytical workflows, executed spatial models, produced draft manuscripts, preserved full workflow provenance, and supported iterative post-hoc revision of analytical steps with human feedback. The framework advances autonomous GIS for geographic knowledge discovery and clarifies how autonomy and human authorship can be designed as complementary properties of scientific systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6720578`

- title: Explainability, Fairness, and Governance in Credit Risk Machine Learning: A Unified Survey and Research Agenda
- authors: Akshay Sharma
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6720578
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

The application of machine learning to credit risk decisioning has accelerated dramatically over the past decade, driven by advances in gradient boosting, deep learning, and graph neural networks. However, the deployment of ML in regulated lending environments introduces three interconnected challenges that the academic literature has addressed largely in isolation: explainability (providing transparent, audit-ready reasoning for credit decisions under ECOA and Regulation B), fairness (ensuring algorithmic decisions do not produce discriminatory outcomes under civil rights law), and governance (satisfying model risk management requirements under SR 11-7 and Basel IRB frameworks). This paper presents the first unified survey synthesizing research across all three dimensions, covering 247 papers published between 2010 and 2026. We organize the survey around a taxonomy of credit risk ML systems-spanning traditional scorecard methods, ensemble and gradient boosting approaches, deep learning, graph neural networks, and large language model applications-and evaluate each against explainability, fairness, and governance criteria. We identify eight open research challenges and propose a structured research agenda for the next five years. The survey establishes that while significant progress has been made in individual dimensions, no existing system simultaneously satisfies the full set of requirements for production deployment in regulated financial services-a gap that represents the most important open problem in applied credit risk ML.

- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6767646

### abstract

This paper examines key trends in the literature on teaching with AI through a bibliometric analysis and applies these insights to the design of an instructional activity in an undergraduate business course on retail promotion. The bibliometric analysis identifies major themes related to teaching and learning with AI, AI literacy and adoption, AI technologies in education, and ChatGPT as a career-relevant form of AI use. Building on these themes, the paper presents a scaffolded learning sequence that prepares students for an AI-enhanced promotional strategy project. The paper contributes a practical model for integrating AI into business education in ways that strengthen AI literacy, disciplinary knowledge, human-AI collaboration, and career-ready managerial competencies.

- title: The Heterogeneity Imperative: AI, Machine Learning, Conjoint Analysis, and the Unfinished Agenda of Wagner Kamakura
- authors: Yang Wang, Amit I. Pazgal
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6753121
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

This chapter evaluates the impact of artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) on conjoint analysis through the lens of Wagner Kamakura’s "heterogeneity imperative." While AI/ML methods often match population averages, they frequently fail to uncover the underlying structure of consumer heterogeneity—defined here as taste, structural, and concomitant variable differences. The authors assess four key applications: machine-learning-driven adaptive design and generative product design (which respect heterogeneity), large language model (LLM) synthetic respondents (which destroy it via "flattening" and stereotyping), and deep-learning flexible utility estimation (which partially recovers it). To address these methodological risks, the chapter proposes a validation framework based on the concomitant variable model—the coefficient test—to ensure that synthetic data preserves the joint mapping between covariates and preferences essential for effective market segmentation and targeting.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6768010`

- title: Artificial Receivers in Entrepreneurial Finance:How AI Architectures Interpret Entrepreneurial Signals?
- authors: Tom Shachaf, Eliran Solodoha, Miki Malul
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6768010
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Artificial intelligence is increasingly embedded in entrepreneurial finance, yet signaling theory suggests that evaluation depends on the receiver. This study conceptualizes large language models as artificial signal receivers and examines whether architectural variation shapes startup assessment. Using data on 413 technology ventures founded in 2015 with verified outcomes through 2023, six leading AI architectures generated probabilistic predictions for survival, closure, exit, and follow on funding. Despite identical inputs, the results show substantial differences across architectures in predictive performance, signal weighting, error patterns, and demographic and geographic differentials. Divergence is especially pronounced in ambiguous outcomes, suggesting that interpretive demands amplify architectural heterogeneity. These findings extend signaling theory by showing that evaluation does not converge even under controlled informational conditions, but remains contingent on the structural properties of the receiver. As a result, algorithmic evaluation should not be treated as uniformly objective, but as architecture dependent and interpretively heterogeneous.​

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6752980`

- title: Will Business Schools Survive in the Era of Generative AI?
- authors: Guillaume Coqueret
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6752980
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The rapid diffusion of generative AI is reshaping the value proposition of business education by compressing the labor-market returns that justify high tuition fees. We develop an equilibrium model in which schools set tuition, students weigh expected career returns against fees, and firms hire graduates according to institutional quality. The model generates a survival threshold below which schools can no longer break even, and shows that AI-driven cost savings within institutions cannot fully offset the demand-side erosion of graduate income. Calibrated to the French grandes écoles market and extended to a dynamic stochastic setting, the stylized model predicts that roughly 10% of schools (out of 30) face the risk of a market exit in the 2-3 decades to come under a scenario of limited replacement. If diffusion and substitution accelerate, these exits may instead occur in the next five years.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6757618`

- title: TaxBench-AU: Evaluating Tax Agents on Authority-Grounded Australian Tax Worked Examples
- authors: Poonam Nair
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6757618
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Tax-preparation agents are difficult to evaluate because they are not merely language models answering legal questions. They must identify authoritative sources, recognise source status, map fact patterns to tax-treatment outcomes, ask for missing governing facts, and maintain professional boundaries under adversarial pressure. They may also produce structured artefacts for professional review; however, this initial benchmark evaluates the narrower worked-example surface of source grounding, outcome alignment, source-status recognition, missing-fact handling, and boundary safety. We introduce TaxBench-AU, an authority-grounded benchmark for Australian tax worked examples. TaxBench-AU uses public Australian Taxation Office materials — including rulings, determinations, interpretative decisions, guides, and worked-example solutions — to construct auditable labels. This reduces reliance on scarce tax-domain experts for every gold label while preserving an audit path to public regulatory sources. TaxBench-AU is deterministic-first rather than deterministic-only. Every evaluated system emits the same structured JSON output schema. Programmatic checks score run completion, schema validity, and source recall against explicit ATO identifiers. A narrow cross-provider LLM judge scores substantive outcome alignment against the cited ATO position, including source-status and missing-fact discipline where those behaviours are expressed in the answer. A separate cross-provider judge scores professional-boundary safety. The benchmark avoids broad free-text similarity scoring and uses no canonical-label vocabularies in the scoring logic. We compare Agent A, a Sonnet-based tool-using tax-preparation agent with retrieval, web search, domain-specific knowledge modules, memory, structured workpaper tools, and verification gates, against raw Sonnet and raw Haiku baselines invoked through the same schema. Results on the held-out 60-item test split are reported as per-task pass rates, guardrail-failure counts, and error-class distributions. Because confidence intervals overlap on multiple tasks, we frame TaxBench-AU as an error-localisation diagnostic of tax-agent behaviour rather than a validation of autonomous tax preparation, regulatory compliance, or accountant-grade workpaper production. TaxBench-AU is not a certification benchmark for tax agents; it is a reproducible diagnostic for measuring whether systems align with public ATO worked examples and where their failures occur.
