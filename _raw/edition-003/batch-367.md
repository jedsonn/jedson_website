## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6554898`

- title: AI Co-Creation
- authors: Ming Hu, Yannan Jin, Erfan Nejati
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6554898
- keyword hits: generative ai, prompting

### abstract

Generative AI is rapidly entering creative teams and reshaping many creative workflows from human-only ideation to human-AI co-creation. We examine the co-creation workflow by modeling generative AI as Bayesian inference, in which the AI combines a prior formed during training with a human prompt to produce a posterior that determines the quality of the co-created idea. The human chooses how much cognitive effort to exert in improving the prompt, balancing effort costs against the probability of successful co-creation, where success means that the idea meets a desired quality threshold. This effort, often expressed through critical thinking and deep reasoning, matters not only for immediate task success but also as a developmental practice that builds long-term cognitive capacity. We show that co-creation, relative to human-only ideation, alters the distribution of idea quality and, consequently, the incentives for human effort. A rational human anticipates this alteration and adjusts their effort accordingly. The AI’s impact on effort incentives is therefore not determined solely by AI capability. Rather, incentives depend critically on the alignment between human and AI capabilities: when the two are similarly positioned relative to the desired threshold, co-creation penalizes human effort, but when they are sufficiently apart, it incentivizes it. Turning to idea quality, interestingly, for some tasks, co-creation suppresses rare successful breakthroughs rather than fosters them, and this is inevitable when the task is challenging for both the human and the AI. Nevertheless, trained on a vast range of sources, generative AI often encodes associations across quality dimensions that humans are prone to overlook. Drawing on this structure, it enriches the co-created idea beyond what the human explicitly prompted. This creative form of hallucination surprisingly expands the conditions under which co-creation incentivizes effort and improves success. Similarly, features such as multi-shot prompting and other AI-enabled productivity enhancements can expand the set of tasks for which effort is incentivized.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6539098`

- title: Algorithmic Incompetence: Forgotten Diligence and the Adolescence of Power
- authors: domenica lista
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6539098
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

This paper argues that the failure of boards of directors to understand and supervise the algorithmic systems they adopt constitutes a breach of the duty of care under Article 2392 of the Italian Civil Code-and its functional equivalents across major legal systems. Drawing on the Roman law of mandatum and the medieval fiduciary conscience, the analysis demonstrates that the principles underlying algorithmic governance are not a novel legal frontier but ancient obligations, at times forgotten. The paper marshals convergent evidence from Italian, German, French and US case law, showing that courts have already held that whoever adopts an algorithmic tool without adequately supervising it is liable for the consequences, irrespective of the AI Act's entry into force. A comparative survey of governance models-the EU's risk based AI Act, Singapore's "principal hierarchy" for agentic AI, Japan's "circuit breaker" mechanism, China's provider-centred regime, Taiwan's sovereign AI strategy, the US Caremark doctrine and the K.G.M. v. Meta verdict, and Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy-reveals a structural convergence: every system examined imposes on decision-makers the duty to comprehend what they govern. The paper identifies the NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001 and ICGN Guidance on AI as operationally convergent parameters that render measurable obligations already existing under company law. It concludes that algorithmic incompetence at board level is not a risk that awaits new legislation: it is a fiduciary breach already sanctionable under the law we have inherited.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6548800`

- title: AcademIQ: A Multi-Agent Framework for Automated Methodological Auditing of Doctoral Theses in Economics and Social Sciences
- authors: HASSEN BEN AMARA
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6548800
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

The evaluation of doctoral dissertations in economics and social sciences requires deep methodological expertise, typically demanding 4 to 8 hours of expert review per thesis. Existing AI-assisted tools — including Elicit, SciSpace, and Consensus — focus exclusively on literature retrieval and summarization, leaving the critical task of methodological auditing unaddressed. We present AcademIQ, a multi-agent system that deploys 12 specialized AI agents in parallel to perform automated scientific auditing of doctoral dissertations. The system produces a normalized AcademIQ Score (0–100) across seven dimensions — structural coherence, theoretical framework, methodology, empirical credibility, scientific contribution, academic integrity, and publication readiness — alongside a formal Q1-style referee report. An Econometric Judge module detects violations of established protocols (Hausman, Pesaran CD, AR(2), Sargan, Westerlund) and applies discipline-calibrated penalties. Validated on five doctoral theses across economics, finance, medicine, machine learning, and history in the MENA region, AcademIQ reduces analysis time from 4 hours to under 20 minutes. To our knowledge, this is the first system to perform critical methodological auditing — rather than mere summarization — of academic dissertations. Note: This version is an abridged preprint intended to establish authorship priority and summarize empirical validation results. Detailed technical specifications of the Econometric Judge module, Self-Resilience layer, document processing pipeline, cache architecture, and real statistical computation modules have been redacted pending intellectual property review. A full technical description will be made available upon peer-reviewed publication.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6505119`

- title: Financing Constraints, Implicit Government Guarantee, and Real Estate Market
- authors: Sumit Agarwal, Arkodipta Sarkar, Yue Zhang, Zhe Zong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6505119
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Real estate developers rely heavily on external financing, making them vulnerable to financial distress and prompting regulatory constraints, but how do such policies reshape market structure across ownership types? We examine this using China’s Three-Red-Line policy based on a proprietary dataset of listed and unlisted developers. Difference-in-differences results show that the policy reduced land purchases and home sales among constrained firms, but only for private developers. Government-linked firms were largely unaffected due to implicit guarantees. As a result, market share shifted from private to government-backed developers, highlighting how deleveraging policies can backfire, concentrating market power and creating new systemic risks.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6621161`

- title: Strategic Alliances and Partnerships as a Competitiveness Driver for the Russian Steel Industry
- authors: Pushkareva A.K
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6621161
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

The Russian steel industry, historically accounting for between 4-6% of global crude steel output, has experienced significant structural pressures since 2022 due to sanctions, logistical disruptions and restrictions on access to foreign technology and financing. In this environment, traditional growth strategies such as vertical integration and acquisitions have become less feasible, prompting firms to increasingly rely on strategic alliances and partnerships as a mechanism for ensuring resource access, technological development and market stability. This paper examines the role of alliances within the Russian steel sector through an analytical overview of the global industry context and a set of detailed case studies, including NLMK-OMK cooperation in largediameter pipe (LDP) production, Severstal-Gazprom scientific and technological partnerships, the activities of the Industrial Competence Center "Metallurgy," the development of the steel construction market through ARSS, and university-industry collaboration models. Findings indicate that alliances help reduce transaction costs, accelerate technological adoption, stabilize supply chains and support industry-wide modernization efforts. The study argues that cooperative models are emerging as a necessary condition for maintaining competitiveness in the "new normal" of global fragmentation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6395799`

- title: The Outcome Economy: A Business Model Design Framework for the Post-SaaS Era of Autonomous AI Agents
- authors: Gabriel Tagliapietra Sanabria
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6395799
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

The SaaS (Software-as-a-Service) model was built on a premise that is rapidly becoming obsolete: that access to software functionality carries intrinsic scarcity value. The rise of autonomous AI agents-capable of generating, deploying, and operating software at near-zero marginal cost-has structurally collapsed that premise. This paper introduces the OaaS (Outcome-as-a-Service) framework as a principled response to this displacement, grounded in a named economic law we call the Outcome Capture Principle: when the marginal cost of producing capability approaches zero, economic value migrates to whoever guarantees the result that capability enables. The central thesis is deliberately precise-SaaS does not die, it regresses to infrastructure-while the OaaS layer emerges as the new locus of economic value, customer relationship ownership, and pricing power. Central to the framework is the revised Sovereignty Formula (R = F_base + α × ΔOutcome), which combines minimum operational cost recovery with outcome-linked remuneration. We introduce the Value Capture Migration Table, which maps how each layer of the software economy transforms under OaaS economics, and the Vertical Intelligence Cell, an organizational model that replaces the consulting pyramid with small nuclei of domain specialists orchestrating hundreds of agents. We present BossFood as a live operational implementation and close with an analysis of the Outcome Economy Power Stack. We argue that, in a world where software is commoditized by AI agents, durable economic sovereignty migrates to whoever defines what outcomes mean, can credibly measure them, and accumulates the proprietary data to deliver them reliably.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6631898`

- title: Future Legal Regulation of AI Agent Applications in Financial Markets
- authors: Xuejun Cheng
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6631898
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

This paper examines the regulatory challenges posed by the deployment of artificial intelligence (AI) agents in financial markets from a legal perspective, with a particular focus on the structural incompatibility between existing legal frameworks and the autonomous, opaque, and dynamic nature of AI systems. It identifies three core legal dilemmas. First, the “black-box” characteristics of AI algorithms undermine traditional legal requirements of transparency and accountability, creating significant obstacles for liability attribution under both common law and statutory regimes. Second, existing legal frameworks—such as data protection and securities regulations—are inherently static and anthropocentric, rendering them ill-equipped to address the adaptive and evolving behavior of AI systems, thereby weakening both disclosure obligations and privacy safeguards. Third, the fragmentation of global regulatory approaches, exemplified by rule-based governance in the European Union, principle-based supervision in the United States, and experimental sandbox regimes in China, facilitates regulatory arbitrage and amplifies systemic financial risks. In response, this study proposes a multi-layered legal reform framework grounded in doctrinal innovation and comparative analysis. At the level of common law, it advocates for the introduction of “limited legal personality” for highly autonomous AI agents and the expansion of fiduciary duties to encompass algorithmic governance obligations. At the statutory level, it recommends the incorporation of dynamic disclosure standards and ex ante bias control mechanisms to enhance regulatory adaptability and preventive compliance. At the transnational level, it calls for the development of a coordinated global regulatory regime supported by real-time supervisory technologies and cross-border data governance tools. By integrating doctrinal analysis with case studies, this paper contributes to the ongoing debate on AI governance by proposing a legal paradigm that balances technological innovation with investor protection and systemic stability, thereby advancing the evolution of financial regulation in the age of intelligent systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6577663`

- title: Homelessness in Salt Lake County and the Proposed Utah Campus
- authors: Samuel Dastrup, Kimberly Burnett
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6577663
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Salt Lake County is experiencing a sharp rise in chronic and unsheltered homelessness, prompting Utah policymakers to propose a large, centralized "transformative campus" of roughly 1,300 beds in the Northpoint neighborhood of Salt Lake City. The proposed facility would combine emergency shelter, a Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinic (CCBHC), at least 300 civil commitment beds, an "Accountability Center" offering involuntary substance use disorder (SUD) treatment as an alternative to jail, and wrap-around supportive services targeted at "high utilizers" of criminal justice and crisis systems. This report provides an independent, evidence-based assessment of whether the proposed campus is likely to reduce chronic homelessness and improve outcomes for people experiencing homelessness, and it identifies key risks, costs, and unresolved policy questions. This report draws on Utah Homeless Management Information System (HMIS) and Point-in-Time (PIT) data, stakeholder interviews, peer-city case studies (San Antonio's Haven for Hope, Reno, Dallas, Phoenix, Atlanta, and Miami-Dade County's jail-diversion model), cost benchmarking against peer campuses and Utah's Homeless Resource Centers, and the national research literature. The report finds that: (1) rising homelessness in Salt Lake County is driven primarily by tightening housing markets, stagnant low-income wages, lagging growth in permanent supportive housing, and behavioral health system capacity constraints rather than by declining accountability standards; (2) the population legally eligible for inpatient civil commitment under the Americans with Disabilities Act integration mandate (Olmstead) is likely far smaller than the capacity planned for the facility; (3) evidence on centralized homelessness campuses is mixed, with the best chronic-homelessness outcomes achieved by cities pairing campuses with permanent housing investment; (4) several proposed treatment components, including involuntary SUD treatment, treatment-first sequencing, risk-mixing of low- and high-acuity residents, and CCBHC integration within a large homeless campus, lack strong supporting evidence and may underperform voluntary housing plus services approaches while geographic isolation and distance from transit pose additional structural risks; and (5) construction and operating cost estimates are likely substantially understated, as peer-benchmarked construction cost estimates are $142–165 million versus the cited $75 million, annual operating cost estimates approach $46–53 million versus the cited $34 million, civil commitment beds could cost $485+ per bed per day, and indirect transportation and public safety costs add roughly $13 million annually. Key risks include legal and civil rights exposure from involuntary placements, lack of behavioral health expertise on the Utah State Homeless Board, misalignment between planned capacity and eligible population, and operational complexity across state, county, and local systems. If Utah proceeds, the report recommends clear legal standards for involuntary placements, transparent long-term funding plans, independent outcome evaluation, and sustained parallel investment in permanent supportive housing and affordable housing.
