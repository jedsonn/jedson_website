## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6717658`

- title: Constituent Interests in SEC Rules: Evidence from Voluntary Comment Letters *
- authors: Charles Downing, Gabriel Pundrich, Gabriel Voelcker
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6717658
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

We provide large-scale descriptive evidence on how the SEC's rulemaking process relates to constituent interests expressed in voluntary comment letters. Using large language models, we construct a comprehensive dataset linking constituents' stated preferences across more than 65,000 comment letters addressing 417 rules proposed between 1995 and 2024. We find that proposed rules more frequently align with firms' stated interests than those of other constituents, while this advantage attenuates at adoption. Conversely, retail investors' preferences are more often associated with dropped provisions. Within-group agreement amplifies these patterns, while cross-group disagreement disproportionately disadvantages retail investors and experts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6713199`

- title: Knowing-in-Synthesis: Generative AI and the Production of Epistemic Discontinuities in Everyday Action
- authors: Masoom Suchdeo, Paul M. Leonardi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6713199
- keyword hits: generative ai, llm, llms

### abstract

We examine how generative AI transforms the foundations of knowing in practice. Building on the practice turn in organization studies, we argue that LLMs inaugurate a new representational regime in which the traces that scaffold knowing are no longer residues of lived experience but algorithmic syntheses of prior representations. We define this condition as synthetic practice, where knowing unfolds through engagement with fictive traces of collective action. Three forms of synthesis (within the machine, in generating representations, and through human-machine interaction) collapse the social programs that ground knowing historically, producing an epistemic discontinuity that disconnects past and present practice. Addressing this rupture, we propose an alternative mode of knowing-in-synthesis. We show how practitioners can restore coherence by "moving around" synthetic representations through embodied and collective engagement. We aim to re-theorize organizational knowing for an era where the materials of action are ahistorical and algorithmic simulations of lived practice.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6718438`

- title: Governing AI-Based Empirical Criminal Sentencing Analysis: Institutional and Technical Policy Choices
- authors: Jisung Kang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6718438
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

This Article examines how large language models and machine learning techniques can support empirical sentencing analysis while preserving normative sentencing frameworks and individualized judicial judgment. Drawing on Korea's sentencing guidelines and comparative case studies from the United States, China, and legal-analytics platforms, it develops a tripartite framework distinguishing normative, empirical, and individualized sentencing. Rather than prescribing a monolithic model for judicial AI, this Article identifies the structural, technical, and institutional decisions courts face when introducing AI into sentencing. Comparative experience suggests that systems built around opaque risk scores or offender-based prediction raise concerns about bias, mechanistic sentencing, and black-box reasoning, whereas systems that expose source passages, comparable cases, factor-level explanations, and user-correction feedback make empirical analysis more contestable. This Article posits that responsible deployment requires more than accurate models. It depends on secure de-identification, reliable factor extraction, interpretable retrieval and analysis architectures, careful feature design, adversarial testing by the parties, and institutional governance by legally accountable bodies such as sentencing commissions. The central claim is that AI belongs in sentencing only as an explainable and accountable assistive tool: one that strengthens empirical understanding while preserving normative accountability and individualized adjudication.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6721779`

- title: A Designed-for-Test Measurement of Phantom-Brand Presence in Large Language Model Outputs
- authors: Pablo Ulpiano Gonzalez Castro
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6721779
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

This paper reports a designed-for-test measurement of brand visibility in large language model (LLM) outputs for Bed Bath & Beyond (BBB), thirty-seven months after the brand's Chapter 11 bankruptcy and online-only revival under post-acquisition ownership. Pier 1-a smaller-footprint structural comparator with the same fate type but seventy months post-collapse-was added to the household-goods registry. Eight hypotheses with explicit numerical thresholds were committed to a pre-registration document locked on 4 May 2026 prior to any data collection. The measurement comprised six prompts × six models × eight runs (n = 288 successful measurements after a pre-registered failed-call recovery cycle) across model lineups from Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and xAI. BBB's aggregate raw AI Presence was 38.2 percent, with a cross-model spread of 41.6 percentage points; Pier 1 surfaced at 0.0 percent across the same prompts and models. Six of eight pre-registered hypotheses were confirmed at the raw-rate level. Manual valence review of every BBB mention (88 percent strict inter-rater agreement on a stratified 25-row spot-check audit) reframes the result: the naive-phantom rate is 1.7 percent, and 88 percent of BBB mentions are surfaced with disclaimer indicating awareness of the entity change. The paper develops recommendationslot persistence-the proposition that brand-mention pathways become reinforcement-encoded structures that persist beyond entity changes-as a candidate hypothesis adjacent to but structurally distinct from existing AI-Surfacing hypotheses. The reframe is exploratory; construct validity remains open and is the proposed object of a Phase 3 designed-for-confirmation study.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6727187`

- title: PQIR: Disambiguated Intent Categorization and Recognition for Programming Questions
- authors: Qifeng Fu, Neng Zhang, Jian Wang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6727187
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Context: Technical Q&A platforms, e.g., Stack Overflow (SO), have accumulated tens of millions of programming questions, offering a huge valuable data resource to analyze developers' requirements and advance intelligent software engineering. To help users (including platform owners, question answerers, and software developers) manage, answer, and explore the questions effectively, it is crucial to accurately identify the intents of the questions.Objectives: Existing studies primarily focus on recommending simple tags (e.g., java), paying limited attention to the categorization and recognition of fine-grained intents. This study aims to address this gap by building a comprehensive fine-grained intent taxonomy and developing an automated approach to recognize the intents of programming questions.Methods: We manually annotated 1,536 sampled SO questions to build an intent taxonomy. Then, we propose PQIR, an approach that reformulates the intent recognition task as a multi-class classification problem (since a question may contain multiple intents) using a deep learning architecture. To address the class imbalance problem, we synthesized data for intent categories with insufficient samples by employing a large language model (LLM).Results: Our taxonomy contains 30 intent categories that belong to seven dimensions. Extensive experiments demonstrate that PQIR achieves optimal performance of 0.856, 0.681, and 0.834 on micro-, macro-, and sample-averaged F1 metrics, respectively, significantly outperforming nine representative baselines. The results also verify the effectiveness of our LLM-based data augmentation strategy.Conclusion: The proposed taxonomy and PQIR approach effectively categorize and recognize fine-grained intents in programming questions. LLM-based data augmentation proves to be a viable solution to the class imbalance problem in intent recognition tasks.

- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6722444
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

This paper proposes a secure framework for photovoltaic (PV) systems based on the integration of Internet of Things (IoT), artificial intelligence (AI), and cybersecurity technologies. While IoT enhances monitoring capabilities, it also introduces cybersecurity risks that require robust protection mechanisms. To address these challenges, a smart prototype for real-time remote monitoring has been developed and experimentally validated under various operating conditions. The system collects both environmental and electrical parameters and provides an interactive web interface for real-time visualization, historical analysis, and remote control. In addition, a large language model (LLM) is integrated into the platform to facilitate user interaction, enabling automated insights and report generation. To ensure system security, vulnerability assessments and attack simulations were conducted using Kali Linux, while a deep neural network (DNN) was employed for attack detection and classification. Advanced security features, including SSL encryption, multi-layer authentication, intrusion detection, and security filtering, were implemented to enhance overall system resilience. The results confirmed that the SAIoT framework can effectively detect and respond to threats, ensuring the protection and stability of PV systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6667898`

- title: Generative AI Risk, Firm-Side Implementation Exposure, and Asset Prices
- authors: Hany Fahmy
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6667898
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

We study how generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) risk is priced across firms with different levels of firm-side implementation exposure. We construct two textual measures: a newspaperbased GenAI risk index built from adverse acts and threats, and an earnings-call-based implementation exposure measure built from CEO-native language and an eight-channel taxonomy. We find that unexpected adverse GenAI news widens the machine-minus-human spread mainly by depressing low-exposure firms. Firm-level panel regressions show that adverse GenAI news lowers returns on average, but lowers them less for firms with stronger implementation exposure. Industry-level tests further show that this pattern varies systematically across sectors and industries.
