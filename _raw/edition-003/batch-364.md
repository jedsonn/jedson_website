### abstract

Since the mid-20th century, the strategic foreign policy approaches of the Netherlands, Belgium, and Luxembourg have been similar, consisting in active participation in European integration and a rejection of neutrality in favor of reliance on the United States and NATO. Since the late 1980s, signs of diverging paths emerged: Belgium and Luxembourg advocate deeper European integration, orienting toward France and Germany, while the Netherlands shows "greater reluctance and ambivalence" due to EU expansion and becoming a net contributor, unlike net-recipient Belgium and Luxembourg. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6293838`

- title: From Monitoring to Authorization: The Structural Shift Emerging in Agentic AI Governance
- authors: Edward Meyman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6293838
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

As AI systems transition from generating content to executing real-world side effects—writing to databases, initiating transactions, modifying infrastructure—the governance problem shifts from monitoring outputs to authorizing actions. Monitoring architectures document what occurred; they do not determine whether an action was permitted before execution. This paper identifies a structural ambiguity emerging across regulatory consultations: the under-specification of the enforcement primitive at the boundary where AI intent becomes effect. Distinguishing observability from authorization, the paper defines the architectural properties of an authorization boundary: non-bypassable gating, deterministic verdict semantics, fail-closed defaults, and policy-state binding. It argues that traditional compliance mechanisms—deterrence, accountability, escalation, and documentation—are structurally mismatched to autonomous systems operating at machine speed. Drawing on cross-jurisdiction analysis, the paper proposes a Minimum Authorization Boundary Contract (MABC) for evidence-grade governance in agentic AI deployments. The argument is structural rather than empirical: the requirements follow from the engineering properties of autonomous systems and the regulatory trajectory of high-impact AI. Monitoring remains necessary, but enforcement must be layered beneath observability to produce architectures capable of both preventing unauthorized actions and documenting what occurred.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6300558`

- title: Execution-Time Authorization for AI Agents: A Formal Framework for Deterministic Governance Boundaries
- authors: Edward Meyman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6300558
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

Autonomous AI agents increasingly execute actions that produce irreversible changes in real-world systems. In regulated domains—including healthcare, finance, defense, and critical infrastructure—the distinction between what an agent is technically capable of doing and what it is authorized to do under governing policy becomes a material governance boundary. Existing AI governance approaches—alignment techniques, guardrails, identity and access management, observability tooling, and orchestration frameworks—address important but distinct problems. None provide deterministic, pre-execution authorization with independently verifiable decision evidence. This paper formalizes execution-time authorization as a distinct architectural category within AI governance. Execution-time authorization is defined as a deterministic, fail-closed enforcement layer that evaluates canonicalized action instances against versioned policy and governed system state prior to execution, producing a replayable authorization verdict and an immutable, provenance-complete decision artifact. The paper makes five contributions. First, it provides a canonical definition specifying the necessary and jointly defining properties of execution-time authorization. Second, it presents a formal mathematical model of the authorization function with determinism, canonicalization, and replay invariants. Third, it establishes testable conformance criteria that distinguish conforming execution-time authorization systems from adjacent governance categories. Fourth, it offers a structured architectural analysis differentiating authorization from guardrails, alignment, identity management, observability, and orchestration. Fifth, it outlines deployment patterns for integrating execution-time authorization within enterprise AI infrastructure. Execution-time authorization is established as a foundational primitive for enforcement-grade AI governance, providing pre-execution, deterministic, and independently verifiable decision control for autonomous systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6299461`

- title: Cross-Agent Governance Alignment (CAGA): A Zero-Knowledge Formalization for Cross-Organizational AI Governance
- authors: Edward Meyman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6299461
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

As autonomous AI agents increasingly coordinate across institutional boundaries in regulated industries, existing governance frameworks prove insufficient. Current models assume either a single organizational authority or full policy transparency between participants—assumptions that do not hold in multi-stakeholder environments where governance constraints encode proprietary risk tolerances, regulatory interpretations, and competitive strategy. This paper formalizes the Cross-Agent Governance Alignment (CAGA) problem: verifying mutual governance compatibility between agents operating under distinct policy regimes without disclosing those policies. We define governance domains and cross-domain interactions, introduce the governance alignment predicate Φ(Dᵢ, Dⱼ, τ), and formalize the problem under an honest-but-curious threat model appropriate for regulated settings. We identify the essential properties any valid solution must satisfy, including alignment correctness, policy confidentiality, governance determinism, evidentiary sufficiency, composable security, and forward secrecy of governance state. We then demonstrate that CAGA is irreducible to existing paradigms, including agent communication protocols, federated learning, secure multi-party computation, single-organization governance architectures, and blockchain-based transparency systems. CAGA is a zero-knowledge coordination problem at the intersection of AI governance, cryptographic protocol design, and multi-agent systems. By precisely defining the problem space and evaluation criteria, this work establishes the foundation for rigorous protocol development while deliberately stopping short of implementation disclosure. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6234218`

- title: K-Lines: A Cognitively-Grounded Four-Memory Architecture for Persistent Conversational AI
- authors: Robert Kende
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6234218
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

We present K-Lines, a cognitively-grounded architecture for persistent conversational AI that organizes long-term memory into four complementary types-episodic, semantic, procedural, and reflective-drawing on Minsky's K-line theory, Tulving's memory taxonomy, Anderson's declarative-procedural distinction, and research on sleep-dependent memory consolidation. The central theoretical claim is that the persistent memory problem in conversational AI is not merely an engineering challenge but a cognitive architecture problem-one that Minsky anticipated decades ago, yet that the current wave of agentic AI systems largely reinvents without acknowledging. Effective long-term memory requires the same functional differentiation that cognitive science has identified in biological memory systems. Episodic memory preserves the temporal fabric of conversational interactions. Semantic memory abstracts stable knowledge as structured entities and facts. Procedural memory, operationalized as K-line templates, captures reusable response strategies through the mechanism Minsky described: partial activation records of successful cognitive episodes. Reflective memory synthesizes higher-order patterns from accumulated knowledge. A consolidation pipeline implements computational analogs of sleep-dependent memory consolidation, transforming labile episodic traces into stable semantic representations and synthesizing reflective insights-mirroring the hippocampal-to-neocortical transfer observed in biological systems. All memory artifacts are stored as human-readable files, preserving the interpretability that opaque embedding-based approaches sacrifice. In an empirical evaluation across eight experiments with synthetic domains, the system achieves 65% average cross-session fact recall-a +53 percentage-point improvement over the 12% context-window-only baseline-with per-fixture recall ranging from 29% to 91% depending on domain difficulty. Critically, the system maintains 100% distractor rejection (versus 34% baseline), never fabricating facts that were not taught, and achieves response quality that outperforms both the context-window baseline and no-retrieval condition. Remaining recall gaps are attributable to extraction model capacity rather than architectural limitations, suggesting a clear scaling path.
