## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7040599`

- title: Educational AI at the Boundary of High Risk: Estonia’s AI Leap as a Stress Test for the EU AI Act and GDPR
- authors: Pablo Martínez-Ramil
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7040599
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Educational Artificial Intelligence (AI) does not become high-risk under the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act merely because it is used in schools. Its classification depends on the function it performs within the educational process: whether it supports learning formatively, or whether it evaluates learning outcomes in ways that affect a student’s educational trajectory. This distinction is especially unstable in adaptive generative AI systems, where the same interactional features that make a tool pedagogically useful may, if repurposed or institutionally relied upon, move it towards the assessment logic of Annex III, point 3(b). This paper uses Estonia’s AI Leap initiative as an early test case for that boundary. It develops four claims. First, the boundary between formative tutoring and high-risk assessment is conditional and dynamic, rather than inherent in the technology itself. Second, the profiling rule in Article 6(3) is best understood as an anti-derogation trigger: it does not independently bring a system within Annex III, but once an educational AI system falls within Annex III, profiling prevents reliance on the derogation from high-risk classification. Third, the initiative exposes a value-chain asymmetry characteristic of public-sector educational deployments built on third-party general-purpose AI models, where downstream actors may acquire compliance responsibilities without full technical control. Fourth, Estonia’s section 20¹ amendment provides an unusually concrete national-law basis for educational AI processing, but it cannot by itself resolve the data-protection problems created by open-input generative systems, especially the incidental capture of special-category and third-party data. The paper concludes that the initiative’s regulatory significance lies less in its present classification than in the governance conditions needed to prevent functional drift and mitigate the data-protection risks created by open-ended student inputs.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7072890`

- title: The ”Digital Filial Piety” Paradox: How Generative AI is Reshaping Intergenerational Communication and Caregiving Narratives in Chinese Social Media
- authors: Dr.  Ken Ip, Tin  Hang Michael Lai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7072890
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid advancement of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) presents profound challenges to traditional social and ethical structures, particularly in societies governed by deeply ingrained cultural norms. This study investigates the emerging phenomenon of "Digital Filial Piety," defined as the use of GenAI to mediate, simulate, or substitute intergenerational communication and caregiving obligations in the Chinese context. Drawing on the Confucian concept of xiào (filial piety), we examine how GenAI-powered applications, including AI companions, deepfake video greetings, and automated advice systems, are adopted by younger generations to fulfill perceived filial duties toward elderly parents and grandparents. Employing a sequential explanatory mixed-methods design, we analyzed 1,000 social media posts from Weibo and Douyin and conducted in-depth interviews with 30 participants across two generations. Results reveal a paradoxical dynamic: while GenAI enables efficient, socially validated performances of filial care, it simultaneously introduces ethical tension and emotional ambivalence, particularly among elderly recipients. Video-based and entertainment-framed AI content garnered the highest engagement, whereas attempts to simulate intimate emotional communication elicited discomfort. This study formalizes the concept of Digital Filial Piety, highlighting the interplay between technological performance, moral responsibility, and social perception. The findings contribute to communication and marketing scholarship, offering a culturally grounded framework for understanding AI-mediated intergenerational interactions and the ethical negotiation of digitally performed care.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7047058`

- title: Geopolitics Meets AI War: Stock Market Evidence from China
- authors: Lin William Cong, Siguang Li, Fan Wang, Yi Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7047058
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

We develop a model in which heterogeneous firms-state-owned enterprises (SOEs) and privately owned enterprises (POEs)-choose among U.S. high-tech inputs, U.S. low-tech inputs, and domestic inputs. High-tech imports enable AI innovation through learning, but require costly external financing. Geopolitical risk (GPR) raises the effective cost of U.S.-origin inputs, while financially constrained POEs face tighter collateral constraints, leading them to reallocate more sharply away from AI-embedded imports than SOEs. Using a continuous difference-indifferences design around the 2018 U.S.-China trade war and LLM-based measures of firm-level geopolitical exposure, we show that higher geopolitical risk reduces high-tech imports, AI innovation, product diversification, and firm fundamentals, with significantly larger effects among POEs. Over the long run, financial markets also price the geopolitical risk: higher GPR exposure predicts positive abnormal returns, reduced debt issuance, and negative cumulative returns around tariff events. The differential financial-friction channel explains why POEs bear disproportionately larger losses, revealing AI learning through imports as a novel mechanism linking geopolitical shocks to real outcomes and asset prices.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.06802v1`

- title: A Multi-Analyst LLM Pipeline for Auditable Rule Discovery Across 68 Public Physiological Corpora
- authors: Dovy Paukstys
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06802v1
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Open physiological corpora are heterogeneous: they use different sensors, labels, sampling rates, recording settings, and clinical endpoints. They can support detector design, but they do not directly specify which detector rules should be built for a new contactless monitoring platform. We report a controlled four-analyst large-language-model (LLM) workflow for converting 68 public physiological corpora, screened for commercial-use compatibility, into an auditable library of candidate rule shapes for prospective validation. Four independent commercial LLM families read the corpus documentation under a controlled prompt and produced 695 candidate rule markers (top-markers). Deduplication retained 649 rule records; a threshold-bounds audit then flagged 51 sanity violations for clamping or curator review. Cross-corpus consolidation produced 436 unique rule shapes. Gate-tagging against two hard invariants, native target-hardware channel availability and no multi-night per-patient personalization, identified 94 build-now detector components across four detector-family buckets. The pipeline does not produce a validated clinical detector. It produces an auditable engineering cascade in which analyst disagreement, threshold checks, curator review, and automated continuous-integration (CI) checks route literature-derived rules toward prospective hardware validation.

- title: AgoraSim: A Hybrid Agent-Based Modeling Framework
- authors: Chung-Chi Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05999v1
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

LLM-agent simulations make natural-language social scenarios easy to instantiate, but their outputs can be overread as predictions and are often difficult to compare with explicit social dynamics. We present AgoraSim, a hybrid agent-based modeling framework for scenario-oriented social reaction analysis. AgoraSim resolves textual or multimodal artifacts into editable ABM configurations, runs ratio-controlled populations that mix LLM, vision-language, custom-endpoint, random, and classical agents, and compares the same scenario against matched classical reference dynamics. All agents emit a shared structured decision object, enabling common action spaces, interaction protocols, metrics, and audit records. Exposed through a local UI, Python SDK/CLI, and REST API, AgoraSim helps users inspect scenario trajectories, compare modeling assumptions, and identify cases that warrant empirical validation.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.06611v1`

- title: Audio Sentiment Analysis via Distillation and Cross-Modal Integration of Generated Multilingual Transcripts
- authors: Andrei-George Durdun, Victor Constantinescu, Radu Tudor Ionescu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06611v1
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Automatically recognizing the sentiment, positive or negative, from speech is a challenging task, requiring both the analysis of vocal inflections and the interpretation of uttered words. Recent solutions rely on audio foundation models to solve the task, but it remains unclear if such models can take all aspects into account. To this end, we propose a multimodal solution that integrates audio and text information via cross-modal transformers, where text transcripts are automatically generated via an automatic speech recognition (ASR) tool. Moreover, we create multiple text modalities by automatically translating the transcripts into multiple languages via machine translation tools. Audio and multilingual text features are combined via a cascaded architecture comprising cross-modal transformer blocks that integrate modalities one by one. We further distill knowledge from the multimodal model, called teacher, into a unimodal (audio only) model, called student. We conduct experiments on a large-scale dataset, demonstrating that the automatically generated textual information can bring significant performance boosts in multimodal sentiment polarity classification. Our ablation study confirms that both automatic transcripts and automatic translations are helpful. Moreover, we show that the audio-only model can be enhanced via distillation, boosting performance without any computational overhead during inference. To reproduce the reported results, we publicly release our code at https://github.com/andreidurdun/cross-modal-audio-sentiment.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.05790v1`

- title: Controlling Tool Use with Heading-Specific Activation Steering
- authors: Yuqi Chen, Vincent Siu, Yang Liu, Dawn Song, Chenguang Wang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05790v1
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Tool-augmented large language models extend their capabilities beyond parametric knowledge through external tools, but tend to invoke them unnecessarily. We investigate whether tool-use decisions have any stable internal representation that can be extracted and manipulated, a question that is non-trivial given that tools exist entirely in context at inference time and have no direct encoding in model weights. We show that steering vectors extracted from heading-anchors positions exert bidirectional causal control over tool-invocation behavior across five open-source models and three domains, suppressing unnecessary tool use most effectively in domains where parametric reasoning suffices. However, geometric analysis reveals that this causal effectiveness does not correspond to clean linear structure: tool-invocation steps exhibit diffuse, bimodal alignment with the suppression vector rather than the consistent negative alignment a linear encoding account would predict, and different tool types recruit largely distinct internal signatures with low cross-tool feature overlap. We hypothesize these geometric properties are indicative of the non-parametric nature of tools, and distinguish tool-use steering vectors from those extracted for parametrically grounded concepts. The relationship between this geometric irregularity and the observed causal effectiveness remains an open question.
