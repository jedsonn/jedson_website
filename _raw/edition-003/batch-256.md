## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6274418`

- title: The Value Gap in Educational Technology: Perceived Learning, Experienced Capability, and the Political Economy of AI-Driven Education
- authors: Kate Busby
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6274418
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Educational technology (EdTech) firms have achieved extraordinary market valuations despite limited evidence of durable learning outcomes at scale. This paper introduces a conceptual framework to explain this paradox: a structural gap between perceived learning value (how learning feels) and experienced learning value (demonstrable capability development). Drawing on research in cognitive psychology, deliberate practice, behavioral economics, and critical studies of educational technology, we argue that commercial incentives systematically favor optimization of perceived value over experienced value. Engagement-based metrics, venture capital growth logics, and AI-driven instructional systems amplify this distortion. We further examine how generative AI intensifies the substitution of fluency for mastery by lowering the cognitive cost of task completion. Finally, we propose a research and design agenda focused on transfer, productive failure, identity transformation, and outcome-aligned revenue models. The long-term viability of EdTech may depend on whether platforms can realign incentives around demonstrable capability rather than engagement performance. Author Note This paper proposes a conceptual framework explaining the persistent divergence between EdTech market valuations and measurable learning outcomes. Drawing from cognitive science, behavioral economics, and critical education studies, it introduces the Perceived–Experienced Value Gap and the Valuation–Learning Decoupling Effect (VLDE). The framework is particularly timely given the rapid integration of generative AI into instructional environments. The paper is intended to contribute to interdisciplinary discussions across education, technology policy, and innovation economics.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6456998`

- title: Institutional Silence and Legaltech in Japan: A Comparative Inquiry Into Access to Justice and the Practice of Law
- authors: Ichiro Kobayashi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6456998
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This article examines Japan's regulatory response to LegalTech and generative AI through the concept of institutional silence-a form of structured inaction that preserves symbolic legal authority while deferring structural reform. Unlike the US and Germany, where LegalTech has prompted litigation and regulatory change, Japanese authorities have relied on modest administrative guidance, keeping LegalTech adoption away from legally sensitive domains and leaving access-to-justice issues largely unaddressed. The article argues that this silence reflects a longterm institutional strategy shaped over time. By incorporating adjacent professions, such as judicial scriveners, into a lawyer-centred professional hierarchy, Japan has contained access-to-justice problems while eliminating challenges to the exclusive authority of licensed attorneys. Generative AI disrupts this equilibrium because AI tools function outside professional frameworks, weakening institutional silence as a governance strategy. Institutional silence can stabilise legal order in the short term, but may become unsustainable in the face of transformative technological change.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6464098`

- title: Hallucinated Authority: AI Citations as Reckless Misrepresentation
- authors: Cheng-chi (Kirin) Chang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6464098
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

When courts sanctioned the attorneys in Mata v. Avianca for submitting AI-generated citations to nonexistent cases, they reached for the vocabulary of negligence. That characterization is wrong. Large language models do not forget cases the way attorneys do; they generate citations that look real but correspond to nothing in any reporter, and they do so at documented rates of 58% to 88% on legal queries, a fact publicly known in peer-reviewed research before most of the incidents now filling the reporters. An attorney who submits AI output without verification does not merely fail a diligence standard. She submits a representation to a court while consciously declining to establish any basis for its accuracy, satisfying the elements of reckless misrepresentation under Restatement (Second) of Torts § 526(b)–(c). The cases that have followed Mata reach instinctively for language that exceeds negligence, calling citations “bogus” and monetary sanctions “insufficient deterrents,” and some have begun to reach the word “reckless” without grounding that intuition in a cross-regime doctrinal framework. This Article supplies that framework. This Article makes three original contributions. First, it introduces the concept of “structurally predictable error” to explain why AI hallucination is categorically different from human memory failure: the error follows from the tool's architecture rather than from individual cognitive lapse, its rate is publicly documented, and a reasonable practitioner in the field either knows or should know it before using the tool. Second, it builds the doctrinal case for reckless misrepresentation by connecting § 526(b)–(c) to the philosophy of AI output, arguing that an attorney who relies on a tool architecturally indifferent to the distinction between true and false adopts that indifference as her own representational posture toward the court. Third, it proposes a graduated three-tier accountability framework that calibrates sanctions to actual culpability, preserving space for good-faith verification efforts and the access-to-justice benefits that AI tools offer to under-resourced practitioners, while refusing to treat willful blindness toward a known category of fabrication as mere carelessness.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6584984`

- title: From Assistance to Cognition: How Generative AI Reshapes Self-Regulated Learning in Higher Education
- authors: Pei Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6584984
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) is rapidly transforming higher education, yet the cognitive and metacognitive mechanisms through which it influences learning outcomes remain insufficiently theorised and empirically tested. Drawing on Self-Regulated Learning (SRL) theory and Cognitive Load Theory (CLT), this study develops and tests a unified process model in which GenAI functions as a metacognitive scaffold that reshapes learning through cognitive resource reallocation. A pre-registered quasi-experimental design was implemented with 386 university students over a 16-week semester, comparing a GenAI-assisted learning condition with a traditional online-learning control group. Structural equation modelling (SEM) with bootstrapped mediation analysis revealed that GenAI use significantly reduced extraneous cognitive load and enhanced metacognitive monitoring, which in turn promoted deep learning strategy adoption and ultimately improved academic achievement. The direct effect of GenAI use on achievement was non-significant, indicating a fully mediated pathway. These findings challenge the prevailing view of AI as a tool of cognitive offloading and instead position it as a metacognitive scaffold that enables deeper self-regulated learning. By integrating SRL and CLT within a large-sample, process-based empirical framework, this study provides novel theoretical and methodological contributions to the emerging field of AI in education and offers actionable implications for the design of AI-integrated pedagogies in higher education.

- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6479920
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Central bank communications, particularly FOMC statements and press conferences, play a crucial role in shaping financial market expectations. Using large language models to quantify central bank content, this paper demonstrates how sentiment aligns with traditional market-based monetary policy measures and quantifies the evolving nature of Federal Reserve communications, particularly the press conference. We show that the press conference correlates with future policy to a greater extent than other communications. While the FOMC statement coarsely signals the current stance of policy, the press conference fine-tunes the message, which helps market participants revise their expectations about future policy.

- title: When Do AI and Emerging Technologies Shape Climate Investment?
- authors: Sean S. Cao, Junyoung Park, Ling Xue, Keer Yang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6480858
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

Firms apply AI and emerging technologies to address climate challenges, known as ClimateTech. We use large language model contextual functions to analyze firm press releases, identify ClimateTech investment projects, and construct a taxonomy. ClimateTech investment concentrates in two categories: multipurpose infrastructure and specialized environmental technologies. Infrastructure technologies generate financial returns but show no measurable environmental impact, while specialized environmental technologies generate neither on average. We investigate why managers invest in specialized environmental technologies and present evidence that the value of emerging technologies is not warranted but depends on how they are deployed in relation to the core business.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6610238`

- title: The Shape of Professional Service Firms under Generative AI: Pinched Waists, Bulging Middles, and the Pipeline Floor
- authors: Kimon Drakopoulos, Ramandeep S. Randhawa, Konstantinos Takos
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6610238
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Professional service firms typically are organized as leveraged hierarchies where a narrow partnership is supported by progressively larger cohorts of junior professionals. The advent of generative AI is leading to significant work compression, affecting junior-intensive tasks disproportionately, suggesting that hierarchies should flatten throughout. We show this intuition holds only partially and fails precisely where it matters most for long-run firm structure. The key mechanism is the interaction between three constraints: the capacity constraint that requires enough hours at each level to deliver projects, the pipeline constraint that requires enough personnel to replenish partners through internal promotion, and the span-of-control constraint that requires enough supervisory and coaching capacity at each level to develop the level below. We develop a continuous-seniority personnel-flow model with endogenous partnership, and a discrete specialization that incorporates supervision explicitly, to demonstrate that generative AI causes two distinct non-pyramidal structures: a pinched-waist structure when differential AI compression inverts the task profile, thinning the middle relative to both the base and apex; and a bulging-middle structure when mid-level task loads resist compression while junior and senior loads collapse. Our framework applies most cleanly to firms whose economics rest on project-based delivery, promotion-from-within, and low capital intensity, that is, the advisory and assurance archetypes of modern professional services; we discuss the boundaries of the framework with respect to implementation-heavy and managed-services archetypes in which capital substitutes for labor and the pipeline constraint is partially bypassed.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6402098`

- title: Defensive AI When Safety Alignment Creates Tort Liability for Medical Information Omission
- authors: David Gringras
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6402098
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

When asked about symptoms by a layperson, most frontier AI models will hedge; when asked about identical symptoms by a physician, the same models will provide the full clinical picture. This Article identifies a paradox that has gone unexamined in the legal literature hitherto: the measures companies adopt to reduce commission-based tort exposure (LLMs giving bad advice) may be generating a distinct and less defensible form of omission-based exposure (LLMs identifying a life-threatening condition and suppressing the finding). Drawing on IatroBench, a pre-registered empirical study I authored, I argue that this scenario is captured by Restatement (Second) of Torts §323 voluntary undertaking doctrine; that Section 230 almost certainly does not shield AIgenerated content; and that Garcia v. Character Technologies (M.D. Fla. 2025) has already permitted product liability claims against a 'chatbot' to proceed. I term the resulting problem defensive AI: a self-inflicted liability cost generated by the very safety measures designed to avoid liability. The rational exit is also the ethical one: provide accurate medical information with appropriate context, which is what these systems already do when physicians are asking.
