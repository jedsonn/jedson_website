## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6858038`

- title: Generative AI, Job Transition and The Future of Work in Nigeria : Towards a Decent Work Response
- authors: Adedoyin Adebayo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6858038
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This article examines the implications of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) for work, job transition and labour governance in Nigeria. It argues that the central challenge posed by GenAI is not immediate mass unemployment, but the gradual reorganisation of tasks, managerial authority, workplace surveillance and skill requirements in ways that may either strengthen or weaken decent work. Drawing on the normative positions of the International Labour Organization, UNESCO, the Council of Europe and the African Union, the article shows that contemporary regulatory thinking increasingly treats AI as a governance issue requiring human oversight, non-discrimination, transparency, accountability and support for worker transition. It then analyses Nigeria’s legal and policy architecture, including the Constitution, the Labour Act, the National Industrial Court’s labour jurisdiction, the Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023, the National Artificial Intelligence Strategy 2025, the National Employment Policy 2025, the National Digital Economy Policy and Strategy 2020–2030 and the Nigeria Startup Act 2022. The article contends that while Nigeria possesses important normative and policy building blocks, its framework remains fragmented and does not yet amount to a dedicated workplace AI governance regime. It identifies key risks for Nigerian workers, including algorithmic bias, intrusive monitoring, opaque decision-making, skill exclusion and the danger that AI may modernise productivity while preserving informality. It concludes by proposing a practical decent work agenda built around human review of significant AI-assisted employment decisions, workplace transparency, data governance, social dialogue, structured reskilling and sector-specific guidance. Ultimately, it argues that the future of work in Nigeria should be shaped by law, policy and a deliberate commitment to decent work and social justice.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6957523`

- title: Anthropomorphism in the Age of Generative AI: ​A Narrative Review of Psychological Impacts on K-12 Students
- authors: Danu Saifulloh Rahmadhani, Arum Fadhilah, Netti Herawati
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6957523
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid adoption of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) in K-12 educational settings has raised urgent questions regarding its psychological impacts on children and adolescents, particularly the phenomenon of anthropomorphism—the tendency to attribute human characteristics, intentions, and emotions to non-human entities. This narrative review aims to map how anthropomorphism is understood and conceptualized in the K-12 educational AI research literature, and to identify whether the meaning of this construct has evolved alongside technological developments in AI. Grounded in the ELIZA Effect framework and Piaget's theory of cognitive development, a total of 21 studies published between 2021 and 2026 were retrieved from the Scopus database and analyzed thematically and temporally. Findings identify three anthropomorphism mechanisms that operate in distinctive ways: Synthetic Empathy, Perceived Affective Alignment (PAA), and Para-social Interaction. The manifestations of these three mechanisms evolve qualitatively across Piagetian developmental stages, from pure cognitive animism in the preoperational stage, to reliance on sensory cues in the concrete operational stage, to the use of anthropomorphism as an instrument of abstract reasoning and simultaneously as an object of critique in the formal operational stage. Temporal mapping reveals a fundamental shift in the nature of identified risks: from ontological-existential concerns in 2021–2022 to epistemic-ethical risks such as information hallucination, data privacy, and emotional dependency in 2025–2026. An inverted U-shaped dose-response pattern was also identified, in which moderate levels of anthropomorphism prove most optimal for student acceptance and trust. This review proposes the Developmentally-Calibrated CASA framework and affirms the urgency of AI literacy that is sensitive to children's developmental stages as a mitigation strategy that can be operationalized in pedagogical contexts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6966058`

- title: Guidance, Ambiguity, and Responsible Generative AI Use in Accounting Education: Mixed-Methods Evidence from Digital Discourse and Indonesian-Dutch Student Surveys
- authors: Gunawan Wibisono
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6966058
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) creates a dual challenge for accounting education: it can support learning while also increasing opportunities for academic misuse. This study develops and tests a model explaining responsible GenAI use intention and AI misuse intention among accounting-related students. Study 1 analyzes YouTube videos, comments, replies, and available transcripts to identify discourse themes around GenAI as a learning assistant, cheating shortcut, policy ambiguity, detection anxiety, ethical reasoning, assessment fairness, professional relevance, and accounting-specific usefulness. These themes inform construct development for Study 2. Study 2 surveys 437 accounting-related students in Indonesia and the Netherlands and analyzes the data using partial least squares structural equation modeling. The results show that institutional AI guidance reduces AI policy ambiguity and strengthens ethical reasoning. Policy ambiguity increases misuse normalization, which in turn increases AI misuse intention. Detection risk reduces misuse intention. Responsible use intention is positively predicted by legitimate AI learning use, assessment fairness, professional relevance, and ethical reasoning. MICOM results support partial measurement invariance across countries, and multi-group analysis shows that the negative effect of institutional guidance on policy ambiguity is stronger among Indonesian students. The findings contribute to accounting education by showing that responsible GenAI integration is not merely a matter of student acceptance but also of institutional guidance, assessment design, ethical reasoning, and clear boundaries for acceptable use.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6968358`

- title: A System Dynamics Decision-Support Model For Governing Generative AI Adoption: Leverage-Point Analysis And Intervention Simulation
- authors: Kelsey  Adrianne Cua, Ezekiel Bernardo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6968358
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The adoption of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) is an engineering systems problem. It is a complex sociotechnical system governed by feedback loops, time delays, and nonlinear interactions that resist conventional management and cannot be analyzed with static methods. Despite rapid uptake, enterprise GenAI adoption has plateaued below 50% of potential users, following a rise–dip–rebound–plateau trajectory that cross-sectional adoption models cannot reproduce. This study develops a System Dynamics simulation model as a decision-support tool for governing GenAI adoption. The model endogenizes technical performance (accuracy, output similarity, hallucinations), the developer and model supply ecosystem, user literacy, trust and trauma dynamics, costs, and interventions, with the adopter population segmented across Rogers' five diffusion categories. Integrating 17 reinforcing and 8 balancing feedback loops, it is validated against an empirical reference mode through behavior-pattern and structure-oriented tests, reproducing the observed trajectory as an endogenous outcome. Sensitivity analysis identifies five leverage points, dominated by developer-ecosystem structure and risk exposure rather than cost or access, and yields four engineering findings: (1) developer-ecosystem growth beyond governance capacity reduces long-run adoption (the AI sprawl effect); (2) trust in AI outputs decays rapidly and recovers asymmetrically; (3) raising user literacy without commensurate reliability improvement reduces adoption among later segments; and (4) interventions interact nonlinearly, with combined deployment producing outcomes inferior to targeted single interventions. Three intervention strategies are designed from the leverage points and evaluated, providing a reusable, quantitative decision-support framework for engineering the responsible deployment of GenAI systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6861738`

- title: Emergence via Phase Transitions: Mechanism Landscapes and Universal Convergence across Complex Systems
- authors: Xuan Khanh Truong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6861738
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

Why do independently trained neural networks converge to the same internal representations [37, 23]? Why does grokking-sudden generalisation after memorisation-follow universal statistics across architectures and tasks [42]? Why do independent evolutionary lineages repeatedly arrive at the same metabolic solutions across 993 yeast species [38]? We propose a candidate explanation for a structural motif common to these phenomena: when a system's energy budget crosses a critical threshold Ec, competing mechanisms undergo a phase transition that drives convergence toward a unique fixed point determined by the system's physical constraint set P. We do not claim to explain all emergence, but to identify a recurring phase-transition structure across convergence phenomena in learning, biology, and physics. Concisely: many emergence phenomena can be understood as phase transitions in mechanism landscapes under physical and informational constraints. We formalise this structural motif as the Hierarchical Emergence Framework (HEF), specified by a six-tuple (R (1) , L, A0, G, mode, E) together with P = (P thermo , P info , Φ), where the translation map Φ is an order-isomorphism of constraint lattices grounded in Landauer's Principle and the Jarzynski Equality. Three theorems follow. The Physical Feasibility Theorem guarantees that all generated entities satisfy thermodynamic and information-theoretic constraints simultaneously. The Energy-Diversity Theorem establishes a phase transition at Ec between an exploration regime and a convergence regime. Universal Feature Convergence then follows via the Banach Fixed-Point Theorem: any two HEF instances sharing P and operating below Ec converge to the same fixed-point representations, independent of initial conditions. A Causal Emergence Theorem additionally shows that the fixed point R∞ has strictly higher Effective Information [21] than the micro-level R (1) , with the gain bounded by a measurable training-dynamics quantity. We validate HEF empirically through 111 grokking experiments (p ∈ {23, 31, 41, 53, 67, 83, 97}, λ ∈ {1, 2}, multiple seeds). Universal Convergence is confirmed : all grokked models converge to 0.9745±0.014 regardless of p, λ, or training fraction (ANOVA p > 0.13; CV= 1.47%). A novel Ec fingerprint is identified: the weight norm ∥w∥ 2 peaks ∼1,050 steps before grokking in 92% of runs, tracing the threephase HEF trajectory. Accuracy curves collapse onto a tanh kink (R 2 = 0.93), placing grokking in the Landau-Ginzburg mean-field universality class. G2 scaling ∆t ∝ 1/(frac • p • λ) is supported across seven primes (β =-1.39 ± 0.20, R 2 = 0.91). HEF makes three falsifiable cross-domain predictions: (P1) anaerobic yeast lineages have higher genomic convergence than aerobic lineages at the same phylogenetic distance; (P2) LLMs trained with higher weight decay produce representations with higher causal potency; (P3) a critical weight-decay threshold λc(p) ∈ (2, 4) exists beyond which grokking fails via mechanism starvation. Code, data, and a diagnostic toolkit (hef-tools) are provided to enable independent replication and application to new systems.

### abstract

AI systems process vast hidden states, but do they exhibit a functional analogue of a "subconscious"? Rooted in Indian Knowledge Systems, this paper proposes the Chitta-Latent Correspondence (CLC) framework, translating Patanjali's Yoga Sutras into measurable geometry of transformer representations from a functionalist stance. The central contribution, Sattva-cos, quantifies valuealigned orientation aimed to serve as a computational marker for screening AI systems in therapeutic contexts. Following a pilot study on GPT-2 XL (OpenAI, 2019), the framework was evaluated in a cross-architectural comparison across three open-weight models: Pythia-6.9b (EleutherAI, 2023), Mistral-7B-v0.1 (Mistral AI, 2024), and Llama-3.1-8B (Meta AI, 2024), yielding five crossarchitecture findings: (1) dispositional geometry peaks at 50% depth across all three models (d = 3.743-5.107), corresponding to the Vijnanamaya Kosha; (2) all 18 layer measurements exceed the large-effect threshold (d > 0.8), with dispositional geometry 4.25-52× stronger than sentiment; (3) nihilistic content produces negative Sattva-cos at every sampled layer in every model, confirmed prospectively; (4) ablation confirms dispositional meaningfulness across architectures (effect ratio 33-209× for Pythia/Mistral; statistically significant but below the pre-registered threshold in Llama-3.1-8B); and (5) a late-layer Rajasic-Tamas geometric crossover emerges uniquely in Llama-3.1-8B at 97% depth. Chitta is not empty, it is geometrically structured, value-oriented, and measurable across three architectures (OSF: 10.17605/OSF.IO/63RB2).

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6966070`

- title: When Generative AI fails, Who Is to Blame? The Effect of Anthropomorphism on Users' Attribution and Willingness to Engage
- authors: Hyun-Jun Jeon, Il Im
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6966070
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, llm

### abstract

Although LLM (large language model) based conversational artificial intelligence (AI) can perform diverse tasks, failures like hallucination remain an inherent limitation due to technological constraints. This study empirically investigates the factors that mitigate the negative impact of AI failures on users’ willingness to engage, and elucidates the underlying mechanisms through a series of experiments. Study 1 demonstrates that perceived anthropomorphism positively moderates the relationship between perceived error and willingness to engage, whereas perceived autonomy does not. Study 2 further examines attributional processes involved, revealing that perceived anthropomorphism leads users to attribute AI failures to external causes, rather than ascribing them to the AI's inherent deficiencies. Collectively, these findings suggest that anthropomorphic design serves as a psychological buffer, sustaining user engagement despite failures in generative AI.
