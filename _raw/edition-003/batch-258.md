## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6617980`

- title: The Evolution of Tokenized Real-World Assets
- authors: Quoc Khanh Nguyen, Talis J. Putnins
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6617980
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

This paper investigates the news-driven factors that predict the adoption of RWA markets. Using a large language model (LLM) to analyze more than 14,000 RWA news articles, we identify key themes and construct a set of RWA indices from text. Our indices show a steadily maturing market, with sentiment becoming more positive, uncertainty declining, topic coverage remaining broad, and news becoming increasingly timely as the ecosystem evolves. We also find that news dynamics and RWA indices (sentiment and uncertainty) significantly predict subsequent RWA market adoption. Among RWA themes, Legal and Regulatory Frameworks, Jurisdictions, and Institutional Adoption dominate the RWA news. Among asset classes, news about stablecoins is more dominant and aligns with their early adoption, followed by U.S. Treasuries. Overall, our research provides a comprehensive overview of the RWA market and serves as a "compass" for understanding how and where the market is likely to grow. In addition, we develop a set of key indices to support the industry and guide future in-depth research.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6626280`

- title: AI Exposure and Equity Market Liquidity: Evidence from a Pre- and Post-GenAI Comparison
- authors: Boon Chuan Lim
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6626280
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

I examine whether firm-level AI exposure relates to equity market liquidity before and after the diffusion of generative AI (GenAI). Using a difference-indifferences design across 394 S&P 500 firms, I compare two non-overlapping windows: 2019 (pre-GenAI) and 2024-2025 (post-GenAI). AI exposure is measured via the Felten-Raj-Seamans occupational AI exposure score mapped to firm sectors. I find that high-AI-exposure firms experienced a statistically significant widening of bid-ask spreads (β = 0.00090, p = 0.025) and an increase in zero-return days (β = 0.00616, p = 0.008) relative to low-exposure firms after GenAI diffusion. These patterns are absent in the pre-period alone, consistent with the spread widening reflecting post-GenAI information asymmetry rather than a pre-existing cross-sectional difference. The results suggest that GenAI diffusion introduces adverse-selection costs into the equity markets of exposed firms.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6610019`

- title: THE LEX AI: MANDATORY AI GOVERNANCE AND THE DIGITAL TRIBUNAL IN INTERNATIONAL ARBITRATION
- authors: Barry Appleton
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6610019
- keyword hits: chatgpt

### abstract

Five years. Five cases. Three jurisdictions. One unmistakable pattern. In 2023, a New York federal court sanctioned counsel for submitting AI-generated, fabricated citations and then doubling down when challenged. In 2024, the Second Circuit entered a disciplinary referral against an attorney who submitted a nonexistent ChatGPT-generated authority. In November 2025, the Qatar Financial Centre Civil and Commercial Court in Doha issued a landmark judgment addressing the same conduct in an employment proceeding. In early 2026, the New York Appellate Division sanctioned counsel who continued filing AI-fabricated citations after being warned. And in April 2025, a petitioner argued before a United States federal court that an arbitrator had outsourced the reasoning behind a twenty-nine-page award to artificial intelligence, thereby presenting the first judicial test of whether AI-assisted award drafting constitutes grounds for vacatur under the Federal Arbitration Act. AI governance in international arbitration is no longer a future concern. It is the governing question of present practice. This article argues that AI regulation now constitutes a distinct mandatory overlay on international arbitral proceedings, analogous to the lex data but constituting a separate and coequal class of obligations. This overlay, designated the lex AI , is anchored in two direct regulatory instruments (the European Union AI Act and ABA Formal Opinion 512) and is further informed by institutional guidance and an emerging indirect regulatory architecture. The article examines Nippon Life Insurance Company of America v. OpenAI , the first tort action naming an AI developer as a defendant in litigation-conduct claims. It critically assesses the ICCA-NYC Bar-CPR Cybersecurity Protocol, identifies a systematic AI governance gap, and proposes five targeted enhancements. The article analyzes the AAA-ICDR AI Arbitrator, the first institutional deployment of AI in the adjudicative function, and its companion Resolution Simulator, identifying their implications for high-risk compliance under the EU AI Act, New York Convention enforcement, and equality of arms. It concludes by presenting a tripartite framework for arbitral governance in the algorithmic age: lex arbitri governs procedural validity; lex data governs informational sovereignty; lex AI governs the digital tribunal. This article is the second of three companion articles constituting what the author proposes to call the Digital Procedural Constitution of international arbitration.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6632339`

- title: Antitrust Enforcement and Product Market Dynamics: Evidence from U.S. Government Procurement
- authors: Johnathan Brogaard, Decio Coviello, Yunying Huang, Stefano Manfredonia
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6632339
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

We examine how antitrust enforcement shapes product market dynamics using U.S. government procurement as laboratory. Leveraging large language models to identify DOJ procurement cases, we document a trade-off between broader market participation and procurement performance. Non-defendant contractors, particularly small, women-, and minority-owned businesses, gain market share as antitrust enforcement dismantles the structural barriers erected by anti-competitive conduct. However, enforcement also impairs procurement performance in affected markets, an effect concentrated among new and inexperienced contractors and complex projects, but largely absent in simpler procurements. Our findings provide new insights into how antitrust enforcement influences diverse stakeholder groups in complex product markets.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6622458`

- title: On the Limits of Moral Surrender to AI
- authors: Eugen Dimant
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6622458
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

If people surrender to AI on cognitive tasks, do they also surrender morally? And if so, are the effects consistent with behavioral contagion found in human-to-human interactions? Across several preregistered, incentive-compatible, between-subjects studies, participants receive prosocial or antisocial behavioral advice attributed to an AI system, to a human source, or to no source, and then make consequential choices in three paradigms: a non-strategic other-regarding allocation, a strategic cooperation decision, and a private (dis)honesty decision under time pressure. AI moral influence is large, directional, and asymmetric. Prosocial AI advice raises other-regarding transfers substantially; antisocial AI advice fails to push behavior downward, even as an evaluation of their language reveals that participants verbally acknowledge the recommendation; antisocial human advice likewise fails to reproduce the downward contagion that the human peer-effect literature predicts. Within the AI source, the prosocial shift exceeds the antisocial shift by a sizeable margin, inverting the antisocial-dominance regularity that defines live-peer behavioral contagion. Five independent evidence tracks converge on the same contrast: behavioral choice, LLM-based semantic classification of open-ended justifications, an independent human-coder validation study that replicates the endorsement asymmetry, a rule-based prosocial-vocabulary dictionary, and unsupervised topic modeling. AI moral influence is directional because it is confirmatory rather than overriding : AI supplies a permission structure for actions that already align with private moral preference, but it cannot supply the community standing required to push agents against their own moral compass. The findings pin down a domain boundary that a symmetric cognitive-surrender extrapolation would miss.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6551539`

- title: Manikutty's Audit Shock Effect
- authors: Anand Manikutty
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6551539
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Open-source software underpins much of the modern digital economy. The prevailing belief within software engineering communities is that transparency improves reliability and security because publicly accessible code allows for broader scrutiny and faster detection of errors. However, the rapid emergence of generative artificial intelligence capable of analyzing large volumes of code may significantly alter the dynamics of vulnerability discovery. This paper proposes Manikutty's Audit Shock Effect, a conceptual framework suggesting that technological advances that dramatically reduce the cost of system inspection can trigger a sudden surge in defect discovery within transparent systems. Because open-source projects expose their codebases publicly, they are particularly susceptible to large-scale automated auditing. The short-term consequence may be a surge in discovered vulnerabilities that overwhelms the maintenance capacity of smaller projects and legacy infrastructure libraries. In the longer term, however, such transparency combined with automated analysis may strengthen the resilience of opensource ecosystems. The paper discusses implications for organizations dependent on open-source infrastructure and outlines directions for future research.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6642584`

- title: Beyond the Commons Toward a Digital Common Heritage of Mankind Framework Algorithmic Enclosure of the Cognitive Commons and the Nagoya Analogy
- authors: El Jih LAATIGUE
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6642584
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The training of large generative models on the accumulated cultural, linguistic and intellectual output of humanity has been analysed, in the dominant Anglo-American and European legal debates, principally through the lens of copyright doctrine-fair use, transformative use, text and data mining exceptions. This article argues that copyright is the wrong instrument, operating at the wrong scale, for a structural transformation that exceeds it. What is occurring is not a succession of infringements of authors' rights but an algorithmic enclosure of the cognitive commons: the privatisation, through the monopolisation of computational capacity to extract latent value, of an intergenerational heritage produced collectively by humanity over millennia. The article develops a framework-the Digital Common Heritage of Mankind (DCHM)-that re-situates the question within the international law regime of the common heritage of mankind and within the access and benefit-sharing regime of the 2010 Nagoya Protocol. The Nagoya analogy is exact: pharmaceutical extraction of genetic resources and traditional knowledge from the Global South was met, in international law, with a binding regime requiring prior informed consent and equitable benefit-sharing. Generative AI extraction of the world's linguistic, cultural and intellectual heritage has been met, thus far, with no equivalent regime. The article proposes such a regime, grounded in four international legal instruments, enriched by analytical concepts drawn from Islamic jurisprudence on the waqf and the common resource (mushaʿ), articulated through the Indigenous Data Sovereignty literature on OCAP and CARE principles, and translated into an operational three-layer architecture spanning international governance, Indigenous data sovereignty, and national sovereign data licensing. The broader theoretical horizon is the shift from intellectual property as a framework protecting individual and corporate interests to cognitive sovereignty as a framework protecting the collective heritage of humanity.
