## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6852200`

- title: AI-Assisted Language Assessment in the Post-ChatGPT Era: A Scoping Review
- authors: Jieun Kim, Hitoshi Nishizawa, Haeun Kim
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6852200
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

The present scoping review synthesizes peer-reviewed journal articles on AI-assisted second language (L2) assessment published between 2023 and July 2025. Following the PRISMA process, 81 articles were analyzed to examine the key characteristics of AI-assisted L2 assessment research, the employment of validity frameworks, and the implications of the findings. The synthesis indicates that studies published after the emergence of ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2022) have largely relied on generative AI, particularly ChatGPT, rather than other supervised AI technologies. Many studies focused on English as the target language, assessed the writing construct, examined foreign language contexts rather than second language contexts, and were conducted in postsecondary settings. The studies primarily investigated agreement between AI and human performance, followed by the impact of AI on language learning and comparative evaluations across AI approaches. AI was most frequently used to score assessments, provide both scores and feedback, and generate test items. AI was employed for both assessment for learning and assessment of learning. The vast majority of studies did not explicitly refer to any validity framework. Although many studies acknowledge the potential of AI-assisted L2 assessment, they rarely argue for its use beyond local contexts. Research gaps and implications for future research are discussed.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6850540`

- title: Linking policy instruments, governance objectives, and stakeholders in AI governance: insights from the policies of the US and EU
- authors: Liu Xianrui, Sicheng Guo, Jing Zhou
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6850540
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The rapid development of artificial intelligence (AI), including generative AI, is profoundly transforming daily life, industry, and public governance, making effective AI governance a critical policy issue. This study conducts a comparative analysis of 71 US and 80 EU AI policies using content analysis and network analysis to examine AI governance across three key dimensions: governance objectives, policy instruments and stakeholders. The results show that both the US and the EU aim to promote robust, trustworthy, and responsible AI, but differ in their governance priorities. In the governance objectives, the US places greater emphasis on technological optimisation to achieve robust and trustworthy AI, whereas the EU assigns more balanced importance to responsibility and trustworthiness, particularly in addressing ethical and risk-related concerns. Mechanisms to Support Implementation and Operationalizability (MSIO) serve as core actionable principles in both contexts. Although most policies address the AI lifecycle as a whole, specific stages such as design and development receive comparatively less attention. In terms of policy instruments, the US relies more on supply-side measures, while the EU emphasises environmental-side instruments. As for the stakeholders, the core governance participants in the US are the government and the research and education organisations. In the EU policies, the government play a central role in AI governance. Furthermore, the findings indicate that effective AI governance depends on the alignment between policy instruments, governance objectives, and stakeholder roles. By linking governance objectives with policy instruments and stakeholder roles, this study provides an integrative understanding of generative AI governance and offers practical insights for designing coordinated, effective, and policy-relevant regulatory frameworks.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6854336`

- title: When one ‘bee’ speaks two languages: A Variationist Linguistic Landscape Study of language choice on Beijing’s public signs
- authors: Ruchen Yu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6854336
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

While the language choice of top-down metro station names sparked heated debates in Beijing, the ubiquitous bottom-up public signs received comparatively less attention. As an early contribution to the Variationist Linguistic Landscape Study (VaLLS) framework, this study combines multivariate quantitative modelling with qualitative walking tours to uncover the language choice mechanisms on bottom-up signs in Beijing. Drawing on 1,332 signs collected across four functional areas, it identifies an area-based proportional hierarchy in multilingual signs’ distribution. However, multivariate logistic regression analysis reveals that sign materiality, discourse type, and authorship are stronger predictors of language choice than functional area, while specific contextual setting introduces further nuances. The study then illustrates the social meanings of language choices through an indexical field, where multilingual signs index creativity more prominently than commercial activities, despite the latter being their widely recognised function. Furthermore, it identifies that the basic communicative functions of multilingual signs are limited. As a comprehensive survey of Beijing’s linguistic landscape, this study addresses the limited generalisability left by isolated case studies to offer empirically grounded practical guidelines for stakeholders. It also introduces a theory-driven approach to using Large Language Models in linguistic research.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6854691`

- title: Geospatial Foundation Models for Mapping Daily Reference Evapotranspiration in Africa
- authors: Chouaib EL HACHIMI, Haider ABBAS, Qihao Weng
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-31
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6854691
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Timely and accurate estimations of reference evapotranspiration (ETo) are critical for effective water resource management and resilient agriculture planning in data-limited, climate-heterogeneous regions such as Africa. Here, we propose a physics-guided two-stage surrogate modeling framework to enable real-time estimation of daily ETo over Africa using satellite-derived surface solar radiation (Rs) and the TabPFN foundation model. First, a hybrid CNN-Windowed-Attention U-Net architecture was developed and trained using the spectral angle mapper loss to harmonize data from the MODIS Terra and Aqua sensors, thereby improving spatial coverage across Africa. Next, to capture radiative transfer processes, we build a surrogate model emulating MCD18A1.062 Rs from top-of-the-atmosphere reflectance and conditioning variables. Then, ETo was estimated in two ways: a physics-guided approach using surrogate Rs and a direct, end-to-end, data-driven approach. The framework was also evaluated against the widely adopted empirical methods, namely Priestley–Taylor, Makkink, Hargreaves–Samani, and Abtew, calibrated, in turn, against the FAO-56 Penman-Monteith as a reference. For Rs, results demonstrate that the surrogate model accurately reproduces Rs variability, with an RMSE of 2.03 MJ.m⁻².day⁻¹. For ETo, TabPFN improves from an RMSE of 0.93 mm.day⁻¹ in the end-to-end approach to 0.79 mm.day⁻¹ in the two-stage approach, outperforming both classical machine learning models and empirical formulations. Furthermore, continental and regional evaluations across nine IPCC climate regions show improved generalization of TabPFN across diverse hydroclimatic regimes, with RMSE values ranging from 0.26 to 0.91 mm.day⁻¹. In contrast, although they require difficult-to-obtain variables, empirical models exhibit systematic errors and limited adaptability to regional variability, with RMSE ranging from 0.49 to 2.94 mm.day⁻¹. With no reliance on costly ground measurements and being fully based on remotely sensed data, this framework can support timely estimation of irrigation water needs across Africa.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6854680`

- title: Beyond Consent: Collective Contestability and the Governance of Generative AI Training Data
- authors: Gary Liu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-31
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6854680
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This article argues that the consent paradigm cannot do the normative work that generative-AI training-data governance now demands, and that the organising principle for the regulatory response should be collective contestability: the standing of affected communities and their representative entities to challenge corpus composition, scope, and downstream synthetic-output infrastructures on an ex-ante and supra-individual basis. Building on but extending beyond existing contestability scholarship, the article relocates the concept along three dimensions: from ex-post to ex-ante, from individual data subject to representative entity, and from discrete decision output to upstream corpus and downstream synthetic-output infrastructure. The relocation is grounded in Pettit's republican theory of freedom as non-domination, bridged to collective form through Viljoen's relational theory of data governance, and operationalised through the Governing Knowledge Commons framework integrated with contextual integrity. A five-function comparative architecture maps European Union and United States (federal and California) instruments across provenance disclosure, opt-out architecture, collective standing, downstream synthetic-output liability, and public-interest intermediary infrastructure, grouped into three analytical clusters. Drawing on controlled clickwrap experiments and on the privacy-cynicism literature, the article documents both the empirical and the structural failure of consent. It concludes with ten operationalisable policy recommendations, each specifying an implementing body, a legal instrument, an enforcement mechanism, and a 90-day compliance milestone. Beyond consent is not without consent: it is consent supplemented by, and where necessary subordinated to, the standing of those whose contributions the corpus has already absorbed.

### abstract

This paper presents TianShu Quant, an LLM-driven platform integrating multi-factor quantitative strategy analysis and market anomaly detection for China’s A-share market. The platform constructs a structured factor library validated through IC analysis and quintile-portfolio back-testing, implements three strategy engines (moving-average crossover, momentum, RSI), and deploys Z-Score anomaly detection alongside LSTM price forecasting. The platform establishes a reproducible, end-to-end pipeline from raw data acquisition to investment-insight generation.

### abstract

This paper reports the v0.17 Premium Kitchenware program of the AIAS Presence Measurement research line. Following the pre-registered v0.17-prereg-r1 protocol, a 16-brand panel spanning three tradition cells (European, American, Japanese) was assembled and tested against the pre-registered substantive hypothesis H_Regime4_kitchenware and against the joint v0.16/v0.17 hypothesis H_IdentityLoad_moderator.
