## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6109432`

- title: Syllabic Agglutinative Tokenizations for Indonesian LLM: A Study from "Gasing Literacy Learning System"
- authors: Hokky Situngkir, Andhika Bernard Lumbantobing, Yohanes Surya
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-02-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6109432
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

This paper presents a novel syllable-based tokenization approach for Indonesian large language models, inspired by the Gasing Literacy Learning System's pedagogical methodology. Drawing on information-theoretic principles, we develop a tokenization framework that segments Indonesian text at syllable boundaries before applying byte-pair encoding, creating a vocabulary that aligns with the language's morphophonological structure. Our approach first identifies high-frequency syllables through rule-based segmentation, then constructs a compact vocabulary of 3,500 tokens that preserves meaningful linguistic units while maintaining coverage through character-level fallback. Empirical evaluation on Indonesian Wikipedia and folklore corpora from Indonesian Culture Digital Library (PDBI) demonstrates substantial improvements over conventional tokenization methods: the syllable-based approach achieves Rényi efficiency of 0.74 compared to 0.50-0.64 for pretrained multilingual tokenizers, while maintaining higher average token lengths (3.67 characters versus 2.72 for GPT-2) despite using a vocabulary an order of magnitude smaller. These gains emerge from the method's ability to internalize character-level dependencies within syllable units, reducing the computational burden on language models while respecting Indonesian's agglutinative morphology. We call the LLM built upon this principle, TOBA LLM (Tokenisasi Optimum Berbasis Aglutinasi), the convergence of human literacy pedagogy with computational optimization principles offers a promising paradigm for developing linguistically-informed tokenization strategies, particularly for morphologically rich and underrepresented languages in natural language processing.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6240619`

- title: AI and the Labor Market: A Worker's-Eye View
- authors: anon Kim, Nathan Mester, Greg Sun
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6240619
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

We collect and analyze data from a survey of just under 2,000 U.S. citizens to study perceptions about the role of generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) in the workplace, as well as changes in career-relevant decision-making since the initial launch of ChatGPT. We report four main findings. First, adoption patterns depend on both the perceived effect of AI on current productivity and on its perceived capacity as a career-relevant learning tool, with the latter being slightly more important. Second, most respondents report dedicating more time to learning now compared to before ChatGPT, and this trend is driven by those who believe AI is a useful tool for learning. Third, while time invested in learning tends to decrease with age, this trend is attenuated among who believe in AI's potential as a learning tool. Fourth, workers who believe that AI is a complement to their own effort increase their effort in response to the introduction of AI while workers who believe that AI is a substitute show no such effect. These patterns are robust to the inclusion of a rich set of controls. Our results demonstrate the importance of worker-level tradeoffs in shaping heterogeneous responses to the introduction of AI and highlight career-long learning as a dimension of AI capabilities that is especially important in the eyes of many workers.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6244519`

- title: Learning to Fuse Aspect-Level LLM Annotations for Low-Quality Ordinal Sentiment Supervision
- authors: Jin Liu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6244519
- keyword hits: gpt-4, large language model, llm

### abstract

Subjective text classification increasingly relies on imperfect supervision that is itself a noisy fusion of heterogeneous signals-user ratings, crowd labels, and foundation-model annotations. In domains where narratives encode multi-criterion trade-offs, collapsing evidence into a single holistic class yields diffuse confidence, unstable targets, and limited utility for uncertaintybased triage. We examine this setting in patient medication reviews, where platform ratings reflect heterogeneous utilities and rubric-guided holistic large language model (LLM) sentiment labeling exhibits persistently high uncertainty under a five-class ordinal scale. We recast supervision construction as an explicit multi-view fusion problem. Using gpt-4o-mini as a foundation-model view extractor, we elicit four clinically interpretable aspect views-efficacy, safety, burden, and cost-each labeled on a three-class polarity scale (NEGATIVE, NEUTRAL, POSITIVE), alongside a holistic five-class confidence vector. We then learn non-negative aspect importance weights via multi-task transformer training and fuse aspect outputs into (i) five-class hard supervision through weighted aggregation and (ii) three-class soft targets through weighted vote distributions, yielding supervision that is robust to incomplete evidence (unmentioned aspects) and cross-aspect conflicts. On 102,061 deduplicated Drugs.com reviews, aspect-derived supervision improves downstream learnability across classical, neural, and transformer probes. ALBERT fine-tuned on aspect-derived hard labels achieves weighted F1=0.756, outperforming rating-derived supervision (0.651) and holistic LLM labels (0.674). These findings support an information-fusion principle for low-quality subjective supervision: extracting structured subdecisions aligned to textual evidence and learning an explicit aggregation rule yields more stable and learnable targets than single-shot holistic labeling.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6300241`

- title: The Sixth Layer: The Legal Infrastructure for Physical Artificial Intelligence in China
- authors: Angela Huyue Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6300241
- keyword hits: foundation model, generative ai

### abstract

This Article offers the first comprehensive study of how law serves as critical infrastructure supporting China’s development of physical artificial intelligence (AI)—AI systems embedded in and operating through the physical world. Drawing on an in-depth analysis of Chinese legislation and enforcement practices governing autonomous driving, robotics, drones, and brain–computer interfaces, this Article argues that Chinese law functions as the sixth layer of the AI ecosystem, alongside energy, compute, cloud, foundation models, and applications. Unlike generative AI, physical AI systems often require testing in public environments, and the acquisition of high-quality training data is more costly and time-consuming. To overcome these challenges, Chinese law has been deliberately engineered to perform three interlocking functions. First, law stimulates adoption through top-level policy design and regulatory sandboxes that authorize large-scale regional experimentation. Wider deployment reduces unit costs and accelerates adoption, while simultaneously generating operational data that enhances model performance and system reliability. Second, law enables unification at scale by imposing technical standards that discipline product design and production. Crucially, these rules also standardize data collection and facilitate the construction of large-scale data platforms. Third, law fortifies China’s competitive moat by restricting the collection of important data and the cross-border transfer of data generated through deployment itself. Taken together, these legal functions—stimulation, unification, and fortification—creates a unique, state-engineered data flywheel by transforming real-world deployment into a durable source of data advantage. This early deployment-based experience and domestic standard-setting also enhances China’s influence within international standard-setting bodies and further expands exports of Chinese AI products overseas. As China approaches the technological frontier, it has begun to impose more stringent legal requirements on its products, thereby positioning itself as an increasingly influential rule-setter in global AI governance. China’s law-as-infrastructure approach carries profound domestic and global implications. By prioritizing rapid deployment and large-scale data capture, it risks weakening labor and safety protections while exacerbating chronic industrial overcapacity. As domestic pressures mount, outward expansion represents the primary pathway through which China can externalize some of the economic and social costs generated by rapid AI-driven transformation at home. At the same time, notwithstanding escalating geopolitical tensions, China’s advances in physical AI may create incentives for American firms to engage in selective cooperation with their Chinese counterparts. However, the U.S. government has found it difficult to emulate China’s model of state-led coordination, given the institutional constraints it faces. Instead, it relies heavily on Big Tech firms, which increasingly constitute the backbone of U.S. competitiveness in physical AI. The United States must therefore strike a delicate balance in determining the extent to which it is willing to tolerate concentrated private power as a substitute for state coordination in the global race for leadership in AI. Above all, the centrality of cost in China’s data flywheel underscores the imperative for the United States to strengthen its industrial capacity. Rebuilding domestic manufacturing is not merely about winning the AI race—it is an urgent matter of national security.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6187958`

- title: Modeling the Dynamics and Impact of Human-AI Interactions with Attention Transition Network Analysis
- authors: Sonsoles López Pernas, Santtu Tikka, Kamila Misiejuk, Eduardo Oliveira, Mohammed Saqr
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6187958
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Network analysis is a well-established approach for the modeling of interactions and relationships across several domains of educational research. However, existing methods face limitations in capturing the temporal order, depth and influence within temporal data. To address these challenges, we introduce Attention Transition Network Analysis (ATNA), a temporal network framework inspired by the attention mechanism in large language models that captures temporal depth where neighboring nodes receive higher attention which decays by distance. The attention and decay can be tuned according to context. Hence, it provides a flexible framework for modeling the temporal depth of relevance. ATNA includes confirmatory testing: bootstrapping to evaluate individual edge stability, case dropping for centrality robustness testing, and permutation for comparing across different processes. Such an extensive level of confirmatory testing affords researchers rigorous mechanisms to ensure that the results of their analysis are genuinely meaningful and not artefacts within the data. In this paper, we introduce the foundations of the ATNA method, compare it to existing methods, describe its mathematical foundations, and illustrate it with a case study in modeling student-AI interactions in programming education.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6381580`

- title: Risks Create a Jagged Frontier of LLM Productivity Gains Across Computer Occupations
- authors: Deepika Chawla, Gagandeep Singh, Elham Khorasani Buxton, Meicen Sun, Lav Varshney, Jeremy Riel, Craig De Voto
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6381580
- keyword hits: claude, gpt-5, llm

### abstract

This paper argues that the frontier of expert-LLM collaboration is uneven, not only because of jagged AI capability but also due to jagged AI risk. For some tasks, LLM systems, including agents, can significantly reduce completion time and labor cost. For others, these gains are constrained, if not offset, by risks. We introduce the first occupation-level quantification of risk-aware productivity gains for expert–LLM collaboration over a realistic task distribution, using standard O*NET computer-occupation tasks. We leverage six frontier models (e.g., GPT-5, Claude Opus) to produce structured risk–reward ratings following standardized definitions and manually verifying a subset for reliability. Reward captures productivity gains, i.e., time/cost savings at a fixed quality target under expert oversight. We consider multiple sources of risk, ranging from incompetence-driven errors (Discrimination, Toxicity, Privacy, Misinformation) to adversarial exploitation (Security, Malicious Actors and Misuse). We aggregate task-level ratings into occupation-level scores, weighting tasks by O*NET measures of task importance, relevance, and frequency. Our empirical analysis reveals that risk varies more than reward, yielding significant differences in risk–reward tradeoff: in safety-critical roles (e.g., information security engineers), gains are offset by risk, whereas in less safety-critical roles (e.g., web developers), gains often substantially exceed risks. As LLM systems improve, incompetence-driven risks may decline, but adversarial risks can persist or increase, leading to the likely persistence of a jagged risk–reward frontier. The risk-aware jaggedness has significant implications for labor policy, research funding, and national competitiveness.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6310740`

- title: AI-Powered Corporate Governance
- authors: Joanna (Xiaoyu) Wang, Seungjoon Oh, Jiung Lee
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6310740
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

We examine how Generative AI reshapes institutional investor behavior and corporate governance. Using the November 2022 ChatGPT release as a shock to investors' AI adoption costs, we employ a difference-in-differences design in which treatment reflects firms' pre-shock exposure to machine-based investors, identified through EDGAR access patterns. We find that following the shock, machine-based investors expand portfolio breadth across industries and shorten investment horizons. At the firm level, we document synchronization among machine-based investors, leading to coordination in AI-powered governance mechanisms. Their presence is associated with higher firm valuations and greater monitoring effectiveness, including higher CEO pay-performance sensitivity, higher turnover-performance sensitivity, and enhanced financial reporting quality. Firms with AI-powered investors reduce long-term investment and total M&A activity, while shifting toward value-enhancing acquisitions and higher-quality innovations. Our findings suggest that AI-powered institutional investors become more effective monitors in the age of AI through lower information acquisition costs and exit-based discipline.
