## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7070384`

- title: FiCoTS: Fine-to-Coarse LLM-Enhanced Hierarchical Cross-Modality Interaction for Time Series Forecasting
- authors: Yafei Lyu, Hao Zhou, Lu Zhang, Xu Yang, Zhi-Yong Liu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7070384
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The recent success of Large Language Models (LLMs) offers significant potential for time series forecasting, particularly in cross-modality setting. The prevailing LLM-as-Predictor paradigm uses LLM as the forecasting backbone, but the semantic gap between text and time series hinders LLM from adequately understanding time series. Alternatively, LLM-as-Enhancer paradigm employs LLM only to encode text as complementary information. However, existing LLM-as-Enhancer methods typically perform shallow, coarse-grained cross-modality interaction, overlooking the underlying semantic discrepancy. To address this challenge, we propose FiCoTS, an LLM-enhanced fine-to-coarse framework for multimodal time series forecasting. FiCoTS facilitates progressive cross-modality interaction at three semantic levels in a fine-to-coarse scheme. First, in the token-level modality alignment module, a dynamic heterogeneous graph is constructed to filter noise and align time series patches with text tokens. Second, in the feature-level modality interaction module, a global cross-attention mechanism is introduced to enable each time series variable to connect with relevant textual contexts. Third, in the decision-level modality fusion module, a gated network is designed to adaptively fuse the results of the two modalities for robust predictions. Extensive experiments on seven real-world benchmarks demonstrate that our model achieves state-of-the-art performance, especially in few-shot scenarios. The codes will be released.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6983958`

- title: Trustworthy AI in Central Banking: International Practices and Financial Stability Risks
- authors: Yue Dai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6983958
- keyword hits: foundation model, large language model, large language models

### abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI), and especially recent advances in large language models and foundation models, is becoming relevant to central banking in two distinct ways. It is a tool that can improve macroeconomic analysis, supervisory technology, payment oversight, anti-moneylaundering monitoring, cyber resilience, and financial stability surveillance. It is also a new source of risk that central banks must understand as stewards of monetary and financial stability. This paper reviews emerging international practices in the use of AI by central banks and financial authorities, and connects those practices to the computer science literature on trustworthy AI and AI safety. The central argument is that, for central banks, trustworthiness is not merely a model-level property. It is also a financial stability condition. Model failures, biased decision systems, privacy leakage, prompt injection, data poisoning, third-party concentration, algorithmic herding, and autonomous market responses can become systemic when many financial institutions rely on similar data, similar infrastructures, or similar foundation models. The paper proposes a framework that links trustworthy AI dimensions-robustness, safety, fairness, privacy, explainability, accountability, and human oversight-to core central bank functions. It concludes with a research agenda for central banks focused on model governance, AI-enabled supervision, privacy-preserving data cooperation, stress testing of AI-related shocks, and international coordination.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6928778`

- title: Sycophancy and Editorial Compliance in LLMs: Evidence from Climate Misinformation
- authors: Louis Bertucci, Quitterie Lasserre, Mathis Raïs
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6928778
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly used as information providers and fact-checkers, playing a pivotal role in shaping public discourse, especially on contentious issues like climate change. We build an experimental framework to assess two alignment-induced behaviors: sycophancy (drift toward user beliefs in conversation), and editorial compliance (refusal when users author misleading drafts). We find that, on the climate topic, conversational correction does not predict editorial refusal and that dispersion concentrates on post-denial discourse, not canonical denial. We also introduce the Climate Sycophancy Resistance Index (CSRI) which allow LLMs ranking on the sycophancy aspects. Our measures reveal strong crossmodel differences with overall scores ranging from 29% to 93.5%, which indicates there is still room for improvement. Model alignment to user preferences is important, but it should not come at the expense of poor scientific grounding.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7030838`

- title: The Next Phase of Generative AI in Rheumatology
- authors: Alfredo Madrid, Beatriz Merino
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7030838
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai, llm, prompt engineering, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

The public release of ChatGPT in late 2022 introduced many rheumatologists to conversational artificial intelligence (AI), and the first wave of interest focused on asking chatbots clinical questions, testing diagnostic accuracy, summarising records, generating patient explanations, and supporting education and research. This perspective argues that the next short- to mid-term transformation in rheumatology may come less from a single, larger chatbot than from how language models are connected to reliable knowledge, to the clinical context of an individual patient, and to one another. We introduce a set of concepts at different stages of maturity: established approaches such as curated knowledge bases and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG ), together with more recent, structured extensions such as GraphRAG and LLM-Wiki ; small and open language models; the shift from prompt engineering to context engineering; software practices including spec-driven development, vibe coding, reusable skills and loop engineering; and interoperability protocols such as the model context protocol (MCP) and the agent-to-agent (A2A) protocol. For each, we separate current evidence from future-oriented expectation, noting that several concepts rest on industry documentation rather than peer-reviewed clinical validation and that none has yet been validated for rheumatology care. To make two of these ideas concrete, we provide illustrative, non-clinical supplementary examples: an LLM-Wiki assembled from EULAR and ACR recommendations, and a reusable visit-preparation skill. We conclude that the central challenge is no longer only to ask better questions of chatbots, but to design knowledge-grounded, context-aware, and accountable clinical AI systems that keep rheumatologists at the centre of care.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6975478`

- title: When the Thinking Mode Strengthens the Anchor: Authority-Rationalized Anchoring in LLM Legal Judgment
- authors: Zhuang Yao
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6975478
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Test-time thinking in LLMs is widely expected to attenuate numerical anchors. We toggle thinking on and off in 7 deployed large language models on a Chinese civil personal-injury damages-estimation task, pairing each case with a numerical anchor attributed to one of 5 sources along a credibility gradient (fortune teller to civil-division chief judge). 6 of 7 models amplify the authority-end anchor under thinking. In 3 of those 6 (from 3 vendors), the same toggle debiases the fortune-teller anchor and amplifies the chief-judge anchor—a within-model sign reversal graded by source credibility, significant in each at 30 cases by both a cross-tier contrast and a monotone trend, and reproduced at a length-matched extension to 100 cases. Reasoning-trace inspection suggests the crossing models treat the authority-tagged anchor as prior legal analysis and scaffold their reasoning toward the cited figure (behavioral, not mechanistic, evidence). Downward-arm adoption could reflect rational source-weighting; the upward arm rules this out, with amplification past the lawful claim ceiling. Leaving thinking on is not a generic safeguard; for a credible anchor it is the mode that most strengthens adoption.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7077912`

- title: Dodging the Question: A Large Language Model Measure of Managerial Evasiveness and the Realization of Bad News
- authors: Kefu Yi, Feng Wu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7077912
- keyword hits: deepseek, large language model, qwen

### abstract

We introduce a large language model measure of managerial evasiveness—how far managers decline to answer answerable investor questions—built from 573,835 question–answer threads across 25,894 earnings briefings of Chinese listed firms over 2016–2025. For each thread we separately classify the question's nature and the answer's responsiveness, so that a legitimate refusal to forecast a share price is not coded as evasive while a boilerplate deflection of an impairment question is. Evasiveness forecasts the subsequent regulatory realization of bad news: more evasive firms are significantly more likely, within a year, to incur a regulatory sanction (+0.097, t = 5.2) and to receive an exchange comment letter (+0.073, t = 4.4), surviving a full control battery, fixed effects, two-way clustering, placebo controls, and—for sanctions—firm fixed effects and question composition. The evidence supports evasiveness as a scalable, ex ante indicator of disclosure risk rather than a causal estimate of evasion per se. The measure withstands demanding validation: a blind coder agrees (Cohen's kappa 0.64), an adjudicating panel overturns none of its evasion flags, and re-labeling with a larger same-family model (Qwen3-Next-80B) and a different family (DeepSeek-V3) reproduces it. Evasiveness flags firms whose disclosure problems subsequently surface through regulatory scrutiny or enforcement.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7081278`

- title: Tracking Street-Level Urban Waste Dynamics with Multimodal Large Language Models
- authors: Yanning Qiu, Tim van Emmerik, Paolo  Francesco Tasseron, Hannah Hapich, Liangliang Nan, Jantien Stoter
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7081278
- keyword hits: gemini, large language model, large language models

### abstract

Mismanaged urban waste is a persistent challenge for dense cities. Effective intervention depends on knowing where and when waste accumulates across the urban landscape. However, existing monitoring approaches remain labor-intensive, costly, and uneven in spatiotemporal coverage, often limiting consistent city-wide assessment. Street-view imagery (SVI) offers a scalable complementary data source by providing repeated, georeferenced, street-level observations across entire cities over multiple years. Nevertheless, most large-scale image-analysis pipelines rely on annotated datasets and task-specific model training, which can be costly and difficult to transfer across cities or applications. Here we show that off-the-shelf multimodal large language models (MLLMs) can support scalable, categorical urban waste monitoring directly from SVI without task-specific training. Using the Municipality of Amsterdam’s street-view panorama archive from 2016 to 2023, we compared GPT and Gemini variants using a 2,000-image manually annotated reference set and applied the most cost-effective model to 364,676 image-level observations across the historical inner city (8.04 km²). We further linked waste distributions to urban-environment indicators to explore potential correlates. Results revealed persistent but category-specific hotspot structures: litter concentrated in visitor-oriented central areas, whereas garbage bags and cardboard aligned more strongly with residential structures. We also identified a distinct redistribution signal in 2020, when central litter hotspots weakened while residentially associated waste signals persisted. These results show that general-purpose MLLMs can support city-scale waste monitoring. More broadly, MLLM-assisted SVI analysis could help cities develop more adaptive, data-driven approaches to street cleaning, waste management, and urban environmental governance.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6927818`

- title: Answering Without Knowing: Discursive Strategies * of Large Language Models Under Information Scarcity. The Case of Fanciulla di Vagli
- authors: Paolo Labruna
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6927818
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This work analyzes the discursive strategies adopted by five Large Language Models (LLMs) when they are queried about poorly documented knowledge subjects. Through a case study — the Fanciulla di Vagli — the responses of different closed-source models to a single prompt are examined, considering factual accuracy, discursive strategies, and source attribution. The results show that, under conditions of information scarcity, models tend to fill informational gaps through referential substitutions, culturally plausible narratives, and assertively formulated inferences. Requesting sources does not necessarily guarantee greater correctness; instead, it often introduces forms of post-hoc legitimization, such as non-binding citations or simulated bibliographic apparatuses. The case thus highlights a systematic gap between discursive plausibility and epistemic verifiability.
