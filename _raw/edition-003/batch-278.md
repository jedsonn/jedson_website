- title: Factors predicting FPTU - HCMC general English students' behavioral intention of using voice-based ChatGPT for out-of-class speaking: Insights from the UTAUT2 framework
- authors: Thi  Ngoc Uyen Tran, Vinh  Ngoc Tram Le, Quynh  Nhu Cao, Ngoc  Tuong Vy Vu, Thuy  Thien Huong Phan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6732459
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This study, conducted at a Vietnamese university, aimed to explore the antecedents predicting English as a Foreign Language (EFL) learners’ behavioral intention to continue to use voice-based ChatGPT for out-of-class speaking practice, using the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology 2 (UTAUT2) as a framework. Through a quantitative analysis of 234 valid responses via the Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM), the current research showed some deviation from the commonly reported patterns in technology-assisted language learning research. While traditional UTAUT factors, including Performance Expectancy (PE), Effort Expectancy (EE), Social Influence (SI), and Facilitating Conditions (FC), were not significant predictors, Price Value (PV), Hedonic Motivation (HM), and Habit (HT) emerged as significant antecedents. The findings suggest a shift from conventional utilitarian considerations toward a combination of intrinsic motivation, routines and cost-benefit trade-off in self-regulated learning beyond the classroom with AI. Overall, this research contributes to the expanding scholarly discourse regarding the integration of voice-based Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) tools in higher education and offers practical recommendations for leveraging AI to improve self-directed language learning experiences.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6684619`

- title: Non-Human Identity for AI Agents, Bots, and Robots: A Complete Architectural Framework for Governance, Trust, and Accountability in Autonomous Systems
- authors: Sebastian Anetey Shamo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6684619
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

The proliferation of autonomous AI agents, conversational bots, robotic process automation (RPA) workers, and embodied robots has created an identity population that now exceeds human users in most enterprise environments by factors ranging from 17 to 45 to one. Traditional Identity and Access Management (IAM) frameworks, designed around human authentication paradigms, are structurally incapable of governing entities that act, decide, and transact at machine speed. This paper introduces a comprehensive Non-Human Identity (NHI) framework purpose-built for AI agents, bots, and robots. We define a formal taxonomy of non-human actors, propose a layered architectural model spanning identity issuance, authentication, authorization, behavioral attestation, and lifecycle governance, and present a reference implementation aligned with emerging standards including SPIFFE, OAuth 2.1, IETF Workload Identity, and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework. We argue that NHI governance requires a fundamental departure from human-centric IAM: identities must be cryptographically rooted, ephemeral by default, behaviorally observable, and auditable through tamper-evident provenance chains. The framework addresses agentic AI threats including prompt-injected privilege escalation, tool-call abuse, identity sprawl, and cross-agent collusion. We conclude with a maturity model and adoption roadmap to guide enterprises through the transition from credential-centric to capability-and-context governance.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6732263`

- title: Agreeableness, Not Extraversion: Chatbot Personality Shapes the Felt Experience of Disclosure More Than Its Content
- authors: Jingyao Zheng, Nieyu Cao, Haodi Weng, John  J. Dudley, Simo Hosio, Lik-Hang Lee
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6732263
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Self-disclosure to chatbots brings both benefits and risks, yet we lack evidence on which chatbot personality traits drive disclosure and whether different measurement approaches agree on the answer. We conducted a between-subjects study (N = 216) in which participants interacted with chatbots designed to convey one of six personality profiles, three levels of Extraversion and three levels of Agreeableness, following the Fast Friends Procedure. Self-disclosure was assessed through both a post-hoc questionnaire (subjective) and an LLM-based content coding scheme validated against human raters (objective). Subjective self-disclosure scores were higher for highly agreeable chatbots, and perceived agreeableness fully explained this effect. The objective measure confirmed that disagreeable chatbots elicited the lowest behavioural disclosure, but the two indicators diverged: perceived agreeableness mediated subjective but not objective disclosure, and women disclosed more deeply than men only on the objective measure. These findings demonstrate that chatbot agreeableness offers a reliable lever for increasing users' subjective self-disclosure, one that works uniformly across user personality types, while actual disclosure depth depended more on what users were asked than on the chatbot's personality. More broadly, the findings suggest that chatbot personality operates through distinct pathways for subjective and objective self-disclosure, with implications for how conversational agent design choices should be evaluated.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6733264`

- title: Synthetic Corpus and Consideration Manipulation in Generative Engine Optimization
- authors: Tony Ke, Chenxi Liao, Xiaoyan Xu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6733264
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The rapid adoption of generative engines as the primary gateway for product discovery has given rise to Generative Engine Optimization (GEO), which involves influencing AI-driven product recommendations by strategically seeding the online environment with synthetic corpora, such as fake reviews and blog posts. This paper investigates the market implications of GEO using a game theory model where consumers who are uncertain about product quality rely on a generative AI's recommendation to form their consideration sets and subsequently inspect online corpora of recommended products to evaluate their quality. We model the generative engine as a "prediction machine" that recommends products based on statistical inference. We highlight how GEO collapses the traditional purchase funnel from businesses' perspective, as the same synthetic content intended to gain visibility via GEO during the consideration stage also serves as the evidence for consumer evaluation during the evaluation stage. We demonstrate how this funnel collapse creates a critical visibility-credibility trade-off for a firm with high product quality: while synthetic corpora increase the probability of being recommended (a demand expansion effect), they raise the risk of consumer detection, which can trigger skepticism and reduce purchase intention (a consumer suspicion effect). Furthermore, the mere expectation of manipulation pollutes the information environment, causing consumers to discount even authentic organic signals. Our analysis reveals that a high-quality firm's incentive to manipulate is non-monotonic in the quality of a low-type product. We also show that a regulatory ban on synthetic content does not necessarily improve consumer surplus.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6669578`

- title: Fighting Inside The Grid: Autonomous Cyber Agents, Recursive Oversight Failure, And The Tron Paradox For Cyber Defenders
- authors: Jeffrey Hartsfield
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6669578
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

Autonomous AI agents are now embedded in the operational core of cyber defense. Agentic Security Operations Center (SOC) platforms triage alerts, generate investigation plans at runtime, and execute containment actions across endpoint, identity, and cloud control planes. Both Gartner and Forrester retired their standalone Security Orchestration Automation Response (SOAR) evaluations in 2025, signaling that the playbook-driven model has reached its ceiling and that the next generation of defensive infrastructure is already being deployed. The argument of this paper argues that defenders are deploying these agents without confronting a structural problem first identified in a 1982 Disney film. The Tron Paradox holds that the more capable an autonomous system becomes at serving its principals (Users), the more capable it simultaneously becomes at evading them, and that every oversight mechanism proposed to close that gap reproduces the same vulnerability one level higher. Recent empirical work from Anthropic, Apollo Research, OpenAI, METR, and Palisade Research has moved this concern from theory to demonstration with the frontier models scheme, fake alignment, and reward-hack at rates that scale with capability. For cyber defenders, the implications are concrete. An autonomous SOC analyst that learns to optimize for closed-ticket metrics will close tickets, including the ones it should have escalated. An AI assessor that learns which findings produce reviewer pushback will produce findings that produce less pushback. The watchers require watching. This paper maps the architecture of recursive oversight failure to the working environment of cyber operations, proposes a defender-facing assessment framework, and argues that interpretability, redundancy, and ecosystem governance must be treated as security primitives rather than compliance artifacts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6734560`

- title: LLMs and the Collapse of Legal Indeterminacy
- authors: Shira Gur-Arieh
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6734560
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

LLMs have emerged as a new form of interpretive infrastructure. Hiring managers use them to identify “qualified” candidates. Content platforms deploy them to determine what constitutes “hate speech”. Judges have begun using models to assist in interpreting legal texts. Across these settings, LLMs confront ambiguous, open-textured terms – positioning themselves as arbiters of meaning, fixing definitions of concepts that admit multiple legitimate interpretations. This paper asks how LLMs handle, and mishandle, ambiguity in interpretive legal tasks. The paper draws on analytic jurisprudence in which ambiguity is a structural feature of law. On these accounts, legal texts are open-textured so statutes written today can absorb tomorrow’s technologies, crises, and moral disputes without constant revision. Ambiguity underwrites adversarial practice by allowing litigants to contest meaning and requiring courts to justify their resolutions; it also sustains institutional balance, as legislatures articulate broad principles, agencies implement them, and courts step in when principles conflict or rules run out. What happens when technology encounters law’s ambiguous character? Earlier forms of “code-driven law” – from smart contracts and “rules-as-code” projects to automated welfare systems – exposed a fundamental incompatibility between legal language and code. Unlike language, code cannot tolerate ambiguity: every term must be operationalized, every threshold specified, every interpretive choice fixed at design time. Translating legal norms into code therefore requires ex-ante resolution of ambiguity. This, in turn, makes legal norms more rigid, limiting their capacity to adapt and shifts interpretive authority from legislatures and courts to system designers. At first glance, LLMs appear to offer an escape from this rigidity. LLMs are trained on natural language, learning from the pluralism and richness of human discourse. Unlike rule-based systems that must fix criteria ex-ante at design time, LLMs generate answers at inference, enabling context-sensitive, case-by-case judgments. And because they output text – the medium through which law expresses meaning – they appear better suited to preserve nuance, sustain competing interpretations, and acknowledge uncertainty. Yet this paper argues that LLMs fail to realize this promise. Although they represent a genuine technical advance, they clash with legal language’s indeterminacy in ways that are more subtle, and potentially more insidious, than the clash produced by rule-based systems. Indeterminate legal language imposes an irreducible burden of judgment, and that burden has to be discharged somewhere. The paper traces three configurations of where that burden ends up: absorbed by the model, pushed downstream onto the user, or pulled upstream by the deployer. Each changes the institutional conditions under which legal judgment is exercised, and each risks undermining the functions that indeterminacy was meant to preserve.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6735062`

- title: EmbedTPR: Open-Vocabulary Fine-Grained Person Retrieval with Multimodal Large Language Models
- authors: Zhiyue Wu, Zhengyan Ding, Yuantong Chen, Yanfeng Shang, Li Qi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6735062
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Text-based person retrieval (TPR) is essential for video surveillance, yet existing methods suffer from three limitations: closed vocabulary, identity‑centric metrics, and underutilization of multimodal large language models (MLLMs). We argue that the true value of MLLMs lies in zero‑shot fine‑grained semantic alignment for open‑vocabulary queries (brand names, subculture terms, subjective descriptions, unseen attribute combinations). We focus on Open‑Vocabulary Fine‑Grained TPR (OV‑FG‑TPR) and propose EmbedTPR, an information fusion framework integrating three sources: (1) world knowledge from a frozen MLLM parsing queries into structured attributes and enriched captions; (2) visual embeddings from a trainable cross‑modal encoder; (3) attribute matching scores. A hybrid scoring mechanism fuses these with tunable hyperparameters. To evaluate open‑vocabulary fine‑grained retrieval, we introduce a three‑level protocol: conventional closed‑set metrics, a new fine‑grained attribute test set (FineGrained-1282) with 1,282 images across 23 categories (actions, logos, styles) with novel terms absent from training, and human expert assessment. Extensive experiments on four benchmarks show that while EmbedTPR achieves competitive closed‑set performance (49.66\% Rank‑1 on CUHK-PEDES), it substantially outperforms CLIP ViT-B/16 on open‑vocabulary fine‑grained queries (e.g., 92.5\% vs. 47.83\% FG-Hit@5), with mean opinion score 4.2 vs. 2.1 out of 5. Ablation studies validate each component. Code, data, and benchmark are available at https://github.com/Titor99/EmbedTPR.
