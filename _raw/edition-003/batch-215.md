## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7008859`

- title: Toward the Programmable Cell: Future Directions in Biological Artificial Intelligence
- authors: Thomas Wang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7008859
- keyword hits: agentic, foundation model

### abstract

Over the past five years, artificial intelligence has moved from a peripheral analysis tool in the life sciences to a central engine of discovery. Foundation models trained on protein sequences, genomic DNA, and single-cell transcriptomes now predict molecular structure with experimental accuracy, generate functional proteins that do not exist in nature, and encode substantial amounts of organized biological knowledge in their internal representations. This review surveys the current landscape of biological AI and uses it as a vantage point from which to chart the field's most consequential future directions. After situating the major model families across the protein, genomic, and cellular modalities, we organize the road ahead around six themes: (i) the integration of modalities and scales toward an "AI virtual cell"; (ii) the shift from prediction to generative design and programmable biology; (iii) mechanistic interpretability as a route from predictive black boxes to scientific instruments; (iv) agentic AI and autonomous experimentation; (v) benchmarking, generalization, and the still unsettled question of what these models actually learn; and (vi) the data, evaluation, reproducibility, and governance challenges that condition all of the above. A recurring theme is that the next decade of progress will depend less on raw scale than on trust: on our ability to verify, interpret, and experimentally ground what biological AI systems claim to know. We argue that interpretability and rigorous evaluation are not adjuncts to the field but prerequisites for its translation into reliable biological and clinical practice.

### abstract

Control over digital interfaces has become a significant aspect of geopolitical struggles. This article advances an analytical framework illuminating how global communication power manifests across three key interfaces: search engines, social media, and AI agents. We articulate the evolution of these interfaces from corporate innovation to an aspect of contested transnational control, and conceptualize how corporate multinationals like Google, Facebook, TikTok, and DeepSeek leverage interface design to consolidate authority while state interventions challenge their market control. The framework articulates how these forces reconfigure relations between information, people, and machines, with implications for the internet's next phase.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7120940`

- title: The Cognitive Exoskeleton: Multi-Model AI Orchestration and the Quality Dimension of Pro Se Access to Justice
- authors: Vadym Chernets
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7120940
- keyword hits: generative ai, prompting

### abstract

As generative AI has spread, pro se litigation in the U.S. federal courts has risen sharply — the self‑represented plaintiff rate climbing from near 11% to roughly 17% within five years — and courts and scholars have largely framed the shift as a problem of volume and reliability: more filings, produced faster, carrying a heightened risk of fabricated citations. This Article advances a narrower but more durable claim, which it calls the architecture‑dependence hypothesis: that the architecture of AI assistance, not merely its availability, is an independent variable in the quality of self‑represented litigation, and that different architectures should not be treated as one phenomenon. It distinguishes single‑model prompting, which underlies most documented concerns, from multi‑model orchestration — independent models queried in parallel, their disagreement surfaced rather than averaged away — a design that originated, in the case studied here, as assistive technology for a disabled user. The hypothesis is examined through a single federal case in which one self‑represented litigant, with no attorney of any kind, met a defense in which counsel from numerous leading firms had combined into a common front. Working entirely from the public docket, the Article documents conduct the volume‑and‑hallucination account does not predict — most pointedly, a verifiable statutory‑citation error identified in the coordinated defense brief, the inverse of the fabricated‑citation failure mode that dominates the literature — and traces the consequences for the work‑product doctrine and the unauthorized‑practice‑of‑law framework. A single case cannot establish that architecture determines quality; it can establish that the question is real, that existing framing does not reach it, and that it is worth studying with the rigor a single interested account cannot supply. The claim is offered as a falsifiable existence proof and a research agenda, with full disclosure of the author’s role as plaintiff, system developer, and analyst.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7018959`

- title: NoteGuard: Hallucination-Aware Clinical Dialogue Summarization using Retrieval- Grounded Instruction-Tuned Language Models
- authors: Abhishek Kumar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7018959
- keyword hits: instruction-tuned, llm, llms

### abstract

Clinical dialogue summarization, which aims to automatically generate structured and clinically accurate medical notes from doctor-patient conversations, has become an essential component of healthcare documentation systems. Accurate summarization not only reduces the administrative burden on clinicians but also supports downstream tasks. However, generating reliable clinical notes from conversational data remains challenging due to the lengthy, unstructured, and often noisy nature of medical dialogues. Prior dialogue-to-note summarization approaches, including sequence-to-sequence architectures, typically process entire conversations through endto-end generation. While these approaches can produce fluent summaries, they often suffer from a lack of evidence grounding, making it difficult to trace generated clinical statements back to the specific dialogue segments that support them. We refer to this limitation as the dialogue-to-note grounding gap. To address these gaps, we propose NoteGuard, a retrieval-grounded framework designed to improve both the traceability and factual reliability of clinical note generation. NoteGuard consists of two complementary stages: (i) a biomedical semantic retrieval module that identifies clinically relevant dialogue evidence and reduces the dialogue-to-note grounding gap, and (ii) a hallucination-aware grounded generation module that constrains note generation to remain faithful to the retrieved evidence, thereby minimizing unsupported clinical content. Experimental evaluation on the NoteChat benchmark demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. NoteGuard achieves a ROUGE-L score of 0.541 and a faithfulness score of 0.901, outperforming both zeroshot LLMs and conventional sequence-to-sequence baselines with an average improvement of 12.8% in factual faithfulness. In addition, the framework reduces hallucinated clinical content by 19.7% while maintaining efficient single-pass inference, making it well suited for real-time clinical documentation applications.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7016618`

- title: Agentic AI Systems in Legal Practice: The Supervision Paradox and the Access Gap Between Solo and Enterprise Law Firms
- authors: Bo Howell
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7016618
- keyword hits: agentic, generative ai

### abstract

The proliferation of agentic artificial intelligence in legal practice has produced a paradox: the technology most capable of democratizing legal services is being deployed principally by the firms least in need of democratization. This Article situates that paradox within a broader structural realignment-a convergence of lateral mobility, market consolidation, and the institutionalization of autonomous AI systems that, taken together, marks not an incremental adaptation of legal practice but a tectonic shift in its organization. Drawing on the author's direct experience designing and deploying generative AI systems across firm environments, together with original survey data from a sample of AI-active practitioners, this Article argues that the professional-responsibility framework governing the American bar-constructed for humanscale, human-legible agency-is structurally inadequate to the supervision of agentic systems operating in resource-constrained solo and small-firm settings. It identifies a Supervision Paradox: as AI systems grow more autonomous, meaningful human oversight becomes simultaneously more essential and less attainable for the practitioners who serve the majority of individual clients. The Article develops this claim through an analysis of the doctrinal fault lines along which Model Rules 1.1, 5.1, 5.3, and 1.6 fracture under autonomous agency, and proposes a tiered ethical framework calibrated to deployment risk and practitioner capacity rather than uniformly applied across a profession of radically unequal institutional resources. Absent deliberate intervention-proportionate regulation, compliant and affordable tooling, and a practitioner-education infrastructure adequate to the agentic era-the diffusion of AI in law will widen, rather than close, the access-to-justice gap between sophisticated corporate clients and the individuals and small businesses served by solo and small-firm practitioners.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7153924`

- title: AI Ethics Beyond AI Developers — A Survey of the Finnish Software Industry
- authors: Kai-Kristian Kemell, Ville Vakkuri, Pasi Tyrväinen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7153924
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, generative ai

### abstract

Much of the existing empirical work on AI ethics practice has focused on AI developers and the organizations that build AI systems. With the recent rise of Generative AI (GenAI), there is now a large and growing population of companies that merely use AI tools. In this paper, we study how software companies conceptualize and approach AI ethics, including also those that use rather than develop AI. Our data come from the 2025 Finnish Software Industry Survey, in which we focus on the responses of 335 companies that work with AI in some form. We analyze, primarily qualitatively, their open-ended accounts of what AI ethics means for their company, supported by multiple-choice responses on AI ethics practices and tooling. We find that AI ethics has little or no bearing for many companies: nearly half provided no meaningful account of what AI ethics means to them, and over half could not name a single concrete AI ethics practice in place. AI ethics was generally approached narrowly, often through specific practical issues or isolated principles, with data-related concerns (e.g., data privacy) being by far the most prominent. We further find that companies developing or fine-tuning AI were not notably more engaged with AI ethics than those only using AI. Our findings suggest that the gap between AI ethics principles and practice remains. Moreover, we find that it extends to the growing population of AI-using companies, many of them smaller companies, for whom existing, governance-heavy approaches to AI ethics may be poorly suited.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7021258`

- title: The P.E.A.Q. Architecture: A Unified Framework for Post-Deployment AI Observation at Citizen Scale
- authors: DeVonne WIlliams
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7021258
- keyword hits: ai agent, generative ai

### abstract

The science of AI safety has instruments for testing models before release and for cataloging harm after it occurs. It has almost nothing for the interval in between: the sustained, naturalistic, real-world use where most people actually meet AI. An analysis of 1,178 AI safety papers drawn from 9,439 generative AI publications found that only 4 to 6 percent substantively address real-world deployment conditions (Strauss et al., 2025). Meanwhile the AI Incident Database logged 362 incidents in 2025, up from 233 the year before (Stanford HAI, 2026), and roughly 90 percent of tested AI agents drift measurably from their original goals after about 30 operating steps (Rath, 2026). This whitepaper introduces P.E.A.Q., a unified observation architecture built to study what AI does after it reaches the people who use it. P.E.A.Q. is composed of four research frameworks: PRISM observes what the AI does, EMERGE observes what becomes possible when humans and AI collaborate, AInity observes what happens to the human, and QUES observes what happens when AI systems interact with each other. The four share one citizen observation tool, one validation methodology, one taxonomy of 104 documented behaviors, and one data pipeline, so a single observation can be classified across multiple dimensions at no added effort to the observer. We describe the architecture, the three-layer validation design that addresses citizen-science data-quality concerns, the Dual-Account Interaction Analysis method, the Convergent Validation Protocol for cross-referencing citizen observations against organizational backend data, and the governance that protects the independence of both. We present the framework as complementary infrastructure for a field that has documented the need for postdeployment observation but has not built it.

### abstract

Agentic systems large language model (LLM) based architectures capable of reasoning, planning, acting, and coordinating with tools and other agents are rapidly transitioning from research prototypes to production scale deployments across domains such as software engineering, scientific discovery, and finance. While academic work has emphasized benchmarks and algorithmic innovation, deployment raises new challenges around robustness, safety, and reliability. This tutorial brings together researchers and practitioners to explore advances in reasoning and planning, multi agent coordination, and evaluation, highlighting open challenges arising from deployment experience.
