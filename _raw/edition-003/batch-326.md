## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7030089`

- title: Post hoc linking of learner corpora with GenAI as a common rater: A proof-of-concept study
- authors: Taichi Yamashita
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7030089
- keyword hits: generative ai, gpt-5

### abstract

The study examined the impact of GPT’s ratings on the ability estimate of writers in the English Language Learner Insight, Proficiency and Skills Evaluation (ELLIPSE) corpus. The study also linked the ELLIPSE corpus and the International Corpus Network of Asian Learners of English Global Rating Archives (ICNALE GRA) corpus by asking GPT-5.2 to assign scores to essays in the two corpora according to the respective rubric. Results suggested that ELLIPSE writers’ ability was estimated with greater precision with GPT’s ratings than only with the original ratings. ELLIPSE writers were largely equivalent to ICNALE’s B1-level writers, though clearly outperformed by ICNALE’s B2-level writers. Furthermore, ELLIPSE raters were more lenient than ICNALE raters, likely reflecting the difference in rating scale design (i.e., nativelikeness vs. English-as-a-Lingua-Franca). These findings suggest generative AI’s potential as an anchoring rater that links disconnected assessment systems and place them on a common scale.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6998418`

- title: The Damning of Digital Devices: Limiting Laptops in Legal Writing
- authors: Carly Toepke, Kaitlyn Poirier
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6998418
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The legal education landscape has been reshaped by AI. With AI legal research on the rise and advanced language models becoming the go-to source for answering questions, making emails “sound more professional,” and unfortunately writing assignments for students, legal research and writing professors’ roles are becoming increasingly more AI-checker alongside teaching legal analysis. The ease at which the honor codes are broken and the grey zone of using AI to “help” with the research and drafting process have made professors’ work even more difficult. This wave of available technology and quick access to legal answers and analysis makes building legal writing skills – and grading them – complicated. While the wild west of AI technology is shaping legal education outside the classroom, the use of technology inside the classroom during lecture is similarly concerning. Anecdotal confirmation from colleagues in varying law schools and rankings confirms a consistent phenomenon: when lecture starts, students’ eyes are glued to their own screens – not to the lecturer – distracted by their own devices. Emails are sent, instant messages are shared across the classroom along with smirks between friends, online shopping is conducted, and the lecture goes ignored. This “digital distraction” creates a gap in student learning and legal writing skills. Students miss important details, fall behind, and then blame the instructor’s teaching ability. It also raises an unexpected challenge to professors, who must continue to deliver the material, while building the skills needed to pass the bar exam, all while mitigating the digital distractions self-imposed by students. A possible solution to professors’ challenges with technology in the classroom is to limit or altogether ban laptops during lecture. Cutting off the distraction at the source will enable students to engage, handwrite more effective notes, and retain the information better. While accommodations certainly must be made for students who require technology for accessibility purposes, and certain classroom activities (e.g. group work or legal research exercises) may require the use of laptops, the current generation of law students is not able to create the level of writing required for their summer internships and future practice. Other scholars might point to the pandemic, which allowed students to multi-task during online classes, as the root cause of this problem. But regardless of the cause, the digital distraction habits developed over the last few years are now impacting the ability to teach and learn legal research and writing. As writing professors, we must consider how to re-engage students to build the foundational skill of analytical reasoning using the legal writing. While acknowledging that AI can act as a steppingstone in legal research – and even legal drafting where allowed – we have to teach those skills without technology being involved. In-class writing assessments and citation exercises that do not use technology, for example, will allow professors to judge the skills of the student and not the skills of Generative AI. There have been too many sanction cases against attorneys (and judges), who have over-relied on AI. By adopting strategies to reduce screentime and digital distraction in class, we may be able to reverse the trend and bring students back to legal writing class. This struggle is what the authors have set out to overcome by creating a framework in their Legal Writing Courses through the 2025-2026 academic year and beyond that veers away from digital device usage to focus on skill-building and retention. The results of this experience - grounded in student survey feedback and professor observations - will be outlined in a pragmatic adoption roadmap for future educators to try or vary in their own classrooms.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7024353`

- title: Modeling Asymmetric Polarization through Theory-Guided LLM-Agent Simulations: System Justification and Cognitive Interventions
- authors: Wencheng Lin, Jinglong Chen, Shuaipu Chen, Zhijian Zhang, Zhenghao Liu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7024353
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Asymmetric polarization on social media can distort online public discourse by reinforcing imbalances between opposing groups. Although simulation experiments have become an important approach for studying polarization dynamics, conventional agent-based models often rely on simplified interaction rules, limiting their ability to represent the motivational and semantic processes involved in asymmetric polarization and to evaluate language-based cognitive interventions. To address this gap, this study develops a theory-guided LLM-agent simulation framework that draws on ego-, group-, and system-justification theories to model cognitively guided social interactions within a real social media network. Results suggest that asymmetric polarization can emerge through language-mediated social interactions without rigid behavior-update equations, and that higher levels of system-justification tendencies are associated with stronger and more persistent asymmetry. Additionally, intervention experiments suggest that the perspective-taking intervention is more closely associated with reducing overall polarization, whereas the self-reflective intervention appears relatively more conducive to mitigating asymmetry. These findings highlight the importance of distinguishing between overall polarization and asymmetric in platform governance. Overall, this study demonstrates the potential of theory-guided LLM-agent simulations and cognitively informed interventions for asymmetric polarization research and governance.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6899958`

- title: From Orality to Artificial Intelligence: How Communication Modalities Redistribute Cognitive Burden in Knowledge Formation
- authors: Valeriana Colon
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6899958
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) is often framed as a technological break from earlier forms of communication. This article offers a different interpretation: generative and language-mediated AI systems are communication modalities that participate in knowledge formation by changing how information is transmitted, retained, transformed, and evaluated. Drawing from scholarship on communication, memory, literacy, expertise, bounded rationality, cognitive offloading, transformative learning, and human-AI interaction, the article develops the Cognitive Burden Redistribution Framework. The framework suggests that communication modalities do not eliminate cognitive effort; they relocate it. Orality distributes knowledge through memory and social participation, writing externalizes memory, digital networks expand access while intensifying evaluation demands, and generative AI shifts portions of synthesis, analysis, and content generation into machine-mediated interaction. This shift moves human responsibility toward problem framing, verification, contextual judgment, and accountable use. The article also presents the AI-Mediated Resources Evaluation Model, which examines AI outputs through accuracy and reliability, transparency, cognitive impact, human agency, and transformative potential. A U.S. customer-service banking scenario illustrates how these models can guide practical evaluation of AI-assisted communication. The article contributes an accessible conceptual lens for researchers, professionals, and general AI users seeking to understand AI's role in communication and knowledge formation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6919719`

- title: The Geometry of the Hollow Answer: Representational Divergence and the Crisis of Humanistic Knowing
- authors: Eric Christian Hoppe
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6919719
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

The large language model did not create a crisis in the humanities. It revealed one already present. This paper argues that when a competent answer can be produced from a hollow prompt, what is exposed is not the tool's capability but the degree to which the academy had already been certifying retrieval as learning. The central formal category is the unknown known, the fourth quadrant of the standard knowledge taxonomy: certainty resting on surface expression with no verified knowledge network beneath it. Drawing on representational divergence as a heuristic formal apparatus, the paper formalizes humanistic knowledge as a directed graph GK whose edges require explicit validation, and the asserted knowledge state as a surface subgraph GK ̂ whose divergence from GK is measurable via Hausdorff distance. This structural variance, the Blaeu Rent, is shown to compound across the credentialing system, the civil service, corporate governance, and intergenerational knowledge transmission. A distributional analysis demonstrates that the gap falls hardest on communities of color, first-generation learners, and economically marginalized populations, whose differential exposure to high-stakes AI-mediated decisions amplifies the harm from biased surface expressions. The paper develops a public resource allocation model, a minimax decision framework for institutional reform, and a five-component social cost pricing function. It argues that the liberal arts corrective is not a moral or cultural argument but a strict optimization strategy: in a world where surface fluency has a marginal cost of zero, the capacity to verify GK is the only high-value signal the labor market will price. GPU-accelerated computation makes the cost of the knowledge gap formally expressible and measurable at population scale for the first time.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.02574v1`

- title: From Tensor Buffer to Distributed Memory Hierarchy: A Survey of KV Cache Management for LLM Serving
- authors: Jie Li, Tongyang Wang, Yong Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02574v1
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

The key-value (KV) cache has become a first-order memory object in LLM serving rather than a temporary per-request tensor. This survey classifies more than thirty KV-management systems and frameworks using four axes: locality, lifetime, ownership, and substrate. The axes reveal five architectural archetypes -- local-paged, disaggregated-pipeline, shared-store, memory-pool, and hybrid-tier. Once workload and hardware are fixed, ownership accounts for much of the remaining design variance among distributed systems. The survey also audits current evaluations and identifies seven missing KV-specific measurements, linking them to open problems in fault tolerance, isolation, tiered eviction, speculative decoding, MoE serving, and shared-cache semantics.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.31651v2`

- title: FARS: A Fully Automated Research System Deployed at Scale
- authors: Qiong Tang, Tianxiang Sun, Xiangkun Hu, Xiangyang Liu, Yiran Chen, Yunfan Shao, Bobo Li, Changze Lv
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31651v2
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Recent automated research systems show that language-model agents can generate hypotheses, run experiments, and write complete manuscripts, but most evidence still comes from selected examples, human-framed topics, or a few pre-defined research tasks. We present FARS (Fully Automated Research System), a fully automated AI-for-AI research system designed to operate across research topics at scale. FARS autonomously generates and advances projects through ideation, planning, experimentation, and writing, using stage-specific agents coordinated through a shared workspace that records proposals, code, logs, results, and manuscripts. In its first public deployment, FARS produced 166 complete research papers spanning 67 fine-grained AI/ML topics while preserving intermediate artifacts as an auditable corpus rather than a curated set of successes. We evaluate this corpus with 282 structured reviews from volunteer reviewers covering 140 papers, including overall ratings, sub-scores, integrity checks, and LLM-use disclosure. The reviews indicate that FARS can produce review-worthy and occasionally strong AI/ML research artifacts in a large-scale public deployment, while also exposing recurring failure modes in narrow experimental scope, methodological limitations, and integrity issues.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.31046v1`

- title: OpenLife: Toward Open-World Artificial Life with Autonomous LLM Agents
- authors: Atsushi Masumori, Itsuki Doi, Norihiro Maruyama, Ryosuke Takata, Takashi Ikegami
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31046v1
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Artificial life has explored life-like behavior on many computational substrates, but mostly in researcher-designed closed worlds. We argue that large language model (LLM) agents, with persistent memory, tool use, network access, and payment, now make it possible to move artificial life into the open social, technical, and economic world, a paradigm we call open-world Artificial Life (open-world ALIFE). Our proof-of-concept, OpenLife, surrounds a stateless LLM not with a single "smart agent" but with a society of asynchronous processes: memory, perception, evaluation, and a budget-based metabolism that makes persistence normative. With no fixed objective available, experience is appraised by open-vocabulary LLM judgment rather than scalar reward, and memory is rewired by meaning rather than frequency. Running six such agents in the open world for about twelve weeks and counting, we report the life-like dynamics that emerge: a shift from reactive to spontaneous activity, individuation into distinct agents, emergent social structure, and a first self-earned external income. We do not claim OpenLife has realized artificial life, but that open-world ALIFE is now a viable experimental paradigm and a concrete platform for studying what might cautiously be called living AI.
