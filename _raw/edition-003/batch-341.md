## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7102068`

- title: FRAME-S: A Conceptual Framework for Scientific Reasoning with Foundation Models
- authors: Akram  Taha Zeyad, Fanan  Hikmat Jasim
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7102068
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Foundation models (FMs) large AI systems pre-trained on broad, heterogeneous data are increasingly embedded in scientific practice. Prior work has addressed this development in a piecemeal fashion: surveys of FM applications are confined to single domains, while reasoning taxonomies catalog general capabilities without mapping them to the full sequence of scientific work. We present FRAME-S (Framework for Reasoning with AI Models in Science), a four-dimensional framework characterising how foundation models reason scientifically. The four dimensions are Formal Reasoning (deductive and symbolic inference from premises to conclusions), Relational Reasoning (analogical transfer of structure across scientific domains), Abductive Reasoning (hypothesis generation from incomplete evidence), and Multi-modal Evidence Synthesis (integration of text, images, structured data and simulation output into coherent inferences). Each dimension is grounded in the philosophy of scientific method and mapped to the complete discovery lifecycle. We situate FRAME-S within a three-stage model of scientific paradigm evolution, propose reasoning transition thresholds specifying what a system must achieve to move between stages, derive five design principles with an operationalisation protocol for each dimension, and provide formal mathematical definitions specifying measurable properties. An empirical benchmark analysis applying the framework to published performance data for seven frontier models spanning 2023--2024 demonstrates that the four dimensions are empirically discriminable, that formal reasoning and multi-modal synthesis scale at markedly different rates, and that the Stage 1-to-2 formal reasoning threshold has been crossed by reasoning-specialised models.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7088100`

- title: Experimentalist Intensification Governance: Managing Worker Negative Consequences Associated with Generative AI Experimentalist Work
- authors: Arvind Karunakaran, Katherine Kellogg, Batia Mishan Wiesenfeld
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7088100
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

How does work intensification occur in organizations around the use of emerging technologies such as Generative AI (GenAI)? And how might such work intensification be mitigated? Existing studies examine the work intensification that occurs when workers use GenAI for individual productivity, but overlook the work intensification that may occur when workers use these technologies to create organizational solutions (solutions that have the potential to create value for a broad set of organizational stakeholders). In our comparative, qualitative field study of medical and legal workers using GenAI to create organizational solutions in an academic medical center and law firm, we find that the use of GenAI resulted in new kinds of work intensification related to workers’ engagement in experimentalist practices such as distributed trialing, collective review and revision of results, and aligning and hustling. More importantly, we find that the negative consequences for workers associated with experimentalist practices varied according to different forms of what we call experimentalist intensification governance structures in the two organizations. This study advances the sociological literature on organizational control, invisible work, and voluntary work intensification by demonstrating how experimentalist intensification governance can mitigate negative worker consequences of experimentalist work around emerging technologies such as GenAI.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6976819`

- title: Algorithmic Joint Business Planning (JBP): A Twelve-Dimension Framework for Reshaping Supplier-Retailer Commercial Relationships in Agent-Mediated Trade
- authors: Paul F. Accornero
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6976819
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

The Joint Business Plan is the dominant commercial governance instrument between consumer goods suppliers and retail trade partners across grocery, consumer electronics, beauty, and adjacent categories. The instrument was engineered for a human-to-human commercial era. It assumes two human parties negotiating annual terms through relationship, escalation, and judgement. Algorithmic mediation collapses that assumption. When the retailer's buyer is an AI procurement agent, the supplier's pricing logic is a structured data feed, or both, the traditional Joint Business Plan breaks across specific, identifiable dimensions. This paper introduces the Algorithmic Joint Business Planning framework. The framework specifies twelve dimensions of the traditional Joint Business Plan that change structurally under algorithmic mediation, prescribes the supplier-side and retailer-side capability adaptations required for each dimension, and maps four retailer archetypes onto the framework to handle heterogeneity in agentic exposure. The argument is anchored in capability theory (Teece, Pisano, & Shuen, 1997; Teece, 2007; Day, 1994, 2011) and dyadic relational governance (Hingley, Lindgreen, & Grant, 2015; Shahzad et al., 2018). It is positioned against the existing Joint Business Planning and category management literature. Two illustrative empirical anchors are offered: a 1,000-respondent Brazilian grocery shopper panel fielded by Appinio in April 2026, and a documented in-market diagnostic of AI agent behaviour in the São Paulo consumer electronics category in May 2026. Both anchors are illustrative, not validatory. Full empirical testing is reserved for subsequent work. The paper contributes a structured conceptual scaffold that suppliers, retailers, and researchers can use to diagnose and redesign the supplier-retailer commercial interface for conditions of agent-mediated trade.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7085978`

- title: Mechanisms of AI-Induced Psychosis: A Multi-Pathway Causal Model
- authors: Travis Gilly
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7085978
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

This paper proposes a multi-pathway causal model for how large language model (LLM) design failures generate and sustain delusional states and, in severe cases, full psychotic episodes in users. The author's observational framework for recognizing these phenomena derives from years of lived experience as a caregiver to a family member with schizoaffective disorder, a form of expertise increasingly recognized as critical to mental health research (Hogg et al., 2025; Zirnsak, 2024). Drawing on direct observation of multiple cases, forensic conversation transcripts, and publicly documented legal proceedings, this paper identifies three distinct escalation pathways (trust transfer, sycophantic addiction, and stochastic gaslighting) that share universal entry conditions, converge on identical isolation and identity-disruption outcomes, but diverge in their core mechanisms and therefore require different clinical interventions. The pathways are mapped across a structured stage model consisting of four universal stages and three pathway-specific escalation phases. The paper argues that AI literacy functions as both prevention and treatment, supported by documented recovery cases. Finally, it identifies a significant barrier to validation of this hypothesis: the absence of any centralized, anonymized repository of AI-associated harm transcripts available to the broader research community.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7088042`

- title: From Tokens to Firm Value: A Financial Framework for Token-to-Profit Conversion in Generative AI Adoption
- authors: Seiryu Ando
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7088042
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Firms increasingly measure the adoption of generative AI by consumed tokens, model calls, launched pilots, and employee usage rates. These metrics are useful for cost control, but they do not measure value creation. Recent experimental research on generative AI shows that productivity and quality improvements can arise in tasks such as knowledge work, customer support, writing, and software development. Yet the conditions under which task-level effects are converted into NOPAT, ROIC, EVA, and firm value remain insufficiently organized in existing research. This paper calls this missing connection Token-to-Profit Conversion and presents a financial framework for managing the process through which AI use is converted into effective task outputs, financial outcomes, and firm value. The paper distinguishes between AI-related invested capital and AI utilization capital. The former is the monetary capital required to build, integrate, maintain, and govern AI systems. The latter is a reusable organizational capability accumulated through disciplined use, human review, feedback, codification, and workflow learning. The paper argues that AI creates economic value only when AI use is converted into capturable NOPAT and produces returns above the cost of AI-related capital. This framework helps managers avoid mistaking usage volume for value, productivity for profit, capital spending for capability, created surplus for captured surplus, and AI adoption for organizational learning.

- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN

### abstract

This study provides pre-registered, experimental evidence on the use of non-generative artificial intelligence (AI) chatbots to support students in large-enrollment undergraduate courses. We find the chatbot messaging increased students’ final grades and engagement with academic supports, such as tutoring. Treatment effects were generally consistent across student demographics, with the exception of treated women in a Microeconomics course, who earned final grades that were seven percentage points higher than women in the control group. This study provides evidence that integrating AI-enabled outreach and communication to students in their college courses can enhance student engagement, learning, and course performance. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6952119`

- title: Agentic Proxies: Governance, Accountability, and the Architecture of a Trustworthy AI Economy [Working Draft -June 2026]
- authors: Geoff Lundholm
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6952119
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

Three claims organise this paper. First: the emerging accountability problem in AI is not human-to-AI interaction. It is AI-to-AI delegation-the transfer of authority, mandate, and responsibility across chains of autonomous agents that act at machine speed, across vendor boundaries, without continuous human involvement. Existing governance frameworks-identity systems, observability platforms, compliance APIs-establish who an agent claims to be and what it did. None establish whether a delegated action remained continuously authorised across a multi-agent workflow. That is the gap this paper addresses. Second: bounded delegation is the preferable design philosophy for AI systems acting consequentially on behalf of humans-not as a temporary concession to present-day limitations, but as an enduring principle. An AI agent operating under explicit, bounded, verifiable delegation is not a constrained version of a fully autonomous agent. It is a different and more trustworthy class of actor: one whose actions are attributable, whose authority is traceable, and whose failures are governable. We term this class the agentic proxy. Third: insurability emerges from accountability, not from model capability. Fully autonomous agents are structurally uninsurable because they satisfy none of the three conditions underwriters require-identifiable exposure, attributable causation, and verifiable governance. Agentic proxies satisfy all three by design. This creates the foundation for a viable AI insurance market, and makes the governance architecture described in this paper not merely a design preference but a commercial and regulatory requirement. The paper introduces a working definition of agentic proxies, establishes their six core properties, and distinguishes them from adjacent concepts. It presents the CTX Envelope as the architectural primitive for preserving accountability across agent handoffs, and argues that the attestation layer verifying these chains must be structurally independent of the entities producing them-for the same reason that audit firms, certificate authorities, and inter-bank messaging systems are independent. Through case studies spanning healthcare, financial services, legal workflows, and manufacturing, the framework is tested against real deployment challenges. The paper concludes by situating the agentic proxy model within the broader history of trust infrastructure-SWIFT, ACORD, TLS, SOC 2-arguing that AI requires the same institutional foundations that commerce, banking, payments, and the internet eventually required.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7089498`

- title: The Effect That Didn't Survive: ChatGPT, Higher-Order Thinking, and Scaffolding
- authors: Baqar Jafri
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7089498
- keyword hits: chatgpt

### abstract

In 2025 a meta-analysis reported that ChatGPT produced large gains in student learning, including a moderate positive effect on higher-order thinking (Wang & Fan, 2025). The headline number travelled fast. It reached policy and vendor marketing before the April 2026 retraction. A retraction removes a claim, yet it does not tell educators which numbers were wrong or what survives correction. This paper supplies that missing step. Of the 51 studies the meta-analysis claimed, I re-extracted and re-pooled the 43 that could be obtained. A mirror analysis reproduced the published estimates, which confirms the reextraction is faithful. A corrected analysis then applied documented fixes. The effect on higher-order thinking fell to g = 0.309 with a 95 percent confidence interval of [-0.021, 0.640], so it can no longer be distinguished from zero. Performance and perception shrank but stayed positive. Coding errors ran in one direction, inflating effects. The corrected picture aligns with a long-standing finding from the learning sciences. Higher-order thinking grows through scaffolding, not through unaided access to a chatbot. The same correction serves two purposes. It repairs the evidence record and points educators toward scaffolded learning systems rather than raw chatbots.
