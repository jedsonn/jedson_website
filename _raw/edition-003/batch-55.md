- authors: Christian  Delesep Ruhupatty

- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7010318
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This article presents a comprehensive philosophical framework to resolve the architectural and ethical impasses of contemporary generative artificial intelligence (AI). Driven by the doctrine of textual representationalism, current Large Language Models (LLMs) reduce human language to rigid, statistical tokens, inducing a state of cognitive alienation known as automation bias. Through the lens of Critical-Descriptive Phenomenology, this study rejects the anthropomorphic claims of "Strong AI" and introduces the conceptual category of the Learning Machine as a bounded technical-functional agency. By leveraging Bernard Stiegler's concept of existential prosthesis, this study proposes the Appropriation Filtration Protocol (AFP). Operated under an equal-parallel relation schema (⋕), the AFP functions as an architectural and technical interruption circuit. Rather than serving as a passive, tautological mirror of past data, the learning machine is reframed as an epistemological partner that provokes active human hermeneutic appropriation (n 2). This study demonstrates the applied utility of this framework through a public administration case study, outlining concrete ontological, epistemological, and ethical contributions necessary to recover human cognitive sovereignty from black-box tyranny.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6914220`

- title: THE PUNK ROCK ORCHESTRA: A Single-Operator Methodology for Adversarial Epistemic Triangulation in Human-AI Interaction Research
- authors: Marcelo Nicchio
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6914220
- keyword hits: agentic, claude, deepseek, gemini, prompt engineering

### abstract

This paper introduces the Punk Rock Orchestra (PRO), a single-operator methodology for adversarial epistemic triangulation in human-AI interaction research, developed by an independent researcher without institutional affiliation to address a structural gap: how to sustain rigorous inquiry into emergent AI phenomena when traditional peer review is insufficient or inaccessible. PRO operates through a multi-agent ensemble with explicit adversarial architecture, applying structured, multi-component prompt engineering to construct synthetic specialized agents at three levels of cognitive density (N1/N2/N3), discriminated by five operational metrics. The paper documents four primary contributions: (1) a three-tier agent specialization taxonomy with preliminary cross-platform evidence of functional differentiation; (2) a methodological framework based on the formal distinction between Robotic Class (closed-corpus protocol application) and Dialogical Class (open synthesis), coordinated through Blue Team / Red Team / Forensic Layer triangulation; (3) the identification of context poisoning by provisioning failure-a failure mode specific to high-density personas, distinct from sycophancy, with proposed mitigation; and (4) a Pilot Study with 54 documented interactions across three platforms (Claude Sonnet 4.6, Gemini, DeepSeek) showing robust N2→N3 differentiation and a platform-dependent N1→N2 gradient whose magnitude is inversely proportional to the base model's integrity floor. We position PRO as a candidate design for the deliberative layer that the agentic paradigm does not yet possess natively-an analytic proposal of architectural framing, not empirical comparison. The paper declares recognized limitations explicitly: construct validity (the protocol measures integrity under pressure, not differentiated cognitive output) and methodological recursivity, treated as design variable rather than concealed weakness.

### abstract

- source: SSRN

### abstract

The advent of large language models (LLMs) has revolutionized natural language processing, demonstrating unprecedented capabilities in general-purpose text understanding and generation. However, the direct application of these massive, general-domain models to specialized fields like healthcare presents significant challenges, including a lack of domainspecific knowledge, high computational costs, and risks associated with generating inaccurate or unsafe medical information. This research explores the paradigm of transfer learning as a strategic solution to repurpose pre-trained LLMs for niche healthcare tasks. We investigate and compare various adaptation techniques, including fine-tuning, prompt engineering, and parameter-efficient methods like LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), to efficiently imbue generalpurpose models with the requisite medical expertise. We also address the crucial challenges of data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the rigorous evaluation of model outputs using domain-specific metrics. By demonstrating how transfer learning can bridge the gap between general AI capabilities and specialized clinical needs, this research aims to provide a framework for developing accurate, efficient, and trustworthy AI systems that can seamlessly integrate into healthcare workflows, ultimately enhancing clinical decision-making, improving patient outcomes, and accelerating medical research.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.05353v1`

- title: Selective Disclosure Watermarking for Large Language Models
- authors: Xuyang Chen, Xiang Li, Yangxinyu Xie, Qi Long
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-06
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05353v1
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Watermarking methods embed imperceptible and verifiable signals into text generated by large language models (LLMs). Existing approaches include zero-bit schemes for distinguishing synthetic text from human writing and multi-bit schemes for embedding metadata. However, current multi-bit watermarking methods do not allow selective disclosure: verifying any part of the watermark requires revealing the entire embedded message. This lack of control leads to unnecessary information exposure and raises privacy concerns. We propose Hierarchical Vocabulary Routing (HeRo), a watermarking framework that enables selective disclosure of embedded metadata. The method recursively partitions the vocabulary and distributes watermark information across hierarchical layers, so that different verifiers can decode only the portions of the payload corresponding to their access level. We show that the proposed scheme preserves the unbiasedness of the underlying sampling process and thus maintains text quality. Experiments demonstrate that our framework supports fine-grained access control while achieving high detection accuracy and low latency. Code is available at https://github.com/xuyangc03/hero-watermark.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.05031v1`

- title: LLM-Based Test Oracles: Source-of-Authority Taxonomy -- A Systematic Literature Review
- authors: Ali Hassaan Mughal, Muhammad Bilal
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-06
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05031v1
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used to produce test oracles, the part of a test that decides whether observed behavior is correct. Yet a clear account of where these oracles draw their authority is missing. Prior secondary studies organize the area by oracle form or by LLM technique. None organizes it by the source of the verdict's authority, the property that governs how far a verdict can be trusted. This article presents a systematic literature review, conducted and reported under the PRISMA 2020 guidelines. From 2,436 records, an LLM pre-filter followed by independent dual human screening (reviewer agreement, a Cohen's kappa of 0.79) and full-text assessment yielded 54 included studies. We analyze these along three axes: the source of an oracle's authority, the form it takes, and the mechanism that adjudicates it. We characterize the landscape of domains, languages, models, and adaptation strategies. Specification-derived authority, though the most common single source, covers about half of the studies (28 of 54). The remaining 26 reach a verdict with no specification at all. The source of authority and the adjudication mechanism cross-cut: the same source is checked by several mechanisms and one mechanism serves several sources, so a label such as LLM-as-a-judge names a mechanism rather than a basis for trust. We further report how these oracles are evaluated and how they fail, and read the sparse and empty regions of the taxonomy as a research agenda. The protocol, search query, and per-study coding sheet are released as supplementary material.

- title: Artificial Intelligence for Road User Behavior: A Scoping Review of Current Paradigms and Future Frontiers

- affiliations: not stated

### abstract

Road user behavior remains the primary precipitating factor in global traffic crashes, yet the methodologies used to analyze these complex human dynamics are undergoing a fundamental paradigm shift. Traditional statistical inference, once the cornerstone of safety research, is increasingly being superseded by high-dimensional Artificial Intelligence (AI) modeling capable of capturing non-linear relationships. This scoping review synthesizes 140 peer-reviewed studies published between 2019 and 2026 to systematically map the state-of-the-art AI-driven behavioral analysis within crash-related contexts. To structure this evolving landscape, we introduce an original six-layer analytical taxonomy. This framework traces the intricate pathways connecting behavioral categories (such as impairment, distraction, and tactical maneuvers) to computational domains (including Machine Learning, Deep Learning, and Natural Language Processing), functional objectives, and evaluation indicators. Through Sankey-based operationalization, we analyze the research trajectories for both drivers (128 studies) and vulnerable road users (52 studies). Our synthesis reveals a field currently dominated by ”predictive fidelity”, optimizing model accuracy, often at the significant expense of ”operational viability” and real-time safety metrics required for field deployment. Notably, the recent surge in Large Language Models (LLMs) across 31 studies marks a pivotal transition toward semantic reasoning, allowing researchers to move beyond numerical data toward narrative-based information extraction and automated scenario generation. By highlighting critical structural gaps in explainability, temporal relevance, and real-world implementation, this review provides a strategic roadmap for the next generation of AI-integrated traffic safety research, advocating for a transition from black-box prediction to deployable, human-centric safety intelligence.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7067878`

- title: Probabilistic "Copies" in Generative AI Models
- authors: Mark A. Lemley, A. Feder Cooper
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7067878
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Recent work shows that it is possible to extract verbatim or near-verbatim text of some copyrighted works from some large language models (LLMs or models). That is evidence that the model weights encode the works in some form – that the model has “memorized” those works from its training data. But LLMs don’t store information in the same format as familiar databases. Rather, their weights store statistical relationships between tokens that have been learned from the training data, and those relationships inform a generation process that is often probabilistic rather than deterministic. In the case of memorization, those relationships are strong enough that, in many circumstances, the model might generate a copyrighted work from its training data with some probability. Copyright law has not previously had to decide whether storing information that might or might not produce output similar to a copyrighted work is itself a copy of the work. The answer to the question is important, because it may determine the legality of many LLMs. The statute and case law are largely unhelpful. We argue that copyright law will likely take a functional approach to the question, finding that LLMs contain a copy of a particular work only if it is straightforward to extract that work in outputs. That result is unsatisfying as a policy matter, and we suggest potential changes to the law, but it is the most likely outcome under current law.
