## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7166809`

- title: AgroYachay: An open-source IoT and large-language-model platform supporting agronomic and economic decision-making for Andean smallholders
- authors: Fred Torres-Cruz, Richar Andre Vilca Solorzano, Dina Maribel Yana Yucra, Vladimiro Ibañez Quispe, Eduardo Leuman Fuentes Navarro
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7166809
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Low-cost agricultural IoT is increasingly affordable, yet turning raw telemetry into practical decisions for smallholders remains a challenge, especially in Andean regions with limited technical support and language access. We present AgroYachay, an open-source platform integrating IoT sensing, large language models and explainable analytics: continuous field monitoring, AI-assisted pest and disease diagnosis, crop-specific recommendations, weather-informed scheduling, and transparent estimation of crop productivity and economic returns — through a multilingual interface (Spanish, Quechua, Aymara) that lowers the language barrier for indigenous farmers. Built on ESP32 nodes, a Flask/PostgreSQL backend and a Groq-hosted LLM layer, it is the open cloud counterpart of AgroCommish. A preliminary validation supports the pest module as a field triage aid. Released under the MIT licence with an archived DOI.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7055340`

- title: Predicting Art Market Prices with AI-Based Aesthetic Evaluation
- authors: Igor Mandel
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7055340
- keyword hits: claude, gemini, generative ai

### abstract

Three generative AI systems-Meta AI, Gemini (Google), and Claude (Anthropic)-were trained on representative examples and subsequently employed to assign aesthetic scores to two distinct sets of paintings. The first set comprised 80 works by 8 artists that had previously been evaluated by human respondents several years prior. The second set consisted of 155 works by five prominent artists (Claude Monet, Salvador Dalí, Andy Warhol, Joan Miró, and Marc Chagall), with 31 paintings per artist, subdivided into the 15 highest-priced and 16 lowest-priced works at auction. In both cases, the aesthetic scores were used to predict auction prices that had been unknown to both the human respondents and the AI systems at the time of evaluation. The principal finding is that AI-generated scores, when applied in a specific analytical framework, predict auction prices substantially more accurately than human aesthetic judgments, with artist-specific model accuracy ranging from 63% to 85%. Additional properties of AI scoring are examined, including pronounced inter-system disagreement, which permits inferences about the distinct evaluative "profiles" of each system. To our knowledge, this type of analysis has not previously been undertaken. The results contribute to the longstanding debate over whether art buyers are driven primarily by aesthetic experience or by other factors. They also raise a methodological question regarding which AI system should be considered most reliable for this purpose.
