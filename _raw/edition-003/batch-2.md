- title: Application of Large Language Models in Clinical Management of Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-cell Therapy Related Adverse Effects
- authors: Xiaojie Liang, Baiwei Luo, BY Lin, Yunong Yang, Jia Cong, Wei Wang, Guangzhong Yang, Dongfeng Zeng
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6860902
- keyword hits: claude, deepseek, gemini, gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Background CAR-T therapy has reshaped the treatment of haematological malignancies, but its toxicities remain difficult to recognise and manage. Whether large language models (LLMs) can support the management of CAR-T therapy-related adverse effects is unclear. Methods We designed 63 open-ended questions to test the 10 widely used LLMs, covering diagnosis, assessment, and treatment for eight common adverse events (AEs) and the general management in CAR-T therapy. Responses were independently scored by ten clinicians, using prespecified 4-point Likert scales for accuracy and coverage. Between-model differences were assessed using the Kruskal-Wallis test followed by Dunn’s pairwise comparisons, and inter-rater agreement was measured using Kendall’s coefficient of concordance. Findings Overall, LLMs approximates clinician-level performance, with a mean accuracy score of 3.05 and a mean coverage score of 3.02. Only 0.9% (117/12600) of LLM responses were rated 1 (completely inaccurate or incomplete). Gemini 2.0 Flash Thinking Experimental achieved the highest overall performance among the evaluated models, with an accuracy score of 3.34 and a coverage score of 3.29. It outperformed the other LLMs across most toxicity domains, except for CAR-T therapy-related coagulopathy, for which Claude 3.5 Sonnet Formal Style showed the best performance. Notably, DeepSeek-V3 and DeepSeek-R1 achieved performance comparable to proprietary models such as GPT-4o and GPT-o1, with accuracy scores of 3.05 and 3.04 and coverage scores of 2.99 and 2.98, respectively, suggesting potential value as scalable and cost-efficient tools. Interpretation LLMs show promising potential as adjunctive tools for supporting the clinical management of CAR-T therapy-related adverse effects. Their performance across diverse toxicity scenarios suggests possible utility in improving access to structured clinical guidance, particularly in settings with limited specialist expertise.

Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and Med-PaLM are transforming healthcare by enabling clinical decision support, patient engagement, and administrative automation. This paper introduces the Enterprise LLM Governance Framework (ELGM²), a multi-layered model integrating lifecycle governance, prompt management, hallucination detection, human-in-the-loop validation, and compliance with global regulations (HIPAA, GDPR, EU AI Act, India DPDP Act, NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001). - title: Can LLMs Perform Geospatial Reasoning? A Case Study for Congestion Pricing in New York City
- authors: Qixian Zhou, Jingyi Li, Xuan Di
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6978200
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, gemini, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, qwen

### abstract

This study evaluates the geospatial reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) using New York City's Congestion Pricing Program as a case study. We benchmark four representative models (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and Qwen) on taxi-trip toll prediction under progressively richer geographic and policy inputs. The results show substantial variation between both models and the input settings. Structured location metadata provides the most reliable grounding, while excessive or redundant geographic context can introduce instability. Error analysis reveals recurring failures in temporal-policy reasoning and model-specific biases. In general, LLMs can help interpret geospatial policies when properly grounded, but they remain unsuitable as standalone, deterministic systems for enforcing transportation policies.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6659409`

- title: Large Language Models and the Information Content of Analyst Reports
- authors: Cheol-Won Yang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6659409
- keyword hits: deepseek, fine-tuning, gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llama, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper investigates whether text analysis through large language model (LLM) can provide meaningful information for future stock returns. For the text of Korean analyst reports, six LLMs are utilized to obtain sentiment score: KrFinBERT, Mixtral, Gemma2, Llama3, GPT-4o, and DeepSeek-R1. In the regression analysis, all LLM sentiment scores are significantly related to stock returns, with GPT-4o performing the best. In the calendar-time based portfolio approach, GPT-4o shows the strongest and most consistent long–short returns across different weighting schemes and return measures. Moreover, GPT-4o provides balanced contributions from both positive and negative sentiment signals, making it particularly effective in mitigating the inherent limitation of analyst reports stemming from the paucity of sell recommendations. In sentiment score changes, KrFinBERT model fine-tuned with Korean financial text shows the highest long–short returns, but its gains are largely attributable to positive sentiment. Overall, our results show that textual sentiment information obtained through LLM has predictive power for future stock returns. Among the six models, GPT-4o performs consistently well across strategies, while the strong performance of KrFinBERT highlights the effectiveness of domain-specific fine-tuning.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6827628`

- title: Back-end Sensitivity and Inferential Drift in the Hedonic Track GPT for Cumulative Animal Welfare Assessment
- authors: Ana Silvia Pedrazzani, Antonio Ostrensky, Carla  F. M. Molento
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6827628
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, gpt-5, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, prompting

### abstract

The growing use of large language models (LLMs) in animal welfare assessment raises a methodological question: whether specialised AI tools remain inferentially stable when back-end systems change invisibly over time. This study audited the Hedonic Track GPT, examining how back-end version and prompt specificity affected fidelity to the analytical object, temporal framing, quantitative pain attribution, empirical anchoring, and operational reliability. A 3 × 2 factorial computational audit was applied across Nile tilapia, broiler chickens, and beef cattle, combining three back-end versions (GPT-5.2, GPT-5.3, GPT-5.4) with two levels of scenario specificity, yielding 18 conditions. Outputs were analysed using parallel analytical workbooks derived from independent ChatGPT- and Claude-assisted extraction, alongside specialist consultation with 11 animal welfare experts evaluating fully specified cases. Prompt specificity emerged as the main determinant of inferential fidelity. Generic prompts more often induced inferential concretisation, shifting the analytical object toward a determinate slaughter episode, whereas back-end variation more strongly affected the quantitative allocation of pain across severity categories than the temporal sequence itself. Temporal segmentation remained comparatively coherent, but boundaries varied with less specific prompting. Empirical anchoring was weak, with fluent justification but limited evidential traceability. Specialist feedback indicated the system is most defensible as a structured expert-support tool within supervised workflows rather than as an autonomous quantifier of cumulative animal suffering. Overall, the findings suggest substantial heuristic value, but quantitative use requires version control, traceability, minimum-input standards, and monitoring for drift.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.15414v1`

- title: AI Trading: Evaluating Large Language Models for Technical Market Analysis
- authors: Geofrey Ntale
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-16
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15414v1
- keyword hits: claude, fine-tuning, gemini, gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llama, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as powerful tools for processing the heterogeneous information environments of modern financial markets. This paper presents a systematic, comparative evaluation of five prominent LLMs: GPT-4 Turbo, Claude 3 Opus, Gemini 1.5 Pro, Llama 3 70B, and the domain-specialized FinGPT, with respect to their capacity for technical market analysis. The evaluation spans four structured tasks: candlestick pattern recognition from OHLCV data, directional signal generation (BUY/SELL/HOLD), backtesting of signal quality through a simulated execution pipeline, and financial report comprehension. Our experimental framework employs rigorous quantitative metrics, including Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown, Sortino ratio, information coefficient, F1-score, and BLEU score. Findings from simulated backtesting indicate that GPT-4 Turbo achieves the highest annualized return and Sharpe ratio among general-purpose models, while FinGPT demonstrates competitive risk-adjusted performance due to domain-specific fine-tuning. Both models outperform a passive S&P 500 benchmark under the tested conditions. The study identifies persistent failure modes across all evaluated models, including numerical hallucination, context-window limitations, and inconsistent performance in sideways market regimes. We conclude that while LLMs hold genuine promise within AI trading systems, robust deployment requires careful task decomposition, rigorous backtesting protocols, and domain-aware fine-tuning strategies.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7153923`

- title: Leveraging LLMs for Automated Requirements Quality Verification in Automotive Software: An Empirical Industrial Study
- authors: Youssef Bikouche, Hamid El Ghazi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7153923
- keyword hits: chain-of-thought, deepseek, fine-tuning, gpt-5, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, prompt engineering, qwen

### abstract

Reviewing software requirements for quality defects is a slow and repetitive task in automotive software development. Requirements Specification Part (RSP) documents follow strict proprietary standards covering naming conventions, control flow notation, and variant applicability rules. When these rules are violated, defects propagate into later development stages and cause expensive rework. This paper studies whether Large Language Models (LLMs) can automatically detect these violations by applying a fixed set of quality rules specified entirely through prompt engineering, with no model fine-tuning. We define 25 verification rules drawn from real industrial practice and evaluate five LLMs (GPT-5-Mini, DeepSeek-V4-Flash, GPT-OSS-20B, GLM-4.7-Flash, and Qwen3-32B) across three prompt strategies (zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought) on two datasets: 80 clean and 80 defect-injected requirements taken from production automotive RSP documents, producing over 8,000 individual LLM inferences. GPT-5-Mini and DeepSeek-V4-Flash achieve a 0% false positive rate with F1 scores above 0.96 across all prompt variants. Weaker models show consistent hallucination patterns tied to specific rule families, and prompt strategy matters for these models while making no difference for strong ones. DeepSeek-V4-Flash, an open-weight model, matches the 0% false positive rate of the top tier and comes within 0.010 F1 points of GPT-5-Mini, making it a practical option for deployments where data must stay on-premise. These results show that near-production-quality requirements verification is achievable without fine-tuning for rule-based tasks of this kind, and identify a capability threshold above which model selection matters more than prompt strategy.
