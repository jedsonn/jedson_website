- title: Rogue AI Agents Won't Be Testifying-You Will: Agentic AI, IP and Liability Risks, and a Path Forward
- authors: Justin Pierce, Heather West, Brandon Phemester
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6631658
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

As enterprises deploy agentic AI systems capable of autonomous action across internal and external platforms, traditional legal assumptions about agency, authority, assent, and accountability are being tested in new ways. This article examines how agentic AI creates heightened intellectual property, contract, and liability exposure when autonomous systems act beyond intended bounds, interact with restricted systems, accept terms, access protected data, or make external representations on behalf of an organization. It argues that although AI agents are not legal actors in the conventional sense, their conduct may still be attributed to the deploying enterprise under established doctrines such as apparent authority, ratification, negligence, and product liability. The analysis identifies four core risk domains: inbound IP and data acquisition risk, loss or impairment of enterprise IP assets, external-facing liability risk, and systemic control risk. It further explains how agentic AI amplifies these exposures by operating at scale, generating persistent logs, and preserving discoverable evidence of decision pathways, prompts, access patterns, and outputs. The article contends that the central governance challenge is not whether rogue AI agents can create legal consequences, but whether organizations understand where AI-driven conduct already binds them. It concludes that effective risk mitigation requires embedding legal constraints directly into system architecture through policy-as-code, least-privilege access controls, monitoring, auditability, human oversight, and lifecycle governance. For companies operating in IP-intensive and regulated environments, agentic AI governance must be treated not as a technical afterthought but as a core component of legal strategy, compliance design, and go-to-market planning.

- title: TABLE OF CONTENTS
- authors: Afiya Amina
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6640721
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The emergence of generative artificial intelligence as a prolific creator of literary, artistic, and musical works has precipitated a profound jurisprudential crisis in copyright law. The foundational premise of intellectual property protection that authorship inheres in human creative effort is directly challenged when sophisticated AI systems produce works of demonstrable artistic and commercial value without any direct human expression in the creative act itself. This paper undertakes a rigorous comparative analysis of the legal frameworks governing copyright ownership in AI-generated works across three major jurisdictions: the United States of America, the European Union, and the Republic of India. Through doctrinal analysis of primary legislation, judicial decisions, administrative guidance, and scholarly commentary, this paper argues that existing copyright frameworks are structurally inadequate to address the novel challenges posed by AI-generated creativity. The analysis reveals that while all three jurisdictions nominally deny copyright protection to purely machine-generated works, each has adopted divergent approaches to the residual question of ownership when human intervention is present but insufficient to constitute traditional authorship. The paper further identifies a critical legislative gap in Indian law, where the absence of AI-specific provisions in the Copyright Act 1957 creates significant legal uncertainty. Drawing on comparative insights, this paper proposes a nuanced legislative framework for India that balances the incentive rationale of copyright protection with the public domain interest in freely accessible knowledge. The paper concludes that a sui generis neighbouring right, coupled with mandatory disclosure obligations and a compressed protection term, represents the most doctrinally coherent and policy-sound approach for Indian legislative reform.

- title: A Hybrid Early-Warning System for Inflation in an Emerging Market: Combining Econometric Models, an Agent-Based Decomposition with Heterogeneous Expectations, a Large Language Model, and a Multi-Output Agent Architecture
- authors: Esteban Labastidas
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6625578
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

We develop and evaluate a hybrid early-warning system for year-over-year (YoY) inflation in Colombia that combines four econometric models (ARIMA, LASSO, ElasticNet, and a weighted ensemble), a reduced-form VAR, an agent-based model with heterogeneous expectations and tradable/non-tradable pass-through (ABM v2), a large language model (LLM) forecaster, and a multi-output agent architecture, integrated through Dynamic Model Averaging (DMA). We evaluate the system on a rolling out-of-sample backtest from February 2010 to March 2026 (n ≈ 194 months) spanning the 2021-2023 inflation surge and its ongoing disinflation. Five contributions emerge. First, an identity-based monthly-to-YoY decomposition applied uniformly across reduced-form models reduces MAE by 15-20% relative to direct YoY forecasting without adding variables. Second, regime-conditional analysis shows that MAE is 2.0-3.0 times larger in surge regimes than in stable regimes across all models. Third, an ABM with regime-dependent heterogeneous expectations reduces full-sample MAE from 0.337 pp (v1) to 0.268 pp (v2,-20.4%) and surge-regime MAE from 0.585 pp to 0.404 pp (-31.0%), with Diebold-Mariano statistic 6.21 (p < 0.001). The accompanying four-channel shock decomposition attributes the 2021-2023 surge primarily to amplification of wage (1.96×) and expectations (1.82×) channels rather than direct import pass-through (1.36×). Fourth, a structured-reasoning LLM forecaster is validated via live-API replication and a 60-call fake-date test; the non-monotonic MAE pattern is shown to reflect target-volatility confounding rather than look-ahead bias. Fifth, a multi-output agent architecture predicts BanRep monetary policy decisions with 77.3% direction accuracy (17/22) and 63.6% exact-magnitude match (14/22) on 2022-2026 meetings, with all prediction errors concentrated at cycle inflection points. The full DMA ensemble achieves MAE 0.26 pp and 94.8% coverage at horizon h = 1.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6625958`

- title: Trending Now, Vision Later: Influencer CEOs and Managerial Myopia
- authors: Kun Luo, Xikai Chen, Bo Qin, Shihu Zhong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6625958
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

This study examines how social media visibility reshapes managerial decision horizons by investigating the impact of influencer CEOs on short-termism in accounting disclosures. Drawing on a large panel of Chinese A-share listed firms (2009-2024), we develop two refined measures: a classification of CEO online visibility based on verified Weibo activity and influence metrics, and a text-based indicator of managerial myopia extracted from Management's Discussion and Analysis (MD&A) sections using a supervised RA-CFGPT large language model trained on manually annotated sentences. These innovations enable precise identification of both CEO social-media prominence and short-term disclosure tendencies. We find that firms led by influencer CEOs exhibit significantly higher levels of managerial myopia in their MD&A narratives. Two mechanisms drive this effect: influencer CEOs adopt more assertive and promotional disclosure tones (consistent with overconfidence from algorithmic reinforcement), and audience-driven traffic pressure amplifies demands for immediate, visible results. The association is stronger among financially constrained firms, younger CEOs, and those with shorter tenure, but attenuated in industries where new media dynamics naturally complement long-term brand-building. Extensive robustness checksincluding instrumental variables, propensity score matching, placebo tests, alternative measures, and quasi-experimental evidence-support the findings. Overall, the results illuminate social media as a distinct, algorithmically mediated source of short-term pressure that operates alongside traditional market forces. By linking CEO influencer status to distortions in accounting disclosures, this study advances our understanding of how digital visibility practices influence the behavioral and organizational roles of accounting information in the attention economy. It highlights important implications for disclosure quality, corporate governance, and the societal construction of managerial accountability in digitally mediated organizations.

- source: SSRN

### abstract

Personal branding has matured from a self-help concept into a measurable driver of professional and commercial outcomes, yet the field lacks empirically grounded frameworks that practitioners can operationalize at scale. This paper introduces the Personal Brand Equity Index (PBEI), a five-dimensional model developed through iterative application across 523 brand-building engagements spanning 14 countries between 2016 and 2025. Drawing on Aaker's (1991) brand equity taxonomy, Kapferer's (1992) brand identity prism, and Labrecque et al.'s (2011) digital personal branding framework, the PBEI operationalizes personal brand equity through five measurable dimensions: Search Salience, Content Authority, Platform Ownership, Social Proof Density, and Narrative Consistency. The framework was refined through three methodological phases: diagnostic pattern recognition across the initial 200 engagements (2016 to 2020), structured pilot testing with 150 clients (2021 to 2023), and scaled validation across 173 engagements (2024 to 2025). Findings indicate that clients who achieved measurable improvement across all five PBEI dimensions within the first 90 days experienced a mean 3.2x increase in inbound professional opportunities over a 12-month period, compared to a 1.4x increase for those improving in three or fewer dimensions. The paper also presents preliminary evidence for a sixth emerging dimension, Algorithmic Entity Recognition, reflecting the growing importance of visibility within large language model outputs and AI-powered search interfaces. Implications for both practitioners and scholars of personal branding are discussed.

[1] I have reviewed the paper titled “Contrastive Learning for Knowledge Tracing,” published in the ACM Web Conference (WWW) 2022. This review forms part of the requirements for my Ph.D. qualification examination in computer science at New Mexico State University. The analysis includes a thorough examination of the paper’s assumptions, methodology, strengths, weaknesses, the targeted audience, and its correlation with my current research interests in knowledge tracing and AI applications in education. This paper addresses the challenge of modeling student knowledge under sparse data conditions by introducing a contrastive learning framework for knowledge tracing (CL4KT). The authors design an end-to-end model that combines a standard knowledge tracing architecture with a contrastive learning objective. They carefully construct domain-specific data augmentations, e.g., masking questions, cropping interactions, permuting sequence order, and replacing questions with easier or harder ones, and incorporate hard negative samples by inverting the correctness of answers to generate semantically similar and dissimilar learning sequences. The model learns to pull representations of similar learning histories closer and push apart those of dissimilar histories in the latent space, using a contrastive loss in addition to the usual next-question prediction loss. CL4KT significantly improves knowledge tracing accuracy on six benchmark educational datasets, demonstrating better generalization from limited student interaction data. The paper’s contributions include a novel application of contrastive self-supervised learning in educational modeling, pedagogy-inspired data augmentation techniques for student sequences, and analysis showing how contrastive objectives enhance the learned knowledge state representations. [2] I have reviewed the paper titled “Knowledge Tracing as Language Processing: A Large-Scale Autoregressive Paradigm,” published in the Conference of Artificial Intelligence in Education (AIED) 2024. This review forms part of the requirements for my Ph.D. qualification examination in computer science at New Mexico State University. The analysis includes a thorough examination of the paper’s assumptions, methodology, strengths, weaknesses, the targeted audience, and its correlation with my current research interests in knowledge tracing and AI applications in education. Knowledge tracing (KT) is the task of modeling a student’s knowledge state over time to predict future performance. This paper reframes this problem using large language model (LLM) techniques. It proposes LLM-KT, a deep learning-based knowledge tracing model built on a decoder-only Transformer architecture. By treating student interaction sequences similarly to language sequences, the model leverages an autoregressive design to capture long-term dependencies in a student’s learning history. The authors evaluate LLM-KT on EdNet, the world’s largest public KT dataset, to explore two questions: (1) Can an LLM-style architecture be effectively applied to knowledge tracing? (2) Does scaling up model size (“continuous extension”) improve prediction accuracy? The paper’s key contributions include introducing a novel Transformer-based KT paradigm, demonstrating its scalability on large-scale data, and providing empirical evidence that larger models can yield better performance in KT. This report provides a structured overview of the paper’s goals, approach, and contributions, analyzes the model design and assumptions, compares it with prior KT methods, and reflects on its implications for AI-driven education research.
