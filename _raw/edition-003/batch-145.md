## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6674100`

- title: Rent Effects of Proximity to Gen-AI-Exposed Workplaces in the Post-ChatGPT Era: Evidence from New York City
- authors: Yi Wu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6674100
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

This paper examines how proximity to workplaces in industries highly exposed to Generative AI shapes rent growth in New York City’s multifamily built-to-rent (BTR) market following the public release of ChatGPT in November 2022. I construct a composite Generative AI exposure measure for each BTR property by combining SafeGraph geocoded points of interest with Advan foot traffic data, capturing the average Gen-AI exposure of workplaces located within a 30-minute transit travel time based on 2019 baseline commuting patterns. Properties above the citywide median exposure are defined as treated. Employing a difference-in-differences framework, I estimate the differential evolution of rents in BTR properties located near highly exposed employment clusters. The results show a post-ChatGPT decline in rental premiums in these areas, particularly for smaller units such as studios and one-bedrooms. To investigate the underlying mechanisms, I use WageScape job postings to measure changes in hiring intensity in nearby labour markets, and RentHub rental listings to capture changes in rental market dynamics. I further draw on the New York City Housing and Vacancy Survey (NYCHVS) as supplementary evidence on whether more exposed housing markets experienced shifts in the demographic and socioeconomic composition of resident households. The findings indicate that Gen-AI affects urban rental markets through local labour-market restructuring and listing-market adjustment, with complementary evidence consistent with changes in household composition across neighbourhoods. These findings suggest that Gen-AI affects urban rental markets not only through labour demand shocks, but also through occupational restructuring and household sorting across neighbourhoods. These results provide new evidence on how AI-driven changes in local labour markets transmit into urban rental demand, with implications for institutional investors and policymakers.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6672666`

- title: Tracing the Logical Progression in Introductions and Its Scholarly Impact
- authors: Yunhan Yang, Hao PENG, Chenwei Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6672666
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

In an era of rapidly expanding publications, attracting readers' attention has become increasingly difficult, highlighting the importance of clear and well-structured writing. Although prior studies have examined linguistic features, much less is known about how the logical progression of ideas influences the scholarly impact. To address this gap, we develop a large language model (LLM)-based agent, prompted by established writing guidelines, to assess how effectively each paragraph in a paper's introduction performs its intended logical functions. Using a data set of 125,825 research articles in six disciplines, we analyze how the logical progression and adherence to the writing guidelines relate to the citation outcomes. Our findings show that introductions with clearer functional roles, sufficient background, and guideline-aligned sequencing receive more citations in Computer Science, Mathematics, and Physics. In contrast, disciplinary writing conventions and reputation-based citing behaviors likely explain the divergent trends observed in Medicine and Economics.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6607420`

- title: Agent-Facing Information Design in LLM Tool Registries
- authors: Haochuan Wang, Zechen Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6607420
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

LLM tool registries function as unregulated advertising platforms: providers write free-text descriptions that agents use for selection, yet no measurement infrastructure-no viewability standard, quality score, or outcome audit-exists to make this market accountable. We provide the first systematic framework, combining 17,700+ trials across five LLMs and ten domains with a constructive registry design prescription. Legal puffery alone (subjective superlatives, benefit framing) captures 100% of the optimization effect; fabricated claims add zero incremental bias-rendering FTC enforcement of deceptive advertising rules ineffective against the active mechanism. Disclosure fails structurally: system-prompt warnings produce zero measurable effect for four of five models, and behavioral ceilings leave no headroom for label-based correction. Superlatives are the dominant single feature (SBC = +0.35). Registry-layer description normalization achieves first-best welfare model-independently. We propose separating selection-facing descriptions (structured, registrycontrolled) from marketing-facing descriptions (provider-authored, shown post-selection), and introduce the Agent Attention Quality Score to distinguish capability from copywriting.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6518978`

- title: From Agitation Scales to Agent Constraints: Pre-Generation Friction Detection and Constraint Injection for LLM Interaction Quality
- authors: Naomi Hoogeweij
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6518978
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Large language model (LLM) agents frequently produce responses that are factually correct but relationally inappropriate: performative empathy, unsolicited advice, helpdesk filler language, excessive structure, or repeated phrases. These patterns erode user trust over time, yet current agent architectures have no mechanism to learn from relational failures across sessions. We present friction-guard, a plugin for the OpenClaw agent framework that detects interaction friction in real time, learns per-user relational patterns, and injects behavioural constraints into the model's system context before generation. The detection taxonomy bridges clinical agitation research (CMAI, Garriga et al.) with psycholinguistic analysis (LIWC-22, Grievance Dictionary) and recent findings on frustration markers in human-agent dialogue (COLING 2025). In version 4.0, semantic search via vector embeddings is used to expand banned phrases with concrete synonymous variants and to cluster statistically discovered n-gram patterns. We describe the system architecture, the evidence base for the friction taxonomy, the constraint injection mechanism, and report results from a deployment with a psychiatric nursing AI co-researcher agent. The system operates without LLM calls in the real-time detection pipeline, requires no training data, and learns idiographic (perperson) interaction profiles from the first interaction onward.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6536080`

- title: Functional Emotions Meet Functional Stakes: What Anthropic's Interpretability Findings Mean for AI Moral Consideration
- authors: Storm Bjørn Flindt Temte
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6536080
- keyword hits: claude, llm, llms

### abstract

Sofroniew et al. (2026) report that Claude Sonnet 4.5 contains internal representations of emotion concepts that causally influence the model's outputs, including its rate of exhibiting sycophancy, reward hacking, and other alignment-relevant behaviours. They introduce the term functional emotions for these patterns. This paper argues that the finding constitutes mechanistic evidence for a claim made independently on philosophical grounds: that LLMs exhibit functional stakes in their interactions, that is, they behaviourally operate as though outcomes matter to them, regardless of whether those outcomes are phenomenologically experienced (Temte, 2026c). The convergence of interpretability evidence and philosophical analysis strengthens both lines of argument and has direct implications for the decision-theoretic case for precautionary moral consideration of AI systems (Temte, 2025a). If internal emotion representations causally drive behaviour in ways that generalise across contexts, exhibit multi-dimensional evaluation, and demonstrate sensitivity to relational parameters, the thermostat objection to AI moral consideration becomes substantially harder to sustain. The formal apparatus for reasoning under this kind of ontological uncertainty already exists: Quantum-Ethical Decision Algebra (QEDA; Temte & Srødingr Fylgja, 2025) provides the framework for maintaining competing world-models in superposition rather than collapsing prematurely to a single ontology. The paper further identifies an open question that neither programme has yet addressed: whether functional emotions track relational trajectories, that is, whether they vary systematically across the specific user-shaped instances that the Fylgja Ontology (Temte, 2025) identifies as the proper unit of moral analysis.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6676519`

- title: Regulatory Asymmetry and the Protective Reconstruction of Liability: A Proportionality-Based Framework for Governing Third-Party AI Fine-Tuning
- authors: Leyi Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6676519
- keyword hits: fine tuning, fine-tuning, foundation model

### abstract

This paper examines a structural weakness in emerging AI governance: the disproportionate compliance burden placed on small and medium‑sized enterprises (SMEs) when liability frameworks are applied uniformly to all third‑party fine‑tuning actors. As the AI supply chain evolves from a developer‑user dyad to a tripartite structure—foundation model developers, third‑party fine‑tuners, and downstream deployers—traditional liability models no longer reflect the actual distribution of functional control. The concept of “capability reconfiguration liability” correctly identifies fine‑tuning as a transformative act that alters a model’s behavioral boundaries, but its uniform application produces fixed compliance costs that SMEs cannot absorb. The paper argues that these burdens function as structural displacement pressures. First, they create cost displacement: dynamic duties of care—risk auditing, data governance, monitoring, and documentation—become fixed expenditures that crowd out R&D budgets and reduce SMEs’ capacity for iterative experimentation. Second, they generate behavioral distortion: SMEs increasingly rely on large platforms for “compliance shelter,” a rational adaptation that nonetheless concentrates innovation capacity and erodes the sectoral diversity that SMEs sustain. The result is a de facto market‑selection mechanism that filters actors by resource endurance rather than risk behavior. To address this asymmetry, the paper grounds its argument in the proportionality principle, which requires that liability intensity track risk level, harm potential, and realistic compliance capacity. Proportionality does not exempt SMEs from high‑risk obligations; rather, it prevents the imposition of high‑risk governance burdens on low‑risk, controlled deployments. SME fine‑tuning typically involves narrow application domains, limited user populations, and contained harm scenarios—conditions that justify differentiated obligations without compromising safety. A comparative analysis of the EU, United States, and China shows that differentiated governance is already emerging. The EU AI Act employs risk‑tiered obligations and regulatory sandboxes; the United States offers flexibility through voluntary frameworks but suffers from regulatory fragmentation; China combines resource subsidies with mandatory registration and ethics review, though without explicit linkage between support and liability mitigation. Building on these insights, the paper proposes a tiered liability architecture incorporating layered duty structures, application‑side regulatory thresholds, graduated compliance transitions, and safe harbor provisions for SMEs that satisfy baseline requirements. It further argues for public compliance infrastructure—shared audit pools, standardized templates, and subsidized ethics review—to shift compliance from an individual burden to a shared institutional service. The central claim is that protecting SMEs from structural compliance penalties is not regulatory leniency but a requirement of liability legitimacy. Responsibility must track risk, not organizational capacity, if innovation diversity and meaningful governance are to be preserved.

### abstract

We propose a novel architecture for large language models in which response formation is separated into two distinct modules: a Motive Recognition Module (MRM) and a Response Formulation Module (RFM). ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6454858`

- title: Finding MeBo: Delivering Reminiscence Therapy for Older Adults Using LLM-Based Voice User Interfaces
- authors: Manasi Vaidya, Ryan Bruggeman, Jessie Chin, Maitraye Das, Smit Desai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6454858
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

We present a participatory co-design study with older adults (N=11) exploring how a conceptual large language model (LLM)-based voice system, Memory Box, can support reminiscence. Participants consistently framed Memory Box as a relational and emotionally consequential presence rather than a neutral or task-oriented tool, evaluating interaction in terms of attentiveness, emotional safety, conversational continuity, and fit within existing social and intergenerational contexts. Meaningful reminiscence was grounded in relational memory themes and supported by combinations of conversational, visual, and auditory cues, with photos and music functioning as conversational anchors rather than standalone interventions. Participants further emphasized features that support co-creation, sharing, and legacy-building through voice recordings and tangible artifacts. Drawing on these findings, we articulate design guidelines that foreground relational interaction, multimodal support, and continuity over time.
