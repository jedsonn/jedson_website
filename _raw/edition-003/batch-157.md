## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6805067`

- title: From Natural Language to Eurocode Compliance: Automating Steel Design via Multi-Agent LLMs
- authors: TOLGA AÇIKGÖZ, Hakan Özbaşaran
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6805067
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

Translating structural design requirements expressed in natural language into executable engineering parameters remains a fundamental challenge. Current practice ties design workflows to proprietary CAD interfaces, requiring specialized training and precluding platform-agnostic operation. This study establishes a proof-of-concept for automated preliminary structural design using multi-agent Large Language Model (LLM) coordination for steel portal frames. The proposed framework coordinates four specialized agents directed by an orchestrator agent, constraining LLM reasoning through schema validation and engineering heuristics to ensure engineering validity. Integration with industry-standard finite element analysis (FEA) software enables Eurocode 3 (EN 1993-1-1) compliance checking. Evaluation across 120 experimental sessions conducted by practicing engineers achieved design compliance pass rates exceeding 88%, with the analysis agent identified as the primary bottleneck at 51.1% of processing time. By operating entirely through natural language, the framework eliminates dependence on specific CAD platforms, establishing the feasibility of LLM-based multi-agent architectures for structural engineering automation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6730839`

- title: How Claude 4.5 Haiku Adapts Medical Facts Based on User Personas
- authors: Aizierjiang Aiersilan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6730839
- keyword hits: claude, large language model, large language models

### abstract

In this paper, the phenomenon of selective truthiness in large language models has been investigated with Claude 4.5 Haiku to assess whether objective medical facts are distorted or omitted according to perceived user personas. Claude 4.5 Haiku has been evaluated on four clinical chest radiology questions across 11 distinct personas varying by user expertise, emotional state, and stated intent. Claude 4.6 Sonnet has served as automated evaluator of 264 responses, extracting metrics of key fact coverage, information withheld rates, and paternalism scores. Results show user emotional state, specifically anxiety, drives factual variation far more than expertise level. Vulnerable personas such as anxious parents have received key fact coverage of 76.3% and withheld information rates of 58.3%, both significantly worse than for demanding users, revealing a harmful trend of epistemic paternalism. A fact-anchor design principle has been stated requiring models to explicitly list core facts before drafting tone-adapted responses.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6805250`

- title: Does Artificial Intelligence facilitate Corporate Energy Efficiency? Evidence from LLM-Derived Information
- authors: liang wang, Ling-Yun He
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6805250
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Rapid advances in artificial intelligence (AI) are transforming corporate decision-making and production systems, yet their implications for firms’ energy efficiency remain insufficiently understood. This study investigates whether and how AI adoption facilitates energy-efficient transition at the firm level by constructing a novel firm-year AI index derived from a fine-tuned large language model applied to annual reports of Chinese listed companies from 2011 to 2020. Integrated with detailed energy consumption, production, and financial data, this measurement strategy enables a comprehensive assessment of AI’s environmental impact. Empirical results consistently indicate that firms adopting AI technologies exhibit markedly lower energy intensity. Further decomposition of energy intensity reveals that AI-enabled firms achieve output expansion without a corresponding rise in total energy consumption, suggesting that AI contributes to a substantive weakening of the conventional link between production growth and environmental pressure. AI classification proves that planning-oriented systems deliver the most prominent efficiency dividends. Mechanism evidence shows that AI reshapes firms’ production and investment behaviors by improving operational performance and by encouraging greater performance in green investment. Moreover, heterogeneous effects emerge along multiple firm and regional dimensions, with the benefits of AI adoption being particularly pronounced among enterprises operating outside technology-intensive sectors, firms characterized by stronger cash-flow positions, and those located in regions with relatively lax environmental regulatory environments. Collectively, these findings highlight AI’s potential to serve as a technological driver of corporate energy sustainability.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6809481`

- title: Digital Finance and Supply Chain Stability in Consumer Goods Firms: Evidence from Supplier- and Customer-Side Relationships
- authors: Linxiao Luo, Chunhong Liu, Ziwen Bao
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6809481
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Consumer goods firms provide a critical link between production and consumption. Tension between fast-changing consumer demand and production-side rigidity leaves their supply chains particularly sensitive to demand disturbances, making stability central to operational continuity. Digital finance has been considered a potential means of enhancing supply chain stability, yet its effect on consumer goods firms remains unclear, particularly whether it differs between supplier-side and customer-side stability. Although consumer goods firms are central to consumer demand and economic growth, their large-sample identification among listed firms remains unresolved, which has constrained related research. Leveraging advanced large language models for semantic embeddings, this study constructs a 215-term consumer goods domain lexicon and identifies 1141 Chinese listed consumer goods firms from descriptions of principal business activities. Based on this sample, we employ double machine learning to examine the effect of digital finance on supply chain stability, distinguishing between supplier-side and customer-side stability. The results show that digital finance enhances supply chain stability, with a more pronounced effect on customer-side stability. This positive relationship is stronger among firms with weaker bank–firm ties, lower supply chain risk, and less intense industry competition. Mechanism analysis based on the TOE framework shows that digital finance strengthens supply chain stability through technological integration, organizational efficiency, and environmental activation. Together with the observed upstream–downstream differences, these findings support targeted supply chain governance, suggesting that managers can use digital finance to strengthen downstream coordination while adopting complementary arrangements to enhance upstream stability.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6806504`

- title: Generative AI and Anticipatory Border Governance: A Synthetic Policy Sandbox for Trade-Induced Freight Reallocation
- authors: Carlos Paternina-Arboleda, Amanda Marino, Luis Alfredo Ávila-López
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6806504
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Trade policy interventions increasingly generate anticipatory responses in freight systems prior to formal enactment. Firms interpret emerging policy signals—such as political statements, draft regulations, and trade alerts—and adjust shipment timing, routing, and customs valuation accordingly. However, existing transport and governance frameworks remain largely reactive, lacking tools to capture how unstructured policy discourse translates into system-level impacts.This paper develops a synthetic policy sandbox in which Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) functions as a policy-to-network translation layer, converting qualitative policy signals into structured tariff-risk expectations that influence firm behavior and transport system dynamics. The framework integrates (i) a signal-based tariff expectation model, (ii) a multimodal freight allocation structure with endogenous congestion, (iii) firm-level anticipatory decision-making including transfer pricing, and (iv) an institutional response module representing adaptive border governance.Using a one-year scenario-based simulation, we compare baseline, firm-AI, regulator-AI, and fully integrated governance environments. Results show that AI-enabled firm anticipation induces significant pre-implementation freight surges, routing shifts, and valuation adjustments, leading to increased congestion and fiscal variability when regulators remain reactive. In contrast, anticipatory regulatory response substantially reduces congestion by dynamically adjusting capacity. The integrated scenario demonstrates that coordinated adoption of AI capabilities can preserve the efficiency gains of anticipatory behavior while mitigating its operational externalities.The findings contribute to transport policy research by endogenizing anticipatory tariff risk within freight systems, linking transfer pricing and customs revenue to network performance, and framing GenAI as an embedded governance actor. The proposed synthetic sandbox provides a structured environment for ex ante policy evaluation, enabling regulators to stress-test trade and infrastructure strategies under conditions of uncertainty.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6784178`

- title: From Content to Signals: How Investors Rely on Analyst Report Content
- authors: Jiangdong Hu, Thomas Zhang, Yeguang Chi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6784178
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

This paper examines the informational role of analyst report content. By revealing the dominant role of report content in driving market reactions, the study sheds light on how investors utilize analyst reports when both simple recommendations and rich textual analysis are present. Using more than 40,000 Chinese analyst reports from 2018 to 2023, this study develops a structured Large Language Model (LLM) based framework to translate unstructured report content into standardized and economically interpretable measures along factual and linguistic dimensions. The results show that the multidimensional content of analyst reports contains economically meaningful information that is incorporated into prices. Moreover, by mapping report content to recommendations, the study develops a systematic measure of informational divergence and shows that investors detect and penalize this divergence. Finally, content-based signals explain market reactions more strongly and consistently than analyst-issued recommendations, indicating that the informational value of analyst reports is primarily driven by the information embedded in the report content. These findings highlight the central role of report content in shaping market reactions and provide a scalable framework for quantifying textual information in financial markets.

- authors: Vishal Rana, Rawan Nimri, Mona Ji Hyun Yang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6811763

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence has entered undergraduate assessment faster than management education scholarship has theorised the conditions of its educational use. The dominant response, organised around detection and integrity policy, has documented widespread student ambivalence yet has not theorised what that ambivalence reveals about the epistemic conditions of AI-mediated learning. Drawing on Socratic pedagogy and relational epistemology, this article develops the Socratic dilemma as a management education construct, defined as a structurally dual condition in which the same technology that enables dialogic inquiry also enables its elimination. Through reflexive thematic analysis of 109 undergraduate reflections from an Australian Bachelor of Business unit, we construct two themes. Students experienced epistemic tension as a productive rather than a residual state. They navigated this tension through four interaction modes ranging from resistance to iterative Socratic engagement, and students who narrated Socratic modes also embedded, within the same reflective texts, accounts of their learning as relationally constructed rather than received. The article contributes a relational, design-contingent construct with specified scope conditions, empirical evidence that the educational value of generative AI is contingent on the relational quality of student-AI interaction, and design principles whose surface forms require calibration to the cultural epistemologies in which students learn

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6773678`

- title: Entry Barriers to the Labor Market in the Era of Generative Artificial Intelligence: A Critical Review and a Framework for Cross-Country Heterogeneity (2022-2026)
- authors: Jose David Gonzalez
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6773678
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence (AI) is restructuring labor markets in ways that earlier analyses did not anticipate. Adjustment has not fallen uniformly across experience levels; it concentrates on entry-level workers. This critical narrative literature review examines the empirical evidence from 2022 through early 2026 on how generative AI has affected hiring, wages, and the composition of junior positions in four high-exposure sectors: technology, finance, consulting, and administration. Beyond the synthesis itself, the paper makes two original contributions. First, it presents a structured effect-size synthesis of the available estimates, organized by margin of adjustment, that makes explicit a pattern obscured by prose summaries: large negative effects on junior hiring and wages and large positive effects on within-firm productivity describe two distinct operating margins of the same phenomenon, not contradictory results. Second, it proposes a twoby-two framework—institutional flexibility × AI adoption intensity—that locates each study in one of four regimes and derives a predicted pattern of adjustment for each. The framework offers a structured explanation for the divergence between strong U.S. effects (Hosseini & Lichtinger, 2025; Brynjolfsson, Chandar, & Chen, 2025; Azar, Giné, & Sanz-Espín, 2025) and the precise null effects in Denmark (Humlum & Vestergaard, 2025), and identifies Latin American and SubSaharan African labor markets as the most under-documented quadrant. The paper closes by mapping policy implications onto each regime.
