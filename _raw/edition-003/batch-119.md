## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6627158`

- title: Understanding Security Issues in Open-source Agentic AI Frameworks: An Empirical Analysis
- authors: Muhammad Hamza, Muhammad Azeem Akbar, Wardah Naeem Awan, Muhammad Shoaib
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6627158
- keyword hits: agentic, large language model, llm

### abstract

Agentic AI frameworks have rapidly emerged as a practical approach for building autonomous, tool-augmented large language model (LLM) applications. While these frameworks are increasingly adopted in software development and organizational workflows, there is still limited empirical understanding of the security issues developers encounter, the underlying causes of these issues, and the mitigation strategies used in practice. To address this gap, we conducted an empirical study by mining and analyzing 777 discussions of security-related issues from three widely used open-source agentic AI frameworks: LangChain, AutoGen, and CrewAI. Our analysis identifies ten categories of security issues, with Secret and Credential Exposure (24.8%) and Authentication and Authorization Issues (23.2%) emerging as the most frequent issues. Further analysis reveals that these issues are primarily caused by dependency and supply chain risks (25.1%), unsafe code or tool execution practices (24.5%), and insecure secret handling (20.1%). To mitigate these risks, developers commonly adopt several security strategies, including secret redaction and secure storage (30.2%), prompt and tool guardrails (20.8%), sandboxed execution environments (19.2%), authentication abstraction improvements (16%), and dependency upgrades and security scanning (13.8%). The taxonomies and empirical evidence presented in this study provide insights into the evolving security landscape of agentic AI frameworks and support researchers and practitioners in designing and deploying more secure and trustworthy agentic AI systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6720239`

- title: Improving the Completeness and Comparability of Segment Disclosures: A Large Language Model Approach
- authors: Yue Liu, Zhiyuan Cheng, Longying Lai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6720239
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, retrieval augmented

### abstract

Segment-level disclosures are a central component of financial reporting, providing insight into firms' internal organization and the allocation of economic activities across operating units. However, segment information is often presented in both qualitative and quantitative forms, dispersed across tables and narrative sections of Form 10-K filings. Empirical research relying on structured databases faces both completeness and comparability challenges, as some firm-year observations may be missing, nested segment disclosures are not captured, and support for longitudinal and cross-firm comparability is limited. This study develops a large language model-based framework to extract segment disclosures directly from Form 10-K filings and to preserve both reportable and nested segment information. We further design a retrieval augmented system that incorporates information across multiple filings to support comparability. We use two representative settings to demonstrate its application: longitudinal analysis within a firm to interpret segment changes over time, and cross firm alignment of geographic segments across firms with different reporting structures. The results indicate that the artifact accurately extracts segment-level information and effectively addresses questions that require cross-period knowledge, demonstrating the potential of LLM-based approaches to enhance the measurement and interpretation of segment disclosures.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6731664`

- title: RM-MLLM: Robust Scene Understanding in Adverse Weather with RGB-Multispectral Fusion
- authors: Hyeongjin Ju, Sanghyeop Yeo, Incheol Park, Youngwan Jin, Yagiz Nalcakan, Shiho Kim
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6731664
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) for autonomous driving often become less reliable in adverse weather, partly because training data are dominated by clear, well-lit scenes, and most models primarily rely on RGB imagery. We address these issues with two contributions. First, we introduce DENSE-QA, a large-scale benchmark for adverse-weather scene understanding comprising 12,997 scenes and over 77,000 instruction-response pairs, built on synchronized RGB and multispectral imagery. Second, we propose RM-MLLM, an MLLM architecture with modality-specific encoders and an adaptive fusion module, trained with a three-stage progressive strategy to better integrate complementary sensor cues. Across multiple LLM backbones, RM-MLLM improves QA performance under adverse conditions. Using only RGB input, the proposed architecture and training already outperform all evaluated baselines, including prompt-augmented models, by over 11 percentage points. Incorporating RGB-multispectral fusion provides an additional 3-point gain concentrated on weather-sensitive tasks. We also observe that fine-tuning a strong RGB-only vision-language model on the same training data yields performance comparable to its zero-shot evaluation, suggesting that DENSE-QA fine-tuning alone does not close the gap to RM-MLLM and that multispectral input provides complementary value. We release the benchmark and models to support future all-weather autonomous driving research https://yonsei-stl.github.io/RM-MLLM.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6731295`

- title: LLM4FM: Enhancing large language models with use case relationships for automated feature model generation
- authors: Zhongjin Yuan, Yi Xiang, Peixi Zhuang, Ning Wu, Han Huang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6731295
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Since software product lines (SPLs) are widely used to analyze the commonality and variability of domain requirements for core asset reuse, automatically modeling SPLs using feature models is conducive to improving requirements analysis efficiency and reducing development costs. Existing methods extract feature model components from unstructured natural language requirements through manually designed heuristics or machine learning methods. The methods based on large language model (LLM) hold an advantage over others due to their robust language comprehension and inference capabilities. Although LLM-based methods effectively capture the superficial hierarchical constraints of the feature model from the semantics of requirements, they struggle to infer implicit associations and complicated crossed constraints between features. UML use case diagrams reveal the structure and behavior of a system from an object-oriented perspective, with overlapping characteristics compared to feature models. Therefore, we propose LLM4FM, an automatic method to generate SPL feature models via an LLM augmented with use case relationships. By incorporating use case relationships into model fine-tuning, this method enhances an LLM in acquiring features and constraints. It compensates for the deficiencies of current methods in reasoning, as well as the difficulty of accurately representing features and their complex interactions. Extensive evaluation on public data and real-world cases demonstrates that LLM4FM delivers superior performance over state-of-the-art methods in terms of \textit{Precision}, \textit{Recall} and \textit{F1-score} of auto-generated feature models.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6711019`

- title: AnuSakthi: An Intent-driven, Neuro-Symbolic Architecture for Universal Programming and Cross-language Interoperability
- authors: N. Thirugnanamuthu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6711019
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, prompting

### abstract

Contemporary software engineering is encumbered by what this paper identifies as the Syntax Bottleneck: the compulsory cognitive overhead imposed on developers who must continuously context-switch between the abstract domain of problem logic and the syntactic formalisms of one or more programming languages. This paper introduces AnuSakthi, a novel intent-driven, neuro-symbolic programming architecture that fundamentally redefines the human-computer programming contract. AnuSakthi accepts developer intent expressed as structured, step-indexed natural language within dedicated .anu source files. A deterministic three-tier compiler pipeline-comprising a structured Intent Parser (Layer 1), a neuro-symbolic Sakthi Compiler Engine (Layer 2), and a sandboxed Foreign Function Interface (FFI) Executor (Layer 3)-transforms this structured intent into an unambiguous Abstract Syntax Tree (AST), synthesises executable code, and orchestrates cross-language library invocations via a Universal Package Manager. Central to AnuSakthi's design philosophy, termed Moolam, is the enforcement of step-indexed algorithmic intent that eliminates the ambiguity traditionally associated with free-form large language model (LLM) prompting, thereby precluding hallucination-induced code synthesis errors. Additionally, the architecture incorporates a context-aware Smart Output Renderer that produces rich, structured visualisations in lieu of raw terminal streams. This paper articulates the theoretical foundations, system architecture, interoperability mechanisms, and projected transformative impact of AnuSakthi on software engineering practice.

### abstract

The dominant approach to AI agent governance treats large language models as software: deterministic systems constrained by rules, guardrails, and categorical restrictions. This approach handles the cases the developer anticipated. It says nothing about what happens when the situation changes. In February 2026, researchers from Northeastern University, Harvard, MIT, Stanford, Carnegie Mellon, and eight other institutions published "Agents of Chaos," a systematic empirical study documenting repeated failures in autonomous AI agents operating in a live environment with real tools (Shapira et al., 2026). The documented failures share a common pattern: an agent received an instruction, encountered a situation the instruction did not anticipate, and improvised badly. The agents had orders. They did not have intent.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6749582`

- title: Comparative Analysis of Artificial Intelligence Test Generation Methods for Compliance Validation in Regulated Enterprise Workflows
- authors: Shruthi Sepuri
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6749582
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, prompting

### abstract

Compliance testing in regulated domains such as finance, healthcare, and insurance remains difficult due to their frequent policy changes, complex multi-step rules, and continued dependence on manual or script-based validation processes. These traditional methods are hard to scale and often fail to capture the full range of regulatory conditions by driving interest in whether recent advances in artificial intelligence (AI) can support automated test generation. This study explores that question through a conceptual comparison of three AI-driven techniques: direct large language model (LLM) prompting, rule decomposition with structured constraint extraction, and a hybrid LLM-plus-rule-verification approach. An organized set of representative regulatory logic patterns drawn from anti-money laundering (AML) thresholds, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) access-control dependencies, and insurance decision pathways was used to reason about how each technique would interpret conditions, capture exceptions, and structure potential test cases. The comparison focuses on four dimensions: regulatory coverage, boundary handling, logical consistency, and redundancy derived from established challenges reported in prior LLM and constraint-based testing literature. The study indicates that direct LLM prompting enables diverse scenario exploration, rule decomposition increases transparency through structured limitations, and a hybrid strategy conceptually delivers the most reliable logical coverage. These conceptual results provide actionable directions for organizations applying AI-driven compliance testing and highlight directions for future empirical validation.
