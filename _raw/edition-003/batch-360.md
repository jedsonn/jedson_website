- posted: 2026-07-23
- source: SSRN

### abstract

This essay argues that AI romantic companions are not a cause of Western fertility collapse but its most fluent symptom-the endpoint of a sixty-year decoupling of intimacy from biological consequence, running from the contraceptive pill through internet pornography and dating apps to algorithmic "love." AI is an accelerant, not an origin. Drawing on the empirical literature on human-AI attachment, the political economy of the "loneliness merchants," and the nature of large language models as sycophantic next-token predictors, it contends that these systems pair an epistemological fraud (fake depth) with a demographic fraud (fake intimacy). It closes with a defense of purposeless-but-meaningful human life and a refusal of the sterile substitute.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.4002091`

- title: Anticipated Emotions in Consumer Demand: Implications for Pricing, Inventory, and Assortment
- authors: Özalp Özer, Arun Rout, A. Serdar Şimşek
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2022-02-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.4002091
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Problem definition: Consumers often buy products before knowing exactly how much they will value them after use. In such settings, they account not only for expected utility, but also for anticipated emotions. Disappointment or elation arises when the realized value of the chosen product falls short of or exceeds what the consumer expected ex ante. Regret or rejoice arises when the realized value of the chosen product turns out to be worse or better than the forgone alternative. We study how these two emotional comparisons shape demand and, in turn, firms' pricing, inventory, and assortment decisions. Methodology/results: We develop a unified consumer-choice framework that embeds both disappointment--elation and regret--rejoice, and apply it to two foundational problems: joint pricing--inventory and joint pricing--assortment decisions. We show that both emotions act like an emotional tax on demand: they reduce willingness to pay and increase price sensitivity, prompting firms to lower prices and inventory. However, the two emotions operate differently. Disappointment reduces demand more broadly because it penalizes products for falling short of expectations, whereas regret depends on how the chosen product compares with competing alternatives. As a result, disappointment has a much larger effect on profits than regret. In multi-product settings, disappointment shifts demand toward safer, more predictable products and supports a common markup across the assortment, whereas regret makes targeted, product-specific pricing more attractive. Managerial implications: Anticipated emotions can meaningfully affect profitability: a 1% increase in aversion reduces optimal profit by 2.4% under disappointment--elation and 0.14% under regret--rejoice. The results imply different managerial responses: firms facing disappointment-averse consumers should emphasize more predictable products, whereas firms facing regret-averse consumers can use differential pricing to retain demand for more uncertain products. Uncertainty can also create value: when consumers place enough weight on a small chance of a very positive outcome, hype can turn the usual emotional tax into an emotional subsidy.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.4529656`

- title: Are Direct Lenders More Like Banks or Arm’s-Length Investors?
- authors: Young Soo Jang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2023-08-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.4529656
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

I examine whether direct lenders, who increasingly displacing banks in private equity (PE) buyouts, lend more like banks or arm’s-length investors. Using novel data, I find that financial covenants are ubiquitous in direct lending. Borrower face more covenants, and violate them more often, than in bank loans. Direct lenders actively intervene post-violation, prompting firms to adopt conservative policies. During the COVID pandemic, they showed greater flexibility than banks, facilitated by more PE sponsor equity injections. Prior sponsor relationships also predict higher credit supply when borrowers are distressed. Overall, similar to banks, direct lenders engage in active monitoring and relationship lending.
