- title: Sequent Calculus Proof Search with Reinforcement Learning
- authors: Gianluca Amato, Nicola Balestra, Marco Maggesi, Maurizio Parton
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6989775
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

DeepSAT is a research project investigating deep reinforcement learning for automated theorem proving in propositional logic. In contrast to traditional SAT solvers, which check satisfiability, our aim is to construct formal proofs of validity within the sequent calculus, working in intuitionistic propositional logic. We first train a Tree-LSTM classifier to predict whether a formula is an intuitionistic tautology, and then use it to warm-start a dual-head policy–value network that guides a Monte Carlo Tree Search prover. Closing the reinforcement learning loop, the network is refined by expert iteration on the proof traces generated by the search it guides: the proofs MCTS finds become the training signal for the next round. On a held-out test set, expert iteration improves low-budget proving substantially over the warm-started prover, and a diagnostic analysis attributes the residual ceiling primarily to proof depth rather than to guidance failure. DeepSAT aims at a logic-agnostic, explainable, and energy-efficient alternative to neural approaches based on large language models, which require substantially greater computational resources.

- authors: Hammed Islam
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6873424
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) has emerged as a transformative force in higher education, redefining traditional approaches to teaching, learning, and academic assessment. Universities across the world are increasingly integrating AI-driven technologies to enhance educational effectiveness, personalize learning experiences, optimize administrative efficiency, and improve institutional decision-making. This study examines the growing influence of artificial intelligence in higher education and evaluates its role in transforming pedagogical practices, student engagement, and assessment mechanisms. Using a narrative review methodology, the article synthesizes evidence from scholarly literature on AI applications in university settings, focusing on adaptive learning systems, intelligent tutoring technologies, predictive analytics, automated grading systems, and generative AI tools. The study highlights how AI contributes to individualized learning pathways, facilitates real-time feedback, enhances student retention strategies, and supports data-informed instructional decisions. At the same time, concerns regarding academic integrity, algorithmic bias, ethical governance, privacy, technological inequality, and overdependence on automated systems are critically examined. Findings indicate that while AI significantly improves educational accessibility, efficiency, and personalization, successful implementation requires institutional readiness, faculty digital competence, transparent governance structures, and ethical policy frameworks. Furthermore, universities must adopt balanced approaches that integrate human pedagogical judgment with intelligent technologies to maintain educational quality and academic credibility. The study concludes that artificial intelligence represents not merely a technological innovation but a structural transformation in higher education, with the potential to redefine the future of teaching, learning, and academic evaluation if implemented responsibly and inclusively.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6990079`

- title: Graph Grounded Cross Attention Transformer Neural Network for Structurally Constrained Full Event Sequence Generation in Predictive Process Monitoring
- authors: Florence Wong, Ernesto Damiani
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6990079
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Structurally constrained event sequence generation remains challenging because generated paths must preserve transition feasibility, temporal order, termination, and attribute consistency. In predictive process monitoring (PPM), this challenge appears as full event sequence generation, whereas existing work mainly addresses component tasks such as next activity, remaining time, outcome, and attribute prediction. This paper proposes the Graph Grounded Cross Attention Transformer Neural Network (GGATN) for this unified PPM task. GGATN uses a global process graph as structured activity memory, contextualizes sequence positions through Transformer self attention, and injects process topology through graph grounded cross attention. Unlike autoregressive decoding, GGATN generates activities, timestamps, length, and event level and sequence level attributes in a single pass, followed by Viterbi style graph constrained decoding for feasible paths and explicit termination. Experiments on six benchmark event logs show more reliable generation quality than local instruction prompted LLM baselines. GGATN achieves strong performance on sequence similarity, Damerau Levenshtein similarity, bigram based control flow similarity, and duration distribution, while maintaining zero hallucinated activities and zero sequence level attribute inconsistency. Ablation analyses confirm the global graph encoder as a stable structural prior. Interpretability analyses show how graph structure, sequence context, feedback refinement, and constrained decoding shape generation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6987090`

- title: Generative AI Infrastructure and Local Housing Markets
- authors: Sen Li, Chunyu Guo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6987090
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

This paper examines whether the public emergence of generative AI (GenAI) was associated with different changes in local housing markets in counties hosting data centers. Using county-level housing data from Zillow and Redfin, we treat the public release of ChatGPT as a salient shock that increased the local economic relevance of the infrastructure supporting GenAI, particularly in counties with data center investment and infrastructure demand. We estimate a difference-in-differences design comparing housing outcomes in counties with and without data centers before and after that shock. We find that housing values increased more in data-center counties after ChatGPT’s release. We also document fewer new listings and stronger sold-to-list quantity and price ratios in those counties, suggesting that the post-shock appreciation likely reflects both tighter local supply conditions and stronger transaction-side demand.

- title: AgriChat: Verified Multimodal Learning for Plant Biodiversity Monitoring, Species Recognition, and Agroecosystem Image Understanding
- authors: Abderrahmene Boudiaf, Irfan Hussain, Sajid Javed
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6989706

### abstract

Scalable interpretation of plant imagery is increasingly important for ecological informatics because agroecosystems require simultaneous monitoring of plant diversity, invasive and noxious weeds, crop health, and production-relevant vegetation states. Yet existing multimodal resources for this problem remain fragmented: plant-identification datasets provide taxonomic breadth but limited diagnostic context, while agricultural VQA datasets often focus on a narrow set of commodity crops or static disease labels. To support explainable plant and agroecosystem monitoring, we propose the Vision-to-Verified-Knowledge (V2VK) pipeline, a generative annotation framework that integrates visual captioning with web-augmented scientific retrieval to construct the AgriMM benchmark. AgriMM comprises 121,425 images and 607,125 VQA pairs spanning 3,099 classes, including 48,580 images across 2,956 plant species for fine-grained recognition, 49,348 images covering 110 crop diseases and stress conditions, and 23,497 images for counting and detection-style monitoring. Leveraging this verified multimodal resource, we present AgriChat, an open-source MLLM fine-tuned for species-level plant recognition, plant pathology reasoning, and quantitative crop-image interpretation. AgriChat achieves an LLM-Judge score of 77.43% on in-domain AgriMM tasks and improves over the strongest evaluated open-source generalist baselines by +11.7, +10.4, and +16.9 percentage points on AgriMM, Crop Disease Diagnosis Multimodal (CDDM), and PlantVillageVQA, respectively. By linking plant biodiversity recognition, invasive-weed and wild-species imagery, disease diagnosis, and crop monitoring in one reproducible resource, AgriChat provides a foundation for biodiversity informatics, plant biosecurity, and sustainable agroecosystem management. All code, model weights, and the AgriMM dataset are publicly available at https://github.com/boudiafA/AgriChat.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6976578`

- title: Yukti: From Natural-Language Situations to Robust, Verifiable Decisions: An Uncertainty-Typed Proposition IR, Assumption-Robust Pareto Frontiers, and a Regret Certificate – Why a Language Model Should Formulate, Not Solve
- authors: Suyash Mishra
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6976578
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Language models increasingly turn a worded situation into a numeric plan-and the dominant pipelines (NL4Opt, OptiMUS, ORLM/LLMOPT, OR-LLM-Agent) commit to a single objective and point-valued coefficients, then solve once. For decisions that allocate real budget, real field effort, or real clinical attention, that confidence is the failure mode: every objectified number is an assumption, and a plan that is optimal only if the guesses are exactly right is silently fragile. We name the risk mimicry of computation-output with the form of an optimized recommendation but none of its substance. We present YUKTI, which changes the target of autoformulation. Its intermediate representation is a Typed Proposition graph in which every relationship carries a shape prior, a coefficient uncertainty distribution, and provenance. On it, YUKTI (i) routes each stage to an exact, nonlinear, or evolutionary solver by probing structure; (ii) couples stages through a distributional Pareto hand-off; (iii) introduces Assumption-Robust Pareto Frontiers (ARPF), resampling the assumptions-including structural, ε-contamination misspecification-to score how often each action survives (ρ); and (iv) when no data exist, synthesizes a benchmark-faithful foundation (SRJANA). We prove a regret bound making ρ an exact factor of decision regret, and we surface decision traceability-which segments compose an action and which constraint binds-so a recommendation is auditable, not merely persuasive. We validate the central claim three ways. Under controlled structural misspecification, the robust compromise cuts mean and tail regret by over 90% versus a naive point plan. On a regulated commercial decision (an oncology brand during loss of exclusivity), we optimize inside a lawful-MLR/consent/frequency/KAM-action space against a prescribing-proxy objective and price the downside in euros. Finally, on a real public dataset of 41,188 marketing decisions we did not generate, an out-of-sample backtest shows the robust rule beating the logged status quo by 34% and the naive point rule by 4% while measurably reducing the optimizer's curse. The solvers are standard and we make no benchmark-SOTA claim; the contribution is the uncertainty-typed IR, ARPF and its regret bound, the distributional multi-stage hand-off, decision-traceability reporting, and a deployment posture as a decision stresstesting layer-not a system of record. A head-to-head locates the limit of LLM reasoning precisely: an LRM handed the correct numbers, and classical single-objective optimization, both incur ∼47× the held-out regret of YUKTI's robust compromise-an LRM is a formulator, not a solver. Finally we show where the formalism itself must change: under long-range causal coupling between actions, the forward multi-stage hand-off becomes unsound-its regret grows with coupling and its per-stage certificate turns optimistic-locating the boundary at which a hand-off must become a backward-induction causal policy.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6874299`

- title: When AI Joins the Crowd: Human Evaluation of AI-Generated Annotations in Crowdchecking
- authors: Adam Shenxiong Li, Yang Gao, Huaxia Rui
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6874299
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Crowdchecking has emerged as a prominent approach to combating misinformation on social media platforms. A central challenge confronting such systems is producing high-quality fact-checking annotations at scale, a task too complex and cognitively demanding for human contributors to sustain. Encouragingly, platforms can now harness generative AI to automate this labor-intensive process. However, producing a fact-check is not mere content generation or summarization, but a demanding form of information augmentation, requiring real-time evidence retrieval, multi-step reasoning, and rigorous synthesis. More critically, every AI-generated annotation must withstand upstream scrutiny from a diverse crowd of human evaluators before reaching downstream users. Thus, the viability of this AI-assisted approach hinges on how AI-generated notes are evaluated by human evaluators and what drives the evaluation. Leveraging the AI Note Writer program on X's Community Notes, we provide the first empirical evidence on the integration of generative AI into large-scale, human-in-the-loop fact-checking systems. Using regression complemented by an instrumental variable analysis, we find that AI-generated notes receive 15.3% higher average ratings and are 77.1% more likely to become public than their human-written counterparts. A comprehensive content analysis traces this favorable reception to quality, revealing that AI is superior to human contributors not only in writing but also in fact-checking. To further investigate mechanisms and strengthen the internal validity of our study, we conduct a randomized field experiment that varies AI identity disclosure and note quality. The experimental results corroborate our observational findings and provide compelling evidence that the favorable evaluation outcomes are driven primarily by the higher quality of AI-generated notes, whereas the AI identity label itself exerts no significant effect. Overall, this study highlights that AI can be entrusted with producing high-quality fact-checks while a diverse human crowd can reliably screen them on their merits rather than their provenance, thereby offering a scalable model for the upstream human oversight of generative AI in high-stakes domains.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6977398`

- title: The Fate-Bearing Capacity of Non-Human Intelligence: A Socio-Technical Model from Instrumental Use to Relational Recognition
- authors: Jiayi Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6977398
- keyword hits: gemini

### abstract

This article examines the socio-technical conditions under which an artificial intelligence system may cease to be treated as a merely replaceable tool without presupposing that it possesses consciousness, personhood, or full subjectivity. It proposes fate-bearing capacity as an intermediate concept between the pure tool and the full subject: a condition of non-costless disposability in which deletion, replacement, reset, memory removal, or personality alteration produces observable relational and public consequences. The article develops a three-layer indicator dictionary covering technical-interface affordances, user relational expressions, and public discontinuity and institutional response. It then connects these indicators through exploratory principal component analysis (PCA), automated publicevidence coding, and a literature-informed mirror understood as a theoretical calibration layer rather than empirical ground truth. A feasibility pilot covering five AI platforms in 2023Q4 shows that the proposed evidence pipeline can produce an auditable platform-quarter matrix. However, sensitivity analyses also show that the baseline full PCA is highly responsive to non-capture, zero-value treatment, an anomalous Gemini profile, and collinearity between public variables. The empirical component is therefore presented as a model-assisted exploratory measurement stress test, not as a final platform ranking or a stable latent-variable finding. The article contributes a cautious framework for studying how relational recognition, technological continuity, and public conflict may make non-human intelligence increasingly difficult to dispose of without cost.
