- title: The Accelerated Decay: Re-engineering Terminal Value in Hyper-Capital-Intensive Technology
- authors: Rakesh KS
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6731365
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The architecture of modern enterprise valuation is fundamentally misaligned with the thermodynamic and economic realities of the artificial intelligence supercycle. For decades, financial models have relied on assumptions derived from the industrial era, where massive capital expenditures (CapEx) yielded persistent, slowly depreciating physical assets. However, the infrastructure powering generative AI operates under fundamentally different physical constraints. This paper identifies a critical, systemic flaw in how equity markets price hyper-capital-intensive technology firms: The Terminal Value Trap. To manage the unprecedented costs of the AI arms race, hyperscalers have engaged in a coordinated accounting manoeuvre the "6-Year Shift" artificially elongating the GAAP depreciation schedules of server hardware to five or six years. Yet, evaluated through the physics-based Entropy Depreciation Model (EDM), the extreme thermal stress, floating-point stochasticity, and relentless silicon innovation dictate that an AI GPU's true economic half-life is approximately 2.3 years. By relying on standard straight-line depreciation, the market systematically breaks the matching principle of economics. Analysts are currently categorizing hundreds of billions of dollars as discretionary Growth CapEx, when the 18-to-36-month technological obsolescence cycle dictates that it is actually disguised Maintenance CapEx required merely to survive. This structural misunderstanding artificially inflates the final year Free Cash Flow in standard Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) models. When standard perpetual growth assumptions (e.g., the Gordon Growth Model) are applied to this inflated baseline, it generates a multi-trillion-dollar Terminal Value projecting steady-state growth that physically cannot exist. To correct this dislocation, this manuscript introduces the Dynamic Decay DCF Model. This framework re-engineers corporate valuation by: Capitalizing Research & Development (R&D) on severely truncated amortization schedules; Replacing static, infinite perpetual growth rates with an exponential Moat Decay Factor (λ) that mimics the rapid decay of a transient technological advantage; and Recalibrating the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) with a specialized Technology Obsolescence Risk Premium. Through empirical case studies spanning the primary infrastructure provider (Nvidia), the hyperscalers trapped on the CapEx treadmill (Microsoft and Meta), and a capital-light software control group (ServiceNow), the analysis proves that standard valuation models are dangerously decoupled from reality. Until financial markets treat AI infrastructure not as a perpetual industrial asset, but as a rapidly expiring, hyper-entropic liability, the valuation of the technology sector remains fundamentally compromised.

- title: Structural Degradation: From Stateless Generation to Layered Software Decay in AI-Generated Code A Three-Layer Taxonomy of Software Decay and Its Governance Implications
- authors: Spark Tsai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6655438
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI introduces a fundamental structural mismatch into software engineering: code is produced through stateless probabilistic generation, yet integrated into systems that evolve through stateful, temporally constrained architectures. While existing discourse concentrates on hallucinations, isolated defects, and productivity gains, it lacks a unified account of the degradation mechanisms unique to AI-assisted development-mechanisms that are neither reducible to individual bugs nor adequately captured by traditional notions of technical debt. This paper proposes a three-layer taxonomy of structural degradation in AI-generated code, organized across the Intent, Structural, and Behavioral layers. We first establish the root cause model: a threedimensional discontinuity-temporal, authorial, and state-based-arising from the interaction between stateless generation and stateful system evolution. We then define and differentiate eighteen distinct degradation phenomena distributed across the three layers, each characterized by its definition, distinction from adjacent concepts, illustrative examples, and degradation path logic. Finally, we demonstrate that these phenomena propagate across layers through identifiable causal mechanisms, and articulate the Visibility Inversion Principle: detection probability is inversely proportional to origination layer depth, while damage magnitude is directly proportional to failure layer depth. The contribution of this paper is foundational. It establishes a coherent ontology of degradation patterns and their cross-layer propagation dynamics, providing a necessary conceptual foundation for future analytical, governance, and architectural research in AI-assisted software engineering.

- authors: Alexandra Carvalho
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6656118
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

China has developed one of the world's most operationally sophisticated AI governance systems. Whilst much Western commentary frames China's approach primarily through censorship or industrial policy, this paper argues that China's regulatory architecture is better understood as a procedural governance infrastructure embedded across the AI lifecycle. Drawing on regulatory instruments, enforcement activity, judicial decisions, technical standards and policy developments through April 2026, this paper analyses China's layered governance model across algorithm regulation, generative AI, synthetic content, ethics review, cybersecurity, content provenance and lifecycle accountability. The paper compares China's approach with the European Union and United States governance models and argues that China has moved further than most jurisdictions in operationalising governance through filing obligations, ethics review structures, technical provenance requirements and procedural enforcement mechanisms. The paper concludes with implications for international companies operating in China and argues that global AI governance is shifting from principle-based oversight towards procedural, technical and operational governance infrastructures.

- title: Safeguarding Your Intellectual Property in the Age of AI: Practical Protections, Legal Gaps, and What Must Happen Next
- authors: Devon J Euring
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6730019
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI has rapidly expanded the intellectual property risk surface for organizations, creating new exposure across copyright, trade secrets, patent strategy, brand protection, vendor management, and workforce behavior. As employees and business functions increasingly use AI tools in daily workflows, sensitive information can enter prompts, training pipelines, logs, retrieval systems, and model outputs in ways that existing governance programs may not fully detect or control. This white paper examines the practical IP risks created by enterprise AI adoption, including trade secret leakage, copyright exposure, ownership uncertainty, premature invention disclosure, and brand misuse. It also surveys the current legal and regulatory landscape, including the continuing importance of traditional IP regimes, the unresolved status of AI training data and output liability, and the indirect role of emerging AI governance frameworks such as the EU AI Act. The paper argues that organizations should not wait for AI-specific IP law to mature before acting. Instead, boards, executives, counsel, security leaders, product teams, and risk functions should implement practical safeguards now: classify crown-jewel IP, restrict high-risk AI use cases, strengthen vendor contracts, preserve auditability, review AI-assisted outputs, train employees on misuse scenarios, and embed provenance and documentation controls across the AI lifecycle. The paper concludes that disciplined AI governance can become a strategic advantage for organizations that protect intellectual property by design rather than treating it as a downstream compliance issue.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6731299`

- title: Not All Language Models Are Generative: A Methods Primer on Encoder Models in Social Work Research
- authors: Brian Perron, Dragan Stoll, Zia Qi, Nari Yoo, Cheng Ren
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6731299
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Encoder-based language models constitute a practical and energy-efficient family of language models for text classification and related natural language tasks in social work research. These models offer advantages in speed, reproducibility, and local deployment over the generative large language models that currently dominate methodological discussions in the field. This methodological paper introduces encoder classifiers to social work researchers and explains how these small, task-specific models differ from generative models in architecture, output behavior, and hardware requirements. We present an empirical comparison using four child welfare classification benchmarks previously evaluated with generative models, demonstrating that a 150-million-parameter encoder classifier matched or exceeded the most compute-efficient generative configuration on three of four benchmarks while processing records 50 to 190 times faster on the same hardware. Although we highlight the practical advantages of encoders for production-scale classification, we also acknowledge limitations, including narrower task flexibility, reliance on labeled training data, and accuracy gaps on tasks with specialized vocabularies. We conclude that encoder classifiers are a practical first-line option for social work classification tasks where reproducibility, modest hardware, and corpus-scale throughput are binding constraints.
