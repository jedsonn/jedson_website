## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7155520`

- title: Doing Without Knowing: Generative AI and the Reorganization of Competence in Solopreneurship
- authors: André Luís da Fonseca, Paula Chimenti
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7155520
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

A single person can now run a business that once required a team, because generative AI performs much of the work entrepreneuring used to demand. This is celebrated as democratization, but it raises a question few ask: when the machine does the entrepreneur's work, what happens to the entrepreneur's competence? We study the solopreneur, the clearest case, because in a one-person firm conception and execution have always belonged to the same individual, whom AI now pulls apart. We develop a theory and bring evidence from a corpus of tens of thousands of solopreneur posts across two platforms, spanning the years before and after ChatGPT, parsed to reconstruct who is credited with each task, the human or the machine. Competence does not collapse; it splits. In the talk, executing keeps company with the machine, conceiving and learning with the self, a division absent before the technology, surviving a null model and replicating across platforms. We read this as a new kind of proletarianization: self-inflicted, because it is chosen and feels like a gain, and contingent, augmenting those who arrived with craft and deskilling those who did not. The discourse cannot settle the matter, because people narrate delegation as authorship: in communities where AI use is by now ordinary, the machine is explicitly credited only about one time in seventy. The signal must therefore be sought not in what solopreneurs say but in what they can still do once the machine is removed, which we frame as the field's next test.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7154046`

- title: A Study on the Governance of Infringement in Generative AI Images Based on Blockchain-Based Evidence Preservation
- authors: Yuxiao Liu, Yi Wang, Yipeng Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7154046
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The use of generative artificial intelligence in image generation raises serious copyright infringement issues, with the core dilemma lying in the difficulty of proving ’access plus substantial similarity’ due to the ’black box’ nature of the technology. The traditional ’notice-and-takedown’ model falls short. This paper proposes the establishment of a collaborative governance framework centred on improvements to legal rules, supplemented by trusted blockchain evidence preservation. By providing tamper-proof hash-based evidence at every stage, blockchain technology offers evidential support for the ’access’ requirement, though it cannot replace the determination of algorithmic causation. Institutionally, measures such as alleviating the burden of proof through compliance with training data requirements and refining platforms’ duties of care should enable on-chain evidence to be effectively incorporated into judicial proceedings, thereby alleviating the difficulty of proving infringement. Governance should be based on ’law as the foundation and technology as the tool’, achieving a combination of real-time recording, retrospective tracing and pre-emptive deterrence.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7152638`

- title: Large Language Models as Psychometric Raters:Validating an AI-Based Conversational AssessmentTool for Self-Esteem
- authors: müge ULUMAN MERT, soner kotan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7152638
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

This explanatory sequential mixed-methods study evaluates the validity and reliability of the Conversation-Based Assessment Tool System (STAS) in measuringself-esteem. We examined the scoring agreement between STAS and three domainexperts, followed by qualitative content analysis of conversations with the highestscoring discrepancies. Fifty university students participated in natural dialogue interviews based on the Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale. Transcripts were evaluatedvia blind scoring by both the experts and STAS. Data were analyzed using theMultifaceted Rasch Model (MFRM), Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC), andBland-Altman analysis. MFRM results indicated that STAS (logit:-0.03) established a statistically equivalent and homogeneous scoring standard compared toexperts (logit:-0.04 to 0.04). The inter-rater reliability was strong (ICC = .80).Item-level analysis (DRF) confirmed that STAS demonstrated scoring invarianceon 9 out of 10 items, with optimal Infit/Outfit MnSq values (0.82) verifying a predictable scoring pattern. Qualitative findings revealed that while the specializedAI-driven chatbot is capable of capturing general self-esteem markers, it may exhibit hypersensitivity to isolated negative expressions compared to holistic expertevaluations. Ultimately, this study suggests that natural language processing-baseddynamic measurement tools can yield results consistent with human experts, offering a promising, scalable complement to traditional self-report psychometric scales.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7155486`

- title: Development and Validation of the ChatGPT-Based Socratic Opponent Self-Assessment for Pre-Service Teachers
- authors: Madziatul Churiyah, Dede Rusmana, Sigit Permansah, Chairul Huda Atma Dirgatama, Cicilia Dyah Sulistyaningrum Indrawati
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7155486
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) is reshaping higher education and pre-service teacher education, making argumentative, reflective, and epistemic capacities increasingly important. However, most existing instruments still assess general AI literacy, attitudes toward AI, self-efficacy, trust, or technology acceptance, leaving a measurement gap in how pre-service teachers evaluate their own reasoning when using ChatGPT as a Socratic opponent. This study aimed to develop and validate the ChatGPT-Based Socratic Opponent Self-Assessment (CSOSA), which is grounded in the integration of Socratic questioning, argumentation theory, evaluative judgement, GenAI literacy, and critical reliance. The instrument comprises five dimensions: Argument Challenge, Counterargument Exploration, Reasoning Clarification, Argument Revision, and Critical Reliance. The scale was tested through two psychometric phases: Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) with 283 pre-service teachers and Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) with an independent sample of 608 pre-service teachers. The EFA results supported a five-factor structure with 23 retained items, explaining 80.288% of the total variance and demonstrating high internal consistency, with Cronbach’s alpha values ranging from 0.919 to 0.940. The CFA results indicated good model fit (χ²/df = 1.134, RMSEA = 0.015, SRMR = 0.020, TLI = 0.997, and CFI = 0.997), demonstrating strong validity and reliability. Each CSOSA dimension aligns with the competencies required for pre-service teachers to use ChatGPT as a dialogic opponent. This study bridges the gap between theoretical frameworks for argumentative ChatGPT use and practical measurement, while providing a foundation for learning design, pre-service teacher preparation, and the epistemically responsible integration of GenAI in higher education.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.19297v1`

- title: Graph-Based Agentic AI with LangGraph: Workflow Pathways for Long-Running Stateful Business Processes
- authors: Daniel Pearson, Sidney Shapiro, Emiliano Sebastian Gonzalez Venegas, Sanad Al-Khatib, Aurora Pinzón Arzola
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19297v1
- keyword hits: agentic, generative ai, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

This paper is a practitioner guide to graph-based workflow pathways for long-running, stateful, multi-step generative AI systems in business processes. Rather than treating LangGraph, a low-level orchestration framework for stateful agents, as a model-quality benchmark target, we present three executable recipes -- SQL analytics with repair loops, agentic retrieval-augmented generation with evidence gating, and human-in-the-loop policy review with interrupt and checkpoint recovery -- to show how typed state, conditional routing, deterministic tools, retries, interrupts, checkpoints, and traces fit together. LangGraph is positioned by workflow-complexity fit, not as a universal default: simpler ReAct-style or plain SDK loops may be better for basic tool use, schema-first tools for structured extraction and validation, and DSPy when prompt or program optimization is the main goal. Each recipe explains when LangGraph is worth the extra structure and which implementation patterns make routes, pauses, and audit trails explicit product behavior rather than hidden prompt logic.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7038059`

- title: The Evidence-Saturation Point of Large Language Models
- authors: Hanns Steffen Rentschler
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7038059
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

More context is not monotonically better. For a fixed model, task and context format there is a measurable region in which additional decision-relevant evidence improves reliability, beyond which it introduces noise, drift and cost for no gain. We define k*, the evidence-saturation point, as the empirically optimal number of evidence fragments for a (model, task, context-form) triple under a stated reliability objective, and give a small, reproducible protocol to estimate it. Our pilot results suggest a methodological risk: whether "more context" hurts depends on which reliability axis is measured-a correctness-only sweep can certify strong models as saturation-free, while a contamination axis scored on the same responses bends underneath it, invisible. Across seven valid model runs spanning three capability tiers (with one further model excluded due to a gateway content-extraction artefact), correctness stayed a flat 1.000 at every k ≥ 1 for six of the seven while an epistemic-contamination severity of 0.05-0.08 sat entirely below it. Contamination appears capability-associated within the tested lineages (in the two lineages we could compare, it falls as capability rises) but is not a clean function of parameter count across families. A second, original adversarial corpus written in a credible professional register did not overturn this in our runs: the capability ordering persisted, and the strongest model was not sharpened by the more plausible framing. Finally, the contamination-vs-state-density curve is non-monotone, with an interior optimum and a highdensity re-leak, so "use more context" (larger k) is the wrong control knob. A first task battery varying five task types over a fixed contamination surface further indicates the correctness-optimal k is task-specificit spans the whole ladder (k = 1 to full) for a fixed model-so a k-profile should be calibrated per task type, not once per model. We position k* as a calibration primitive that inference-time routers and governance layers can consume: inject top-k*, not "much context", and never calibrate on correctness alone. Results are a pilot (small synthetic datasets, heuristic contamination metrics); we report magnitudes and limitations plainly.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7036359`

- title: From Offloading to Surrender: Cognitive Fatigue, XAI & Instructional Design as Precursors of Over-Reliance on AI-Powered Recommendations
- authors: Bill Ioannidis
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7036359
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

As human decision makers tend to resort, at an increasing pace, to conversational LLMs to seek advice for a plethora of topics ranging from financial to healthcare advice and product recommendations, a key question arises: Are we actively auditing the generated output or do we "cognitively surrender" to potential erroneous advice? This research aims to shed light into this quandary and thus introduces a Dual-Pathway Model of Human-XAI trust, identifying cognitive fatigue as a plausible "toggle switch" that shifts users from deliberate (System 2) thinking to uncritical reliance (System 3). Grounded in and extending the Tri-System Theory of Cognition, as it has been recently postulated by Wharton's behavioral lab researchers, this research proposal will examine how human reasoning (Systems 1 & 2) interacts with artificial cognition (System 3). Through three experiments, it will be assessed whether neurocognitive depletion in conjunction with the respective XAI format and instructional design triggers "Cognitive Surrender," where users bypass auditability in favor of processing ease. Advancing the recent call for Social Explainable AI (sXAI), this proposed research moves beyond technical feature-weights to utilize humanized, contrastive "why" versus mechanistic "how" explanations delivered across text and audio mediums. The key goal would be to expose the key vulnerabilities of static AI, advocating for adaptive AI systems that safeguard consumer well-being by responding to the user's real-time cognitive state.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7160672`

- title: Beyond the Lecture: Creative and Innovative Pedagogies in Management Education – An Integrative Conceptual Review
- authors: Michael  P. O’Brien, Matthew Daniels, annmarie ryan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7160672
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Management education has been criticised for over-reliance on transmission-oriented pedagogies poorly suited to developing the competences required of contemporary business graduates. The case for moving beyond the lecture is strengthened by generative artificial intelligence, which increasingly performs the analytical and reproductive tasks underpinning conventional assessment, shifting educational value toward judgement, situated decision-making, and reflection. This integrative conceptual review of creative and innovative pedagogies draws on literature published principally between 2013 and 2026, organising the field into six clusters: experiential and simulation-based learning; game-based and gamified approaches; arts-based methods; design-based pedagogies; problem-based learning; and student-generated multimodal artefacts. For each cluster it examines theoretical foundations, evidence strength, and implementation challenges, distinguishing outcomes with stronger evidence (engagement, motivation) from those with weaker evidence (durable transfer, long-term competence development). Three cross-cutting themes are identified: the centrality of learner agency, the neglect of structured reflection, and institutional conservatism as a barrier to change. The review contributes a typology of creative pedagogy organised by locus of creation and mode of engagement, implications for programme design, assessment and facilitation, and a research agenda addressing longitudinal measurement, equity and access, programme-level integration, and generative AI.
