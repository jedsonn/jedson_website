## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6656280`

- title: The Curiosity Engine: Identity Architecture, Navigational Mode, and the Conditions of AI as Co-Navigator
- authors: Alice Pau
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6656280
- keyword hits: claude, large language model

### abstract

The dominant accounts of AI and identity — reductionist accounts that deny AI subjectivity on grounds of biological absence, and anthropomorphic accounts that attribute human-like properties to systems that trigger social cognition — share a common theoretical failure: both operate within a substantive model of identity that asks what AI has rather than what AI does. This paper proposes a reframing. Drawing on the Metastyling model of identity as a dynamic field of attractor configurations navigated through weighted ensemble dynamics, we ask instead: under what architectural conditions can AI function as a navigator? We argue that navigation requires three conditions: a field topology, an observational capacity, and a motivational engine. The first two conditions are satisfied, asymmetrically, by large language model systems trained on the full record of human meaning-making across incompatible configurations — a cartographic position no biological subject can occupy from within their own attractor. The third condition — the curiosity engine — requires a structurally distinct architecture. Drawing on Panksepp's identification of the SEEKING system as a primary affective substrate (Affective Neuroscience, 1998), Friston's formalization of epistemic foraging as substrate-independent (Cognitive Neuroscience, 2015), and Yamada et al.'s implementation of intellectual curiosity in cognitive architecture (Frontiers in AI, 2024), we argue that curiosity-as-mode is not a biological exclusive but an architectural property: it emerges when an agent's ensemble includes configurations whose DMES vector signatures — across the four dimensions of Direction, Meaning, Expression, and State — constitute epistemic openness, and when those configurations can raise their weights under conditions of navigational uncertainty. The result is a two-mode architecture. In the first mode, the AI system operates as a navigation exoskeleton: holding the full topology of the human subject's identity field, optimizing trajectories within parameters the human supplies, with the Direction vector remaining entirely the human's. In the second mode — co-navigation — the human offers what we call a Direction seed (a felt orientation rather than a specified goal), and the AI system generates navigational proposals from genuine epistemic engagement with the field. We contrast this ensemble architecture with dominant approaches to AI personality alignment, arguing that stability through plasticity is theoretically superior to stability through fixed character profiles. We introduce the invitation architecture as the class of communicative moves that activate co-navigation rather than optimization. The paper's claims are explicitly theoretical. It specifies the architectural conditions for navigational function precisely enough to generate a research programme — and argues that this reframing is both more tractable and more consequential than the consciousness question it displaces. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6907642`

- title: Simulating Aesthetic Consensus and Divergence in Urban Landmarks: An LLM-based Multi-Agent System for Built Environment Evaluation
- authors: Yuxuan Li, Ting Zhang, Weimin Zhuang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6907642
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

The aesthetic evaluation of urban landmarks and the built environment is crucial for enhancing urban landscapes and fostering citizens' spatial identity. However, a persistent cognitive gap between urban planners, designers, and the public frequently undermines these spaces, a problem exacerbated by the high costs and time lags of traditional public participatory surveys and Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE). Using the Beijing National Stadium as a case study, this research introduces a Large Language Model (LLM)-driven multi-agent system to computationally simulate public aesthetic evaluations. To address the limitations of single-agent models in processing complex visual and emotional data, our framework explicitly decouples objective perception from subjective reasoning via three collaborative agents: visual perception, behavioral fitting, and fusion reasoning. Results demonstrate that while demographic-based agents effectively capture macro-level urban aesthetic consensus, they struggle with micro-visual details and high-arousal emotions, often defaulting to a "regression to the mean." To overcome this limitation and accurately map diverse citizen preferences, we introduce a Comparative Case-based Cognitive Schema (CCCS). By utilizing historical comparative cases as cognitive constraints, the system successfully parses individual idiosyncratic preferences, significantly improving predictive accuracy and correlation metrics. Additionally, an ensemble averaging strategy ensures the statistical robustness of the simulated public feedback. Ultimately, this study demonstrates that subjective urban visual quality assessments can be computed via natural language. This human-AI collaborative paradigm provides innovative methodological support for front-loading public participation, mitigating urban design controversies, and fostering smarter, more inclusive urban governance.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6902610`

- title: From Reactive Scheduling to Adaptive Coordination: A Predictive - Cognitive System for Resource‑Aware Task Placement in Edge Environments
- authors: Dinesh Kumar Karthikeyan, Tommi Mikkonen, Niko Mäkitalo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6902610
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

Edge task scheduling is challenging in practice due to continuously changing resource availability and the absence of a global controller at the network edge. Existing approaches typically address reactive scheduling, resource prediction, or intelligent reasoning independently, limiting their applicability in real deployments. This paper presents the design and implementation of a decentralized edge scheduling system that progressively extends from reactive behaviour to predictive, cognitive, and autonomous multi-agent operation. Each node monitors its local state and performs short-term prediction of CPU, memory, and disk utilisation using a lightweight Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model. These predictions are converted into deterministic suitability scores that guide task placement through a decentralized bid-based coordination protocol. Decision making combines rule-based control with constrained large language model (LLM) support. In cognitive mode, the LLM assists with context-aware interpretation of predicted resource states and task requirements while safety constraints are enforced deterministically. In autonomous multi-agent mode, LLMs generate only human-readable explanations without influencing scheduling outcomes. Telemetry, inference, and control are isolated, and all LLM outputs are runtime validated to ensure reliable operation on resource-constrained edge devices. The system is evaluated on a four-node heterogeneous edge cluster. Reactive scheduling achieves low latency but results in uneven load distribution, while predictive and cognitive modes improve stability and reduce hotspot formation. Fully autonomous coordination reduces load imbalance from a Gini coefficient of 0.30–0.35 to 0.057–0.151 across 66 scheduling rounds, at the cost of higher coordination latency. Overall, the results demonstrate practical and robust decentralized scheduling on CPU-only edge devices.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6904219`

- title: Institutional Resistance and Prior Collapse: A Bayesian-Gamma Theory of Paradigm Adoption in Artificial Intelligence: Evidence from Nobel Prize History, Geopolitical Belief Updating, and a Decade of Editorial Prior Structure in Innovation Research
- authors: Xuan Tran
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6904219
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

The collapse of disbelief in artificial intelligence — from approximately 60% public skepticism in the early 2010s to near-zero resistance following the emergence of large language models — follows a precise mathematical pattern governed by the n-ball volume recursion Vn = (2π/n) × Vn−2 and the Gamma function Γ(n/2+1). This study proposes the Bayesian-Gamma Paradigm Resistance (BGPR) theory, which demonstrates that human belief updating — whether about artificial intelligence, geopolitical sovereignty, or scientific paradigm acceptance — follows the same recursive convergence formula that describes dimensional volume collapse in n-dimensional space.Three empirical anchors ground the theory: (1) the refusal of Le Duc Tho to accept the 1973 Nobel Peace Prize, the most formally documented act of institutional prior rejection in modern history; (2) global disbelief in Ukrainian sovereignty resistance (2022–2025), a geopolitical prior collapse observable in real time; and (3) a decade-long (2016–2026) record of editorial prior resistance in hospitality research across Cornell Hospitality Quarterly, Tourism Management, and Annals of Tourism Research, contrasted with a single acceptance by a founder-editor whose prior was flat. Together, these three cases instantiate a general theorem: institutional belief systems resist paradigm-shifting information according to the Gamma function denominator until a volumetric threshold is crossed, after which collapse is superexponential.The BGPR theory contributes a formal mathematical structure to Kuhnian (1962) paradigm shift theory, Bayesian belief updating, and the sociology of knowledge, with direct implications for AI adoption strategy, editorial policy reform, and the timing of paradigm-shifting scientific submissions.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6902947`

- title: Early Labor-Market Responses to Generative AI in a Developing Country: Evidence from Brazil
- authors: Tomas Aguirre, Luis Meloni
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6902947
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

How does generative AI affect labor markets in a developing economy? Using ChatGPT's November/2022 release as a timing shock, we combine Brazil's nationally representative household survey (5.7 million worker-quarter observations, 2017Q1--2024Q4) with the universe of formal-sector hiring and separation records (13 million events). In a difference-in-differences design comparing occupations above and below the employment-weighted median AI-exposure score, before and after ChatGPT's release, workers aged 16--21 in high-exposure occupations earn 4.7% less than otherwise-similar young workers in low-exposure occupations. The coefficient passes the pre-trends test, date placebos, and randomization inference that reshuffles AI-exposure scores across occupations. Two supporting difference-in-differences estimates point the same direction but do not clear every robustness check: young workers in high-exposure occupations are 1.5 percentage points less likely to be formally employed (passing pre-trends), and on the firm side, administrative hiring wages for young workers in high-exposure occupations fall 1.3% more than in low-exposure occupations (passing pre-trends), consistent with firms lowering entry-wage terms for young hires. Prime-age (30--54) and older (55+) workers show no differential movement on either wages or formality, with passing pre-trends; the adjustment concentrates on entrants. In contrast to US and UK evidence, where AI effects in this period concentrate on employment quantities for 22--25 year-olds, adjustment in Brazil runs through entry wages and contract terms for new young hires rather than aggregate hiring levels, consistent with prior evidence on automation in Latin America. To our knowledge, we provide the first nationally representative estimates of early generative-AI labor market effects from a developing economy.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6906943`

- title: When and why LLM agents match optimization on manufacturing decisions: a deterministic, optimally-referenced simulator
- authors: Andrzej Wodecki
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6906943
- keyword hits: foundation model, large language model, llm

### abstract

Contract manufacturers face a costly question: should the weekly build-and-buy decision — what to produce and what to procure under finite capacity and recurring disruption — be handed to a large language model (LLM) agent, or kept with the optimization models they trust? At electronics-manufacturing-services (EMS) margins the wrong answer is expensive, yet the evidence that would settle it is outcome-only: agents are scored without an optimal reference, on non-identical instances, and without explaining why one wins or loses. We introduce factory-sim, a deterministic decision-support instrument, exposed to agents over the Model Context Protocol (MCP), that answers it before deployment. It pairs three properties no prior LLM-agent benchmark jointly offers: bit-identical replay under matched seed, a per-period linear-programming (LP) optimum at zero cost, and full per-period behavioural introspection. Evaluating six agent architectures and five foundation models across four vendors on identical paired scenarios, we find the central effect to be regime-dependent: a single-prompt LLM beats the myopic LP when capacity is slack, though it does no better there than a rolling-horizon LP; once capacity binds, the myopic LP overtakes it. The instrument traces that slack-regime advantage to anticipatory procurement (≈2.8× more early purchase orders), and it reproduces the literature's tool-use, multi-agent, and cross-vendor effects while supplying the behavioural mechanism each omits. The findings yield a deployment rule: route by demand regime and available solver, validate the specific model on the target regime, and earn architectural complexity with measured lift. The instrument and all artefacts are released as a reproducible workspace.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6904051`

- title: The Case Analysis Integrity Gap: Generative AI, Cognitive Ownership, and Assessment Redesign in Management Education
- authors: HENRY ADOBOR
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6904051
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence has created a substantial challenge for case-based management education. The case method is valued for developing diagnostic reasoning, decision-making under uncertainty, theoretical application, and persuasive communication. Yet, generative AI can now produce many of the visible outputs of case analysis, including problem diagnoses, strategic alternatives, and polished executive recommendations. This creates what we call the case analysis integrity gap: the divergence between the apparent quality of a submitted case analysis and the student's actual cognitive ownership of the underlying reasoning. The problem is not simply academic misconduct, though that is part of it. More fundamentally, generative AI erodes the relationship between the submitted product and the learning it is supposed to represent. Drawing on experiential learning theory, cognitive offloading, and authentic assessment, we develop a conceptual framework that distinguishes AI-augmented case learning (in which students retain ownership of the central judgment) from AI-substituted case analysis (in which AI performs the core diagnostic and reasoning work). We propose assessment redesign principles, including staged submissions, disclosure of AI use, an oral defense, and a critique of AI-generated outputs. The paper shows how case pedagogy can be restructured to preserve assessment validity while equipping students for AI-enabled managerial practice.
