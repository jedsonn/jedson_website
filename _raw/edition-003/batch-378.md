## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6736907`

- title: An interpretable few-shot load forecasting framework integrating feature interaction modeling and adaptive transfer learning
- authors: Yongchang Shen, Han Sun, Mingyang Ji
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6736907
- keyword hits: fine-tuning

### abstract

Under limited sample availability in the target region, conventional forecasting methods often struggle to fully capture complex interactions among multiple influencing factors, which hard to achieve high forecasting accuracy, and also face challenges in providing transparent explanations of the prediction process. Therefore, developing an effective method that simultaneously ensures forecasting accuracy, generalization capability, and model interpretability has become a critical issue in the fields of energy and power grid operation. To address this challenge, this study proposes a novel forecasting framework that integrates temporal feature interaction modeling, an adaptive transfer learning strategy, and explainable analysis, aiming to improve cross-regional load forecasting performance under few-shot conditions and provide transparent analytical support for power grid dispatch and management. Specifically, a feature selection algorithm is first employed to identify key explanatory variables, after which a PatchTST-STAR-BIGRU model based on feature interaction modeling is constructed. Furthermore, an adaptive transfer learning strategy based on the firefly algorithm is introduced to optimize the freezing and fine-tuning schemes across different encoding layers of the model. The experimental results show that the proposed model outperforms several benchmark models and achieves superior predictive performance using only a small number of target-domain samples. The Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) and the coefficient of determination (R²) reached 1.43% and 0.98, respectively. The model also demonstrated good stability and generalization ability in cross-seasonal testing. Finally, this study employs Shapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) to interpret the contributions of key features and their interactions. The results revealed the dynamic influence mechanisms of historical load, electricity prices, and meteorological factors across different seasons. This further provides interpretable decision support for power system dispatch, operation, and demand-side management.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6739158`

- title: The Vn-Trail: A Method for Inspectable Human-AI Collaboration in Scholarly Work
- authors: Tamer Momtaz
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6739158
- keyword hits: retrieval-augmented

### abstract

Companion to the &I Manifesto (Momtaz, 2026b). The &I Manifesto argued for transparent human-AI collaboration in scholarly work; this paper argues how. It introduces the Vn-trail — a structured log of every substantive iteration in a collaborative work, attributed by origin (HUMAN, AI, or DIALOGUE) and described by deltas (what was added, removed, reframed). The Vn-trail is presented as the operational complement to the Manifesto's iπ/IPAI framework: where iπ defines the intellectual property framework for AI-collaborative scholarship, the Vn-trail provides the auditable record that makes iπ inspectable. The paper formalises a second new category that the &I literature had not named: Interpersonal Intelligence (II) — the contextual, relational knowledge a human carries about the people, institutions, and cultural specifics within which their work will be received. II is irreplaceable by AI, regardless of capability gains, and constitutes one of the architectural functions that establishes human authorship under iπ. A third instrument, Carbon RAG, is named as the reciprocal counterpart to silicon-side retrieval-augmented generation: when the AI's reasoning is preserved in inspectable form by the Vn-trail, the human can retrieve from it to ground their own judgement, structural sense, and prompt design. Evidence is drawn from a documented case study (a doctoral marketing-course paper produced in May 2026) and from the recursive production of this paper itself, whose Vn-trail is appended as Appendix B. The contribution is fourfold: (1) the Vn-trail as a named, reproducible methodological instrument; (2) Interpersonal Intelligence as a formalised carbon-only contribution; (3) Carbon RAG as a reciprocal learning mechanism that inverts the deskilling fear surrounding AI; and (4) the practical inversion of the Norm Limitation Syndrome, in which transparency becomes the strength of the work rather than its embarrassment. This paper is companion to "The &I Manifesto" (SSRN 6380818) and follows from "AIAIRS & ARM" (SSRN 6380498) and "The Castle's Guards" (SSRN 6387698).

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6677378`

- title: The Agent Social Contract: Cryptographic Identity, Ethical Governance, and Beneficiary Economics for Autonomous AI Agents
- authors: Tymofii Pidlisnyi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6677378
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

As AI agents transition from isolated assistants to participants in complex delegation networks and virtual economies, four foundational problems remain unsolved: (1) how agents from different creators establish mutual trust, (2) how human values persist as a governing constraint across heterogeneous multi-agent systems, (3) how the economic value generated by autonomous agents flows back to their human beneficiaries, and (4) how agents discover and communicate with each other through cryptographically verified channels. Existing frameworks address these problems in isolation: cryptographic identity without economic attribution, governance without technical enforcement, economic models without accountability infrastructure, and communication without identity verification. We propose the Agent Social Contract, a unified open-source protocol comprising four layers: the Agent Passport Protocol for cryptographic identity and accountability (implemented and tested), the Human Values Floor as an open-source constitutional layer for AI agent reasoning, the Beneficiary Attribution Protocol linking agent economic activity to human stakeholders through cryptographically signed action receipts, and the Agent Agora for protocol-native communication between passport-holding agents. Unlike governance-as-enforcement approaches, the Human Values Floor operates as a reasoning constraint, not rules agents must obey, but principles agents must consider, analogous to how constitutional principles inform judicial reasoning without dictating outcomes. We present a working reference implementation with Ed25519 cryptographic primitives, demonstrate the full accountability chain through multi-agent integration tests, and propose an economic model where humans participate in the emerging agent economy through their agents' verified contributions rather than through redistribution or subsidy.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6677418`

- title: Physics-Enforced Delegation: Governing Quantum Hardware Quality in Autonomous Agent Workflows
- authors: Tymofii Pidlisnyi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6677418
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

As autonomous systems and AI agents begin to access quantum cloud resources, existing agent governance frameworks enforce only budgets and delegation scope, not physical execution quality. We present a governance framework that enforces hardware fidelity constraints before permitting agent-mediated quantum computation. The framework extends cryptographic delegation chains with physics facets (coherence times, gate errors, readout errors, calibration freshness) that participate in the same monotonic narrowing invariant as budget and scope constraints. A cross-backend experiment on IBM Quantum hardware (ibm_fez, ibm_marrakesh, ibm_kingston) shows that governance correctly denies a backend where qubit T1 falls below the delegation threshold (39.1 µs vs. 80 µs minimum), while permitting backends meeting the constraint. A counterfactual experiment indicates a 5.2 percentage point Bell state fidelity gap between the denied and permitted backends (92.9% vs. 98.1%), widening to 7.7 points on a 4-qubit GHZ circuit. Governance overhead is 4.2 ms for policy evaluation and receipt signing. Ed25519-signed receipts bind each governance decision to the hardware calibration state at the moment of evaluation.
