This framework explicates the entropy growth of semantic information and the mechanisms of bias amplification during symbolic sequence generation. ### abstract

Traffic accident prevention requires not only accurate risk prediction but also the ability to communicate context-specific warnings to drivers in an understandable and timely manner. This study proposes a hybrid artificial intelligence framework that integrates machine learning–based accident severity modeling, real-time contextual data processing, and large language model (LLM)–driven semantic warning generation for intelligent driver assistance. - title: LLM-guided Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning with Error Accumulation Avoidance Ability for Long-Horizon Robotic Manipulation Tasks
- authors: Jielin Tang, Nan Xie, Beier Lu, Xiangrong Gong, Jingzhi Zhao, Jinglin Chen, Zihang Tong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6838201
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Although reinforcement learning has been widely applied to trajectory planning for robotic manipulation tasks, the terminal positioning error of individually trained reinforcement learning (RL) policies accumulates over time during long-horizon robotic manipulation tasks, significantly reducing the overall task success rate. To address this issue, we propose a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework guided by a large language model (LLM). The LLM parses natural language instructions into ordered subtask sequences and automatically synthesizes reward functions for RL-based trajectory planning. For large-displacement motion subtasks, RL policies generate coarse trajectories, after which a purpose-built stereo visual servoing module corrects the end-effector pose. This module combines eye-in-hand and eye-to-hand cameras, performs feature matching and triangulation, and applies closed-loop proportional control to drive the residual error below a tight tolerance, thereby halting error propagation to downstream subtasks. Evaluated on six long-horizon manipulation tasks from RLBench and a custom industrial assembly benchmark, the framework achieves an average success rate of 77.7 %, substantially outperforming existing RL-based and LLM-guided planners. Ablation studies reveal that removing the visual servoing correction causes the average success rate to drop from 77.7 % to 37.2 %, confirming its central role in suppressing error accumulation. The approach is further validated on a physical 6-DoF collaborative robot, demonstrating reliable execution of precision assembly tasks. This research bridges the gap between high-level task planning and low-level execution accuracy, providing a solution for reliable long-horizon robotic manipulation.

### abstract

Trust in artificial intelligence is widely discussed as a quantity to be calibrated, yet the calibration literature treats trust as scalar: a gap between a user's reliance and a system's reliability, to be narrowed by correcting over-trust and under-trust. This paper argues that trust in large language models is better understood as three distinct relations; operational trust in competence, epistemic trust in truthfulness, and emotional trust in safety, that operate on different evidence, carry different risks, and fail in different ways. In human relationships these three dimensions are correlated, and generalizing across them is adaptive: a person who is demonstrably competent is, on average, also more likely to be honest and to mean us well. The result is a directional and ordered failure we name the trust leak: warranted operational trust silently inflates epistemic trust, which in turn inflates.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6845159`

- title: Climate Risk Disclosure and Market Perception: Evidence from a Large Language Model
- authors: Po-Lin Wang, Dan Yang, Jian Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6845159
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

This paper examines the relationship between climate risk disclosures in the MD\&A and Risk Factors sections of 10-K filings and market-based measures of firm risk for Russell 3000 firms. Using LLM-based and dictionary-based textual analysis, we distinguish between physical and transition risk disclosures. We find that greater overall climate disclosure is associated with higher market-implied firm risk, but the effect depends on disclosure type: physical-risk discussion is linked to more favorable assessments (consistent with a reassurance interpretation), while transition-risk discussion is linked to less favorable assessments (consistent with a red-flag interpretation). The transition-risk association is context-dependent: it is substantially attenuated, and in some cases reversed, for firms in transition-vulnerable industries. Following the SEC's March 2022 climate-disclosure proposal, the red-flag association on general climate disclosure attenuates as well, consistent with disclosure standardization improving the informational value of climate-related text. Our results suggest that effective climate policy should move beyond simple disclosure mandates and encourage the separate reporting of physical and transition risks, as investors appear to process these disclosures differently.
