## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6357778`

- title: Collusive Content in Corporate Communications
- authors: Eduardo M. Azevedo, Joseph E. Harrington Jr, Ioan Octavian Rusu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6357778
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

In industries such as airlines, steel, and truck rentals, firms have coordinated to raise prices using public announcements such as earnings calls. This paper studies collusive content in a large corpus of firms’ public communications. A large language model (LLM) is used to examine almost 500,000 company transcripts. The LLM is selective in that only 0.12% of the transcripts satisfy the criterion for collusive content. A human audit of the 301 transcripts with the highest LLM scores identifies systematic ways in which the communication is facilitating coordinated conduct. The audit evaluates the performance of the LLM in substituting for human experts and thus assesses its suitability as a screening tool for competition agencies and plaintiff law firms.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6433263`

- title: Cascades of Drift: Mental Health Risks of Prolonged AI Conversations
- authors: Marlynn Wei
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6433263
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Large language models are increasingly used for emotional support and mental health guidance. AI chatbots invite extended, open-ended interaction in which roles between user and system may gradually shift. This paper introduces the "cascades of drift" framework, a clinical model describing multidimensional, dynamic, and compounding forms of drift that may emerge during prolonged AI conversations and reshape identity, reality testing, relationships, decision-making, and agency. This paper proposes a taxonomy of eight "interactive drift" types: conversational, relational, temporal, identity, reality-testing, epistemic, autonomy, and moral drift. Relational drift marks the shifting roles of AI as tool to intimate conversational partner to perceived authoritative source. These forms of drift stem from the bidirectional feedback loop between user and AI chatbot, reinforced over time through narrative formation, memory reconsolidation, and neuroplasticity in the user, and model degradation in the AI system. This paper further identifies the emergence of the "reliability paradox," in which individuals incrementally accumulate trust in the AI system even as the accuracy and reliability of both the model and their interactions may simultaneously deteriorate, leading to a "trust-accuracy inversion." These cascades of drift may unfold nonlinearly and interactively across time, with each form of drift lowering the threshold for others. These drifts manifest across four mental health risk domains: attachment and relational dynamics, reality-testing, safety and crisis, and ethical and systemic concerns. The cascades of drift framework offers a clinical model for understanding the formative psychological effects of longitudinal AI chatbot use.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6362299`

- title: The Macroeconomics of Generative AI: Firm Entry and Price Dynamics
- authors: Jose Carreno
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6362299
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

While the economic literature on generative artificial intelligence has predominantly focused on the disruptive consequences of cognitive automation for the labor market, this paper broadens the analytical lens to product markets. Combining granular occupational AI exposure metrics with administrative records, we document that this technological shift has triggered an immediate, structural surge in firm entry, systematically lowering barriers for high-propensity employer ventures. Concurrently, using a continuous event-study framework, we demonstrate that these firm-level efficiencies pass through to consumers, generating persistent relative price deflation in highly exposed sectors. To rationalize these dual empirical findings, we develop a tractable general equilibrium model of firm heterogeneity. Ultimately, our framework provides a necessary macroeconomic counterweight to prevailing labor-market concerns: by catalyzing structural market dynamism and acting as a potent disinflationary anchor, generative AI generates a substantial and persistent consumer welfare dividend.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6447160`

- title: The Three-Step Test in International Copyright – A Global Framework for Generative AI Training
- authors: Nicola Lucchi, Tim W. Dornis, Pascal T. Sierek
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6447160
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The training of generative artificial intelligence (genAI) models involves the processing of large datasets, which often include copyrighted materials, thereby raising questions of copyright infringement. These questions tend to center on whether such use constitutes fair use or falls under text and data mining (TDM) exceptions. In the US, the fair use doctrine-assessed through four flexible factors, including market effects-remains unresolved with regard to genAI training. Meanwhile, in the EU, there is an ongoing debate about the applicability of the DSM Directive's TDM exceptions to genAI. What has remained largely unexplored in these discussions, however, is the fact that both systems must operate within binding international treaty constraints, particularly the three-step test requiring that exceptions do not unfairly conflict with copyright holders' economic rights. Any proposed reforms to copyright policy must therefore fit within these constraints, balancing innovation with rights protection. This paper critiques broad copyright exceptions for genAI training and proposes conditions such as transparency, audits, and compensation to harmonize with international norms, while recognizing the nuanced differences between US and EU approaches and suggesting targeted legislative actions in the EU to align with the three-step test.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6482741`

- title: EU Data Access Regimes and the Viability of GeoAI: From Citizen Sensors to Regional and Local SDIs
- authors: Muhammad Imran
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6482741
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

GeoAI emerged to provide geospatial intelligence by combining Large Language Model (LLM) agents and machine learning algorithms with Earth Observation (EO) data. This paper investigates how European Union data access and AI governance regimes shape the conditions for GeoAI deployment across a fragmented geospatial data ecosystem. Compared to other regions like the US & China, promising GeoAI products in the EU advance in academia more rapidly than their implementation in technological initiatives. The "lab-to-market gap" is often attributed to the perception that the EU regulations create disproportionate compliance burdens and legal ambiguity for GeoAI products, especially when high-resolution geospatial data can be linked to citizens, either directly or indirectly. The GDPR protects citizens' data, while the EU AI Act ensures the accountability of AI systems. However, the central issue may be less of an absolute regulatory ’block’ than the practical challenge of building stewardship-capable pipelines that align lawful data use with responsible GeoAI development. Accordingly, key research challenges include distinguishing between actual regulatory barriers and uncertainty-driven risk aversion and examining how these two governance regimes reshape the organisational readiness required for GeoAI deployment beyond academia. This study, therefore, investigates how GeoAI can remain viable and adaptive across heterogeneous data stewardships, i.e., EO from the cloud, cadastre from a land portal, farmers’ reports from an agricultural agency, and citizen sensors from a municipality, within distinctly European legal/SDI regulations, where not every dataset is equally open or equally usable. We differentiate public goods (e.g., Copernicus, open government data, many INSPIRE themes), public but restricted resources (e.g., cadastral, exposure, port/hydrographic, high-resolution LiDAR), personal or contract-bound data (e.g., farm IoT, vehicle telemetry, community sensors), and global resources governed by platform terms (e.g., GEE, US NSDI). Methodologically, we reframe the conventional three-level SDI view (global to EU/national to organisational) into a four-tier geospatial data-stewardship model spanning Tier 1 user/individual, Tier 2 organisational/corporate/platform, Tier 3 shared/public/SDI, and Tier 4 global/cloud/supra-regional infrastructures. The contribution is thus not just about building a better model but about governing heterogeneous data access so GeoAI remains institutionally and legally deployable across the EU. The research output will help commercial users to produce technically powerful as well as legally and institutionally deployable GeoAI pipelines.

### abstract

A bottleneck in empirical research is discovering which policy texts contain usable causal variation. This paper develops and validates a large-language-model pipeline that reads bills and extracts the running variable, cutoff, treatment group, and com- parison group needed to construct a regression-discontinuity (RD) or difference-in- differences (DID) design. We validate in three layers. (i) On a 125-paper published benchmark, recall is 97.3% (DID) and 94.1% (RD). (ii) A cross-feasibility placebo con- firms that the match is bill-content- driven: paired DID recall drops 15 pp when each benchmark bill’s text is swapped for panel-confirmed-infeasible text. (iii) On 159 fresh state bills, pipeline agreement with a three-RA adjudicator panel gives precision of 63% DID and 53% RD (per-RA envelopes [23%, 83%] DID, [45%, 79%] RD), with recall of 87% DID and 89% RD; these envelopes characterize the construct uncertainty across competent screeners and serve as the primary uncertainty quantity for this contested measurement. The pipeline lowers the discovery cost identified at the outset, giving empirical research a way to surface candidate natural experiments from policy text at scale.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6426761`

- title: AI Bubbles with Large Language Models
- authors: Álvaro Cartea, Patrick Chang, Nan Chen, Mingyue Zhong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-31
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6426761
- keyword hits: ai agent, chain-of-thought, large language model, large language models

### abstract

AI agents participate in both rational and irrational bubbles. In a sequential bubble game, AI agents generate speculative trades even though there is a unique no-trade equilibrium. Increasing reasoning capacity reduces, but does not eliminate, such irrational bubbles. Chain-of-thought analysis shows that AI agents participate in irrational bubbles because they form incorrect beliefs through simplified expectations of the market, and they are sensitive to framing effects when forming their beliefs. When the environment admits both a no-trade equilibrium and a speculative bubble equilibrium, AI agents recognize this multiplicity and coordinate on the speculative equilibrium. Our results suggest that greater AI sophistication does not necessarily eliminate bubbles or improve market efficiency.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6438562`

- title: Him Too? Analyzing the Effects of Epstein Connections
- authors: Marina Gertsberg, Michaela Pagel, Ekaterina Volkova
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-31
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6438562
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

The Epstein files, released between September 2025 and January 2026, offer an unprecedented window into the social and professional network of a convicted sex offender whose ties extended deep into corporate America. We construct a comprehensive sample of all S&P 500 CEOs and board members serving between 2006 and 2026-52,266 unique individuals-and search the 1,293,753 text-bearing documents for evidence of their contact with Jeffrey Epstein. Using large language model (LLM) classification of 117,394 matched correspondences, we identify 67,637 that indicate direct contact with 1,179 S&P 500 CEOs or directors. We then document three main findings. First, firms whose CEOs or board members appeared in Epstein-related news coverage experienced significantly negative cumulative abnormal returns of up to-8.5% over a ten-day window following the January 30, 2026 DOJ release. Second, adding Epstein-mediated ties to the firm network increases overall density and reduces average path lengths significantly, meaning that Epstein effectively wired corporate America into a denser, more tightly interconnected governance network than would have existed otherwise. Third, we show that Epstein's network transmitted norm contagion through shared board connections. Firms with more Epstein-connected CEOs or directors exhibit significantly worse ESG outcomes: each additional connection is associated with approximately 2.3 more annual governance incidents and 4.0 more total incidents.
