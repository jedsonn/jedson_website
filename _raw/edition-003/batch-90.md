## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6773558`

- title: Language Models are Not the Intelligence Core: Token Probability, Prompt Dependence, and Rule-Feedback Intelligence
- authors: Haozhe Gan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6773558
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This position paper argues that current large language models (LLMs) should not be treated as the primary path toward general intelligence. Their central mechanism is probabilistic generation over token sequences, which produces linguistically plausible approximations rather than grounded judgments formed through causal understanding, rule constraints, action, memory, and environmental feedback. The paper advances three claims. First, an LLM output has practical meaning only when it is interpreted, validated, or constrained by an external system; the model itself does not possess intrinsic grounding in the relevant world state. Second, token probability is not the same as the probability used by experts when judging real-world conditions, risks, or consequences. Third, prompt dependence reveals that current LLMs function more as amplifiers of human intelligence than as autonomous general agents. At the same time, the paper recognizes the historical importance of LLMs: they solve the language-interface problem. They allow machines to receive human intentions in natural language and return machine results in human-comprehensible language. The paper concludes that future AI progress should shift from LLM-centrism toward rule-feedback intelligence: systems in which language models act as interface and coordination layers, while rules, verifiers, memory, tools, and action-feedback loops supply the conditions for reliable learning and decision-making.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6811522`

- title: When Intonation Determines Meaning:Evaluating ChatGPT and Gemini on Pragmatic Equivalence in Spoken Jordanian Arabic​
- authors: Nimer Abusalim, Mariam  Azzam Dar Baker, Maryan Alsawaeer, Oraib Barakat Alshmaseen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6811522
- keyword hits: chatgpt, gemini, large language model, llm

### abstract

This study investigates whether contemporary speech-enabled large language model (LLM) translation pipelines can identify and render pragmatic meaning encoded solely through intonation in spoken Jordanian Arabic. While end-to-end speech translation systems have achieved substantial gains in semantic and syntactic accuracy, their handling of prosody-dependent pragmatic meaning remains poorly understood. To examine this limitation, the study constructed a dataset of twenty short conversational utterances, each realized in two prosodic forms: a warm rising contour signaling support, admiration, reassurance, or encouragement, and a sharp falling contour signaling annoyance, sarcasm, reluctance, or criticism. Lexical content was held constant to isolate prosody as the only variable. A native speaker recorded all items, which were submitted as audio input to the speech-enabled ChatGPT and Gemini translation pipelines, yielding eighty English translations in total. The results show that both systems consistently preserved propositional meaning but failed to reliably convey prosodically encoded pragmatic distinctions. Warm utterances were typically rendered as neutral or mildly polite, while sharp utterances were often neutralized or, in Gemini’s case, misinterpreted as enthusiastic. Sarcasm, irritation, and passive-aggressive refusal were rarely expressed in the output. Key phono-pragmatic cues such as vowel lengthening were ignored, and religious expressions undergoing pragmatic refunctionalization were translated literally across prosodic conditions. These findings indicate that current LLM-based speech translation pipelines do not effectively exploit prosodic cues when pragmatic meaning is carried exclusively by intonation, underscoring the need for prosody-aware speech processing and pragmatically informed model training.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6773438`

- title: Generative AI and Cognitive Delegation in Legal Practice
- authors: Gaston Rey
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6773438
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence, large language model, large language models

### abstract

Recent decisions around the world-Mata v. Avianca in the United States, the Sullivan & Cromwell apology in the U.S. bankruptcy courts, and an emerging Argentine line of cases-, have made clear that the problem posed by generative artificial intelligence in legal practice is not, properly speaking, the technology itself. It is the structural transformation in how legal text is produced: an unprecedented capacity to outsource professional cognitive functions (the search for authority, the testing of arguments, the very drafting of pleadings), to probabilistic systems whose outputs cannot be authenticated from within. This article advances the thesis that the central legal-ethical problem is cognitive delegation, not artificial intelligence; that the danger of generative tools in litigation is not their tendency to produce absurd outputs but their fluency in producing plausible ones; and that the lawyer's signature is best understood not as a procedural formality but as a mechanism of institutional imputation. Generative AI does not merely automate drafting. It destabilizes the institutional relationship between legal text, professional judgment, and responsibility. The argument proceeds through a comparative reading of the international cases, an analysis of the architecture of plausibility in large language models, a critique of the 'human in the loop' formula, a survey of emerging professional standards, and a discussion of when negligent reliance on AI may cross into procedural bad faith.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6773638`

- title: LLMs Should Not Judge: Rule-First Harnesses as the Governing Layer for Legal AI A Working Paper on Prompt Dependence, Harness Sovereignty, and Rule-Feedback Legal Intelligence
- authors: Haozhe Gan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6773638
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

This working paper argues that current large language model (LLM) systems are being placed at the wrong layer of authority. Pretraining has already produced models with strong semantic competence: they can understand natural language, extract facts, draft arguments, and explain complex materials. The central problem is no longer that LLMs cannot speak; it is that they are still asked to judge. In high-stakes domains such as law, this is an architectural error. LLMs should not be treated as decision sovereigns. They should serve as semantic interfaces, candidate generators, and explanation engines under a rule-first harness that owns the variables, constraints, permissions, verifiers, objectives, escalation rules, and feedback memory. The paper distinguishes model-first LLM systems from rule-first harness systems. In model-first systems, prompts temporarily supply task boundaries and human preference alignment modifies the model toward acceptable responses. In rule-first systems, domain rules are external, explicit, auditable, and enforceable. The harness does not merely evaluate the model after the fact; it governs the task before the model can produce a final answer. The paper reviews recent work on evaluation harnesses, tool-use environments, agent benchmarks, and execution feedback, including lm-evaluation-harness, HELM, SWE-bench, WebArena, OSWorld, ToolSandbox, tau-bench, ReAct, RAG, Toolformer, and related agent benchmarks. It argues that these works point in the right direction but have not yet elevated harnesses from evaluation infrastructure to rule-sovereignty layers. Law is used as the primary test case because legal work is simultaneously linguistic and rule-governed. A reliable legal AI system should not merely imitate legal writing. It should extract facts, map them to legal elements, verify citations, enforce procedural constraints, account for evidence, and optimize under explicit legal and strategic objectives. The paper concludes that the next stage of LLM development should not be dominated by larger models, more data, or softer preference alignment, but by rule-first harnesses that turn semantic competence into verifiable domain intelligence.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6738058`

- title: High Probability Convergence Bounds for Non-convex Stochastic Gradient Descent with Sub-Weibull Noise
- authors: Makhamadamin Abrolbekov
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6738058
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This research investigates the convergence properties of the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithm within the context of complex non-convex optimization problems. Unlike the majority of existing theoretical approaches that focus solely on establishing convergence in mathematical expectation, this study is dedicated to establishing strict convergence bounds with high probability. The practical significance of these results is demonstrated through their direct applicability to stabilizing the training of massive neural network architectures, including Large Language Models (LLMs) and transformers, which frequently encounter chaotic, heavy-tailed gradient noise. The investigation systematically examines two distinct theoretical scenarios: objective functions that satisfy the Polyak-Lojasiewicz condition under sub-Gaussian noise, and general smooth non-convex functions subjected to gradient noise modeled by a sub-Weibull distribution. The primary scientific contribution of this research is the derivation of an adapted concentration inequality designed for martingale difference sequences with heavy tails. Furthermore, this study presents a resource-efficient post-processing methodology to select the optimal model state from the optimization process. By removing the reliance on average-case analysis, this work provides robust theoretical guarantees that align with the practical realities of deploying modern machine learning algorithms

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6773239`

- title: A-Index: Rethinking Scholarly Influence When Your Audience is an Algorithm
- authors: Shan Jiang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6773239
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, gemini

### abstract

This abstract uses plain language. Researchers have long been measured by how often other researchers cite their work. But today, millions of people get their knowledge from AI systems like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini-not from reading journal articles directly. This means a new kind of scholarly influence has emerged: the degree to which a researcher's ideas shape what AI systems know and say. This paper proposes the A-Index: a multi-level framework for measuring the degree to which a scholar's work shapes what AI systems retrieve, synthesize, attribute, and communicate to the world. A-Index comprises of six metrics. At the article level, it asks: does AI retrieve this paper, and does it actually reason from it? At the concept level, it asks: do different AI systems describe this idea consistently, and has the idea spread so deeply that AI applies it without being asked? At the individual level, it asks: does AI correctly credit the right researcher, and how large is that researcher's overall AI footprint? The paper also offers practical guidance: publish openly and early, coin distinctive terms for new ideas, write in clear declarative sentences, and maintain a presence across multiple platforms that AI systems index. At the same time, the paper warns against treating the A-Index as an institutional evaluation tool. Metrics that become targets get gamed. The goal is awareness-helping scholars understand a transformation that is already underway, before it is too late to shape it.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6814822`

- title: Security Attack and Defense Strategies for Autonomous Agent Frameworks: A Layered Review with OpenClaw as a Case Study
- authors: Luyao Xu, Xiang Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6814822
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Autonomous agent frameworks built upon large language models (LLMs) are evolving into complex, tool-integrated, and continuously operating systems, introducing security risks beyond traditional prompt-level vulnerabilities. As this paradigm is still at an early stage of development, a timely and systematic understanding of its security implications is increasingly important. Although a growing body of work has examined different attack surfaces and defense problems in agent systems, existing studies remain scattered across individual aspects of agent security, and there is still a lack of a layered review on this topic. To address this gap, this survey presents a layered review of security risks and defense strategies in autonomous agent frameworks, with OpenClaw as a case study. We organize the analysis into four security-relevant layers: the context and instruction layer, the tool and action layer, the state and persistence layer, and the ecosystem and automation layer. For each layer, we summarize its functional role, representative security risks, and corresponding defense strategies. Based on this layered analysis, we further identify that threats in autonomous agent frameworks may propagate across layers, from manipulated inputs to unsafe actions, persistent state contamination, and broader ecosystem-level impact. Finally, we highlight potential key challenges, including research imbalance across layers, the lack of long-horizon evaluation, and weak ecosystem trust models, and outline future directions toward more systematic and integrated defenses.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6813572`

- title: FiCoTS: Fine-to-Coarse LLM-Enhanced Hierarchical Cross-Modality Interaction for Time Series Forecasting
- authors: Yafei Lyu, Hao Zhou, Lu Zhang, Xu Yang, Zhi-Yong Liu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6813572
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The recent success of Large Language Models (LLMs) offers significant potential for time series forecasting, particularly in cross-modality setting. The prevailing LLM-as-Predictor paradigm uses LLM as the forecasting backbone, but the semantic gap between text and time series hinders LLM from adequately understanding time series. Alternatively, LLM-as-Enhancer paradigm employs LLM only to encode text as complementary information. However, existing LLM-as-Enhancer methods typically perform shallow, coarse-grained cross-modality interaction, overlooking the underlying semantic discrepancy. To address this challenge, we propose FiCoTS, an LLM-enhanced fine-to-coarse framework for multimodal time series forecasting. FiCoTS facilitates progressive cross-modality interaction at three semantic levels in a fine-to-coarse scheme. First, in the token-level modality alignment module, a dynamic heterogeneous graph is constructed to filter noise and align time series patches with text tokens. Second, in the feature-level modality interaction module, a global cross-attention mechanism is introduced to enable each time series variable to connect with relevant textual contexts. Third, in the decision-level modality fusion module, a gated network is designed to adaptively fuse the results of the two modalities for robust predictions. Extensive experiments on seven real-world benchmarks demonstrate that our model achieves state-of-the-art performance, especially in few-shot scenarios. The codes will be released.
