## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6761073`

- title: Bridging the Efficiency Paradox: A Systematic Mapping Study on Verification Paradigms in LLM-Generated RTL
- authors: Pin-Hua Chen, Chih-Cheng Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6761073
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

As Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate exceptional capabilities in code generation, their application within the Hardware Description Language (HDL) design flow has become a focal point for both academia and industry. However, compared to the software domain, hardware design imposes far more stringent requirements on correctness. The potential stochastic defects in LLM-generated code pose substantial challenges for backend verification, leading to what we term an "Efficiency Paradox": accelerated front-end generation paired with decelerated back-end verification. Reported figures across the surveyed literature suggest that the first-pass functional correctness of code generated by monolithic models is frequently below 60% on standard benchmarks, with verification convergence cycles growing non-linearly with design complexity. To clarify the development status and challenges in this field, this study conducted a Systematic Mapping Study (SMS) of peer-reviewed literature on LLMs applied to Register Transfer Level (RTL) design published between January 2020 and January 2026 (with the primary high-quality corpus concentrated in 2023–2025, reflecting the period of rapid growth in this field). The initial search yielded 1,245 records. After deduplication and title/abstract screening, 107 papers entered full-text eligibility assessment. Using the exclusion criteria (EC1–EC7) we excluded 41 papers, and a subsequent Empirical Rigor Score (ERS) screening with threshold ERS≥1.0 excluded a further 4 early papers that lacked quantitative data or relied solely on ”toy examples,” for a combined 45 exclusions and a final corpus of 62 included primary studies. Analysis of these 62 studies indicates that the field is undergoing a paradigm shift from early ”one-shot syntactic generation” toward ”verification-centric, agent-based collaborative systems.” This study establishes a four-stage verification paradigm taxonomy, identifies distinctive defect categories associated with LLM-generated RTL (syntax hallucination, logical inconsistency, and semantic injection/security vulnerabilities), and critically examines the limitations of current evaluation metrics. Several scope limitations apply: the corpus is dominated by Verilog/SystemVerilog studies; FPGA-level validation is reported in only 11% of included studies; and cross-phase Pass@1 comparisons pool heterogeneous benchmarks and should be read as indicative trends rather than controlled comparisons. The results aim to provide a structured technical roadmap for researchers and EDA practitioners, guiding future construction of more reliable, intelligent chip design flows that incorporate self-healing capabilities.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6739860`

- title: When to Act, When to Ask: Simulation-Based Evaluation of Human-in-the-Loop Decision Policies for Enterprise Agents
- authors: Kshitij Kumar Singh Chauhan, Shreya Garg
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6739860
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Enterprise assistants powered by large language models (LLMs) face a fundamental dilemma: when should they act autonomously, and when should they escalate or ask for clarification? Overly aggressive autonomy risks errors and erodes user trust, while excessive deferral frustrates users and reduces efficiency. We present ActAsk, a framework of executable decision policies that formalize "when to act vs. when to ask" in enterprise contexts. Policies include confidence gating, scope restrictions, confirmations, fallbacks, and audit logging. A calibrated autonomy controller tunes thresholds under safety constraints using a constrained contextual bandit. Unlike prior reports of unverifiable field trials, we evaluate ActAsk entirely through simulation: (1) offline replay of synthetic IT/HR question-answer datasets; (2) scenario-based transcript surveys measuring trust and satisfaction; and (3) policy ablations. The results show that ActAsk reduces incorrect answers, improves escalation precision, and increases perceived trust. Contributions include a taxonomy of executable policies, reproducible simulation methods, design guidelines, and governance templates for responsible enterprise adoption.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6754899`

- title: Convergent Correctness in Stochastic Code Generation: A Generate-Verify-Repair Architecture for Deterministic Validation of Autonomous AI Coding Agents in Regulated Environments
- authors: Rafael Cadenas
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6754899
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as autonomous code-generation agents in enterprise software development. However, the stochastic nature of autoregressive token sampling introduces a fundamental tension with the zero-defect requirements of regulated industries such as retail banking and financial technology, where frameworks including the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX), the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI-DSS), and federal banking examination guidelines mandate demonstrable control over software change processes. This paper formalizes the problem as one of bounded stochastic generation and proposes a Generate-Verify-Repair (GVR) architecture that interposes a pipeline of deterministic verification oracles between the probabilistic generation function and the acceptance of its output. The architecture implements five independent verification layers-git state validation, stack-adaptive compilation, independent test execution, static application security testing (SAST), and structural schema validation-wired into a closed-loop error correction cycle that injects typed failure diagnostics as repair context for subsequent generation attempts. Preliminary evaluation through an in-depth single-case study within a proprietary enterprise monorepo suggests that the GVR loop achieves high detection reliability for mechanically verifiable defect classes (compilation errors, test regressions, phantom builds, known vulnerability patterns) while converting raw stochastic output into a convergent search process over the space of correct programs. The architecture preserves the generative model's full creative capacity while constraining its output space to the subset satisfying formal correctness predicates, establishing a defense-in-depth methodology for the safe adoption of AI-accelerated development in compliance-critical domains.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6770198`

- title: From Active Learning to Inference-Time Governance for LLM-Assisted Code Generation: A Systematic Literature Review and Design Framework
- authors: Ketut Adnyana, Andreas Schwung
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6770198
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) show strong potential for code generation, but their outputs remain vulnerable to ambiguity, missing context, and uncertain correctness. These limitations are especially critical in safety-relevant engineering settings, where generated software must satisfy domain constraints, control logic, verification requirements, and traceable release processes. This paper presents a systematic literature review of active-learning-related mechanisms for uncertainty-aware code generation, with a focus on industrial automation, programmable logic controller (PLC) engineering, and verification-governed control software. The review covers three evidence layers: Active Learning (AL) foundations, AL-related mechanisms in LLM-based code generation, and PLC- and safety-oriented code synthesis. The final corpus comprises 152 studies from Scopus and IEEE Xplore. The findings show that AL can be reframed from a training-time sample-selection paradigm into an inference-time governance mechanism for LLM-assisted code generation. In this framing, uncertainty becomes a decision trigger for clarification, context retrieval, candidate comparison, verification-mediated refinement, selective human oversight, escalation, abstention, or fail-closed release. From this synthesis, we derive a design-oriented framework for trustworthy LLM-assisted PLC synthesis, centered on admissibility, verification, traceability, and controlled release under unresolved uncertainty. The paper contributes both a structured cross-domain review and a practical framework for uncertainty-governed code generation in safety-relevant engineering workflows.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6747579`

- title: Reasonable Adjustments for Neurodivergent Students in Higher Education: Generative AI, Accessibility, and Mediated Authorship
- authors: Daniel Morgan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6747579
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Neurodivergent students face significant disadvantages in higher education, where assessment often privileges neuro-normative written expression. This article argues that Large Language Models (LLMs) should be framed as accessibility tools that scaffold expression while preserving the student’s intellectual authorship, reframing the debate from academic integrity to equity. The argument is grounded in UK equality law and contends that the Court of Appeal’s decision in University of Bristol v Abrahart requires universities to distinguish competence standards from the methods used to assess them. On this basis, the regulated use of LLMs may amount to a reasonable adjustment where a student’s barrier lies in written expression rather than reasoning. The article advances a four-pillar model for responsible integration, addressing risks such as algorithmic bias, opacity, and data privacy. It concludes that LLMs, situated within traditions of mediated authorship, can support inclusion without displacing academic standards.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6759444`

- title: Artificial Obedience: How Large Language Models (LLMs) May Normalize Hierarchical Communication
- authors: Nusse Mellgren, Sebastian Wärmländer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6759444
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

In this study, we argue that repeated interaction with large language models (LLMs) may condition users into authority-driven, directive communication habits that can spill over into human-human interaction. LLMs imitate human patterns of communication and create structurally asymmetric interactions where users occupy positions of interactional authority, thereby fostering directive and efficiency-oriented communication patterns that may gradually evolve into enduring habits, schemas or behavioral scripts. Drawing on theories of power, habit formation, and social practice, and using military communication as an analogy, we propose that habits formed during human-LLM communication can transfer to human-human interactions via a process we call computer communication spillover . We conceptualize this process as cue-triggered habit activation in structurally similar contexts. Potential spillover effects include reduced politeness, lower tolerance for dissent, more control-oriented communication, and a reduced tendency to accept other people´s perspectives. The habits likely develop gradually and outside users’ awareness, which raises legal and ethical concerns about responsibility. Taken together, these dynamics highlight a broader societal risk of generative AI, namely, the subtle normalization of hierarchical and instrumental communication styles in everyday human interaction.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6779013`

- title: On the Gap Between Declarative Vulnerability Knowledge and Secure Code Generation in Large Language Models
- authors: Dang H. Vu, Lam D. Dao, Anh M. T. Bui, Phuong  T. Nguyen, Davide Di Ruscio, Massimiliano Di Penta
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6779013
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly integrated into security-critical software engineering tasks, including vulnerability detection and secure code generation. Although LLMs are trained on large-scale corpora that may include vulnerability-related information, such as the Common Weakness Enumeration (CWE), it remains unclear to what extent they encode and can recall such declarative knowledge. Furthermore, the relationship between this knowledge and procedural secure coding capability remains uncertain. In this paper, we conduct a large-scale empirical study to examine the relationship between declarative vulnerability knowledge and downstream code generation behavior. We operationalize declarative knowledge through a set of controlled recall-based probing tasks that measure the model’s ability to reproduce CWE identifiers, names, and descriptions, and contrast this with performance on security-oriented code generation benchmarks. Our results reveal a consistent gap between declarative knowledge and procedural mitigation capability. While models demonstrate non-trivial lexical fidelity in recalling vulnerability definitions, this knowledge does not consistently prevent insecure code generation. We further observe that the effectiveness of such knowledge depends on how vulnerability information is expressed in prompts. Explicit taxonomy-aligned cues are associated with improved security outcomes, whereas semantically equivalent but more abstract descriptions are less effective. These findings suggest that the utilization of recalled knowledge may rely on surface-level associations rather than robust vulnerability reasoning. Taken together, our observations highlight the distinction between declarative knowledge recall and mitigation capability in LLM-based coding systems, and suggest potential implications for evaluation practices and prompt design.

### abstract

While traditional AI and data-driven facilities management approaches have improved building operational efficiency, they remain constrained by centralized organizational structures which are vulnerable to cyber-attacks, limited contextual understanding, and decision-making structures that exclude key stakeholders from the governance process. This paper introduces a novel AI and data-driven distributed governance framework for smart building management that integrates decentralized autonomous organizations (DAOs), digital twins, large language models (LLMs), and blockchain technology. This framework aims to enable transparent collective decision-making through a DAO governance platform, implements data-driven management using IoT and digital twins, incorporates LLM-based virtual assistants for enhanced decision support, and utilizes blockchain for secure building automation. A full-stack decentralized application is developed to facilitate user interaction with these integrated components. The system was evaluated for cost-efficiency, scalability, data security, and usability using the System Usability Scale (SUS). Expert interviews were also conducted to evaluate its practical benefits and challenges.
