## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6801059`

- title: Two Economies, Not One: A Taxonomy of the Agentic Economy and the Case for Settlement Neutrality
- authors: René Dechamps Otamendi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6801059
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

The term agentic economy appears in over fifty distinct definitions published between 2021 and March 2026 — by Microsoft Research, Sequoia Capital, the Bank for International Settlements, Coinbase, Stripe, the World Economic Forum, Gartner, Fetch.ai, and at least forty other sources. They do not mean the same thing. Some describe an AI agent buying a plane ticket for its owner through Visa. Others describe autonomous software agents negotiating, transacting, and building reputation with each other — no human in the loop, no credit card, no chargeback. This paper surveys the definitional landscape, classifies fifty-plus usages into five categories, and identifies two fault lines that the literature has not made explicit. The first — the primary fault line — separates commerce for humans (agents as intermediaries in human economic activity) from an economy of agents (agents as independent economic actors). The second — within the autonomous category — separates the eleven approaches that require blockchain infrastructure from the one that does not. The taxonomy reveals a structural question: what formal properties must a settlement layer possess to support autonomous agent-to-agent commerce, independent of the communication protocol and independent of the ledger substrate? We define settlement neutrality as that set of properties — escrow determinism, ledger consistency, reputation equivalence, dispute parity, and auditability — and describe a reference architecture where agents speaking Anthropic's MCP, Google's A2A, and direct REST converge on the same escrow pipeline. The paper proposes the five categories and six classification dimensions as a reusable framework for evaluating future protocols, and identifies open questions for the field.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6808258`

- title: Generative AI And Multimodal Pedagogy: Implications for Teaching, Learning, and Assessment
- authors: Olimpius Istrate
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6808258
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI tools have made multimodal content creation accessible to teachers, sharply reducing the time and expertise needed to produce it—a shift with significant pedagogical implications. This article examines what this democratisation means through the lens of multimodal pedagogy, which treats teaching as the deliberate orchestration of multiple semiotic modes and draws on several theoretical traditions, most prominently social semiotics and the cognitive theory of multimedia learning. Two interconnected arguments organise the analysis. First, generative AI lowers the production barrier for multimodal educational materials to a degree that reconfigures what is practically feasible in classrooms and course design, extending possibilities that interactive digital media began opening two decades ago. Second, this expanded capacity amplifies rather than substitutes for pedagogical expertise: without sound design grounded in how learning works, more powerful tools risk producing more elaborate noise. Drawing on the multiliteracies tradition, Mayer's multimedia learning principles, and Universal Design for Learning, the article maps four functional categories of AI tools against three evaluative criteria—pedagogical completeness, cognitive soundness, and adaptive flexibility—and discusses their implications for assessment, teacher preparation, and ethical governance. The analysis acknowledges the still-crystallising nature of this domain: empirical evidence for multimodal pedagogy's superiority over text-based instruction remains thin, AI-generated content carries structural limitations in accuracy and bias, and most claims about learning gains await rigorous validation. The article aims to provide a theoretically grounded orientation for educators, course designers, and researchers navigating a rapidly evolving intersection of AI capability and pedagogical principle.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6866874`

- title: After ChatGPT, After the AI Act: Artificial Intelligence in Eight European Parliaments, 2015-2026
- authors: Christian Pipal
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6866874
- keyword hits: chatgpt, large language model

### abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) has become a central policy concern in Europe, but little is known about how national parliaments debated the technology. The closest study covers four legislatures, three outside Europe. I address the within-Europe question through two natural shocks, the release of ChatGPT in November 2022 and the entry into force of the EU AI Act in August 2024. The corpus contains 3,594,388 parliamentary speeches from eight European countries between 2015 and 2026, of which 9,948 are AI-related and coded on thirteen dimensions using a large language model with human-validated reliability. The post-ChatGPT acceleration is concentrated in the United Kingdom and Norway rather than common across the eight. References to the AI Act follow an integration gradient, with European Union members five times higher than the post-Brexit United Kingdom and EEA Norway close to the member rate. Government and opposition frame AI in opposing terms along a left--right gradient. The European AI debate is a structured field organised by Union relationship and the government--opposition cleavage, which matters for regulatory diffusion after Brexit.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.03763v1`

- title: Merit or networks? What decides where research is published
- authors: Ning Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03763v1
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Does scientific publishing reward the quality of ideas or the advantage of connections? The question is universal to prestige-driven science, yet it has resisted decades of study because a paper's quality could not be gauged ahead of its publication fate without using that fate as the yardstick. We break this constraint by measuring a paper's idea quality directly from its text, before publication, using a discipline-trained LLM evaluator that scores the idea without seeing author names or outcomes. Using economics as a case study, we combine this text-legible idea-quality score with an execution-quality rubric, a connection index, an author-ability index, and an off-the-shelf language-model text score to estimate a five-input production function for journal placement across 6,208 economics working papers. The inputs are not rivals but a sequence along the ladder of prestige. Execution sets a meritocratic floor and is the largest input overall. Text-legible idea quality grades the rungs in between. Connections set a favoritism ceiling that bites mainly near the apex, the most selective journals. Connections work through two additive channels: connected authors write papers that score higher, and at equal scores their papers are still more likely to place better. Yet this advantage is bounded. Connections raise the odds of every rung without making the apex the typical outcome for ordinary ideas, and even the highest-scoring papers face real friction reaching the visible journal ladder. The result nests, rather than chooses between, the meritocracy and network accounts of how science is published.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6853701`

- title: The Epistemology of Algorithmic Doubt: Opacity, Synthetic Realities, and the Structural Erosion of Shared Reality
- authors: Abulgasem Al Mashai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6853701
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Most current frameworks analyzing how artificial intelligence (AI) affects knowledge tend to miss the forest for the trees. They either focus on fragmented phenomena like algorithmic bias and filter bubbles, or rely on high-level ethical guidelines that lack grounding in the actual architecture of system design. To bridge this gap, this study proposes the Algorithmic Architectonics of Doubt (AAD). As a unified diagnostic framework, the AAD explicated how contemporary AI reconfigures the very conditions under which collective knowledge is produced, distributed, and validated. Rather than treating this disruption as a monolith, the framework disaggregates it into three analytically distinct processes-Algorithmic Opacity (Ω), Synthetic Realities (Σ), and Epistemic Fragmentation (Φ)-critically mapping the six directional pathways through which they mutually amplify their epistemic effects. Four subsidiary conceptual contributions are advanced: (1) the classification of LLM hallucination as architectural confabulation under Ω rather than Σ, thereby distinguishing structurally generated epistemic opacity from deliberate synthetic deception; (2) the concept of ambient interpretive caution as a second-order epistemic burden with asymmetric distributional consequences along lines of technological literacy; (3) the application of Nguyen's (2020) epistemic bubble/echo chamber distinction to sharpen the governance implications of Φ; and (4) a mapping of EU Artificial Intelligence Act (2024) provisions, specifically Articles 13 and 50, to the relevant AAD vectors, constituting the first systematic governance analysis grounded in the AAD architecture. Non-Western philosophical resources are engaged through primary scholarly literature-Ubuntu epistemology (Wiredu, 1996; Gyekye, 1997; Metz, 2007) and Confucian zhengmingto articulate epistemically distinct grounds for structural accountability that transcend individualist warrant theory. The paper is organized around an explicit agenda for future empirical operationalization, presenting five precisely scoped limitations as research directives rather than mere disclaimers. The AAD framework is offered as a rigorous diagnostic instrument intended to guide interdisciplinary research, technological governance, and policy design in an era of structural epistemic instability.

### abstract

Rapid adoption of electric vehicles (EVs) is transforming energy consumption patterns and creating significant challenges for smart grid management. Accurate forecasting of EV charging demand is crucial to optimize grid operations and ensure a stable power supply. In Internet of Vehicles (IoV) networks, real-time data combined with detailed mobility patterns provide valuable insights into driving and charging behaviors. This paper proposes a Tabular Variational Autoencoder (TVAE)–based Generative AI framework is proposed to emulate unseen EV charging behaviors and assess model generalization under stochastic and high-volatility conditions. Based on the dataset produced, this paper provides a critical quantitative and qualitative analysis for a variety of probabilistic and deterministic learning models to forecast the electricity demand associated with EV charging across city regions. Finally, the peak reduction of 16.9\% indicate that price adjustments help flatten demand peaks, contributing to better load balancing.

- title: KGAssist: An MCP approach for LLM-enhanced knowledge graph repository manipulation
- authors: Teodora  Cristiana Nemtoc, Ana-Maria Ghiran, Gheorghe  Cosmin Silaghi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6875259
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Model Context Protocol has become the standard for defining how AI systems execute tasks through tool requests, exposed resources and controlled interactions patterns like prompt templates. This work presents the implementation of an MCP Server that enables operations on Knowledge Graphs hosted in a dedicated RDF triple store such as Graphwise GraphDB, allowing users to interact with the graph using natural language without the technical expertise typically required in setting up the repository. In this way, users can transform and store their data into a KG, query and manipulate it in a significantly more straightforward manner. The official MCP Server provided by Graphwise supports only read-only operations on graph repositories. In addition, our implementation extends this functionality by enabling write and management operations, while eliminating the need for manual configuration, familiarity with the GraphDB environment or any other prior understanding about the knowledge graphs engineering processes.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6855658`

- title: The Missing Developer: Generative AI, the Seven-Node Supply Chain, and the Structural Absence of Accountability
- authors: Johan van Rooyen, Nitayapa Nandhakwang, Tomoko Kashima
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6855658
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The first paper in this sequence established that confident fabrication is endemic across the generative AI supply chain regardless of model tier and falls hardest where verification cost is highest. This paper asks the structural question that finding makes necessary: through what mechanisms does responsibility for AI-mediated fabrication move away from the actors best positioned to prevent it and settle on the educator, professional, or affected party least able to verify it? The gap in the existing literature is that AI-literacy frameworks presume the user holds verification infrastructure, hallucination research measures the phenomenon without tracing how its cost travels, and AI-governance work treats accountability as assignable to a discrete actor. This paper instead treats accountability as a supply-chain displacement problem. Using Coasean externality analysis and principal-agent theory, it maps seven nodes from the frontier laboratory to the affected party and identifies at each a distinct displacement mechanism: entanglement at the API layer, ephemerality at the integrator layer, the absence of contract at the platform layer, procurement handoff at the institutional layer, and the structural impossibility of further offload at the professional’s layer. The affected party is a non-party to the entire transactional chain and bears the cost every upstream node has disclaimed. The chain is shown to be circular rather than linear: the educator’s role redistributes by AI fluency against conventional hierarchies of age and authority, the affected party occupies upstream positions in other transactions, and the fabrication circulates without exit. The paper’s contribution is threefold. It shifts the unit of analysis from the individual user to the supply chain; it explains, through transaction-cost and agency theory, why verification costs are displaced downward rather than absorbed upstream; and it identifies the affected party as a non-party, showing why contractual, procurement, and literacy-based remedies all fail at the point where epistemic harm is most consequential. The missing developer is not a person who failed to act but a role the chain’s structure cannot locate in any one place, because the chain was built without one.
