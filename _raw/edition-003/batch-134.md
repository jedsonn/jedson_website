## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.5051800`

- title: Impression Management: AI-based Evidence from Earnings Guidance
- authors: Jonathan Berkovitch, Doron Israeli, Ron Kasznik
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2025-01-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.5051800
- keyword hits: chatgpt, large language model

### abstract

We examine whether firms use sentiment within earnings guidance to manage impressions and whether this use of sentiment is associated with investor reactions to the disclosed information. Using 18,046 firm-issued earnings guidance press releases from 3,619 firms over 2001 to 2022, we apply FinBERT, an AI-based pre-trained large language model, to measure positive tone, negative tone, and sentiment in disclosure titles, the first ten 250-word portions of the text, and the full text. We find that title sentiment is more positive than text sentiment on average, and that this gap is substantially larger when the underlying text conveys negative information. Consistent with impression management, more negative tone in the text does not translate into a correspondingly more negative, or less positive, title. We also find that sentiment is highest in the earliest portions of the disclosure and declines as the text progresses, indicating that firms place more favorable language in more salient parts of the release. These patterns do not appear in ChatGPT-generated titles for the same disclosures or in Wall Street Journal articles covering earnings guidance. We further find that title sentiment is incrementally associated with abnormal stock returns and lower abnormal trading volume, beyond the sentiment of the text itself, and that only the earliest text portions are strongly related to returns. Overall, our evidence suggests that firms use sentiment to manage impressions within disclosures and that investors incorporate these cues into prices and trading activity.
