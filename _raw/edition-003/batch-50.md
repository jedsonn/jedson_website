## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6876161`

- title: Value Contamination in Policy Simulation: Measuring How LLM Policy-Value Orientations Shape Predicted Administrative Burden Effects
- authors: Yichao Jin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6876161
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used to simulate behavioral responses to policy interventions, but their simulated treatment effects may depend on latent policy-value assumptions. This paper develops an audit framework for measuring value sensitivity in LLM-generated policy simulations. Using administrative burden in vaccination access as a testbed, I evaluate nine LLMs across six model families. Each model completes a policy-value orientation battery and then predicts vaccination willingness for 27 synthetic profiles under low-and high-burden access conditions. The results do not support a simple composite-contamination account. A composite policy-value contamination (PVC) index does not predict cross-model variation in administrative-burden effects. Dimension-specific checks suggest that collective obligation is positively associated with larger burden effects, contrary to the original PVC weighting, but this pattern is exploratory given the small number of model-level observations. Narrative frame-decomposition shows that access-barrier and coercive-backlash rationales are associated with larger burden effects, while collective-responsibility rationales are associated with smaller effects. The strongest evidence comes from a within-model frame-manipulation experiment: autonomy and equity/access frames increase predicted burden effects, whereas collective-obligation frames reduce them. The findings show that LLM policy simulations should not be treated as neutral behavioral forecasts, but neither are they well described by a single composite ideological score. Value sensitivity appears through specific orientation dimensions, expressed interpretive frames, and prompt-induced governance lenses. The paper contributes a framework for auditing LLM-based policy simulations by linking measured value orientations, simulated treatment effects, narrative mechanisms, and within-model frame responsiveness.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6902284`

- title: Semantic-Logic Dual-Path Fusion for LLM Rumor Detection: Structural Causal Modeling and Dual-Constraint Feature Graphs
- authors: Weiheng Liang, Jin Yang, Siyu Li, Puting Luo, Haonan Deng, Tianrui Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6902284
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Generative Artificial Intelligence powered by Large Language Models is proliferating rapidly, yet detecting LLM-generated rumors has emerged as a critical technical challenge. Traditional detection models primarily focus on text source identification, over-relying on "Traceability Features" and thus failing to identify covert rumors that are semantically coherent yet logically fallacious—a phenomenon we term "Traceability Preset Bias." This paper proposes LRDetect, a framework that constructs causal graphs among text features through causal inference and leverages the do-operator to perform causal intervention, eliminating confounding bias from traceability features and compelling detectors to focus on semantic and logical veracity. Further, we construct a Contextual Causal Feature Graph governed by dual constraints—Logical Transitivity and Contextual Transitivity—to extract contextual inconsistency features revealing logical fallacies. Experiments on public benchmark datasets demonstrate that LRDetect achieves 93.16% F1-score, outperforming state-of-the-art methods by 2.76 percentage points. Cross-model evaluation reveals that LRDetect maintains an average F1-score of 91.23% across 8 mainstream LLMs, exhibiting superior generalization capability. This work establishes a theoretical foundation for model-agnostic rumor detection by applying causal inference to LLM-generated content analysis.

- authors: Shuo Zhang, Zecheng Li, Shudan Lin, Jiahai Zhang, Yuheng Zhou, Lulu Wang, Bixin Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6908620

### abstract

Smart contracts have become an integral part of most modern blockchain systems, playing a pivotal role in their operation. However, security vulnerabilities in smart contracts can lead to significant financial losses. Given the remarkable capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) in code understanding and analysis, it is worthwhile to explore how state-of-the-art LLMs can enhance the detection of smart contract vulnerabilities.In this paper, we propose a collaborative detection method that combines semantic analysis performed by LLMs with a lightweight deep learning model. Rather than relying on LLMs for direct vulnerability detection, we engineer tailored prompts that guide the model to perform multidimensional semantic analysis of smart contract code, yielding structured insights. Subsequently, we utilize a pre-trained CodeBERT model to encode these insights into dense feature vectors, which facilitate vulnerability detection via a lightweight, efficient deep learning classifier.Experiments on real-world smart contract datasets show that our approach achieves accuracy rates of 99.85%, 93.2%, 99.33%, and 98.13% for four types of vulnerabilities: reentrancy, timestamp dependency, unchecked external calls, and strict equality to Ether, respectively. These results demonstrate that the synergy between LLM-based semantic analysis and a lightweight detection model offers a novel and effective solution for automated smart contract auditing. This approach requires no expert knowledge, remains intuitive and easy to implement, and exhibits strong scalability.

- title: Self-Improving Alpha Mining for Quantitative Trading via Multi-Agent Large Language Models with Knowledge Base Accumulation
- authors: Son  Minh Vu, Trung  The Pham, Viet  Hong Tran
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6906675
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Quantitative trading strategies depend on discovering novel alpha factors that capture market inefficiencies, yet traditional approaches rely on manual feature engineering, which is time consuming and requires substantial domain expertise. This paper proposes an automated framework that leverages large language models (LLMs) to systematically generate, refine, and evaluate trading alpha strategies through a multi-agent pipeline comprising a WriterAgent, JudgeAgent, and BacktestEngine, organized in a nested-loop architecture. The key contribution of this work is the systematic integration of WorldQuant 101 Formulaic Alphas as a structured knowledge prior, rooted in proven formulaic patterns rather than unconstrained free-form synthesis. This design significantly narrows the search space, reduces invalid code generation, and accelerates convergence of the refinement loop. Experiments on Vietnamese stock market data demonstrate that the proposed framework consistently generates alpha factors with statistically significant Information Coefficient (IC) values and positive Sharpe ratios within a short generation time. Furthermore, the incorporation of WorldQuant 101 Formulaic Alphas consistently outperforms unconstrained LLM-based generation in both alpha quality and convergence efficiency, establishing a scalable, robust, and domain-grounded approach for automated alpha mining.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6910006`

- title: A Reproducible Protocol for Evaluating Behavioural FSM Repair with Large Language Models
- authors: César Andrés
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6910006
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Context: LLM-assisted repair of finite state machines (FSMs) lacks a reproducible reporting discipline that keeps pipeline terminals and oracle outcomes on the same case condition slots. Objective: Specify a reproducible evaluation protocol and a measurement framework with frozen slot records for oracle-gated FSM patch repair with LLMs, separating repair executability (did the slot reach behavioural scoring?) from repair effectiveness (did vali- dation Behavioural Pass Rate (BPR) move on evaluated slots?). Method: The protocol defines diagnostic conditions (C–E), repair-representation variants, slot-level readouts, and audit regeneration of summary readouts from frozen summaries without re-running the LLM. An illustrative pilot freeze on 30 cases (three separately frozen repair-representation arms) demonstrates protocol application; diagnostic-granularity contrasts use the default repair-representation arm only. Results (illustrative pilot; descriptive only): No complete repairs in any cell. On condition E, each arm recorded three effective repairs on the same replicate bike_rental slots (one behavioural scenario, not three independent systems) while evaluated-slot denominators differed; cross-arm overlap of effective-repair identifiers applies to E only. One additional effective repair appeared under trace feedback (D) with the operation-inferred arm. Under the operation-inferred arm on E, the arm-level executability summary reports patch-application failure count zero yet seven failed slots remain alongside 23 evaluated slots; runner failures and abstentions (distinct prescoring terminals) account for the gap. Conclusion: The contribution is the evaluation protocol and measurement frame rather than a repair method or benchmark; the pilot illustrates application under stated limits without establishing general FSM repair behaviour.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6909831`

- title: Schema-Aware Localisation (SAL): Live Schema Grounding and Hallucination Validation for Oracle NL2SQL
- authors: Sanjay Mishra, Divya Chukkapalli, Ganesh R. Naik
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6909831
- keyword hits: gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Large language models can generate fluent SQL from natural language, but on real enterprise Oracle databases they frequently fail at execution time: columns and aliases are hallucinated and dialect-specific syntax is missed, leading to ORA-00904 invalid-identifier errors. In this setting, failures are primarily due to missing schema grounding — the model cannot know which tables and columns actually exist.This paper introduces Schema-Aware Localisation (SAL), a lightweight middleware layer for Oracle NL2SQL that requires no model retraining. SAL queries Oracle's USER_TAB_COLUMNS catalog to build a live schema map, selects a relevant table subset for each question (falling back to the full schema for multi-table queries), and injects this ground-truth context into the LLM prompt. Generated SQL is then checked by the Hallucination Index (Hidx), which validates every alias.column reference against the live catalog, automatically rewrites predictable prefix errors, and otherwise triggers a structured retry with itemised corrections.We evaluate SAL on 500 TPC-H natural language questions executed against a live Oracle Autonomous Database 23c instance using GPT-4o-mini. Without any schema grounding, execution-grounded truth (EGT) is 2.2% (12/500). A hand-written static schema hint brings EGT to 62.0%. SAL, with no manual schema curation, achieves 62.6% EGT (96% simple, 95% medium, 40.7% complex) while reducing execution failures from 97.6% to 2.6%.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6842719`

- title: Programming a Biological Cell: a review
- authors: Philip Treleaven, Adam Benmohamed, Weining Lin, Anish Sathyanarayanan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6842719
- keyword hits: claude, llama, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper reviews research into programming a biological cell (e.g. synthetic biology) treating cells like "codeable" machines. For biology, arguably, the endpoint is what we refer to as a DNAprogrammable biological microcomputer (Treleaven, 2021; Treleaven & Schoernig, 2025): a genetic engineering tool analogous to CRISPR (Thurtle-Schmidt & Lo, 2018) or Evo 2 (Evo 2, 2025). Notably, a cell comes with all the "hardware" and the information processing mechanisms. The challenge is creating the DNA, RNA and protein strands to "program" the cell. Currently, machine learning (ML) is driving advances in synthetic biology through strands' decoding, DNA editing, and Biological LLMs. As a case study, we present BioVibe, a working prototype Vibe coding environment for biological programming. BioVibe illustrates the development of a web-based platform (Node.js/Express) that connects a Groq-hosted LLM (LLaMA 3.3-70B) to seven live biological databases via Model Context Protocol (MCP) tool-calling: UniProt, PDB, KEGG, PubMed, the iGEM Registry, NVIDIA BioNeMo Evo 2, and a built-in Sequence-level engine. Scientists interact through natural language to design genetic circuits and select BioBrick parts. To generate and validate DNA sequences, BioVibe uses a layered sequence strategy: verified BioBrick sequences are fetched from the iGEM Registry, codon optimization is handled by CodonTransformer, and Evo 2 is reserved only for novel sequences with no registry equivalent, exploring the cell-as-computer analogy-all within a single integrated environment. For BioVibe to be a "real" genetic engineering tool it will need to include a measure of DNA "quality", and also to incorporate AI "guardrails" (Mishra, 2025) for "governance". This paper is targeted at computer scientists and laypersons interested in programmable biology (indepth knowledge of genetics is not a prerequisite). Its contribution is fourfold: ▪ "Codeable cells": to introduce biological information processing using computer science terminology. ▪ Programmable biology: to review the wealth of AI-driven research in programmable and synthetic cell biology, which treats cells as "programmable" machines. ▪ Vibe coding: to provide a general introduction to Vibe coding. ▪ BioVibe: as a case study, the design and implementation of BioVibe, a Vibe coding environment (cf. Claude Code) for programming cells is presented. BioVibe implements the four-layer Vibe architecture described in this paper-Intent, Translation, Execution, and Verification-as a functional web application with live connections to biological databases and AI models.

- title: Urban Sensing Meets Multimodal LLMs: A Systematic Review and Framework for Human-Centric Urban Intelligence
- authors: Haoxiang Zhao, Tianming Liu, Yuan Lai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6922210
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

How city planners, public agencies, and communities make consequential decisions depends on the quality and interpretability of urban intelligence—yet transforming the vast multimodal data generated by contemporary cities into insights that genuinely reflect human needs, lived experiences, and values remains an unresolved challenge. A growing body of research addresses this gap by integrating urban sensing technologies with multimodal large language models (MLLMs) to generate human‐centric insights. Drawing on 75 peer‐reviewed studies published in 2024–2025, we reveal three advances in sensing–MLLM integration. First, visual, numerical, and linguistic sensing approaches expand the dimensions of urban life observation. Second, actionable insights into spatial quality, human activities, public opinion, and extraordinary events are derived through MLLM-based reasoning to support responsive decision-making. Third, integration patterns vary across urban domains, with text- and image-based workflows most prevalent, demonstrating context-sensitive methodological strengths for policy applications. These advances are synthesized into a three-layer framework that broadens the analytical scope for studying human–environment interactions in cities and informs planning and governance responses to human needs. Moving beyond fragmented technological descriptions to a structural account, this synthesis highlights how integrated sensing–MLLM systems support human-centric urban intelligence across data, analytics, and actions.
