## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6918142`

- title: A Low-Burden AI Pipeline for Geriatric Depression Risk Stratification Across Seven National Aging Cohorts
- authors: Wushu Chen, Huibing Li, Ziqing Lun, Jiakai Lin, Xingpei Li, Huihui Yang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6918142
- keyword hits: large language model, qwen

### abstract

Background: Depressive symptoms in older are common and often under recognized. Scalable and interpretable tools are needed to identify older adults who may require further screening or assessment. Methods: We analyzed harmonized records from seven aging cohorts, including CHARLS, KLOSA, LASI, ELSA, HRS, MHAS, and SHARE. The primary outcome was elevated depressive symptom risk, defined as a depression score z value greater than 1 within each cohort and analytic sample. Model performance was evaluated using validation AUC, calibration, threshold specific metrics, and decision curve net benefit. SHAP was used for interpretation. A Qwen based large language model was used as a post hoc narrative module to generate constrained decision support explanations. Findings: The 15 feature Tier 6 model achieved the best balance of discrimination and data availability, with a seven cohort mean AUC of 0.7568 and 447,682 complete cases. Adding higher burden physical and behavioral measures reduced the mean AUC to 0.7338 and complete cases to 200,162. Tier 6 was also the most stable model across sensitivity analyses. The largest incremental gain driven mainly by self-rated health. Decision-curve analysis supported the use of Tier 6 as a threshold-sensitive screening-support model. At a 0.30 threshold, Tier 6 showed high sensitivity and negative predictive value with modest positive predictive value. The Qwen based narrative workflow generated all requested reports and has been released on Hugging Face. Interpretation: A 15 feature LightGBM model provided a stable, interpretable, and data efficient tool for risk stratification of elevated depressive symptoms in older adults.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6923333`

- title: Reconstructing Authority around AI-Generated Claims: A Practice-Based Study in Management Accounting
- authors: deng dejun dejun, Yuanfeng Luo, Xiaoqing Li, Feng Hu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6923333
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI (AIGC) produces fluent forecasts and strategic scenarios that resemble finished managerial analysis, creating a specific challenge for management accountants: they are expected to evaluate and defend outputs they did not fully produce. Drawing on a comparative processual case study of two Chinese technology firms—42 semi-structured interviews and non-participant observation—this paper examines how management accountants reconstruct their cognitive authority in AIGC-intensive settings. We identify three micro-practices through which accountants make AI-supported judgement organisationally defensible: algorithmic interrogation (tracing the premises behind AI outputs), hybrid decision-making (negotiating responsibility for AI-shaped options), and contextual sensemaking (anchoring generated scenarios to actual organisational constraints). The two cases further show that adoption arrangements matter: rapid rollout left authority reconstruction individually exposed, while staged adoption created shared review spaces in which individual interrogations became durable collective routines. The paper contributes to practice-based accounting research by identifying AI-generated claims as a new object of practice, and extends the algorithmic knowledge work literature by showing that generative AI reconstitutes rather than simply displaces professional authority.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6922888`

- title: Why Write in the Age of AI: Rethinking Human Voice and Agency in Algorithmic Culture
- authors: SVETLANA NIKITINA
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6922888
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The proliferation of generative artificial intelligence is not merely transforming writing as a skill but reconfiguring it as a human practice. This article examines how encounters with AI tools reshape the meaning of writing as a site of subjectivity, agency, and self-construction for students. Drawing on qualitative analysis of student reflections from a science fiction literature course, the study treats student responses not simply as data but as articulation of the emergent understanding of authorship and creativity in the times of algorithmic mediation. Comparing their own and AI-generated texts, students ask what makes their human voice distinctive and valuable, and what constitutes authorship and originality when advanced machine intelligences are taking over writing. While recognizing the efficiency and generative capacity of AI systems, they reaffirm human writing as irreducible to output or product, associating it instead with the process of emotional expression, reflection on lived experience, and personal position development. Writing emerges as a process of self-construction in the Foucauldian sense, through which individuals shape their intellectual, ethical and civic values. The article proposes a reorientation of writing pedagogy toward preserving the human voice and agency while fostering critical engagement with AI tools.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6845958`

- title: Generative Brand Narrative Intelligence: A State-aware Multimodal Agent Framework for Autonomous Emotionally Adaptive Brand Communication
- authors: Adithya Reddy Nalla
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6845958
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Contemporary AI systems address brand communication along one of two axes: they either monitor public narratives about a brand (inbound narrative intelligence, as operationalized by platforms such as Pulsar and PeakMetrics) or they generate isolated content pieces on demand using generative AI tools. Neither axis supports a third, operationally critical capability: continuously generating, governing, publishing, and learning from brand narratives as a persistent autonomous service. This paper introduces Generative Brand Narrative Intelligence (G-BNI) to designate and formalize this missing capability, and presents the Emotionally Adaptive Brand Narrative (EABN) framework as its first concrete implementation. EABN is a multimodal multi-agent system orchestrated via LangGraph in which seven specialized autonomous agents coordinate across a state-aware narrative lifecycle, the paper's central architectural contribution. Every content artifact transitions explicitly through defined states (Planned, Generated, Pending, Approved, Published, Analyzed), transforming content from an ephemeral output into a managed operational entity. An Emotional Adaptation Engine (EAE) governs narrative tone through structured rotation across eight emotional registers (Ambition, Transformation, Confidence, Future Success, Belonging, Innovation, Leadership, Independence), while a layered Emotional Anti-Repetition Mechanism enforces sustained diversity through phrase filtering, structural variation, and cliche detection. A Controllable Autonomy governance interface preserves human editorial judgment through selective component regeneration without disrupting pipeline continuity. G-BNI is formally defined with five testable qualifying properties and evaluated against existing system categories including inbound NI platforms, generative AI tools, social schedulers, multi-agent frameworks, and marketing automation systems. The EABN framework is the only system satisfying all five properties simultaneously. We contribute a formal G-BNI theoretical construct, a replicable seven-agent architecture, a structured evaluation framework with twelve metrics mapped to five research questions, and a research agenda for autonomous AI-native brand communication systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6929497`

- title: AI-Assisted Measurement of Interorganizational Trust in Blockchain Ecosystems: A Design Science and Pre-Empirical Validation Approach
- authors: Reza Barkhi, David P. Tegarden
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6929497
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Blockchain technologies are increasingly adopted to support interorganizational collaboration, information transparency, and decentralized governance. Yet, despite blockchain’s characterization as a ”trustless” infrastructure, effective collaboration among organizations continues to depend on relational trust and governance mechanisms that remain insufficiently operationalized especially when they have mixed-motives to compete and cooperate. Scholars face growing challenges in developing theoretically grounded measurement instruments for emerging digital contexts where empirical data are limited or evolving. This study proposes an AI-assisted, theory-driven approach for developing and evaluating measures of interorganizational trust in blockchain-enabled collaborations. Drawing on interorganizational trust theory, we develop a multidimensional trust measurement framework encompassing ability, benevolence, integrity, cooperation, monitoring, and trust. Using a design science research approach, generative artificial intelligence is employed as a theory-constrained methodological assistant to support construct operationalization, survey item generation, and model prototyping. We conduct a pre-empirical validation using synthetic data and partial least squares structural equation modeling (PLS-SEM). The findings demonstrate that the proposed measurement architecture exhibits strong convergent validity and theoretically consistent relationships among trust antecedents, cooperation, monitoring, and trust. The results further illustrate a substitution effect in which higher levels of perceived trustworthiness reduce reliance on monitoring while strengthening interorganizational cooperation. Beyond blockchain settings, the study contributes a replicable AI-assisted methodology for theory-driven measurement development and early-stage model validation in emerging digital ecosystems. The study contributes to information management research by extending interorganizational trust theory to decentralized digital environments, advancing AI-assisted methodological approaches for construct development, and providing practical guidance for managers seeking to strengthen governance and trust in blockchain-enabled collaborations.
