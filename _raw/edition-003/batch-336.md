## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6927858`

- title: From Scenario to Cadence: Predictive Planning as a Continuous, AI-Enabled Strategic Discipline
- authors: Tim Woodring
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6927858
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Scenario planning and strategic foresight were built for a world in which analytical work — scanning the environment, constructing plausible futures, stress-testing strategy — was slow, expert-dependent, and expensive. Generative artificial intelligence has collapsed the marginal cost and latency of exactly those tasks, yet the methodology of organizational foresight remains episodic and calendar-bound, and the term now entering the executive vocabulary to name the shift — "predictive planning" — circulates as enterprise-software marketing and operations-research jargon with no strategic-management theory behind it. This paper defines predictive planning as a leadership discipline: the continuous conversion of weak signals into staked strategic decisions, using AI to decide faster than the operating environment changes. We argue that when the cost of foresight approaches zero, the binding constraint on strategy shifts from analytical quality to planning cadence and decision commitment — a shift captured by an asymmetry test comparing an organization's decision-cycle time to its environment's rate of change, and one that reframes the unit of analysis from the scenario to the loop. We formalize the discipline as a four-phase continuous cycle — Scan, Story, Stake, Steer — specify the division of labor between AI and human judgment, position it as a continuous operationalization of dynamic-capabilities microfoundations, and advance seven propositions with explicit boundary conditions, including the enduring limits of prediction under radical uncertainty. The paper contributes a construct, a process model, and a testable research agenda.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7076641`

- title: Culturally Strategic: A Mixed-Methods Analysis of Cultural Identity Framing and Public Reception in Global Public Relations
- authors: Wenqi Marcus Wu, Haochen Liang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7076641
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

The failure of an organization’s standardized global messaging to resonate with the deeply held cultural values of local publics can create significant challenges for multinational corporations (MNCs). Moving beyond static metrics of effectiveness, this study analyzes global communication as a dynamic process of boundary spanning and identity alignment. Drawing on Social Identity Theory (SIT) and Zaharna’s (2022) framework of communicative logics, this research investigates how Nike utilizes attribute framing to signal in-group status across diverse cultural contexts. Employing a cultural relativism approach, this study uses a mixed-methods design to analyze N = 169 digital posts over a 12-month period, comparing the MNC’s communication in the United States and China. First, Large Language Model (LLM)-assisted Natural Language Processing (NLP) was utilized to quantitatively measure the structural frequency of communicative logics. Second, a qualitative thematic analysis was used to understand the latent cultural symbols and localized meanings embedded. By providing insights into cultural identity and framing within the Nike case, this research advances Social Identity Theory (SIT) by demonstrating how corporate identity signals in-group/out-group status across borders, extends Zaharna’s (2022) framework of communicative logics through operationalizing attribute framing as a strategic mechanism, and offers practitioners guidelines for transitioning from a mindset of separateness to cultural co‑meaning making within organizations.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6781380`

- title: Supervising Risk: A New Deal for Artificial Intelligence
- authors: Kevin Werbach, Peter Conti-Brown
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6781380
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Artificial intelligence has broken through business and society to an astonishing extent, with astonishing speed. Government responses have struggled to keep pace with the opportunities and risks that this revolutionary set of technologies represents. Part of the challenge in regulating AI risk is a failure of imagination. Efforts to date have drawn on legal frameworks relevant only to particular AI harms, as well as on largely inapt historical analogies. AI is too dynamic, distributed, and diverse for such approaches. Mostly missing from this discussion is perhaps the closest model for the risks that AI innovation, in particular AI foundation models, impose: banking. In this Article, we describe the problems that AI risk management efforts are trying to solve and compare them to the problems that policymakers and market participants have tried (with some success) to address over two centuries of effort in bank regulation and supervision, in the United States and throughout the world. In the process, we identify promising paths for AI risk management to follow, including on-site and continuous risk monitoring, institutional risk designations, and stress testing. We also discuss core challenges of risk management in finance, and ways for AI regulatory systems to avoid them. These include problems of shadow banking, bailouts, and (lack of) government coordination. Our comparative analysis offers a novel and powerful direction for the critical task of reining in AI risks, while supporting innovation and value creation. Finally, we propose the creation of an AI Risk Supervisor to implement these ideas. This entity would be structured to comply with recent changes in Supreme Court jurisprudence on agency independence without losing the technical credibility necessary for success. This exercise of regulatory innovation for AI is therefore valuable as a model for institutional innovation in the new environment of the administrative state.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6932098`

- title: Service-Induced Congestion in Memory-Constrained LLM Serving
- authors: Ruicheng Ao, Jing Dong, Gan Luo, David Simchi-Levi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6932098
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

In large language model (LLM) serving, each request accumulates persistent graphics processing unit (GPU) memory during service as its key-value cache grows with every generated token. Under high concurrency, aggregate memory usage therefore increases endogenously over time: the service process itself creates future capacity pressure. When memory capacity is exceeded, systems evict active requests, discarding cached state and restarting them later, which wastes computation and reduces throughput. We develop a discrete-time dynamical model of memory-constrained LLM inference that captures admission, memory growth, and eviction under continuous batching. In the saturated-input regime, the system admits both eviction-free fixed points and limit cycles with evictions. For homogeneous workloads, we show that the eviction-free equilibrium is unstable and that, except for a Lebesgue-measure-zero exact-capture set, the system converges to a unique worst-case limit cycle that is asymptotically stable outside this exceptional set, with throughput losses as large as 50%. For heterogeneous workloads, we prove a stability criterion in the two-class common-input setting and explain how the survival-polynomial mechanism generalizes to multiple classes and heterogeneous-input lengths. Under an input-dominated scaling regime, coprime decoding lengths stabilize the eviction-free equilibrium, while non-coprime lengths create synchronized modes that drive instability. These results characterize when workload heterogeneity desynchronizes completions and helps stabilize memory-constrained serving. More broadly, we identify service-induced congestion as a structural instability mechanism and derive scheduling design principles for sustaining high throughput.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7079541`

- title: Reporting Transparency for AI in Applied Linguistics: An L2 Writing Audit and Proposed Standard
- authors: Michele Carlo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7079541
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Artificial intelligence has entered applied linguistics faster than the field has developed conventions for reporting how these tools are used, threatening the reproducibility of a fast-growing literature. We examine this problem in a high-adoption case: second language (L2) writing research. Following a fully manual PRISMA screen of 6,174 deduplicated records, we audited 180 articles from 15 CALL and applied linguistics journals (2020–2026): 135 LLM or hybrid studies and 45 automated writing evaluation (AWE) studies, scoring each against an 8-item Reporting Completeness Index (RCI) covering the parameters that determine whether an AI output could be reproduced. Every article was independently double-coded (composite ICC = .96; screening κ = .88). Most studies named the model (84.4%), but few reported a version (22.8%) or disclosed prompts (23.9%), and decoding parameters (2.8%), randomness control (2.2%), and linked artifacts (4.4%) were nearly absent; the average study reported fewer than two of eight items (M = 1.84). LLM and AWE studies did not differ on the full index, AWE studies scored higher on shared cross-tool items, and reporting declined modestly over time among LLM and hybrid studies. We propose the tiered AI-Write Transparency Checklist as a minimum reporting standard for AI-assisted L2 writing research, portable across applied linguistics and educational research more broadly.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7017118`

- title: The Empty Gate Why the Light We Blame Is Not the Thing That Profits from the Dark
- authors: Johan van Rooyen, Nitayapa Nandhakwang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7017118
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

A worker applies for a job that does not exist, is screened by a system that may never have read the application, and is thanked, in warm and individual language, for an interest no one received. This paper makes a single argument about that experience, and it cuts against the common story. The common story blames artificial intelligence: the machine took the work, the machine guards the door, the machine is the enemy. We argue the opposite. The corruption of the hiring market, the posting with no vacancy behind it, the rejection that imitates a consideration that never happened; is old, human, and profitable, and it lived in the dark for years before the technology arrived. Generative AI did not create it. AI is the light that made it cheap to see at scale. And the money proves the point: when a worker applies into a void, the gain flows to whoever is paid for volume rather than for matches, the platform billed per click, the firm posting a vacancy it will not fill to look as though it is growing, the screening vendor selling a tool for the flood. None of that money reaches the model. The flame profits from nothing. We name the conditions under which this happens, selection under opacity and simulated consideration, distinguish honest automation, which we defend, from costumed automation, which we do not, and show why the instinct to extinguish the light serves only the dark that the light exposed.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6930662`

- title: The Ossification Frontier for Operational Processes
- authors: Xing Hu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6930662
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This paper studies the ossification decision in recurring operational processes: once flexible execution is available, which parts should be hardened into standardized process capital, which should remain adaptive, and when should that boundary move? Generative AI makes this problem especially salient with the prevalent use of model-mediated execution, but the framework applies more broadly to dynamic standardization problems beyond AI. We formulate a hidden-state dynamic process-design model in which a manager chooses the width of a standardized backbone, preserves scarce specialist capacity for uncovered and failed cases, and can audit to learn whether expansion to standardization is safe. The model features a latent reliability frontier, stochastic failures, failure-induced rework, convex congestion, redesign frictions, and targeted frontier audits. Several results emerge. First, the relevant expansion criterion is queue-adjusted reliability, not raw predictive accuracy. Second, when failures generate su!ciently costly recovery work, higher workload can induce a retreat to the core: the optimal backbone narrows rather than widens to protect specialist capacity. Third, exception-process design is a first-order complement to standardization: reducing recovery burden can expand the safe scope of standardization without improving predictive performance. Fourth, learning is localized at the boundary and depends on environmental durability: routine operation reveals only what is already standardized, so targeted frontier audits are most valuable when codified gains are su!ciently persistent. Under economically interpretable monotonicity conditions, these forces imply a cuto" width within the ranked design space. A numerical study and an empirical design show how the framework can be implemented with enterprise workflow data.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7082923`

- title: From Tool to Data: Understanding Holistic Urban Perception across 369 Chinese Cities using MLLM-generated Images
- authors: Hongzeng Zhang, Jiayu Pan, Yongping Zhang, Weiwen Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7082923
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Urban perception refers to the cognitive representations through which cities are understood as integrated socio-spatial entities, and provides a critical lens for interpreting human–city interactions and their behavioural logic. Most prior work focuses on specific perceptual attributes or local spatial units and relies heavily on labour-intensive data collection and manual annotation, constraining scalability and cross-city comparison. To address these challenges, this study proposes a novel framework that leverages the generative outputs of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) to examine holistic urban perception across 369 Chinese cities. Rather than treating MLLMs solely as analytical tools, we conceptualise them as data sources whose outputs encode collective human knowledge, cultural experience, and socio-symbolic meanings. The results reveal a discernible but overlapping regional structure in the perceptual embedding space, with Western cities showing greater internal heterogeneity and Northeastern cities exhibiting stronger internal coherence. Inter-city perceptual similarity also forms meaningful community structures that do not simply reproduce administrative or geographical divisions, while Quadratic Assignment Procedure (QAP) analysis shows that perceptual similarity is significantly associated with geographic proximity, economic development, population size, industrial structure, provincial affiliation, administrative level, and population flow patterns. By advancing this shift from ”tool” to ”data”, this study provides a scalable computational pathway for understanding urban perception, and highlights the broader methodological and epistemological potential of MLLMs in urban science and computational social research.
