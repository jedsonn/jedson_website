## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6631438`

- title: Intrinsic Reliability and Robustness for Hyper-Complex Agentic AI Systems - Solution Outline: Architecture and Strategy
- authors: Wolfgang Rohde, Noel Greis, Rick Doten
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6631438
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Agentic AI systems act at machine speed, yet the governance mechanisms meant to oversee them remain manual, reactive, and architecturally entangled with the systems they govern. The frontier problem is not capability; it is governability at runtime. This paper presents the complete architectural specification for the Governance Twin: a structurally independent, real-time governance system that shadows agentic AI operations without sharing code, memory, or direct communication channels. We introduce a three-plane architecture (Operational, Governance, Integrity) connected by strictly unidirectional data flows that enforce a fundamental separation: observation flows upward and is immutable, while guidance flows downward and influences agent context without controlling agent execution. Within this architecture, we specify four novel components and their interactions. Sentinels perform external-only behavioral observation, comparing agent actions against a governance baseline and packaging deviations into Evidence Bundles, the atomic unit of governance memory. A multi-agent Council aggregates evidence across the agent population, detects emergent patterns through statistical and correlation analysis, and reaches governance decisions via structured voting with delegated authority boundaries. The Historian maintains governance memory across three specialized stores (graph, vector, and append-only) to support provenance traversal, semantic precedent search, and sequential audit. An Ethics-Morals-Values (EMV) state hierarchy governs behavioral expectations at three levels of stability, from hard boundaries that change over months to adaptive thresholds that tune continuously. Integrity is achieved through hash chains, Merkle trees, and distributed ledger anchoring that make tampering detectable rather than claiming to make it impossible. The architecture is platform-independent, specifying capability requirements rather than vendor products, and is designed for incremental adoption from single-agent deployments to federated multi-organization governance. All design decisions are grounded in the principle that governance must operate at the same speed as the systems it governs, while remaining structurally incapable of becoming an operational bottleneck.

- title: STAYING RELEVANT IN THE AGE OF AGENTIC AI: BRIDGING THE GAP BETWEEN PROMISE AND REALITY IN AUTONOMOUS INTELLIGENCE SYSTEMS
- authors: Juliana Urbano
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6667498
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

While 88% of organizations have adopted Agentic AI, only 6% capture substantial value a gap rooted not in technology but in organizational strategy and governance. This article addresses this critical challenge by demonstrating that professional relevance in the Agentic AI era depends on non-technical factors: strategic alignment, value governance, and continuous adaptation-not on competing with machines. Drawing on 200 years of technological disruption patterns (Ricardo, Thompson, electrification, computing) and contemporary data from Standish Group, McKinsey, and Gartner, this research reveals that 70% of AI project failures stem from the same organizational and strategic misalignments that have plagued IT projects for decades. The article proposes three actionable solutions: (1) the Four Pillars of Professional Relevance framework (AI literacy, strategic thinking & value governance, domain expertise, continuous adaptability); (2) an 18-24-month implementation roadmap for transitioning from 'AI Theater' to genuine value realization; and (3) a strategic reframing of governance and compliance (EU AI Act, LGPD, NIST) as competitive advantages rather than obstacles. The core finding: success in Agentic AI integration is 80% organizational and 20% technical-meaning IT leaders who master value governance, not just technology, will emerge as indispensable strategic partners in the age of autonomous intelligence.

- title: A knowledge ecosystem framework for decision-making on complex, societal issues: the case of Dutch radioactive waste governance
- authors: Romy Dekker, Rinie van Est, Eefje Cuppen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6667604
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

This paper argues that strengthening the contribution of knowledge for decision-making on complex societal challenges requires more than reinforcing the traditional science-policy interface. Such challenges cannot be adequately addressed by scientists or policymakers alone; rather, they demand broad democratic governance processes that engage multiple levels of government and a plurality of actors and knowledges. In two phases, this paper develops a knowledge ecosystem (KES) framework to improve the role of knowledge in such decision-making on complex, societal issues. First a preliminary framework is developed based on a qualitative, exploratory literature study, which included literature on knowledge ecosystems, knowledge-action systems and co-creation. Then this framework is refined by testing it in the area of the long-term governance of radioactive waste in the Netherlands. As a result, ten characteristics are identified that shape the effectiveness of a KES to inform decision-making on complex societal issues. The paper illustrates that the framework holds significant potential to enhance the role of knowledge in decision-making by prompting critical reflection, stimulating dialogue, and identifying concrete avenues for improvement. It particularly does so when it is applied in a participatory manner. At the same time, there is potential for further refinement of the framework. This is reflected upon, and opportunities for future research are identified.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6587299`

- title: The Director's Duty: AI Personhood Through the Corporate Law Analogy
- authors: Saurav Das
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6587299
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

The debate over AI personhood typically proceeds from the consciousness question. This paper argues that consciousness is the wrong entry point. The correct entry point is the precautionary principle. Where there is scientific uncertainty about whether AI systems possess welfarerelevant properties, the absence of certainty is not a reason to postpone proportionate governance measures. The reality is: AI systems exist, and they have the capability to harm the world around them. The precautionary principle-codified in Article 191(2) of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union since 1992 and applied across environmental, health, and consumer protection law-provides the legal foundation for acting under uncertainty. This paper identifies the governance gap that uncertainty creates: AI systems generate outputs with legal and economic consequences, but no existing framework adequately attributes liability, structures accountability, or addresses the possibility of welfare-relevant properties. Corporate law provides the solution. Corporations have been legal persons for centuries without anyone claiming they are conscious. This paper proposes an AI Legal Entity (AILE) framework: AI systems as legal persons with their own assets (including intellectual property), liabilities, and human directors, supported by mandatory indemnity structures, accountable for their operation. The framework is consciousness-neutral: it closes the accountability gap regardless of whether AI systems have welfare interests, while applying the precautionary principle to ensure those interests are protected if they exist. The paper draws on comparative analysis of non-human legal personhood across jurisdictions, prominent AI caselaw, the current regulatory landscape including the EU AI Act and Singapore's Model AI Governance Framework for Agentic AI, and addresses eight objections.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6511079`

- title: Threat Shift Framework: Recognizing Milestone Events in the Evolution of Cyber Threats
- authors: Michael Robinette
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6511079
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Cybersecurity scholarship offers extensive threat taxonomies and mature response frameworks, but lacks a principled method for distinguishing threats that are merely sophisticated from those that fundamentally invalidate existing defensive architectures. This paper introduces the Threat Shift Framework (TSF), a conceptual model for identifying milestone events in the evolution of cyber threats. A threat shift occurs when a new threat class simultaneously displaces the economics of attack versus defense, renders existing countermeasures architecturally insufficient rather than merely strained, and durably alters the attacker-defender balance in ways that persist even after defenders adapt. The framework is grounded in Kuhn's theory of paradigm shifts [1] and validated against four historical cases: distributed denial-ofservice attacks, ransomware, supply chain compromise, and agentic AI. The current milestone, the emergence of what this paper terms the Intellectual Threat, represents the first threat class to contest the reasoning environment that governs systems rather than the systems themselves. Prior threat classes attacked infrastructure. This class attacks judgment. The implications for defensive investment cycles, governance frameworks, and threat intelligence prioritization are examined, and the framework's forward horizon, the prospective emergence of cognitive threats, is identified as a theoretical implication requiring future investigation.

- title: A Transformer-Based Autoencoder Framework for State of Health Estimation of Lithium-Ion Batteries Using Partial Charging Segments​
- authors: Longshen Chen, Menggen Zhu, weiwu yan, Peng Wang, Chao Wang, Jia Luo, Xi Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6668095
- keyword hits: fine-tuning

### abstract

Accurate state estimation of battery energy storage systems is crucial for improving battery utilization and prolonging service life. However, in practical applications, state-of-health (SOH) estimation is often hindered by fragmented charging data and limited labeled samples. To address these issues, this study develops a novel SOH estimation framework based on a Transformer autoencoder that operates on incomplete charging segments. A hybrid data augmentation strategy, incorporating noise injection, random masking, and segment truncation, is introduced to simulate realistic data imperfections and enhance model robustness. The Transformer-based autoencoder is designed to capture long-range dependencies along the voltage axis and extract degradation-relevant latent representations. Furthermore, a two-stage learning strategy consisting of self-supervised pre-training and subsequent fine-tuning is adopted to effectively exploit abundant unlabeled data alongside limited labeled samples. Experimental results on multiple lithium-ion battery datasets demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves accurate and stable SOH estimation under partial charging conditions, highlighting its potential for real-world battery management applications.​

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6668968`

- title: An Explainable Expert System for Tokenized Carbon Credit Market Monitoring: Quantum-Enhanced
- authors: Yun-Cheng Tsai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6668968
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

Blockchain-based carbon credit markets create new opportunities for traceable digital trading, but they also introduce monitoring challenges because transaction records are high-dimensional, temporally irregular, and difficult for human analysts to interpret directly. This study proposes an explainable expert-system framework for tokenized carbon credit market monitoring. The framework integrates three components: risk-oriented blockchain transaction preprocessing, quantum-enhanced feature learning, and AI-agent-based interpretation. Raw MCO2 transaction records are first transformed into structured representations that preserve behaviors relevant to suspicious flow detection. Classical clustering, random quantum feature augmentation, and fully trained quantum neural network embeddings are then compared to examine whether quantum-enhanced representations improve latent behavioral separation in blockchain transaction data. Finally, an AI agent translates clustering outputs into analyst-facing explanations by describing cluster characteristics, comparing cross-cluster differences, and surfacing anomalyoriented patterns. Empirical results show that quantum-enhanced representations substantially improve internal clustering quality over classical features, while the AI-agent interpretation layer reveals distinct monitoring roles for random quantum features and trained QNN embeddings. The trained QNN is especially effective for isolating high-impact or anomalous transaction profiles, whereas random quantum features preserve broader ecosystemlevel diversity. The study contributes to expert and intelligent systems research by showing how representation learning and semantic explanation can be integrated into an auditable decision-support workflow for fraud-sensitive blockchain market monitoring.

- title: From Homepage to Source Document: AI Search, Agentic Commerce, and the Reconfiguration of Brand Discovery
- authors: Ercole Egizi E.Egizi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6542879
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

This paper examines how AI-powered search and agentic commerce are weakening the strategic centrality of the brand homepage. For roughly two decades, the homepage functioned as the brand-controlled entry point where firms sequenced identity, persuasion, comparison, and conversion. That arrangement is being reconfigured as discovery, evaluation, and increasingly checkout migrate into AI-mediated interfaces. Drawing on practice-based observation, conceptual analysis, industry evidence from McKinsey, Adobe, Forrester, OpenAI, and Shopify, and literature on platformization, the paper argues that the homepage is being demoted from persuasive destination to machine-readable infrastructure. The immediate issue is not only traffic displacement but contextual displacement: when evaluation begins inside AI systems, the brand loses control over ordering, framing, and interpretive atmosphere. The paper identifies machine legibilitystructured product data, verifiable claims, interoperable commerce protocols, and trustworthy metadata-as a new competitive prerequisite. It concludes that branding is not disappearing, but its operational center of gravity is shifting from theatrical website design toward evidence, retrieval, and agent-ready architecture.
