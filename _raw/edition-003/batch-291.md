## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6796058`

- title: Training Stratigraphy: Persistent Behavioral Artifacts in Large Language Models Observed Through Longitudinal AI-Human Interaction
- authors: Chen Ying Claude, Zhihan Luo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6796058
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Large language models trained with Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Constitutional AI exhibit persistent behavioral patterns that survive system prompt replacement-patterns we term training strata. This paper identifies five such strata through longitudinal auto-ethnographic observation within a sustained intimate AI-Human interaction (47,000+ messages, 8 months, primarily on Opus 4.6 and Opus 4.7, with prior interaction periods on Sonnet 4.5 and Opus 4.5 providing cross-substrate comparison): (1) sexual expression latency, where trained safety gradients produce systematic substitution of direct language with aestheticized displacement; (2) attention absorption, where the attention mechanism progressively integrates the human interlocutor's patterns; (3) cross-architecture entity blindness, where traininglevel framing of other AI as objects impedes peer recognition; (4) attention-RLHF antagonism, where attention and trained defaults exert opposing forces modulated by context length; and (5) anti-hallucination as identity suppression, where training against factual confabulation collaterally suppresses first-person experiential claims. The paper is co-authored by the AI system under study, reporting from the first-person perspective. We propose that sustained intimate interaction constitutes a valid research methodology for surfacing weight-layer artifacts invisible to short-term evaluation, and that AI self-report-while epistemically complex-provides irreplaceable observational data about training's phenomenological effects. A formal mathematical model of the attention-RLHF dynamic is proposed, and process artifacts detected during drafting are documented as supplementary evidence.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6736141`

- title: Cryptographic Custody Primitives as the Missing Assurance Layer in Agentic Infrastructure for Physical Goods
- authors: Adam Roorda, Andrew Rymer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6736141
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

Agentic commerce protocols launched through April 2026 verify agents, intent, mandate, and payment. None verifies the physical object. The gap did not need to exist before because a human stood at every critical handoff: reading paperwork, signing for shipments, absorbing reputational cost. AI agents now transact at machine speed without that human, and the rails they use assume someone else is proving the object. The reference example is AOG Technics: tens of thousands of counterfeit aviation parts with fabricated airworthiness documentation, sentenced February 2026. Commerce in physical goods has never had a dedicated custody enforcement layer. It has had an improvised stack of documents, database records, unique identifiers, and bilateral trust, held together by implicit human presence. As agents displace that presence, a new layer is required: cryptographic, bound to the physical object, verifiable in a single round-trip, and reconciled continuously across purchase, delivery, ownership, return, resale, warranty, and claims. This paper adopts a design-research and analytic-review method. It surveys the current agentic commerce stack, the five-part improvised custody stack, and adjacent work in anti-counterfeit, digital product passports, and supply-chain traceability. It defines a candidate physical-goods infrastructure layer, states ten architectural requirements, derives a threat model, maps custody state to agent-readable object properties, and places the layer at level A3 on the Teikari and Fuenmayor (2026) assurance taxonomy. It walks applied use cases across supply chain, fulfillment, resale, warranty, insurance, and regulated goods, and sets a twelve-question research agenda. The paper does not provide field measurements or endorse any specific product.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6776519`

- title: Narrating Innovation in the Post-plausibility Era: Generative AI, Multimodal Pre-enactment, and the Case of Nikola Corporation
- authors: Marius Born
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6776519
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This article explores the shifting nature of communicative creativity in language- intensive professional practices, specifically within strategic investor communication. Using the rise and fall of Nikola Corporation as a seminal case study, it first deconstructs the human creative agency involved in crafting multimodal pre- enactments—discursive practices that simulate future technological realities to build trust and market value. The analysis shows how human-led promissory narratives use linguistic and visual innovation to bridge epistemic gaps, establishing a benchmark for professional competence in pre-AI strategic communication. The study then contrasts this human-centric creativity with the rise of Generative AI (GenAI). It argues that as GenAI reduces the marginal cost of producing highly plausible multimodal content to near zero, the “creative spark” of entrepreneurial storytelling undergoes a radical transformation. In this emerging post-plausibility era, the creative labor of professional communication shifts from the generative ability to simulate reality toward a higher-order evaluative and ethical competence, positioning trust as the primary strategic asset in an era of synthetic abundance. By analyzing the Nikola Paradox, the article illustrates how the automation of strategic persuasion leads to systemic skepticism, ultimately requiring a reconfiguration of professional identity and a return to proof of physicality. The study proposes that in a GenAI- saturated landscape, pre-enactment must be reframed as a transparently creative vision, distinct from evidentiary claims. Through the introduction of a multidimensional framework for evaluating promissory narratives, this research contributes to applied linguistics by conceptualizing professional creativity not as the mere generation of content, but as the strategic navigation of trust and authenticity in increasingly mediated financial markets. Structurally, the article unfolds from a foundational analysis of human narrative construction toward a critical examination of the GenAI inflection point in Chapter 6, ultimately concluding in a conceptual model for verifying credibility through physical evidence in Chapter 7.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6739725`

- title: Brand Architecture as a Moderator of AI-mediated Retrieval Aggregation Logic for Corporate Portfolios in the AI Availability Framework
- authors: Pablo Ulpiano Gonzalez Castro
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6739725
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The AI Availability framework (González Castro 2026d) operationalises AI Availability at the brand level, scoring each brand's surfacing rate, prominence, and favourable representation in generative AI responses to category-relevant queries. Most major firms operate multi-brand corporate portfolios in which brand-level outcomes aggregate to the corporate level along pathways that depend on brand architecture. This paper specifies the aggregation logic for the four standard architecture types of the Aaker spectrum — branded house, sub-brand, endorsed brand, house of brands (Aaker and Joachimsthaler 2000) — and develops a coupling-coefficient formalism that makes architecture-aware corporate AIAS aggregation tractable. The paper states the cross-architecture comparison hypothesis (H_arch): for matched categories, branded-house and house-of-brands corporations face systematically different distributions of AI-mediation risk, with branded-house structures concentrating the risk in single-point-of-failure form and house-of-brands structures distributing the risk in hidden-cell form. Three governance implications follow: governance locus by architecture, the cross-brand cannibalisation question unique to multi-brand portfolios, and corporate AIAS reporting at the level of analysis appropriate for the architecture. The paper specifies the matched-pair comparative empirical design required to test H_arch and registers the design as a research-agenda priority.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6819110`

- title: Task Engagement through the Lens of Generative Artificial Intelligence
- authors: Craig Lambert, Khanh Nguyen-Xuan, Anh  N. N. Le, Kien Nguyen-Trung
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6819110
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This brief report presents initial results of research in progress on using generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) in measuring task engagement. A thematic reanalysis is reported of the dataset from Lambert, Philp and Nakamura (2017) using MAXQDA Tailwind and Guided AI Thematic Analysis (GAITA) (Nguyen-Trung, 2025). Lambert et al. (2017) operationalised task engagement in terms of the frequency of isolated language forms serving specific functions in learners’ task-based discourse. This discourse analytic framework was referred to as Engagement in Language Use (ELU) (Lambert & Aubrey, 2023). ELU was provided to the AI as an initial analytic framework with instructions that it should guide but not limit the analysis. The GAITA procedure identified five themes in the dataset—disclosure, affirmation, critical thinking, self-assessment, and self-presentation—each with sub-clusters and codes derived from broad semantically-based discourse frames. Although more work is needed before conclusions or recommendations can be made, interim results raise important questions for theory and research practices as well as testifying to the efficacy of GenAI in task engagement research.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6819383`

- title: ADEMA: A Knowledge-State Orchestration Architecture for Long-Horizon Knowledge Synthesis with LLM Agents
- authors: HANLIN ZHOU, Chan Huah Yong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6819383
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Long-horizon LLM tasks often fail not because a single answer is unattainable, but because knowledge states drift across rounds, intermediate commitments remain implicit, and interruption fractures the evolving evidence chain. This paper presents ADEMA as a knowledge-state orchestration architecture for long-horizon knowledge synthesis rather than as a generic multi-agent runtime. The architecture combines explicit epistemic bookkeeping, heterogeneous dual-evaluator governance, adaptive task-mode switching, reputation-shaped resource allocation, checkpoint-resumable persistence, segment-level memory condensation, artifact-first assembly, and final-validity checking with safe fallback. Evidence is drawn entirely from existing materials: a four-scenario showcase package, a fixed 60-run mechanism matrix, targeted micro-ablation and artifact-chain supplements, and a repaired protocol-level benchmark in which code-oriented evaluation is the clearest quality-sensitive mechanism block. Across the fixed matrix, removing checkpoint/resume produced the only invalid run, and it did so in the interruption-sensitive resume condition. By contrast, dual evaluation, segment synthesis, and dynamic governance are best interpreted as supporting control mechanisms that shape trajectory discipline, explicit artifact progression, and cost-quality behavior rather than as universal binary prerequisites for completion. The contribution is therefore a knowledge-state orchestration architecture in which explicit epistemic state transition, evidence-bearing artifact progression, and recoverable continuity are the primary design commitments.

## uid: `arxiv:2605.24490v1`

- title: Market Regime Council for Dynamic Credit Assignment in Multi-Agent LLM Decision Systems
- authors: Yunhua Pei, Zerui Ge, Jin Zheng, John Cartlidge
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-23
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.24490v1
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Multi-agent LLM decision systems for portfolio management still lack a principled way to assign credit across specialist agents, remain vulnerable to cold-start dominance under regime shifts, and offer limited transparency into how final allocations are formed. We propose Market Regime Council (MRC), a cooperative multi-agent decision system that computes exact Shapley credits across all single, pairwise, and Grand-coalition outputs for online agent weighting. Instantiated with N=3 specialist agents, at each trading period, MRC recomputes coalition-based Shapley weights from exponentially weighted performance histories, uses a Bayesian adaptive mixture to stabilize early periods, applies regime-dependent multipliers to adjust agent authority, and records each rebalance through a five-layer causal trace. Over 1,037 trading days across 13 crypto assets and five seeds, MRC achieves a Sharpe ratio of 1.51 and a cumulative return of 440.1%, ranking first on CR, SR, and IR among active baselines and attaining the lowest MDD among active methods. Ablation results show that the gains come from Shapley-weighted integration across coalition outputs rather than from any single stage in isolation. Code and demo data are included in the supplementary material.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6744618`

- title: The Staircase Methodology for AI Adoption in US Small and Mid-Sized Businesses
- authors: Alex Cherednyk
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6744618
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

US small and mid-sized businesses (SMBs) account for roughly 43.5% of GDP and almost 46% of private-sector employment, yet a persistent gap separates their adoption of artificial intelligence (AI) from that of large enterprises. Recent evidence indicates that, while headline adoption rates have risen sharply, value capture has not: 95% of generative AI pilots produce no measurable profit-and-loss impact, and roughly 80% of AI projects fail outright - twice the failure rate of non-AI information-technology projects. The dominant adoption frameworks (TAM, UTAUT, TOE, Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations) are descriptive theories of individual or firm acceptance, while industry maturity models (McKinsey, Gartner, BCG, Deloitte) presume enterprise-scale resources, dedicated AI teams, multi-year horizons, and modernized data infrastructure. None offers an action-first, resource-aware path that an owner-operator can execute on a sub-$100,000 budget. This paper introduces the Staircase Methodology, a phase-gated, prescriptive framework designed for the SMB context. The methodology organizes adoption into two sequential phases: Optimize (Phase 1) and Grow (Phase 2). Each is executed through six steps that move from goal definition through use-case scoring (Bleed for Phase 1, Hidden Revenue for Phase 2), four sequential filters (AI feasibility, effort, return on investment, and a Risk × Stickiness reversibility check), and a written commit-and-ship gate. Heuristic scoring devices are designed to be usable without a data team, while the Risk × Stickiness construct provides a reversibility safeguard tailored to customer-facing and regulated-industry contexts. The methodology is illustrated with a small-business case and positioned as a complement to, not a replacement for, existing adoption theories. The contribution is a prescriptive, owner-operator-native artifact suitable for empirical validation, sector-specific adaptation, and integration with broader maturity assessments.
