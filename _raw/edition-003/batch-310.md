## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6847421`

- title: Dissecting the Virtual Cell: A Review of Mechanistic Interpretability for Single-cell Biological Foundation Models
- authors: Olivia Denvis
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6847421
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Single-cell biological foundation models (sc-FMs)-transformer networks such as scGPT, Geneformer, scFoundation, scBERT, and Universal Cell Embeddings, pretrained self-supervised on tens of millions of single-cell transcriptomes-have rapidly become standard tools for cell-type annotation, batch integration, gene-network inference, and in silico perturbation. Yet they remain black boxes: it is unclear what biology they internalize and whether the structure they learn reflects causal regulatory logic or merely statistical co-expression. A young but fastmoving literature has begun to answer these questions by importing the toolkit of mechanistic interpretability (MI)-probing and representational geometry, sparse-autoencoder dictionary learning, attention analysis, circuit tracing, and causal activation steering-and adapting it to the peculiarities of the transcriptomic domain. This review organizes that emerging field. We (i) summarize the architectures and tokenization schemes that make sc-FMs amenable (and resistant) to MI; (ii) present a taxonomy of MI methods as applied to sc-FMs; (iii) synthesize the empirical findings, which converge on a striking "knowledge-without-logic" picture-models encode richly organized biological knowledge (pathway membership, protein-protein interactions, subcellular localization, developmental manifolds) in interpretable, often low-dimensional and cross-model-convergent geometry, while encoding surprisingly little of the causal regulatory logic that would let them beat simple linear baselines at perturbation prediction; (iv) highlight a constructive frontier in which interpretability is used not only to audit but to extract compact, performant algorithms from model internals; and (v) lay out the methodological pitfalls and open problems. We argue that sc-FMs are an unusually favorable testbed for MI because ground-truth biological databases supply external validation that natural-language interpretability lacks, and that the field's central open question is whether scale, architecture, or training objective can close the gap between learned knowledge and learned mechanism.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6950560`

- title: The Use of AI-Generated Feedback in an Indonesian EFL Writing Class: Student Perceptions
- authors: Muhammad  Ahkam Arifin, Tom Salsbury
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6950560
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

An increasing number of studies in second language (L2) writing have explored students’ perceptions of the use of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) chatbots (e.g., ChatGPT). However, few focused on how their perceptions may change after engaging with the AI system under teacher instruction for an extended period of time. They mostly explored the perceptions of L2 students who had little to no experience using GenAI technology in their writing classrooms. They also mostly relied on self-reported data with limited attention given to student-GenAI chatbot interaction logs. Therefore, this classroom-based study explored how English as a foreign language (EFL) writing students shifted their perceptions after using ChatGPT to help them revise their essays with teacher-prepared prompts for seven weeks. It also investigated their perceptions of how AI-generated feedback could help them improve their writing skills, including idea development, organization, lexical resource, and grammatical range and accuracy. The findings show that they became significantly more confident in the AI tool’s ability to help them evaluate their EFL essays and believed that this AI system could help them improve their writing skills in both higher-order and lower-order aspects. Nevertheless, they also expressed a number of concerns related to the use of AI feedback.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6949947`

- title: Designing materials with the ”Yes-Man”: EAP Teachers' Critical Use of Generative AI
- authors: Lada Smirnova
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6949947
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Although research on teachers' and students’ engagement with Generative Artificial Intelligence (Generative AI) chatbots for designing and adapting language learning materials has grown substantially in recent years, there has been little discussion of using Generative AI for these purposes in English for Academic Purposes (EAP) university classrooms. Recent, increasingly rapid advances in Generative AI tools and their ubiquitous use by students have heightened the need for EAP materials and assessment revisions, and such research could contribute to making decisions in this process. Drawing on sociocultural theory and narrative inquiry methodology, the study investigates the experiences of three EAP teachers interacting with AI, and the article presents instances of how the teachers develop and adapt EAP materials in the multilingual context of a pre-sessional, skill-based EAP module at a UK university. Drawing on Sharples’s (2023) five practical roles of possible AI assistance, the study shows how EAP teachers use Generative AI for adjusting the difficulty of texts, simplifying terms, creating tailored activities, and beyond. The article concludes with implications for EAP teacher training and development, and its preliminary findings can inform a much wider range of EAP teacher educators, module designers, and policy makers.

- title: Foundation Models for SAR Remote Sensing Image Analysis: A Comprehensive Survey
- authors: Pinyi Huang, Li Liu, Huali Xu, Yongxiang Liu, Xueqiong Li, Shuanghui Zhang, Taoli Yang, Jiehua Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6878580
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Synthetic aperture radar (SAR) image analysis has attracted increasing attention due to its important role in all-weather and day-and-night Earth observation. Although deep learning has substantially advanced this field, existing methods are still largely task-specific and lack transferability across tasks and scenes. Recently, foundation models (FMs) have shown great potential for building more general and scalable SAR representations, but their development is still challenged by the unique imaging mechanisms and data characteristics of SAR. To promote future research, this paper presents a comprehensive review of SAR image analysis in the FM era. We summarize the main challenges, benchmark datasets, and evaluation settings, and provide a systematic taxonomy of existing methods. We also discuss representative downstream tasks and highlight promising future research directions.

### abstract

In the past year, the focus of AI in education has shifted from generating content to evaluating its quality. While large language models can now produce vast amounts of material in seconds, ensuring that this content is accurate, reliable, and pedagogically sound remains a challenge. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6949559`

- title: FllumaOne: A Code-Native Multimodal CAD Dataset with Executable Programs and Kernel-Validated Feature Histories
- authors: Jizong Zhan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6949559
- keyword hits: qwen

### abstract

Parametric computer-aided design records both final geometry and the ordered construction history that determines how a part can be edited. Datasets for editable CAD research should therefore expose modeling operations, parameters, and feature dependencies together with validated geometry. We introduce FllumaOne, a code-native multimodal CAD dataset whose models are generated by executable Python programs in Flluma, a Qt/C++ OpenCASCADE-based CAD system. Each sample aligns its program with a structured feature tree, a training-oriented intermediate representation, STEP geometry, a surface point cloud, natural-language descriptions, metadata, and eight canonical visible-edge renderings.The primary release, FllumaOne-100K, contains 100,000 accepted samples across four template-level complexity regimes. Programs are executed and retained only after kernel geometry, solid validity, and export checks; release reports also record modality completeness and split-level duplicate tests. A Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B LoRA baseline trained on 80,000 samples achieves 99.98% Python syntax validity, 99.97% Flluma build success, and 99.14% STEP-export validity on the held-out 10,000-sample test split. For the 9,909 predictions converted to surface point clouds, the mean normalized Chamfer Distance is 0.002124. The dataset supports conditioned CAD reconstruction, executable program synthesis, feature-tree prediction, B-Rep analysis, retrieval, design completion, and editable reverse engineering.
