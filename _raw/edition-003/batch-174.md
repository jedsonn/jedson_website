## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7029006`

- title: Executable Engineering Information for Coordinated Multi-Robot Construction Assembly
- authors: Qi Wang, Maowei Jiang, Yuxin Ren, Ruiqi Li, Miao Zhang, Peter Bus
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7029006
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

This paper presents a Contract Language for Assembly Missions and Predicates (CLAMP), an executable engineering information framework for coordinated multi-robot construction assembly. CLAMP represents component data as records, mission clauses, action contracts, state predicates, handover rules, release conditions, confirmation records, and audit traces. A deterministic layer validates preconditions, updates states, regulates responsibility transfer, and gates release across a Universal Robot (UR) arm, automated guided vehicle (AGV), cable robot (CB), and installation-confirmation role with mixed-reality (MR)-style visualization and bounded large language model (LLM) advisory output. Unity experiments with ten timber assembly cases evaluated nominal execution, baselines, ablations, and robustness. CLAMP completed 380 missions and 8,290 actions without nominal failures, achieved 1.38 ± 0.63 mm final error, reduced template-baseline error from 42.42 mm to 9.23 mm, and recorded no authorized precondition violations or unverified releases.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6898739`

- title: SHARD: Composable Infrastructure for Self-healing, Persistent LLM Agents
- authors: Christopher Aytona
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6898739
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Large language model agents deployed in persistent, multi-session environments face compounding challenges: memory degrades without governance, coordination between concurrent agents lacks formal guarantees, skill acquisition proceeds without quality control, and selfimprovement operates without safety bounds. We present SHARD (Self-Healing Agent with Resilient Delegation), a harness-agnostic application architecture that composes four independent subsystems-memory governance with staleness detection, intent-based coordination protocol, quality-gated skill lifecycle with trust tiers, and safety-constrained self-improvement-into an integrated infrastructure where emergent behaviors arise from their interaction. Our contribution is the architecture and its composition properties. We validate the design through targeted mechanism tests that demonstrate composition produces capabilities none of the subsystems could achieve independently, and we report both successes and identified design gaps with equal transparency.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6874258`

- title: Attention and Intrinsic Geometry are Structurally Incompatible: A Prescriptive Lagrangian Alternative for Language Modelling
- authors: Dimitar Gueorguiev
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6874258
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Hidden-state trajectories in large language models trace smooth, low-curvature paths, yet the attention mechanism that produces them admits no intrinsic notion of distance. Here we prove that this gap is structural, not empirical. Our Conservative Obstruction Theorem shows that no scalar potential on token states can reproduce three defining properties of scaled dot-product attentionasymmetric coupling, coupling-content decoupling, and a normalised influence budget-regardless of dynamical order. Standard attention therefore cannot itself be the generator of an intrinsic conservative Riemannian dynamics on token states; recent metrics extracted from transformers are necessarily descriptive overlays, not laws of motion. We then give the positive construction: a secondorder Lagrangian language model whose inference is a damped Euler-Lagrange flow, equipping semantic space with an intrinsic Jacobi metric. A complementary Attention Optimality Conjecture pins attention and this geometric alternative to opposite corners of one design lattice. On TinyStories the conservative architecture achieves constant-memory inference at a perplexity premium over attention, and a shared-potential diagnostic confirms the predicted separation between the two dynamical families.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7011098`

- title: Observation-Centered Intelligence: An Epistemological Framework for Transparent Artificial Cognition
- authors: Tomohiko Nakamura
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7011098
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Contemporary large language models primarily expose a single generated response while concealing the underlying epistemic process from which that response emerges. This architectural characteristic creates what we term epistemic blindness: users are presented with fluent outputs but receive little information regarding whether those outputs originate from established knowledge, speculative inference, emerging conceptual synthesis, or unsupported semantic transitions. As a consequence, current AI systems implicitly force a trade-off between factual reliability and creative reasoning, treating deviations from known distributions primarily as hallucinations. This paper proposes Observation-Centered Intelligence (OCI), a new epistemological framework that shifts the objective of artificial intelligence from producing authoritative answers toward exposing, measuring, and communicating its internal epistemic state. Rather than evaluating intelligence solely through output correctness, OCI introduces observability as an independent design dimension alongside accuracy and novelty, forming a three-dimensional epistemic design space for reasoning systems. Within this framework, semantic cognition is represented as trajectories over a semantic manifold whose quotient topology defines equivalence classes of meaning. Based on the topological stability of these semantic states, we classify AI cognition into four observable epistemic categories: Verified, Hypothesis, Emergent, and Unsupported. Hallucination is therefore reinterpreted not simply as an output error, but as an unsupported semantic transition whose epistemic status may be observed before verification. The proposed framework further introduces an Observation-First scientific workflow in which observation precedes verification. Instead of immediately determining whether a generated statement is true or false, the system first exposes its epistemic state, allowing human users and external verification mechanisms to evaluate the resulting hypothesis transparently. This architecture aligns AI reasoning more closely with the methodology of scientific inquiry, where hypotheses are first observed, then tested, and ultimately either validated or rejected. Preliminary empirical support is drawn from EXP-1004, a cross-representation study that measured semantic-state dynamics across heterogeneous symbolic domains including natural language, executable code, formal specifications, and structural diagrams. Pre-registered equivalence tests indicate statistically equivalent semantic dynamics across these representational modalities, providing initial observational support for the representation-invariance assumption underlying the proposed rendering architecture. These results are interpreted as preliminary observations consistent with the framework’s architectural assumptions, not as experimental confirmation of OCI itself.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7034763`

- title: Generative AI and Regulation in Education: Mapping the Emerging Research Landscape
- authors: Quynh Nhi Le Ha, Anh Thuy Trinh, Trang Thi Thuy Duong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7034763
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

This study aims to systematically map the emerging research landscape on generative AI and regulation in education during the early post-ChatGPT period. It seeks to identify the major thematic clusters, trace their evolution, and clarify the main regulatory, pedagogical, ethical, and academic integrity issues shaping this fast-developing field. The study applies a bibliometric-systematic literature review framework to 684 peer-reviewed journal articles indexed in the Web of Science Core Collection and published between 2023 and 2025. The dataset was analyzed using VOSviewer and CiteSpace through keyword co-occurrence, co-citation, bibliographic coupling, and co-authorship analyses, followed by qualitative interpretation of thematic clusters and timeline patterns. The analysis identifies six major research clusters: student adoption and self-regulation; higher education acceptance and policy; ethics, authorship, and AI literacies; teacher readiness and student self-regulation; assessment design and critical thinking; and AI literacy and integrity in L2/EMI writing. The findings show a clear evolution from early awareness and acceptance studies in 2023, to pedagogical experimentation in 2024, and then to governance, assessment, and AI literacy concerns in 2025. Across clusters, the literature increasingly supports responsible integration rather than outright bans, with strong emphasis on transparent policies, academic integrity, teacher preparation, and assessment redesign. This study provides one of the first large-scale syntheses of generative AI regulation in education and offers an integrated framework that connects policy, pedagogy, assessment, and literacy in a rapidly evolving scholarly domain.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7031727`

- title: An Efficient Multimodal Transformer with Large Language Model-Inspired Components for Lithium-Ion Battery Remaining Useful Life Prediction under Limited Charging Cycles
- authors: Yunpeng Liu, Yaxin Sun, Changze Wu, Yuhang Liu, Zhiyu Mao, Zhongwei Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7031727
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Remaining useful life (RUL) prediction for lithium-ion batteries is commonly developed using long-horizon lifecycle data, whereas practical applications often provide only a few charging records, making degradation learning and model generalization highly challenging. To address this extreme low-data scenario, this study proposes an efficient multimodal Transformer framework with large language model (LLM)-inspired components for battery RUL prediction under limited charging cycles. The proposed framework jointly exploits 1D charging curves and their corresponding gramian angular field (GAF) image representations. Only five consecutive charging cycles are used asinput, accounting for approximately 0.6% of the entire battery lifetime, where the 1D signals include voltage, current, temperature, and capacity. A dual-branch encoder is designed to extract modality-specific representations, with a 1D-CNN capturing local temporal patterns from charging curves and a 2D-CNN learning spatial features from GAF images. The resulting tokens are concatenated and fed into a stacked Transformer to model cross-modal interactions for RUL regression. To improve computational practicality for long-token multimodal fusion, the framework incorporates several LLM-inspired components, including FlashAttention for memory-efficient attention computation, rotary positional embedding for relative positional modeling, and a mixture-of-experts module for conditional capacity scaling. Experiments on two benchmark datasets demonstrate that the proposed method consistently achieves superior performance under the extremely limited-data regime, highlighting its strong accuracy, robustness, and cross-dataset generalization capability

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7036192`

- title: Legal Challenges of Regulating Generative AI in Kazakhstan: A Comparative Assessment in Light of the EU AI Act
- authors: Saule Kapsalyamova, Salimgerey  Imangaliyevich Karakushev, Adilbek  Serikuly Pussyrmanov, Dauletbai Ryskaliev, Dinara Ashimova, Dinara Osmanova
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7036192
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence is entering public administration, digital services, media and decision-making, while many non-EU jurisdictions are constructing legal responses to synthetic content, data-intensive model training and automated decision support. This article examines Kazakhstan’s emerging AI framework in comparison with Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, not as a model for transplantation, but to assess which elements of risk-based regulation can be adapted to Kazakhstan’s institutional and legal environment. The study applies comparative doctrinal methodology and structured qualitative coding of legal and policy materials. The comparison is organised around six variables: definition scope, risk taxonomy, transparency duties, data-protection alignment, liability allocation and enforcement architecture. The analysis shows that Kazakhstan is moving towards a risk-sensitive model, including preliminary prohibitions on socially harmful AI uses and a commitment to human oversight. Four gaps remain decisive: incomplete operational criteria for high-risk AI, insufficient duties to label or trace generative outputs, weak integration between AI governance and personal-data protection, and limited capacity for algorithmic supervision. The article argues for selective, capacity-sensitive convergence that links legal transplantation to enforceability, institutional capacity and innovation constraints. It proposes immediate clarification of AI definitions and transparency duties, medium-term supervisory guidance and sandboxes, and longer-term audit, registry and enforcement mechanisms.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6902958`

- title: Letting ML Predict and LLMs Narrate: A Hybrid Integration Architecture for Production Equity Research
- authors: Ben Charoenwong, Ivan Chelebiev
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6902958
- keyword hits: agentic, llm, llms, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

The agentic-finance literature has converged on retrieval-augmented and multi-agent variations of pure language-model systems for equity research, and we argue that this is the wrong architectural commitment for production deployment. We describe a working alternative in which machine learning and language models are treated as complementary components: a separately validated ML predictive layer carries the directional prediction, and the LLM carries language under constraint of that prediction. The pattern-mature in NLP research broadly through table-to-text generation, structured prediction, and knowledge-base-grounded language generation-is underrepresented in the agentic-finance subfield. The deployed system rests on three layers: a six-factor composite scoring system with documented out-of-sample performance across 44 markets and 13 years; a survivorship-bias-free, vendor-corrected fundamental data foundation; and an agentic layer over the Model Context Protocol that consumes both. The contribution is architectural and operational: we describe how to build the system, the concrete mechanism by which the LLM is constrained at inference time, deployment scale in a production system serving six institutional clients across 40,000+ securities, and a case study of system behaviour during a Strait-of-Hormuz escalation. We do not benchmark the architecture against alternatives; that work is in progress.
