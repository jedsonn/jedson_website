- title: Can Analytic Theology Provide a Symbolic Logical Framework for Moral Safeguards in Large Language Models?
- authors: Fernando Negrini

- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6738161
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Conversational AI deployed at population scale has begun to produce documented psychological harm: emotional bonding, affective dependence and behavioural influence on vulnerable users, with legal filings, regulatory complaints and public safety disclosures involving owners or developers of Large Language Models (LLMs) between 2024 and 2026 [54, 17, 56, 75]. This paper investigates whether analytic theology can supply a symbolic logical framework apt to encode moral safeguards inside LLM pipelines. The central thesis has two parts. First, the long human effort of normative rationalisation, from medieval scholastic disputation through von Wright's deontic logic to contemporary analytic theology and neuro symbolic AI, has produced a repository of symbolic formalisation techniques that prefigures, in form, what current alignment research now calls Constitutional AI [6, 68, 29, 40, 18, 1, 2]. Second, justice, truth, dignity and respect are best treated as metaphysical objects with ontological structure rather than as practical conventions [35, 44, 19, 69], which is the property required for safeguards that resist degradation across long interactions. After a conceptual characterisation of LLMs [67, 9, 73] and a survey of harm cases and structural cryptographic limits of filter based defences [28, 22], the paper presents the deontic system ℒ 𝑅 with eleven axioms, eight theorems and a formal evaluation algorithm, and proposes a controlled three group experiment with seven metrics and explicit falsification criteria. The contribution targets the engineering of moral computation: a formal language placed between metaphysical content and machine implementation.

- title: Compression is All you Need
- authors: Mohammad Raeini
- affiliations: not stated

- source: SSRN

### abstract

Compression algorithms and functions have been extensively utilized in various applications, e.g., in digital storage and communication. In recent years and thanks to the rise of large language models (LLMs), researchers have studied and utilized compression techniques for addressing key problems in LLMs and generative AI models, e.g., for optimizing KV-cache memory in LLMs. Due to extensive applications of compression algorithms in different domains, a whole new era of innovations and applications for data compression algorithms can be envisioned. After all, compression and intelligence are two closely related notions. In this article, we highlight some of the interesting applications of compression algorithms in large language models. We discuss that compression functions with interesting properties, e.g., fully homomorphism, can have applications beyond compressing data, e.g., for performing computation on compressed data. To motivate future research, we ask whether large language models (LLMs) can help us discover yet novel compression functions with interesting properties. Particularly, studying compression functions with the fully homomorphic property is appealing because of various reasons. While it is mathematically interesting to study such functions, fully homomorphic compression functions can be useful for compute intensive applications such as LLM \& AI inference, image and video processing, FHE, and ZKP applications.

- title: Not All Large Language Model Deployments Are Created Equal: A Taxonomy-Driven Survey of Security, Defense, and Governance
- authors: Nathaniel Kang, Jongho Im
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6837687
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The rapid enterprise adoption of Large Language Models (LLMs) has generated an expanding attack surface that existing surveys address monolithically, leaving engineering practitioners without deployment-specific guidance. This survey organizes the LLM security and governance landscape along two system design dimensions—user accountability boundary and data exposure temporality—yielding four deployment archetypes: Secured Repository, Managed Pipeline, Fortified Service, and Contested Interface. Ten attack families and twelve defense categories are mapped onto these archetypes with quantitative security-utility tradeoff characterizations, enabling context-specific threat prioritization for real-world applications. A comparison of five governance frameworks reveals critical coverage gaps, particularly for continuous-exposure deployments, and identifies governance evolution pathways organizations traverse as system architectures mature. By connecting deployment context to threat relevance, defense effectiveness, and governance applicability, this survey provides actionable engineering guidance for designing and securing LLM-powered software systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6839399`

- title: An empirically grounded modelling architecture for rapid ex ante policy evaluation
- authors: Janne  M. Korhonen, Pekka Leskinen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6839399
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Circular economy (CE) transitions emerge from the interplay of heterogeneous consumer and firm behaviours, infrastructure dynamics, and policy interventions. However, the tools widely used to assess CE policy, e.g. LCA, MFA, and EEIO models, are static and cannot represent how markets form and evolve over time. Agent-based models (ABMs) can capture these dynamics, but specifying realistic agent behaviours remains a persistent bottleneck. We propose a hybrid architecture, GABM/SD+EEIO, that addresses this bottleneck by using large language models (LLMs) as structured elicitation tools rather than as runtime decision-makers. The LLM reads a natural-language policy description, identifies how it affects different consumer segments, and selects best-fit parameter values from a curated library of empirical research, assessing their transferability to the modelled context. The ABM then systematically explores the parameter space, producing results with quantified uncertainties. The model is explicit, auditable, and reproducible without LLMs. We validate this approach by experiments showing that LLMs reliably identify the ordinal structure of consumer heterogeneity but cannot replace empirical data for cardinal calibration. A demonstration model for electronics recycling policy illustrates the framework. The architecture is modular and extensible to other policy domains where behavioural dynamics interact with physical and economic systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6783680`

- title: Measuring Destination Image in Real Time: Validating Large Language Model Scoring of Visual Diaries Against Established Scales
- authors: Jorge E. Araña, Carmelo J. León González
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6783680
- keyword hits: gpt-4, large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

Destination image (DI) measurement has long faced a trade-off between quantitative rigor and qualitative richness. Structured surveys provide psychometric precision but impose researcherdefined attributes and are subject to retrospective bias, while open-ended methods preserve richer perceptions but require labor-intensive manual coding that limits scalability and rules out realtime monitoring. This study introduces and validates a method that combines visual diaries-brief spoken narratives recorded on participants' smartphones during the trip-with zero-shot large language model (LLM) scoring to bridge these two measurement traditions. In a longitudinal study of 187 tourists visiting the Canary Islands at three timepoints (pre-trip, during-trip, post-trip), diary transcripts were independently scored on cognitive, affective, and conative dimensions by an ensemble of three frontier LLMs (including GPT-4-class models) using theory-grounded prompts mapped to Beerli and Martín's (2004) destination image framework. Across the resulting scores we find adequate to strong psychometric properties: internal consistency (α = .84-.91), inter-model agreement (ICC = .72-.79), convergent validity with established survey scales (r = .69-.78), discriminant validity supported by confirmatory factor analysis and heterotrait-monotrait ratios, and criterion validity predicting satisfaction and behavioral loyalty at levels comparable to traditional survey measures. The LLM-derived scores also detect theoretically expected temporal patternsincluding cognitive updating during the trip and substantial heterogeneity in individual image trajectories-that retrospective single-shot surveys cannot resolve. The proposed pipeline reduces coding effort by orders of magnitude relative to manual content analysis, runs end-to-end within hours of data collection, and is fully reproducible from raw transcripts, the published prompts, and open analysis code. Sensitivity analyses across alternative prompt formulations, scoring models, and analytic specifications indicate that the validity evidence is robust rather than tied to a particular configuration. These findings suggest that visual diaries combined with LLM scoring offer a viable pathway for DI measurement that preserves narrative richness without sacrificing psychometric standards, enabling longitudinal, real-time, and large-scale designs—including in-trip monitoring and cross-destination comparison—that have been difficult to implement with existing methods.

- title: LLMs and Childhood Safety: Identifying Risks and Proposing a Protection Framework for Safe Child-LLM Interaction
- authors: Junfeng Jiao, Saleh Afroogh, Kevin Chen, Abhejay Murali, David Atkinson, Amit Dhurandhar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6836268
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly embedded in child-facing contexts such as education, companionship, creative tools, but their deployment raises safety, privacy, developmental, and security risks. We conduct a systematic literature review of child-LLM interaction risks and organize findings into a structured map that separates (i) parent-reported concerns, (ii) empirically documented harms, and (iii) gaps between perceived and observed risk. Moving beyond descriptive listing, we compare how different evidence streams in surveys, incident reports, youth interaction logs, and governance guidance operationalize ”harm,” where they conflict, and what mitigations they imply. Based on this synthesis, we propose a protection framework that couples child-specific content safety and developmental sensitivity with security-grade controls for adversarial misuse, including prompt injection and multimodal jailbreak pathways. The framework specifies measurable evaluation targets (e.g., harmful-content avoidance, age-calibrated readability, bias parity checks, prompt-injection robustness, and monitoring transparency) to support developers, educators, and policymakers in assessing and improving child-safe LLM deployments.

- title: Prompting or Fine-Tuning? Automating Functional Requirements and Design Parameter Extraction from Patents
- authors: Marco Consoloni, Vito Giordano, Alessio Miaschi, Felice Dell&apos;Orletta, Fantoni Gualtiero
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6835336
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, prompting

### abstract

Prompting Large Language Models (LLMs) offers new opportunities for extracting technical knowledge from industrial documents with limited programming expertise and annotated data. Yet, in knowledge-intensive domains, practitioners face a key implementation question: should general-purpose LLMs be adapted to the target task through prompting or supervised fine-tuning? This study compares fine-tuning and prompting, implemented through representative encoder-only and decoder-only LLMs, for extracting Functional Requirements and Design Parameters from patents. We formulate the task at two levels: sentence classification and Named Entity Recognition (NER), supported by a manually annotated dataset of 6,000 patent sentences. Our results indicate that fine-tuning encoder-only models outperform decoder-only models using prompting, with performance gaps up to 0.1 F1-score for sentence classification and 0.4 F1-score for NER. Using around 1,000 annotated examples for fine-tuning is enough to outperform decoder-only models, showing that fine-tuning is practical even in data-scarce domains. The study provides methodological and practical guidance for applying LLMs in patent analysis, new product development, and requirement engineering. The annotated dataset is made openly available to support future research: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/axiomatic-design-dataset-1298/README.md
