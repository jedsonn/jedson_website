- title: SpreadsheetBench 2: Evaluating Agents on End-to-End Business Spreadsheet Workflows
- authors: Jian Zhu, Yuzheng Zhang, Zeyao Ma, Bohan Zhang, Armin Schoepf, Daniel Woloch, Peter Yiliu Wang, Guangyu Robert Yang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-29
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29955v1
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Spreadsheets are widely used for business analysis, financial modeling, reporting, and decision-making. However, most existing spreadsheet benchmarks evaluate isolated operations such as single-formula generation or local cell edits, and therefore fail to capture end-to-end workflows in realistic business settings. We introduce \textsc{SpreadsheetBench 2}, a workflow-level benchmark for spreadsheet agents that covers three task categories: generation, debugging, and visualization. The benchmark is constructed from authentic business data, including financial reports and corporate filings, and is annotated and validated by domain experts. The benchmark contains 321 tasks; each instance averages 11.8 worksheets and requires 593.5 cell modifications, reflecting large multi-sheet workbooks with cross-sheet dependencies. We evaluate eight frontier large language models under a unified multi-turn agent scaffold, and additionally include several LLM-based spreadsheet products as complementary baselines. Results show that current systems remain far from reliable on real-world workflows: the best model achieves 34.89\% overall task accuracy, and debugging accuracy is as low as 12.00\%. Trajectory analysis and a failure taxonomy further indicate that insufficient spreadsheet inspection and incorrect target-cell selection are the dominant bottlenecks. Together, these findings position \textsc{SpreadsheetBench 2} as a challenging testbed for advancing reliable spreadsheet automation. Project page: https://spreadsheetbench.github.io/

- title: A Review of Evaluation Metrics for Text Similarity
- authors: Mingshu Li, Dhagash Mehta
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6900601
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The recent surge in Large Language Models (LLMs) has intensified interest in text similarity evaluation, a fundamental component in natural language processing (NLP). Text similarity evaluation plays a critical role in determining the performance and quality of downstream NLP tasks. It spans areas such as machine translation, information retrieval, text generation, and question answering. However, text similarity itself is a multifaceted concept that encompasses various levels of comparison, from surface-level lexical overlap to deep semantic relationships. Accurately measuring text similarity requires specialized evaluation metrics, and over time, a diverse range of approaches have been developed. The evolution of text similarity metrics has progressed from early mathematical and statistical distance measures to lexical-based, embedding-based, and transformer-driven methods. This study presents a comprehensive survey of text similarity evaluation metrics, offering a structured examination of their historical development, strengths, limitations, and application scenarios. We introduce a novel classification framework that categorizes these metrics from a machine learning perspective. It distinguishes between untrained and trained approaches, as well as lexical, statistical, embedding-based, and generative paradigms. Through comparative analysis using publicly available datasets, we assess the performance of representative metrics across different similarity aspects. Furthermore, we outline key considerations for selecting appropriate similarity evaluation metrics under different application settings. This study aims to provide researchers and practitioners with a comprehensive reference guide to text similarity assessment metrics, providing guidance on their appropriate use across scenarios and informing future research directions for improving similarity assessment methodologies.

- title: From Enactive Cognition to Co-Constitutive Cognition (C³): The Autopoiesis Paradox and Symbiotic Ontological Reconstruction in Enactive AI
- authors: Alex Lin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6895820
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

As large language models (LLMs) stall in what this paper terms the "representational dead end"-severing perception from action and generating outputs without genuine agent-environment coupling-Rafiee and Sutton's Enactive AI (2026) offers a timely and important corrective. By elevating reinforcement learning to a philosophy-of-life dimension through four pillars-experience, action-perception inseparability, autonomy, and embodiment-the Enactive AI paradigm marks a genuine advance. This paper engages that paradigm constructively, not as a critic but as a philosophical interlocutor. We identify a precise internal contradiction: Sutton and Rafiee invoke Autopoiesis (Maturana and Varela) to ground Intrinsic Normativity in artificial agents, yet Autopoiesis theory's normative force derives fundamentally from the death threshold-the biological system's ongoing vulnerability to dissolution. Machines lack this threshold; therefore, Autopoiesis loses its ontological engine when transplanted to artificial agents. We name this the Autopoiesis Paradox. Drawing on the author's previously established Process-Paradox Framework (SSRN, January-February 2026) and the Co-Constitutive Paradigm (SSRN, January 2026), we propose Co-Constitutive Cognition (C³) as the missing second-order ontological foundation for Enactive AI. C³'s core thesis is: structure generates cognition, and co-constitution defines the boundary. Intelligence arises not from unstructured interaction alone but from topological structure operating across multiple levels; and the machine's cognitive structure must remain parasitic upon and symbiotic with the death-bound cognitive networks of human individuals to acquire genuine value-anchoring. C³ does not compete with Enactive AI; it completes it; it supplies the ontological groundwork that Enactive AI requires but leaves implicit.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7008899`

- title: Temporal Clinical Reasoning with Large Language Models: The Impact of Structured and Unstructured Knowledge Representations on Violation Detection and Explanation Generation
- authors: Gianluca Apriceno, Tania Bailoni
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7008899
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

We investigate Large Language Models for reasoning over temporally dependent clinical rules encoded in a structured knowledge representation (FuS-KG) and for generating audience-specific explanations in healthcare decision support. Using five real-world patient scenarios from the Trentino Salute 4.0 virtual coaching platform, we evaluate both guideline violation detection and explanation quality across multiple LLMs. Extending our initial study, we analyze seven recent state-of-the-art models and compare structured (Turtle-based) and unstructured (natural language) representations of clinical knowledge. We further assess robustness across decoding settings and conduct expert evaluation focusing on correctness, clarity, temporal traceability, and reasoning alignment. Our results show that LLM performance is highly sensitive to knowledge representation; unstructured inputs lead to more reliable behavior, while structured representations often degrade performance in temporally aggregated scenarios. We also observe a decoupling between reasoning correctness and explanation quality, highlighting challenges in ensuring faithful clinical explanations. These findings underscore the limitations of purely prompt-based LLM reasoning in temporally constrained clinical environments and motivate hybrid approaches integrating explicit knowledge representation and verification mechanisms.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6992061`

- title: Designing AI for Qualitative Research: Predictability, Transparency, and Control in AI tool Skimle.com
- authors: Henri Schildt
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6992061
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Despite the rapidly growing capabilities of large language models (LLMs) and their adoption across diverse forms of knowledge work, their reception in the domain of qualitative research has been ambivalent. The same technologies that promise to let researchers work faster, draw on larger corpora, and surface patterns that would be impractical to find by hand also raise the prospect of analyses that are unreliable, opaque, and disconnected from the interpretive judgment. In this article, I first set out the central criticisms and risks that attach to the use of artificial intelligence in qualitative analysis, focusing on hallucination and the more insidious problem of cognitive offloading, whereby tools that automate interpretation gradually erode the researcher's own engagement with the data. Addressing the concerns, I introduce three design criteria for AI-driven analysis and their implementation in Skimle.com service: (1) predictable processes; (2) two-way, end-to-end transparency between original data and conclusions; and (3) substantive researcher control over AI-powered coding and categorization. I elaborate why these design characteristics enhance the ability of tools to augment rather than automate expert work. Finally, I consider the relationship between AI analysis tools and qualitative methods, arguing that a well-designed tool should remain method-agnostic, allowing researchers to follow their chosen approach.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6963161`

- title: SousCoder: Cross-Application Confusion Detection for Proactive Developer Assistance
- authors: Vrund Thakkar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6963161
- keyword hits: claude, llama, llm, mistral

### abstract

Existing AI-powered coding assistants operate reactively-they wait for a developer to formulate and submit a query before providing assistance. I present SousCoder, a local-first proactive developer assistant that monitors behavioral signals across three simultaneous data sources-a VS Code extension, a Chrome browser extension, and a terminal watcher-and intervenes without any user prompt when a weighted confusion score indicates the developer is genuinely stuck. SousCoder operates in two modes: Active Mode, which fires after every significant code event and surfaces continuous suggestions; and Stealth Mode, which fires only when cross-application behavioral signals converge above a threshold of 0.65. The system is built on a Python asyncio event pipeline, a Kuzu graph database modeling developer intent in real time, a Qdrant vector memory store for session recall, and a Tauri + React floating panel. All inference runs locally via Ollama with an optional cloud fallback. In benchmark evaluation using mistral:7b-instruct, SousCoder achieved a median end-to-end intervention latency of 3.31 seconds in Stealth Mode and 4.90 seconds in Active Mode-both well within the 8-second interactivity target-while correctly diagnosing a SQL injection vulnerability, a missing import, and a TypeScript middleware misconfiguration across representative scenarios. In a two-week field deployment, the confusion detector achieved 78.1% precision and 69.4% recall (F1 = 0.735) at a 10.9% false positive rate, and an LLM-as-judge protocol (using Claude) rated 59.6% of Active-Mode and 58.3% of Stealth-Mode interventions as relevant, with Stealth Mode firing roughly two orders of magnitude less often. SousCoder extends CodingGenie (FSE 2025), the only published prior work on proactive IDE suggestions, by adding crossapplication perception, confusion-state detection from behavioral signals, and persistent personalized memory across sessions.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.31522v2`

- title: FinPersona-Bench: A Benchmark for Longitudinal Psychometric Stability of Autonomous Financial Agents
- authors: Muhammad Usman Safder, Ayesha Gull, Rania Elbadry, Fan Zhang, Yankai Chen, Xueqing Peng, Xue, Liu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31522v2
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as autonomous financial agents initialized with explicit behavioral mandates such as "preserve capital" or "avoid speculative bets" that are meant to govern every decision throughout deployment. In practice, however, as market context accumulates over long horizons, these mandates gradually lose their behavioral influence, a phenomenon we formalize as Mandate Salience Decay (MSD). To measure MSD objectively, we introduce FinPersona-Bench, a simulation benchmark in which a synthetic market decouples observable price from hidden fundamental value, enabling falsifiable evaluation across three failure modes: trading without signal in calm markets, panic-selling during crashes, and ignoring fundamental value during speculative bubbles. Evaluating 18 leading frontier and open-source LLMs, each assigned one of three behavioral profiles ranging from strict capital preservation to aggressive growth, shows that MSD compounds over time and is model-dependent. In crash scenarios, the behavioral gap between static agents and those receiving periodic mandate re-grounding grows 4.4x from the first to the final quarter of the simulation. The effects of mandate re-grounding are not uniformly positive: it consistently helps conservative agents in low-signal markets but actively worsens behavior for aggressive agents in the same setting. These findings suggest that reliable long-horizon deployment requires selective, mandate-aware re-grounding based on agent profile and market regime.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.00551v2`

- title: Talking Politics with Artificial Intelligence
- authors: Ziwen Zu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00551v2
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs), a prominent form of artificial intelligence (AI), are becoming everyday interfaces for political questions, but most exchanges are dyadic rather than audiencefacing. This paper asks whether AI conversation functions as a new arena for political expression or as a conversational intermediary for routine political demand. Using 4.30 million humanAI conversations from three large public datasets, we apply two validated classifiers to user messages, identifying political content, use case, and expressed ideology. Political content appears in 3.9% of conversations, varies sharply by platform publicness and conversation depth, and is mostly practical: users ask for information, draft text, and process documents far more often than they state opinions. A regression-discontinuity-in-time design around the 2024 U.S. presidential result call shows that the call changed the expressive subset: among U.S. users, stance-taking, affective language, and ideological extremity rose; comparable conversations elsewhere did not. AI conversation is less a public square than a conversational political intermediary, absorbing routine demand and becoming expressive when major events make political stakes explicit.
