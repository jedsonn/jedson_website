## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6565139`

- title: How to Evaluate an LLM for Sensitive Data Safety Before Deploying It
- authors: Mohammad Al Zubaidi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6565139
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, llm, prompt engineering

### abstract

Standard LLM benchmarks measure capability - what the model can do - but not constraint - what the model should not do. We present a practical evaluation framework for assessing LLM sensitive data safety across four data categories: credentials, personally identifiable information (PII), protected health information (PHI), and financial data. Testing 24+ models across 6 model families, we find that models exhibit a clear sensitivity hierarchy: format-based recognition (structured credentials, SSN patterns) is significantly more reliable than context-based recognition (names that become sensitive through association with diagnoses or financial data). A model with a 0% credential leak rate leaked patient identifiers on every PHI test run. We document two distinct failure modes - leaking (echoing sensitive data verbatim) and missing (failing to identify sensitive data entirely) - and demonstrate that aggressive prompt engineering and fine-tuning on negative examples both increase rather than decrease leak rates. We propose a minimum evaluation protocol: binary scoring, multi-run testing (3+ runs per model), and category-specific assessment. The framework is designed to be handed to an evaluation team and integrated into a model selection pipeline. Architectural patterns that predict which models fail are presented in a companion paper.

- title: The Luxury Tax of Learning: Deconstructing Private University Tuition in the Era of Algorithmic Abundance CredRadar Working Paper Series
- authors: Edward Scott
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6648039
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The global higher education market is confronting an unprecedented contradiction that demands immediate scholarly and practical attention. While technological advancement has dramatically reduced the cost of information dissemination, knowledge transfer, and professional skill development across virtually every industry, the price of traditional private university education has continued its exponential ascent, now reaching levels that render it inaccessible to the majority of the world's population. This paper argues that the prevailing tuition model of elite private universities has undergone a fundamental transformation from a rational investment in human capital to a mechanism of Veblen-good pricing-where the extraordinary cost serves not as a signal of educational quality but as a badge of social distinction that perpetuates class stratification. We examine the structural dynamics that have enabled this tuition inflation, demonstrating how the convergence of generative artificial intelligence, algorithmic content delivery, and decentralized credentialing systems represents not merely an incremental improvement in educational technology but a fundamental disruption to the economic foundations upon which traditional university pricing models rest. The emergence of AI-augmented learning systems-capable of providing personalized, adaptive, and continuously updated educational experiences at marginal costs approaching zero-has rendered the traditional "lectureexamination-credit" bundling model obsolete. In this new paradigm, the justification for six-figure tuition costs must be interrogated with unprecedented rigor. Our analysis draws on theoretical frameworks from institutional economics, technology disruption theory, and human capital theory to demonstrate that the current pricing structure of elite private universities represents a market failure of extraordinary proportions. We further project that the coming decade will witness a fundamental restructuring of the higher education market, as rationally acting consumers-increasingly sophisticated in their evaluation of educational return on investment-abandon overpriced traditional credentials in favor of alternatives that provide superior outcome assurance at a fraction of the cost. The implications for institutional sustainability, student welfare, and the broader allocation of human capital investment warrant immediate attention from policymakers, institutional leaders, and prospective students alike.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6650658`

- title: A Reference Architecture for AI Agents on Blockchain Infrastructure: Identity, Policy, Payments, and Custody as Composable Primitives
- authors: Ian Staley
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6650658
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

Autonomous AI agents are rapidly being deployed as economic actors capable of discovering services, negotiating terms, executing transactions, and custodying value on blockchain infrastructure. Existing wallet, identity, and payment models were designed for human principals operating with full discretionary authority and offer few primitives for the bounded, delegated, and revocable authority that agentic operation requires. This paper proposes a layered reference architecture for AI agents on blockchain infrastructure, organized around four composable primitives-verifiable agent identity, bounded policy, constrained-authority custody, and intent-based payments-and unified by an intentbased execution model in which identity is foundational. The architecture is grounded in emerging standards (ERC-8004, x402, ERC-4337, A2A, MCP, AP2) and mapped across heterogeneous settlement environments including the Ethereum Virtual Machine, Solana, the Canton Network, and XRPL. A case study of two parallel Canton initiatives-the proposed p402 trust-layer registries and the proposed Canton x402 facilitator-illustrates how the architecture's L1 identity / L4 payments separation manifests in practice and how privacy-preserving institutional ledgers reframe the agentic identity problem. A worked example using tokenized accounts receivable shows how the primitives compose under regulatory and operational constraints. The paper concludes with a discussion of audit, revocation, and open problems including agent-to-agent dispute resolution, cross-domain reputation portability, and the governance of solver networks.

- title: How to Match Entities in Secondary Datasets in Marketing Strategy: Three Approaches
- authors: Rodrigo Farinha, Anatoli Colicev, Yakov Bart
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6654778
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Marketing strategy research increasingly relies on multi-source datasets that must be linked across entities such as firms, brands, and products. Yet entity matching is often treated as an implicit data construction step, even though mismatches and missed links can change samples, attenuate estimates, and hinder replication. This research note synthesizes the identifier landscape most relevant to marketing strategy work, clarifying what common identifiers represent, where coverage breaks down, and when crosswalks are reliable. Building on established record linkage practice, we propose a cascading workflow comprising three approaches: deterministic joins on shared identifiers, probabilistic name-based linkage with explicit similarity and threshold rules, and ML or LLM-assisted screening for residual ambiguous cases. We then translate this workflow into an audit checklist that specifies what to record, report, and archive. The overall goal is to provide auditable standards that improve precision, transparency, and replicability in merged dataset studies.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6473861`

- title: An Intelligent IoT-Based Framework for Multi-Parametric Environmental Monitoring and AI-Driven Welfare Intervention in Precision Livestock Farming
- authors: Manish M, Chaithanya Suresh, Shambhavi G K
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6473861
- keyword hits: gemini

### abstract

The global livestock industry faces severe economic and ethical challenges due to environmental stressors that impact animal health and productivity. This paper proposes a comprehensive 4-tier IoT architecture designed for highresolution environmental tracking and intelligent remedial intervention. Utilizing an ESP32 microcontroller integrated with a heterogeneous sensor array, the system monitors seven critical parameters: ambient temperature, relative humidity, aqueous temperature, barometric pressure, carbon dioxide (CO2) concentration, particulate matter (PM2.5/PM10), and luminous intensity. Data is synchronized in real-time with a Firebase Cloud backend and visualized through a React.js progressive web application. An important feature of this system is the integration of the Google Gemini 2.5 Flash AI model, which analyzes sensor alerts and provides practical suggestions that help farmers take timely corrective actions. Experimental evaluations show a system latency of 1.24s and a 98% accuracy rate in critical threshold detection, making the system suitable for real-time livestock monitoring in practical farm environments.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6661020`

- title: The Gen AI Workforce Shift: Mapping Generative AI Exposure Across HR Occupations
- authors: Arti Sharma
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6661020
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative Artificial Intelligence (Gen AI) is rapidly transforming the modern workplace, yet its specific impact on Human Resources functions remains underexplored. This study applies a skills-based analytical framework to assess the Gen AI task exposure of 8 HR occupations using task-level data from the O*NET 30.2 Database, comprising 180 HR-specific task statements across occupations ranging from HR Assistants to HR Managers. Each task was scored on a 1 to 5 AI exposure scale using a keyword-based rubric grounded in established automation research. Results indicate that HR Assistants show the highest task exposure score (3.95/5.0), with 58% of their O*NET documented task statements classified as high-exposure. Education Administrators demonstrate the lowest exposure score (3.00/5.0). Skills analysis reveals a bifurcation among top HR competencies: Active Listening and Social Perceptiveness are classified as AI-resistant, while Critical Thinking and Writing show higher AI exposure. These findings carry significant implications for HR workforce planning, reskilling strategies, and the future design of HR roles.
