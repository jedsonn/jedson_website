- authors: Walter Sengik da Cruz, Mateus Panizzon
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6723472
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Purpose: The main objective of this research is to design and evaluate the usefulness of an artifact for assessing conversational dropout between users and chatbots based on text-based conversational AI models, aiming to advance prescriptive knowledge in the field by integrating quantitative and qualitative metrics.Design/methodology/approach: From the perspective of Design Science Research (DSR), the study uses a corpus of 849 real interactions from three scenarios of Conversational AI: SaaS, Software Factory, and English School. The methodology applies generative AI models to extract subjective variables and Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) to validate the relationships among the agent’s conversational quality (D2), the agent’s trust and clarity (D3), the agent’s engagement and empathy (D4), and conversational dropout (D1).Findings: The results reveal the “Persistence Paradox”: low technical performance does not act as a trigger for immediate abandonment (H1 rejected), but rather as a catalyst for compensatory effort. Dropout occurs through exhaustion, when the user’s adaptive effort is no longer justified by the perceived value. The theoretical model showed that conversational quality (D2) has a strong impact on trust and clarity (D3), as well as on engagement and empathy (D4).Practical implications: The study provides a prescriptive diagnostic artifact for managers, enabling them to identify the user’s “point of exhaustion” and optimize escalation to human support in a predictive manner.Originality/value: The research redefines conversational dropout as the outcome of the depletion of the user’s adaptive capacity, shifting from the paradigm of accuracy to the paradigm of fluency and effort management.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6725154`

- title: Application Scenarios and Perceived Importance of Generative AI in Construction Safety Knowledge Management: A Multi-Stakeholder Empirical Study
- authors: Liying Wang, Botao Gu, Yuecheng Huang, Dongping Fang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6725154
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) is opening up new opportunities for the intelligent advancement of construction safety knowledge management (CSKM). However, its potential applications across the CSKM lifecycle, as well as differences in perceptions among key stakeholders, remain underexplored. Drawing on semi-structured interviews with 24 construction safety experts, this study identified six functional roles through which GenAI could support CSKM and synthesized 24 potential application scenarios. A questionnaire survey and mixed-design ANOVA were then used to examine stakeholder differences in perceived importance. The results revealed a significant three-way interaction among stakeholder type, CSKM stage, and GenAI functional role. Across CSKM stages, the knowledge acquisition and creation stage was rated as the most important, whereas the knowledge transfer and sharing stage received less emphasis. Across functional roles, trustworthy and compliance-oriented governance of construction safety knowledge was considered the most critical. Stakeholder perceptions also showed marked heterogeneity: general contractors attached the greatest importance to GenAI-enabled CSKM, whereas supervisors assigned relatively lower importance, and the four stakeholder groups differed substantially in the functional scenarios they prioritized for future development. By integrating scenario importance with the degree of stakeholder divergence, the scenarios were further classified into four clusters: the core consensus zone, stakeholder-sensitive zone, exploratory potential zone, and conflict-coordination zone, with tailored development pathways proposed for each. These findings provide a theoretical basis for developing integrated strategies for GenAI-enabled CSKM and practical guidance for the intelligent transformation and performance improvement of safety knowledge management in the construction industry.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6725558`

- title: TRAINING ON TRIAL: INSIGHTS FROM BARTZ AND KADREY
- authors: Barbara Bruni
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6725558
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, large language models

### abstract

This Article examines the first federal decisions addressing whether the use of copyrighted books to train large language models constitutes fair use: Kadrey v. Meta and Bartz v. Anthropic. Both courts held that training on lawfully acquired works was fair use, while diverging in their treatment of pirated materials and in aspects of their market-harm analysis. Using these cases as doctrinal case studies, the Article argues that generative AI exposes structural tensions within fair use doctrine. In particular, AI training unsettles the traditional relationship between transformativeness under the first factor and market substitution under the fourth. It also places pressure on the idea/expression distinction, complicates the assessment of emerging licensing markets, and brings renewed attention to public-benefit considerations in technology cases. Beyond substitution, the Article identifies a distinct "value-extraction" concern: AI systems may appropriate expressive value from copyrighted works without replenishing the incentive structure copyright is designed to sustain. The Article's primary contribution is to identify and analyze these tensions, while offering some suggestions for how courts might approach them in future cases.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6722700`

- title: The AI Democratization Paradox: How Mid-Market Tools Created a New Implementation Gap for Hispanic SMBs
- authors: Humberto Inciarte
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6722700
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

The dominant narrative in artificial intelligence (AI) discourse holds that AI has been democratized: enterprise-grade large language models are now accessible to any business owner with an internet connection and a credit card. This paper argues that this framing conflates two distinct dimensions of democratization-access and implementation-and that conflating them obscures a new and consequential gap. Drawing on verified pricing data from Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google; AI failure-rate research from the RAND Corporation, MIT, and Boston Consulting Group; and demographic and economic data on Hispanic-owned small and medium-sized businesses (SMBs) from the Stanford Latino Entrepreneurship Initiative, the Brookings Institution, and the U.S. Census Bureau, the paper develops three findings. First, AI access is genuinely democratized: a Hispanic owner-operator pays the same per-token inference rate as Microsoft. Second, AI value capture is not democratized: between 74% and 95% of organizational AI initiatives fail to produce measurable returns, with people, process, and contextual judgment-not algorithms-accounting for the bulk of failure. Third, Hispanic SMBs sit at the worst intersection of these two facts: maximum access, minimum complementary infrastructure, and a mid-market tool ecosystem designed for English-speaking corporate buyers. The paper proposes the implementation-with-context framework, operationalized through the Agentes Para Tu Negocio model, and outlines directions for empirical validation. The argument is offered as a synthesis and theoretical contribution rather than primary empirical research.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6726479`

- title: Governing the Invisible: Strategic De-categorization, Value Capture, and the Safety-Wealth Trade-off in Gen-AI Startups
- authors: Chen Ye, Dequn Teng
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6726479
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

Research summary: AI uncertainty is a defining Grand Challenge requiring transparent innovation, yet governance introduces severe regulatory risks. Analyzing 846 high-substance generative AI startups (2018–2024), we find that firms disguising their AI capabilities behind functional labels raise 31% less capital. Despite this penalty, strategic invisibility has quadrupled since 2019, and the discount attenuated post-ChatGPT, indicating investors increasingly reward regulatory foresight. Eventually, this study diagnoses a critical market failure in AI governance: the safety-wealth trade-off creates an information asymmetry trap where the most capable ventures are incentivized to become invisible, weakening the transparent innovation required to address this Grand Challenge. Managerial summary: Generative AI is creating both extraordinary value and intense regulatory scrutiny. In this study of 846 high-substance Gen-AI startups, we show that ventures that scrub AI terminology from their public identity raise roughly 31% less capital than otherwise comparable peers. The cost is real, yet the share of ventures choosing invisibility has quadrupled since 2019, and investors have started treating invisibility as regulatory foresight rather than weakness. For founders, the safety-wealth trade-off is a quantifiable choice; for investors, about one in eighteen capable AI firms is hiding in plain sight; for policymakers, the most capable AI firms are increasingly exiting the public category on which governance relies, turning AI oversight into a lemons market.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6730423`

- title: Moral Rights, Sustainability and the Use of Generative AI in the Field of Research and Education: Unfolding a Complex Relationship
- authors: Christophe Geiger, Francesca Di Lazzaro
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6730423
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This article examines the evolving complex relationship between Generative Artificial Intelligence (Gen AI) and moral rights within copyright law, with particular attention to the use of copyrighted materials in research and education. Adopting a combined theoretical and conceptual methodology, it argues that Gen AI simultaneously advances and challenges the human-rights-based objectives underpinning copyright systems. While current copyright debates surrounding Gen AI have focused predominantly on economic rights, the implications for moral rights have until recently received comparatively limited scholarly and regulatory attention, despite their central importance to authorship, attribution and integrity. Gen AI has significant potential to facilitate access to information and personalized learning, thereby supporting the right to research, the right to education and participation in scientific progress under the international human rights framework and SDG 4. At the same time, it poses substantial risks to moral rights, including misattribution, distortion of authorship and threats to scientific integrity, particularly in research and educational contexts. The article argues that moral rights should become a central component of Gen AI governance through a transparent, human-centric copyright framework and effective remedial mechanisms capable of addressing moral-rights violations while fostering ethical innovation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6729902`

- title: Perceived Cognitive Assistance and the Construct-Proliferation Challenge: an integrated evaluative judgment that is never finished. Comparator Selection, Rival Scoping, and a Falsifiable Boundary Agenda
- authors: Dmitrii Gimmelberg, IVETA LUDVIGA
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6729902
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Perceived Cognitive Assistance (PCA) measures the retail trader's felt expansion of cognitive capability at the moment of a trading decision when a large language model (LLM) is available as a reasoning partner. This paper argues that PCA validation should be understood as a continuous process rather than a one-off psychometric event. The reason is not that the current evidence is absent, but that PCA sits in a fast-moving technological and theoretical neighbourhood where rival constructs may strengthen as LLM use, instruments, and user practices evolve. We reconstruct the evidence chain behind PCA's current boundary claim. Before the wider audit, PCA had already been tested against its proximal Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB) neighbours: perceived usefulness, perceived ease of use, trust in the LLM, and trading self-efficacy. A later construct-proliferation audit broadened the search surface through eight a priori domains, three AIassisted literature reports, human source verification, citation-integrity checking, and consolidation. This process identified seven neighbouring construct families: AI self-efficacy, cognitive absorption, cognitive load and workload, illusion of explanatory depth, AI dependence and over-reliance, tasktechnology fit, and explanation quality or perceived understanding. The audit concludes that these neighbours matter for interpretation and future monitoring, but currently available instruments do not provide matched-domain comparators that justify a new confirmatory-factor-analysis battery. The present claim is therefore bounded: PCA has defensible local construct distinctness, while its longerterm validity should remain open to continuous re-audit as better-matched instruments and longitudinal evidence become available.
