- title: Towards Autonomous Decision-Making: A Survey of Multimodal Medical Reasoning
- authors: Lujie Zheng, Huayi Wang, Yue Wang, Wenjie Deng, Chunlai Dong, Yuyang Xu, Danny  Ziyi Chen, Jian Wu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6732520
- keyword hits: agentic, foundation model, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Multimodal medical reasoning, a cornerstone of clinical decision-making, is essential for accurate diagnosis, treatment planning, and patient management. With the rapid progress of large language models (LLMs) and multimodal foundation models, enabling machines to reason over heterogeneous medical data has become a central challenge in medical artificial intelligence. This survey presents a comprehensive review of multimodal medical reasoning in the era of multimodal large language models. Specifically, we categorize existing methods into three stages: multimodal data understanding, which focuses on reasoning-oriented data curation and representation learning; multimodal knowledge utilization, which studies how external medical knowledge is constructed, retrieved, and integrated during inference; multimodal agentic analysis, where reasoning actively guides evidence exploration, hypothesis refinement, and action selection in dynamic clinical environments. These stages delineate a trajectory of increasing decision-making autonomy, offering a principled roadmap for advancing multimodal medical reasoning from model-centric approaches toward agentic and system-level clinical intelligence. To support reproducibility and ongoing research, we maintain an up-to-date repository of articles and resources at https://github.com/ZJUMAI/Multimodal-Medical-Reasoning.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6763360`

- title: Identifying Focal Constructs with Large Language Models: A Comparative Framework and Case in International Business
- authors: Xintong Wang, Xiaodie Pu, Alain Chong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6763360
- keyword hits: deepseek, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, transformer model

### abstract

The proliferation of textual data in international business (IB) research—from corporate disclosures to social media—has created unprecedented opportunities to measure latent constructs computationally. Yet IB scholarship lacks systematic guidance on selecting among rapidly evolving text-analysis methods, leaving researchers to rely on familiar but often limited approaches such as manual coding or dictionary-based techniques. This editorial addresses this gap by introducing a comparative framework that evaluates four natural language processing (NLP) approaches along the methodological frontier: classical machine learning, fine-tuned transformer models (BERT), locally deployed large language models (LLMs), and cloud-based API LLMs. We provide annotated data, open-source code, and practical trade-off discussions covering accuracy, computational cost, and scalability. To demonstrate the framework, we apply all four approaches to a substantive IB task—identifying nationalistic rhetoric in 47,298 annual reports of 5,519 Chinese listed firms from 2010 to 2023. Our results reveal that while traditional machine learning achieves strong baseline performance (91.6% accuracy), the fine-tuned FinBERT model offers the best balance of accuracy (93.5%), efficiency, and cost. Cloud-based LLMs such as DeepSeek-V3 attain the highest zero-shot accuracy (94.8%) but involve greater monetary expenditure. We validate the resulting nationalism construct through statistical benchmarking against established measures, an event study exploiting the 2018 U.S.–China trade war as an exogenous shock, and regression analyses linking firm-level nationalist rhetoric to cross-border M&A premiums and outward FDI patterns. Theoretically, we argue that text-derived construct measurement should be recognized as essential methodological infrastructure for IB, enabling scholars to operationalize previously intangible phenomena—such as ideology, cultural narratives, and geopolitical sentiment—and integrate them into theory testing and development.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6743358`

- title: Prompts and Algorithmic Agencies in AI Generalization: Problem Constitution in Using GPTs
- authors: Zaldy Carreon De Leon Jr, Michelle Carreon De Leon, Keith Manansala Macalintal
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6743358
- keyword hits: chatgpt, gemini, generative ai, large language model, large language models, prompt engineering

### abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI), particularly generative AI systems such as ChatGPT, Gemini, and similar large language models, has become increasingly embedded in educational contexts, reshaping how learners and educators access information, generate content, and construct knowledge. Central to these systems are prompts, which function as structured instructions guiding AI outputs, and algorithmic agency, which refers to the system's capacity to interpret inputs and autonomously generate responses that may influence human decision-making. Despite their growing use, concerns persist regarding AI generalization across tasks, domains, and culturally diverse contexts, as well as issues related to bias, misinformation, and overreliance on machine-generated outputs. This study examined the interplay of prompt structures, cultural and linguistic diversity, prompt literacy training, and policy frameworks in shaping the effective and ethical use of generative AI in education. A descriptive-correlational and quasi-experimental design was employed with 487 respondents. Findings revealed that prompt structures moderately influence AI output quality (TWM = 3.37), particularly in terms of relevance and coherence, while transferability remains limited. In ASEAN multilingual contexts, significant challenges were identified (TWM = 3.50), including cultural misalignment, language variability, and Western bias in AI responses. Furthermore, prompt literacy training produced a statistically significant improvement in knowledge, attitudes, and perceptions (p < 0.001), with a mean gain of +0.91, indicating enhanced AI literacy and ethical awareness among participants. Lastly, policy features demonstrated a strong positive relationship with awareness, adherence, and perceptions of responsible AI use (r = 0.69, p < 0.001), highlighting the importance of governance frameworks such as DepEd Memorandum No. 054 and the Data Privacy Act. The study concludes that effective integration of generative AI in education depends on the alignment of technical design (prompt engineering), contextual responsiveness (culture and language), human capacity development (training), and institutional governance (policy). These findings underscore the need for comprehensive strategies to ensure ethical, context-aware, and pedagogically sound use of AI in educational systems.

- title: From Regulatory Text to Structured Data: A Domain-Specific LLM Framework for Extracting Hydropower Operational Constraints
- authors: Faisal Bin Ashraf, Hong-Jun Yoon, Carly H. Hansen, Thomas  A. Ruggles, Debjani Singh, Esther S. Parish, Shih-Chieh Kao
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6949505
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llama, llm, llms, prompt engineering

### abstract

Operational constraints governing hydropower facilities are buried in lengthy regulatory documents with inconsistent formatting, yet no scalable method exists to extract them. Here, we present a regulatory interpretation framework that pairs domain knowledge guided prompt engineering with multi-criteria evidence ranking system, implemented using openly available large language models (LLMs) deployable on local infrastructure. The framework utilizes expert regulatory reasoning, distinguishing mandated minimums from operational capabilities, resolving conflicting values across document sections, and producing traceable, audit-ready outputs (extracted value, regulatory context, and verbatim evidence) and applies it to U.S. Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) licenses and other hydropower regulatory documents. We evaluated four open LLMs, Llama 3.2 3B, Llama 3 8B, GPT-OSS 20B, and Llama 3.3 70B using a representative set of 50 hydropower regulatory documents across ten operational variables. For simple structured variables (project names, locations, generation capacity), the 70B model achieved 90-98% accuracy, while complex variables requiring interpretation of scattered regulatory text showed substantially lower performance (0-43% for zero-shot extraction). For a complex variable tested (minimum flow requirements), a targeted multi-stage extraction approach improved accuracy from 43% (zero-shot) to 76% (targeted). Overall accuracy across all ten variables was 52.7% (Correct + Partially Correct), with substantial variation by variable complexity. Among the four models tested, only the 70B model was able to follow the complex regulatory reasoning prompts; smaller models failed systematically via false negatives and hallucinations rather than reduced accuracy. The framework shifts regulatory data extraction from document-level classification to fact-level extraction with audit trail, substantially reducing expert effort.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6985050`

- title: SESP: Semantic Entropy-based Self-play Security Alignment Mechanism for Large Language Models
- authors: Haodong Sun, Lifeng Cao, Zhengyuan Zhao, Du xuehui, Aodi Liu, Yongfei Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6985050
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, large language model, large language models, llama, llm, llms

### abstract

Periodic data updates are essential for large language models (LLMs) but introduce critical vulnerabilities to data poisoning. During iterative fine-tuning, adversarial threats can degrade safety guardrails, causing models to output compliant answers to harmful prompts they should inherently refuse. Traditional static defenses struggle with these dynamic threats and often incur a significant "alignment tax." To ensure safe updates, we propose the Semantic Entropy-based Self-play Security Alignment Mechanism (SESP). SESP formulates the alignment process as an iterative zero-sum game, utilizing Semantic Entropy as a dynamic payoff function to quantify and minimize the epistemic uncertainty of model responses. By continuously projecting outputs onto a historical safety manifold and competing against past versions of itself, SESP autonomously identifies and rectifies unsafe compliance introduced by poisoned data. Extensive empirical evaluations across diverse model architectures demonstrate that SESP effectively neutralizes broad-spectrum adversarial perturbations. It achieves an Attack Success Rate (ASR) reduction of up to 90.1% against threats including False Data Injection and Backdoors. Specifically, on Llama3-8B, SESP lowers ASR to 4.5% under injection attacks, outperforming advanced defenses like RCD and PEU. Simultaneously, SESP preserves general capabilities, maintaining a Benign Answer Rate (BAR) consistently above 98%, proving its ability to enhance security without the performance regression typical of conventional strategies.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6878281`

- title: KGReasoner: Knowledge Graph-Enhanced Retrieval and Reasoning with Large Language Models
- authors: Kairui Song, Yiming Tao, Zhihao Zhang, Chenyu Lai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6878281
- keyword hits: gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

Knowledge-intensive question answering requires both accurate retrieval of relevant evidence and coherent multi-hop reasoning over interconnected facts. While retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has emerged as a promising paradigm for grounding large language models (LLMs) with external knowledge, conventional RAG methods rely primarily on semantic similarity for retrieving isolated text chunks, which often results in disconnected fragments that lack the relational structure necessary for complex reasoning. Knowledge graphs (KGs) provide structured representations of entities and their relationships, offering a natural complement to text-based retrieval. In this paper, we propose KGReasoner, a unified framework that integrates knowledge graph-enhanced retrieval with structured reasoning for knowledge-intensive question answering. KGReasoner comprises three key components: (1) a KG-guided subgraph retrieval module that efficiently identifies query-relevant subgraphs using a lightweight MLPbased triple scorer with directional structural distance encoding and dynamic subgraph size adjustment; (2) a structured context organization module that transforms retrieved subgraphs into coherent reasoning paths by extracting relation chains from query entities to answer candidates, and augmenting these paths with associated text chunks to create hybrid structured-unstructured contexts; and (3) a weak-to-strong reasoning alignment module that improves LLM reasoning capability on structured contexts through debate-augmented training and ensemble verification. Extensive experiments on three benchmark datasets-WebQSP, ComplexWebQuestions, and HotpotQA-demonstrate that KGReasoner achieves state-of-theart performance, outperforming existing methods including SubgraphRAG with GPT-4o by 1.7 Hit@1 points on WebQSP, 2.3 Hit@1 points on CWQ, and 2.3 EM points on HotpotQA. Ablation studies confirm the contribution of each component, and analysis shows that the improvements are particularly significant for complex multi-hop reasoning questions.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.27023v1`

- title: Just how sure are you? Improving Verbalized Uncertainty Calibration in Medical VQA
- authors: Eren Senoglu, Federico Toschi, Nicolo Brunello, Andrea Sassella, Mark James Carman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-25
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27023v1
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms, prompting, qwen

### abstract

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) applied to Medical Visual Question Answering (VQA) tend to produce overconfident outputs regardless of actual correctness, and existing verbalized confidence calibration methods, developed primarily for text only LLMs, do not account for the multimodal nature of medical image understanding. This work proposes a training based framework that finetunes MLLMs to improve their calibration using a composite loss function combining a Brier style calibration term, an anchor regularizer that prevents confidence collapse toward extreme values, a contrastive image text alignment term, and a KL based model stabilization term. The alignment signal is derived from a $2 \times 2$ factorial perturbation design that crosses image presence with text integrity, probing the reliance of the model on visual modality input versus language priors. Finally, a top K KL divergence regularizer is used to protect the answering ability of the model during finetuning. Across three Medical VQA benchmarks and two architectures (MedGemma 4B IT and Qwen2 VL 7B Instruct), our method reduces calibration error by 60% or more, and improves discrimination by 26% or more, while preserving predictive accuracy. On average across benchmarks, the technique outperforms prompting based, sampling based, and training based approaches, and ablation experiments confirm that each component of the loss function is indeed necessary for improving the calibration. All code for the experiments is publicly available.
