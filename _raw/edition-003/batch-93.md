### abstract

Employee retention prediction has become a strategic priority in Human Resource Management (HRM), particularly in highly competitive sectors. ### abstract

This study maps how research on artificial intelligence (AI) for innovation has evolved across two periods: the pre-generative-AI era (2010–2022) and the post-2023 diffusion of large language models. Using the Web of Science Core Collection, a curated corpus in business, management, and economics was analyzed with three complementary science-mapping techniques—co-citation, keyword co-occurrence, and bibliographic coupling—implemented in VOSviewer. The comparison reveals five robust research streams: (1) strategy and economics of AI as a general-purpose technology; (2) digital transformation and dynamic capabilities; (3) behavioral, services, and market perspectives on adoption and customer experience; (4) organization/HRM and innovation management, including human–AI teaming and trust; and (5) value creation and performance via capability bundles (data, technology, skills, and governance). These results help scholars and decision-makers connect AI capabilities to innovation outcomes in the generative-AI era.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6831599`

- title: From Curiosity to Healthcare: Understanding Cognitive Profiles and Adoption Pathways of Large Language Models for Healthcare
- authors: Rita Phillips
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6831599
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly integrated into healthcare-related information seeking and patient support. However, little is known about the psychological, social and sociodemographic mechanisms underlying their adoption. The present study investigated healthcare-related LLM use by integrating Technology Acceptance Model 3 (TAM3) constructs, cognitive benefit–risk perception profiles and broader social determinants. A convenience sample of 507 participants completed an online survey assessing healthcare-related LLM use, TAM3 subdimensions and anticipated benefits and concerns. Hierarchical cluster analysis identified four user profiles, which were subsequently examined using baseline and multi-group structural equation modelling. The findings demonstrated that perceived usefulness, behavioural intention and perceived enjoyment were central predictors of healthcare-related LLM use. Migration background and technological habituation also significantly contributed to adoption. Furthermore, structural adoption pathways varied substantially across user profiles, highlighting heterogeneous patterns of AI acceptance. The findings emphasize that healthcare-related LLM adoption represents a multidimensional process shaped by affective evaluations, prior technological experience and socially embedded interpretations of benefits and risks.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6850138`

- title: EconLogic-BSQA: A Closed-World Benchmark for Evaluating Belief Sufficiency and Decision Commitment in Large Language Models
- authors: Xinhai Sun
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6850138
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly evaluated for business and economic reasoning, yet many benchmarks reward plausible answers without testing whether a model has sufficient evidence to justify a decision. This makes it difficult to distinguish context-grounded reasoning from pretrained knowledge, leakage, or unsupported commitment. We introduce EconLogic-BSQA, a closed-world benchmark for evaluating belief sufficiency, abstention, and decision commitment in business settings. Derived from EconLogicQA, the benchmark transforms sequential economic event chains into fictional business micro-worlds governed by explicit local rules, controlled evidence conditions, and verifiable decision constraints. EconLogic-BSQA contains 580 curated instances spanning full-context, missing-evidence, missing-rule, ambiguous-evidence, counterintuitive-rule, and sequential-reveal conditions. Each instance asks whether the available local information is sufficient to proceed, reject, request more information, or defer. Experiments with seven contemporary LLMs show that strong models still overcommit under incomplete evidence and often rely on external business priors instead of prompt-contained rules. These results show that exact-match accuracy alone is insufficient for trustworthy commercial reasoning evaluation and motivate complementary metrics such as Unsupported Commitment Rate (UCR), Appropriate Deferral Rate (ADR), Verifiable Decision Rate (VDR), and Global Knowledge Leakage Rate (GKLR). EconLogic-BSQA provides a benchmark framework for evaluating context-grounded, uncertainty-aware, and leakage-resistant commercial AI systems.

- title: LLMs for the Unknown: Generalizing Clinical Entity Linking to Unseen Mentions and Concepts
- authors: Javier Castell-Díaz, Jesualdo  Tomás Fernández Breis, Catalina Martínez-Costa
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6854334

### abstract

Medical entity linking is essential for structuring clinical narratives, allowing to normalize clinical mentions to standardized terminologies. Despite recent advances based on pretrained language models and reranking strategies, generalization to unseen mentions and unseen concepts remains a major challenge. In particular, most retrieval-based pipelines often rely on selecting the top-ranked candidate after reranking, without fully exploiting the contextual information of the clinical span. In this work, we investigate how large language models can be incorporated into a retrieval and reranking pipeline to improve final candidate selection. The proposed pipeline combines bi-encoder retrieval, cross-encoder reranking, and a large language model that selects the final concept from the reranked candidates by leveraging the full textual context of the mention, including both sentencelevel and section-level information. Our pipeline was evaluated on well-known, publicly available clinical entity linking benchmarks that cover different clinical domains: an English dataset that focuses on clinical findings, procedures, and body structures (SNOMED CT Entity Linking Challenge); and three Spanish datasets that include diseases (DisTEMIST), procedures (MedProcNER), and symptoms (SympTEMIST). Our results show that integrating an LLM as part of the pipeline improves the generalization capabilities of the approach. Our approach shows improvements across the various datasets tested, obtaining an increase in performance of up to 15% compared to state of the art methods.

- title: Position: Prioritize Identifying Structure, Not Complex Models, for Scientific Discovery
- authors: Tyler H. McCormick
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-30
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02632v1
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Modern Machine Learning (ML) and Artificial Intelligence (AI) models, especially large language models (LLMs), are increasingly used to generate scientific hypotheses and mechanistic explanations from observational data. This position paper argues that in the high-dimensional proxy regimes where modern ML excels, mechanistic learning is generically underdetermined: many incompatible mechanisms induce essentially the same observational relationships on the support of the data, so predictive success and coherent explanations are insufficient evidence of mechanism discovery. This underdetermination becomes uniquely hazardous with large language models (LLMs), which tend to collapse large equivalence classes of explanations into a single fluent narrative. This paper proposes concrete standards for ``mechanistic ML,'' and argues these norms are necessary if LLM-centered workflows are to support science rather than merely simulate it.
