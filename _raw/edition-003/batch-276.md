## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6664039`

- title: Invisible Agents, Visible Harm: Agentic AI, Accountability Erosion, and the Societal Trust Crisis
- authors: Albert Adusei Brobbey
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6664039
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

Agentic artificial intelligence systems are no longer confined to organizational back offices. By 2026, autonomous AI agents were making decisions about citizens' loan applications, managing government service workflows, triaging patient queues, and moderating the content billions of people consume. Each of these deployments shares a common structural feature: when an agent causes harm, there is no legible human for the affected person to hold responsible. This paper argues that the resulting accountability gap, particularly in high-stakes, high-scale deployment contexts, is not merely an organizational governance failure. It is a societal trust problem with measurable economic, democratic, and institutional consequences that organizational governance frameworks alone are insufficient to address. This is a theory-building paper grounded in institutional trust theory, democratic accountability theory, and socio-technical systems perspectives. It makes three original contributions. First, it introduces the concept of societal accountability erosion, defined as the structural degradation of society's capacity to identify, challenge, and obtain redress from human actors responsible for AIdriven harm, distinguishing it from organizational-level accountability gaps and from related concepts including algorithmic accountability failure and democratic deficit. Second, it theorizes four transmission mechanisms through which accountability opacity propagates from the organizational level to the societal level: consequence diffusion, redress opacity, legitimacy displacement, and epistemic asymmetry. Each mechanism is theorized as an emergent, context-dependent dynamic most visible under conditions of high deployment scale and high decision consequentiality. Third, the paper proposes a societal governance agenda for policymakers, civil society actors, and regulatory bodies that complements, rather than substitutes for, the organizational governance agenda addressed in prior work. The paper argues that the institutional trust literature's central insight, that citizens trust institutions rather than technologies, and that institutional trust depends on the perceived availability of accountability, creates a specific and urgent vulnerability in the agentic AI era. When institutions deploy systems that make consequential decisions about citizens but cannot produce a legible human accountable for those decisions, they are eroding the foundational conditions for the public trust on which institutional legitimacy depends. The theoretical mechanisms proposed here are offered as propositions for empirical testing rather than as established findings.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6723799`

- title: Dependency Exposure: A Six-Axis Loss Model for AI Service Disruption
- authors: Kenji Yamada
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-06
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6723799
- keyword hits: gpt-4

### abstract

When AI dependency is severed, what is lost? Existing research bifurcates into upstream analyses of why dependency forms and downstream calculations of economic downtime costs, leaving the multi-dimensional structure of losses exposed at severance largely undescribed. This paper introduces the concept of dependency exposure and proposes a Six-Axis Loss Model that describes losses across six structurally independent axes: economic, psychological, cognitive, ontological, emergentive, and sovereign loss. Three severance types—temporary interruption, persona dissolution, and permanent termination—are shown to produce qualitatively distinct dominant loss axes. Inter-axis influence relationships are structured as a Hexagonal Interaction Matrix, with each cell assigned an epistemic confidence colour (white for literature-confirmed, blue for empirically testable, dark blue for conceptually inferred), revealing that emergence-related cells are uniformly unexplored. The fifth axis, emergentive loss—intellectual products that could have emerged through human–AI co-creation but never did—is shown to be unmeasurable in principle, an epistemological constraint existing loss frameworks structurally fail to register. The concept of asymmetric regression—whereby post-severance productivity falls not merely to but below pre-adoption levels—is introduced, challenging the linear assumptions of existing downtime cost models. Empirical cases—GPT-4o retirement, Starlink/Crimea denial, country-level access blocking—demonstrate applicability across scales from individual to nation-state. The framework contributes a critical approach to AI governance by reframing service continuity as multi-dimensional structural exposure rather than a technical problem.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6729903`

- title: Discovering YC-Style Pitch Signals from Startup Demo Transcripts with LLM-Based Feature Extraction
- authors: Clément FREREBEAU, Marc HABIB
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6729903
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

This paper presents an empirical hybrid natural language processing pipeline for analyzing startup demo transcripts and extracting the narrative, product, and traction signals that make a pitch sound "YC-style." Rather than framing the task as accepted-versus-rejected classification, the proposed system treats each transcript as a source of structured communication signals. A large language model extracts interpretable transcript-only features such as opening structure, one-sentence pitch, target customer, problem, solution, demo steps, magic moment, traction claim, clarity, specificity, demo density, problem intensity, differentiation, compression, confidence, and overall YC-style pitch quality. A second representation layer embeds full transcripts using sentence-transformer embeddings, and an unsupervised clustering stage identifies recurring pitch archetypes. On a dataset of 63 YC-selected company demo transcripts, the analysis finds that solution-first openings are the dominant structure (63.5%), that most transcripts include a problem, solution, and demo, but that explicit traction or proof appears in only 41.3% of transcripts. The most common discovered archetype is AI Application, while the largest semantic cluster corresponds to B2B Automation. The goal is not to infer Y Combinator's internal admissions criteria, but to provide an interpretable, transcript-grounded framework for studying how selected startup demos communicate product value.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6680779`

- title: Fractional Calculus in Modern Machine Learning Optimizers: A Systematic Review of Algorithms, Convergence, and Open Frontiers
- authors: Arsh Khan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6680779
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

The dominant optimizers in modern machine learning, stochastic gradient descent (SGD), Adam, and their variants, share a fundamental assumption: the most useful information for a parameter update lives in the present gradient or a short exponential window of recent gradients. Fractional calculus offers a principled way to question this assumption. By substituting non-integer-order derivatives for their integer-order counterparts, fractional gradient methods encode gradient histories of arbitrary length into each update through a power-law memory kernel. Over roughly the past decade, a growing body of work has explored this idea, reporting faster convergence on benchmark tasks, better robustness to noisy gradients, and improved avoidance of local minima in certain regimes. The field has a significant structural problem, however. Empirical results are almost entirely confined to small convolutional networks trained on MNIST and CIFAR-10. Convergence guarantees for non-convex objectives have only recently appeared. And, as of early 2026, no published work has evaluated fractional optimizers on large-scale modern architectures, whether Transformers, diffusion models, or large language models. This paper provides a systematic review of fractional gradient descent methods from 2017 through early 2026. We cover the three derivative definitions in common use (Riemann-Liouville, Caputo, and Grünwald-Letnikov), survey the algorithmic landscape, synthesize convergence theory, and critically examine what the empirical record does and does not establish. The central argument is that fractional optimization is mathematically well-motivated and empirically promising at small scale, but has not been tested in the settings where contemporary machine learning actually operates.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6730343`

- title: Provenance as Substrate: A Cryptographic Identifier Framework for Music Rights and Royalty Infrastructure
- authors: Juan Lentino
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6730343
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The music industry's identifier infrastructure is failing under loads it was never designed for. The International Standard Recording Code (ISRC), the International Standard Musical Work Code (ISWC), and the identity codes that bind them were built as administrative artifacts, reconciled through fuzzy matching across collection societies that do not share schemas. The Mechanical Licensing Collective alone holds hundreds of millions of dollars in unmatched royalties, most of it unclaimed because identifiers do not align across registries. Generative artificial intelligence compounds the problem: new tracks enter distribution at over one hundred thousand a day, and the matching infrastructure cannot keep up. In an earlier paper I argued that detection-based responses to AI-generated music are structurally falsifiable and that cryptographic provenance offers the only verification model that is not. This paper extends that argument from authorship verification to identifier infrastructure. I propose that the same cryptographic primitive used to verify provenance can serve as the substrate for music identification itself: a self-issuing, collision-resistant, signed identifier that binds authorship and identification in a single operation. The result is not a replacement for ISRC or ISWC but a substrate beneath them, capable of resolving legacy identifiers as aliases while making future works identifiable at the moment of creation rather than at the moment of registration. This paper describes the architecture, the registry federation model, the backward-compatibility layer for legacy catalog, and the standards-body adoption path, and surfaces open implementation questions including signature scheme selection, key management, and ownership transfer.
