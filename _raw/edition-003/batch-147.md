## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6697398`

- title: From Behavioral Traces to Actionable Guidelines: A Comprehensive Survey of Representation, Optimization, and Verifiable Generation in Intelligent Systems
- authors: Murad  Mustafa Badarna, Loai AbdAllah
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6697398
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Behavioral traces are increasingly captured across enterprise systems, user interfaces, clinical platforms, and embodied environments, creating new opportunities for intelligent systems to move beyond descriptive and predictive analytics toward actionable decision support. However, the relevant literature remains fragmented across process mining, user behavior mining, predictive and prescriptive monitoring, imitation learning, offline reinforcement learning, and large language model (LLM)-based generation.This survey synthesizes these disconnected research streams through a unified framework organized into four stages: behavior representation and abstraction, behavior understanding, behavior optimization, and guideline generation. We define a guideline as a structured, human-readable, and verifiable artifact that specifies applicability conditions, ordered or conditional actions, exceptions, expected effects, and evidence links.Based on this conceptualization, we review the principal methodological families contributing to each stage, highlighting their strengths, limitations, and interactions. We identify the key technical gap as the absence of end-to-end approaches that transform behavioral traces into deployable, evidence-grounded guidance artifacts.We further propose a research agenda centered on causal validity, communicability, robustness under distribution shift, and verification mechanisms for generated guidance (Pearl, 2009; Imbens & Rubin, 2015). The central argument of this survey is that the next generation of intelligent decision-support systems must not only learn predictive or optimal behavior, but also generate structured, auditable, and operationally actionable guidelines grounded in empirical evidence and validated through explicit verification processes.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6692385`

- title: Two-Stage AI Adoption in Technology-Taker Economies: Platform Capture, Infrastructure, and Firm Constraints in Latin America
- authors: Carolina Curvale
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6692385
- keyword hits: gemini, generative ai

### abstract

Consumer-side generative AI encounter in Latin America is shaped by institutional and infrastructural channels specific to technology-taker economies, but not those the standard political-economy literature predicts. This paper develops a two-stage framework, distinguishing consumer-encounter from firm-expectation, with distinct binding constraints at each stage. Stage-1 analysis draws on a weekly Google Trends panel of generative AI search interest across seven platforms for nineteen Latin American countries (2020–2024), cross-validated against Cloudflare Radar and Similarweb. One robust finding emerges: the combination of political stability with weak state capacity predicts Gemini's market share through an ecosystem-default mechanism concentrated in low-formal-sector, thinly engaged consumer markets (joint institutional F(3,12) = 5.21, p = 0.016; robust to bootstrap inference and outlier exclusion). Two additional patterns — internet penetration individually predicting encounter velocity (standardized β = 0.66, p = 0.099) and regulatory quality individually correlating with platform diversification — are suggestive individual-coefficient findings not clearing joint-family significance. Stage-2 analysis draws on employer-expectation data from the WEF Future of Jobs 2025 survey for Argentina, Brazil, Colombia, and Mexico, revealing that firm-side constraints are operational-regulatory and human-capital rather than macro-institutional. Each stage falls outside the scope of the standard institutions-and-investment literature in a different way: the real-options mechanism requires irreversible commitment under political uncertainty, a condition consumer-side adoption of zero-marginal-cost products does not satisfy and firm-side adoption satisfies only partially. The paper develops evidence consistent with a scope limitation on the institutions-and-investment literature.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6696358`

- title: The AI-Augmented Product Manager: How Generative AI Is Reshaping Product Discovery, Strategy, and Execution
- authors: Muhammad Shah
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6696358
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence is changing the role of the product manager. Product managers have traditionally worked at the intersection of customer needs, business goals, technical feasibility, and cross-functional execution. That role remains important, but the work inside the role is changing. Tasks that once required significant manual effort, including customer research synthesis, competitive analysis, product documentation, roadmap preparation, prototype exploration, and stakeholder communication, can now be accelerated through AI-assisted workflows. This paper examines how generative AI is reshaping product management as a knowledge-work function. It argues that AI does not remove the need for product judgment. Instead, it changes where product managers create value. The emerging product manager must become stronger at problem framing, evidence evaluation, strategic prioritization, responsible experimentation, stakeholder alignment, and decision accountability. AI can support analysis, ideation, and execution, but it cannot own the product outcome. Human product leadership remains essential because product management requires judgment under uncertainty, ethical trade-offs, organizational alignment, and accountability for market and user impact. The paper proposes a practical framework for the AI-augmented product manager across five domains: discovery, strategy, execution, communication, and governance. It also identifies risks, including overreliance on AI-generated outputs, shallow validation, biased synthesis, weakened customer empathy, documentation inflation, and unclear accountability. The paper concludes that generative AI will not make product managers obsolete. It will raise the standard for product managers who can combine AI fluency with strong human judgment.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6605358`

- title: AI-Driven Cloud Security Frameworks for SMEs
- authors: Jaymeet Miteshkumar Patel
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6605358
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence, llm

### abstract

Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) are moving critical workloads, data, development pipelines, and customer-facing services into public and hybrid cloud environments at the same time that the cyber threat landscape is being reshaped by identity-centric attacks, software supply chain compromise, and the rapid operationalization of generative artificial intelligence. This paper presents a broad, coverage-oriented review of AI-driven cloud security frameworks for SMEs from 2023 to March 2026. Rather than testing a single hypothesis, the study organizes the current landscape around SME constraints, cloud-native attack surfaces, recent regulatory developments, and the practical role of artificial intelligence in threat detection, triage, prioritization, response, and governance. Recent official guidance and peer-reviewed literature show that SMEs remain disproportionately affected by skills shortages, fragmented tooling, and compliance burden, while attackers continue to exploit weak credentials, misconfigurations, exposed assets, and human trust. At the same time, AI-enabled security operations are becoming more viable for smaller organizations through managed telemetry pipelines, cloud-native application protection platforms, anomaly detection, and LLM-assisted security workflows. Based on the review, the paper proposes a vendor-neutral reference framework for SMEs built on five layers: telemetry and asset visibility, AI analytics, policy and access control, orchestrated response, and governance and assurance. The paper also examines implementation barriers including model drift, privacy risk, cost control, false positives, data sovereignty, and adversarial abuse of AI systems. Finally, it identifies research opportunities in real-time virtual machine protection, explainable risk scoring, cross-cloud identity graphing, privacy-preserving analytics, and blockchain-assisted evidence trails. The paper concludes that AI can materially improve SME cloud security when deployed as a disciplined augmentation layer inside zero-trust, cloud-native, and compliance-aware architectures rather than as a standalone replacement for security fundamentals.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6709360`

- title: Autonomous, Agentic and Generative AI Systems -The Future of Business Technology: Triumph of Human Augmentation or Harbinger of Human Diminishment? A Critical Examination (2025-2035)
- authors: Misbah Ul Islam, Wang Fang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6709360
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent, generative ai

### abstract

The rapid maturation of Agentic AI autonomous, goal-directed systems capable of multi-step reasoning, adaptive planning, tool integration, and independent execution and Generative AI, which creates novel content, code, designs, and insights, is fundamentally reshaping business technology. This article critically examines whether these autonomous systems represent a triumph of human augmentation delivering unprecedented productivity, innovation, and cognitive liberation or a defeat for human agency, through risks of skill atrophy, ethical erosion, accountability diffusion, and power concentration. Drawing on the most recent 2025-mid-2026 empirical evidence from Gartner, Stanford HAI, McKinsey, Deloitte, ABI Research, and others, the analysis reveals explosive growth alongside significant implementation challenges. Key data points underscore the momentum: Gartner projects that 40% of enterprise applications will feature task-specific AI agents by the end of 2026 (up from <5% in 2025), while the agentic AI market expands from approximately $7.6 billion in 2025 to $10.8 billion in 2026, with longer-term forecasts reaching $139-196 billion by 2034 (Gartner, 2025a; Fortune Business Insights, 2025). Generative AI spending is anticipated to reach $644 billion globally in 2025, a 76.4% increase yearover-year (Gartner, 2025c). Stanford HAI's 2025 AI Index Report documents record private investment ($109.1 billion in the US alone in 2024) and enterprise adoption rising to 78% of organizations (Stanford HAI, 2025). Through a systematic integrative review methodology and critical constructivist lens informed by philosophy of technology and STS, the article evaluates productivity gains (20-40% in general knowledge work; hundreds of percent in targeted processes), real-world deployments (e.g., Salesforce Agentforce, JPMorgan, Walmart), governance deficits (only ~20% of firms have mature frameworks per Deloitte, 2026), ethical risks, and workforce implications. Findings indicate outcomes are conditional: strong governance, human-centered design, and ethical stewardship can steer toward augmentation and flourishing; inadequate attention risks diminishment and unintended consequences. The article concludes with strategic imperatives for responsible integration, arguing that autonomous systems will ultimately reflect the values and choices embedded within them. By prioritizing hybrid human-AI symbiosis and proactive governance, businesses can harness these technologies as a genuine win for humanity rather than a subtle defeat. This balanced, forwardlooking analysis contributes to critical contemporary debates on the future of intelligent technologies in business.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6600538`

- title: The Autonomous Agentic Store: Architecture, Safety Requirements, and a Constitutional AI Deployment Framework for Fully Staffless Physical Retail
- authors: Sudhir Vissa
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6600538
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent, llm

### abstract

This paper proposes and architecturally defines the Autonomous Agentic Store: a physical retail and hospitality environment in which every customer interaction, operational function, and back-office business process is orchestrated by LLM agents, with no human staff required on premises during standard operations. Two structural forces make 2026 the deployment window: frontier LLM capability has crossed the behavioral threshold required for retail floor operation, and a 31% increase in US retail labor costs since 2019 makes human-staffed retail economically unsustainable at existing margins. The central thesis distinguishes this from prior automated retail: the AI agent is not a layer beneath a human brand — it is the brand. The paper presents a six-layer store intelligence stack; scope-bounded agent role and escalation contracts for eight named agents; a two-phase physical actuation model transitioning from agent-directed human task workers to narrow-scope robotic actuators on existing infrastructure; a full business intelligence stack replacing inventory management, omnichannel commerce, financial management, and strategic growth functions; an after-hours autonomous operations framework; and a self-sustaining economic model demonstrating 8.3-month payback with annual operating cost dropping to $42,100 at Month 30 against a $227,475 human-staffed baseline. The author holds 42 patent grants (539 citations, h-index 11) in ML, edge intelligence, IoT, and context-aware systems directly applicable to the proposed architecture.
