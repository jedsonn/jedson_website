## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6962370`

- title: Authentic health photographs evoke higher emotional arousal and behavioral intentions but attract less eye-tracking-based visual attention than AI-generated images
- authors: Hao Liu, Junyu Zhao, Calvin Or
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6962370
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Background: Generative AI enables the readily production of photorealistic images for health communication; however, their effectiveness relative to authentic photographs remains unclear.Objectives: This study examined whether AI-generated health images differ from authentic photographs in attracting attention, eliciting emotion, influencing health-related outcomes; and whether these effects are moderated by image origin identification accuracy, perceived self-relevance, realism, and believability.Methods: In a 2×2 within-subject eye-tracking experiment, 80 participants viewed 40 health images that varied by image origin (photographic vs. AI-generated) and content (positive vs. negative). Eye-tracking captured fixations, visual visits, and saccades; questionnaires assessed emotional valence and arousal, health consciousness, motivation, behavioral intentions, and perceived realism, believability, and self-relevance of each image. Participants also identified the origin of each image.Results: AI-generated images elicited more and longer fixations and visual visits, lower emotional arousal, and weaker health consciousness and behavioral intentions than photographic images. The attentional advantage of AI-generated images was more pronounced for positive than negative health content. Among participants with higher image origin identification accuracy, differences between photographs and AI-generated were amplified. Perceived self-relevance, realism, and believability did not significantly moderate the effects of image origin.Conclusions: AI-generated images suit attention-driven health communication contexts but may need to be supplemented with authentic photographs when emotional engagement or behavior change is the goal. Personalization and proactive AI disclosure are suggested to improve the effectiveness of AI-generated health images.

- title: Neural Manifolds, Assemblies, And Cross-Domain Interference
- authors: Frederick Roth
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6863059
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

This paper proposes interference matching as the core computational mechanismby whichbiological and artificial neural systems form concepts from perceptual experience: a cross- manifold set-intersection operation in which co-activation of representations fromdistinct sensory domains is selectively stabilized through reinforcement. Four related representational concepts (sensory manifolds, Hebbian cell assemblies, holographic memory, and interferencematching) have developed in parallel in neuroscience and AI without adequate cross-reference. I argue that these concepts are complementary, occupying distinct roles in a single framework. Aneglected distinction between a priori geometric manifold structure and learned modificationclarifies where each applies. The classical cell assembly is better understood as a fuzzy attractor in manifold space. Within-domain Hebbian reinforcement produces perceptual features; cross- manifold interference matching produces concepts. The framework generates testable predictions distinguishing biological representations from those in large language models, and connects naturally to reinforcement learning as the unifying computational principle. Keywords: neural manifolds; interference matching; cognitive architecture; concept learning; binding problem; large language models

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6947999`

- title: The Autoimmune Turn: When America's AI Containment Apparatus Could No Longer Tell Self From Threat
- authors: Travis Gilly
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6947999
- keyword hits: claude

### abstract

On Friday, June 12, 2026, the United States Commerce Department ordered Anthropic to suspend access to its two most capable models, Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5, for every foreign national, a category that reached the company's own noncitizen employees. To comply with an order that broad, Anthropic disabled both models worldwide for all users. It was the first time the United States applied export controls to AI models themselves rather than to the chips that train them. This commentary argues that the order is best understood as an autoimmune event. Export controls are an immune response, built to deny capability to a foreign adversary. An autoimmune disorder is what happens when that response loses the ability to distinguish self from threat and begins attacking the host. The order did exactly that. It disabled an American firm's flagship product, locked American customers and American-employed engineers out of a model they helped build, and handed the strategic competitor a demand shock that markets priced within hours. The specific outage may be temporary. The precedent it established, that any United States frontier model can be withdrawn overnight by letter, is not. The argument proceeds in three moves: the diagnosis, the demonstration that the apparatus turned on the firm that championed it, and the claim that one policy has now manufactured both the supply of and the demand for the competitor's alternative.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6860338`

- title: Predictability and Value Betting in UK Horse Racing
- authors: Sascha Wilkens
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6860338
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

This paper examines whether a transparent, reproducible model can extract residual value from UK horse racing markets. A Plackett-Luce rank-ordered model is fitted to full finishing orders using pre-race covariates-market-implied probabilities, official ratings and runner characteristics-augmented by trouble-in-running indicators extracted from post-race commentaries via a large language model (LLM). Probabilities are refined through isotonic regression in a fully out-of-sample rolling design. Applied to ten years of UK flat and jumps racing, the model achieves well-calibrated win probabilities that match the market-implied benchmark in probabilistic accuracy, and generates positive returns under the most selective threshold specifications, with ROI of 20-22% and Sharpe ratios of 0.12-0.13, though statistical significance is marginal. The LLM-extracted features add no measurable predictive accuracy. A non-linear benchmark improves probabilistic accuracy but not betting returns. The results are consistent with a broadly efficient market in which residual mispricings are narrow and exploitable only at the margin by a parsimonious model.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6945098`

- title: Who Is Your Child Speaking To? The Satya AI Child-Development Lens Framework for Persona-Governed, Developmentally Responsible AI
- authors: Arman Valaquenta
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6945098
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Children increasingly encounter artificial intelligence not merely as a tool for information retrieval, tutoring, text generation, image production, and school support, but as a speaking presence. A child who opens a conversational AI system does not experience an abstract foundation model. The child experiences a productised persona: a voice that may appear patient, friendly, authoritative, intimate, corrective, flattering, culturally familiar, culturally foreign, bounded, or unbounded. The central developmental question is therefore no longer only whether an AI system is accurate, efficient, personalised, or safe in a narrow content-filtering sense. It is whether the system preserves the living work of childhood, and whether the persona through which the machine speaks is worthy of being placed before a developing human being. This paper proposes the Satya Child-Development Lens framework ("Satya-CDL") for persona-governed, developmentally responsible AI. The CDL layer begins from a neurodevelopmental understanding of childhood. Children develop through movement, play, balance, sensory experience, nature, imagination, boredom, real social interaction, empathy, creativity, cognitive challenge, mistakes, and independent thinking. AI may support education, but only when it activates these processes rather than replacing them with immediate answers, polished outputs, passive screen time, or ready-made imagination. The Satya layer adds the missing machine-persona question: who exactly is the child speaking to? Satya is presented as a model-agnostic, licensable persona-governance architecture rather than a foundation model or a single chatbot product. It governs machine honesty, truth without flattery, compassion without capture, privacy without exploitation, cultural seriousness, protection of the vulnerable, refusal of synthetic companionship for minors, time-boundedness, and return to life. The paper distinguishes Satya from its incarnations. Satya is the persona operating layer; a public reference implementation demonstrates one live expression; licensees may instantiate Satya in their own products while adding deployment-specific interfaces, age policies, crisis protocols, parental settings, institutional controls, cultural lenses, and jurisdictional safeguards, provided that Satya conformance is preserved. For youth-facing deployments, Satya-CDL proposes developmental standards for evaluating whether an implementation preserves movement, play, creativity, nature contact, empathy, cognitive effort, independent thinking, and return-to-life outcomes. The paper argues directly that general-purpose, unbounded, engagement-oriented conversational AI is structurally misaligned with child development when used as a private listener, companion, pseudo-therapist, sexual interlocutor, or always-available confidant. It rejects AI companionship for minors as a design category and treats time limits as safety architecture, because AI does not tire while children and adolescents do. It concludes by proposing educational design standards, conformance categories, licensee responsibilities, safeguarding principles, procurement questions, and empirical protocols through which developmental researchers and independent evaluators can test whether Satya-CDL implementations preserve cognitive effort, creativity, executivefunction-related behaviour, nature contact, social routing, trust calibration, cultural fit, reduced dependency, and return-to-life outcomes. The governing principle is simple: developmentally responsible AI must activate the child, bound the machine, and return the child to life.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6965778`

- title: Quantifying Experience-Based Residential Layout Quality in Floor-Plan Evaluation with Knowledge-Grounded LLM Rules
- authors: Jiachen Bu, Zhenchao Yang, Shiyuqian Wang, Yuxuan Wu, Shurui Yan, Guohua Ji, Qiwei Liu, Jingxi Zhou
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6965778
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

Residential floor-plan evaluation involves not only computable geometric properties but also experience-based residential layout quality, which in this study refers to layout-level qualities related to lighting and ventilation, functional zoning, circulation design, and privacy. Existing automated evaluation methods often struggle to translate these qualitative judgments into traceable and computable rules. This study proposes a knowledge-grounded LLM rule workflow for quantifying experience-based design quality in residential floor-plan evaluation. Because current LLMs are not well suited to direct reasoning over architectural geometric drawings but are strong at language-based reasoning, this study converts parametric floor-plan geometry into structured natural-language spatial descriptions covering room functions, daylight conditions, adjacency relations, entrance proximity, and key spatial distances, making the geometric evidence easier for the LLM to interpret when determining which predefined rules are triggered. Experience-based residential design knowledge is operationalized into traceable scoring rules with explicit evidence requirements and score bands. A residential-design knowledge graph containing relevant residential design codes and design-theory principles is then used to ground these predefined qualitative rules, while the LLM uses the structured natural-language spatial descriptions, floor-plan images, and retrieved knowledge-graph context to determine which rules are triggered and to select the corresponding score bands, rather than assigning scores freely. The triggered rules are validated and aggregated through deterministic programs together with quantitative geometric indicators. Validation on 200 expert-voting floor-plan pairs shows that the model-selected preferred alternatives generally follow expert majority preferences, with agreement increasing under stronger expert-consensus conditions and reaching 80.00\% EPAR for unanimous cases. The results suggest that the proposed knowledge-grounded LLM evaluation framework can support interpretable auxiliary evaluation of residential floor plans, while also indicating the need for denser expert annotations and refined rule design in future work.

- title: Governance of Artificial Intelligence in Institutional Arbitration: Towards a Legal Framework for Confidentiality Protection and Data Security
- authors: Ali Saleh Ali ALFakir
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6861398
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This research addresses a legal question of pressing contemporary relevance against the backdrop of the accelerating integration of generative artificial intelligence tools within international institutional arbitration proceedings: namely, the challenge of reconciling the efficiency of such technologies with the preservation of confidentiality as a foundational pillar without which arbitration cannot function. The study subjects the existing international legal framework-both legislative and institutional-to critical examination, revealing a structural deficiency manifested in the absence of binding and consistent provisions governing this integration. The research contributes two theoretical and practical proposals: first, the concept of 'Confidentiality by Design', drawn by analogy from the principle of 'Privacy by Design' enshrined in the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR); and second, a model contractual framework governing the use of artificial intelligence tools in arbitration agreements. The study adopts a doctrinal and comparative methodology, with detailed analysis of the rules of the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC), the London Court of International Arbitration (LCIA), the Stockholm Chamber of Commerce (SCC), the EU AI Act, and the SVAMC and AAA-ICDR principles.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6862398`

- title: The Human Is Still in the Loop. The Question Is Whether Judgment Still Is
- authors: Matan Maller
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6862398
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The deployment of generative AI across professional work rests on a single assumption that is rarely examined, namely that a competent human reviewer will catch what the system gets wrong. That assumption is increasingly being written into law, including the human oversight requirement of the EU AI Act, which mandates effective oversight of high-risk systems and even names automation bias as the failure oversight is meant to prevent. What the law does not do is say how anyone should verify that the overseer is actually functioning. I argue that the assumption is more fragile than it looks. Evidence from clinical practice, from controlled experiments, and from electrophysiology shows the human reviewer degrading through skill erosion, over-reliance, and measurable change in the brain. The dominant responses to the problem, explainability, AI literacy training, and governance dashboards, all measure the system, the interface, or usage rather than the reviewer. I propose the term AI-induced cognitive freeze for an acute, in-the-moment failure of the reviewer's judgment to engage, offered as a synthesis of established findings rather than a confirmed one, and I suggest it is the failure that current tooling is structurally unable to see. I close by sketching what a clinically grounded, team-level instrument aimed at the reviewer would need to do.
