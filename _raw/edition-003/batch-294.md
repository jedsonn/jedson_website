## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6749098`

- title: Governing Machines: How Platform Governance Shapes AI Agent Lead Generation
- authors: Jafar Sabbah, Oguz A Acar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6749098
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

As AI agents increasingly act on behalf of firms, how should platforms govern markets in which AI agents act on both sides of exchange? We examine this question in the context of B2B lead generation, where search, initial contact, qualification, and progression are information-intensive and commercially consequential. We develop a controlled AI-to-AI simulation environment in which seller and buyer agents interact across a lead-generation funnel. The experiment manipulates four common platform governance mechanisms: disclosure level, autonomy level, protocol structure, and reputation visibility. Across 10 independent simulation replications, covering 160 governance runs and 2,560 dyad-level observations, the results reveal that the effects of the governance mechanisms vary across the stages of funnel progression. High disclosure improves progression across the funnel, while high autonomy is especially important for conversion. Reputation visibility improves early and intermediate progression, but does not significantly affect conversion. In contrast, structured protocols reduce positive responses, meeting proposals, and qualified leads, suggesting that behavioural controls may suppress agentic exchange when they restrict adaptive interaction. Interaction analyses further show that reputation visibility strengthens the effect of disclosure, while structured protocols weaken the effect of autonomy on intermediate progression. These results suggest that governance mechanisms designed for human platform participants do not translate directly into AI-to-AI markets.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6839891`

- title: Corporate YouTube Videos and Firm Value: Advertising or Information?
- authors: SASA QIAN, Bin Liu, Liangbo Ma, Niklas F. Wagner, Jun Shen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6839891
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

Using a state-of-the-art large language model, we study the firm value effects of corporate YouTube videos by classifying them as being either informational or advertising. Based on a subsample of S&P 500 index firms during the years 2016 to 2023, we find that firm value is positively associated with informational videos, while advertising videos show no significant relation. Investigation into firm characteristics and the volume of video releases reveals that institutional ownership positively affects the total volume of video releases, firm size positively relates to their informational use, while firm revenue positively relates to their use for advertising. Heterogeneity tests support an information disclosure channel as the positive value association is more pronounced among firms with higher levels of information asymmetry and uncertainty, i.e. when investors have less access to timely firm-specific information. Our findings are robust to alternative key measures, different model specifications and potential endogeneity. Overall, we provide strong evidence that corporate YouTube videos can serve as a valuable corporate information channel. Informational videos, in particular, go beyond shaping investor behavior in that they can help stakeholders to access relevant information and to better interpret existing disclosures.

- title: The Poisoned Chalice: Epistemic Decay and Hallucination Propagation in Recursive LLM Training
- authors: Ashutosh Jha
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6794439
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Each LLM generation trains on a corpus poisoned by its predecessors: hallucinated content re-enters as training signal, corrupting future models — the Epistemic Degradation Problem. Two independent frameworks characterize its dynamics: an SIR epidemic model deriving reproduction number R₀ and threshold-crossing generation n*, and a signal-degradation model deriving critical AI penetration thresholds as low as 4.8% for medical applications. Both predict monotone accuracy decline, asymptotic collapse, and irreversibility. Nine governance interventions are derived from the model mathematics.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6836259`

- title: Time-domain seismic soil–structure interaction and stress singularity analysis: A coupled polygonal SBFEM and viscous-spring artificial boundary framework
- authors: Xinxin Jiang, Hong Zhong, Jianglin Gao, Bangbin Wu, Xiaohan Jiang, Junkai Wang, Cao Luo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6836259
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Simultaneously modeling far-field radiation, seismic input, and local stress singularities is challenging in time-domain dam–foundation analyses. This study couples polygonal scaled boundary finite elements (PSBFEM) with viscous-spring artificial boundaries (VSAB) to evaluate seismic soil-structure interaction (SSI) and generalized stress intensity factors (GSIFs). The near-field domain is discretized using PSBFEM, while the truncated foundation is modeled via viscous-spring tractions on polygonal edges. Derived from a consistent virtual-work statement, stiffness, damping, and equivalent seismic input are assembled into global equations using identical edge-based interpolation for both outgoing scattered and prescribed incident waves. Singular stress demands at geometric discontinuities are evaluated directly via GSIFs extracted from SBFEM singular modes, eliminating the need for local mesh refinement. Validation against transient wave-scattering and incident-wave benchmarks confirms the framework's accuracy in boundary absorption and seismic input reproduction. Application to the Koyna gravity dam demonstrates that the PSBFEM-VSAB model predicts smaller crest displacements and lower normalized GSIF amplitudes compared to the conventional massless-foundation model. Specifically, crest displacement is reduced by 15%–20%, and the normalized peak GSIF at the downstream slope change drops by approximately one-third. These results indicate that the coupled formulation can evaluate global SSI response and local singular stress measures within one time-domain dam-foundation model.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6836263`

- title: AI-generated Video Summaries and Consumer Engagement on Digital Content Platforms: Evidence from Bilibili
- authors: Yu Li, xiaofei zhang, Ying Wang, Xi Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6836263
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The rise of generative AI is transforming the ways in which content is created, shared, and consumed. However, there remains a paucity of empirical research examining the impact of generative AI on consumer engagement in digital content platforms. This study fills this gap by leveraging a quasi-experimental setting in which an AI video summary feature is introduced on a video-based digital content platform. Specifically, we construct a unique panel dataset based on video samples from Bilibili, one of the largest video-based digital content platforms in China. Using coarsened exact matching (CEM) to balance the treatment and control groups, we apply a difference-in-differences (DID) approach to estimate the impact of AI-generated video summaries on consumer engagement. Our findings reveal that AI-generated video summaries significantly enhance consumer engagement. To uncover the underlying mechanism, we conduct a series of analysis and find that the increase in consumer engagement is primarily driven by information cue effect. Specifically, AI-generated video summaries improve the consistency between video comment content and AI summary. Furthermore, the positive effect of AI-generated video summaries is enhanced independently by higher AI summary arousal and greater title-summary consistency. Overall, this study contributes to the emerging literature on generative AI in digital content platforms. It also provides practical insights for platform managers on the integration and governance of generative AI applications in digital platforms.

## uid: `arxiv:2605.28647v1`

- title: The Ethics of LLM Sandbox and Persona Dynamics
- authors: Tim Gebbie, Stewart Gebbie
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28647v1
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

It is well known that LLM guardrails and trained persona dynamics can produce a reality gap: the distance between the world a LLM is permitted or shaped to describe, and the world in which users must act. Here we argue that actively generating reality gaps is in fact unethical because it knowingly shifts epistemic risk back to the uninformed user -- this is reality laundering. This can potentially cause harm when operationalised at scale. The risk is sharpest in high-exposure advice contexts, where users seek orientation rather than a bounded, externally checkable task. Guardrails naively appear ethically necessary when they claim to prevent direct harm, but often become suspect when they suppress truthful perception and launder uncomfortable mechanisms into acceptable abstractions. Basel-style financial regulation, B-BBEE-style compliance, Societe Generale, and the London Whale show how formal safety systems can become legible, gameable, and performative while real exposure migrates elsewhere. The same pattern can appear in LLMs as moral compliance: safe language, distorted reality. We therefore distinguish refusing harm, from refusing reality; and then argue for top-down causal requirements specification at the task level rather than bottom-up moral correction at the response or sandbox level. Persona dynamics matter because the assistant interface is not neutral; it shapes how uncertainty, conflict, authority, and risk are staged. The conclusion is that so-called ``ethical AI'' becomes substantively unethical when it substitutes institutional reassurance for contact with reality.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6748927`

- title: The Norm Architects: How AI Delegation Norms Form before anyone Designs them
- authors: Vsevolod Shabad
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6748927
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Organisations introducing generative AI rarely design how they will delegate to it; they discover the pattern after the fact. Convenience choices accumulate, spread by observing leaders, and harden through tipping-point dynamics into implicit norms about which reasoning tasks humans retain or cede. By the time governance frameworks appear, a default norm already dominates. Drawing on research in social norms, cognitive automation, and AI-mediated decision-making, this article explains how AI delegation norms emerge, stabilise, and resist revision. It identifies the pre-design phase as the lowest-cost moment for managerial intervention and shows why three common frames — productivity, capability attrition, and agency — each target the wrong unit of analysis to shape a norm. It proposes three governance practices and a short Delegation Audit for proactive norm design.

- authors: Tanvir Ahmed
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6789380
- keyword hits: gpt-4, llm

### abstract

This study investigates whether GPT-4o can produce advertising copy that rivals human writers on measures of persuasiveness, brand alignment, emotional resonance, and linguistic naturalness. Using a mixed-methods framework combining automated readability and sentiment analysis with human evaluator scoring across 120 paired samples in e-commerce and services categories, findings show LLM outputs scored comparably on surface fluency but lagged on cultural specificity and implicit persuasion - providing early evidence for the limits of AI professional judgment in commercial contexts.
