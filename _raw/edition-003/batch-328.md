## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7036831`

- title: A Comprehensive Survey on Hyperspectral Imaging in Computer Vision
- authors: jincheng li, Qi Bi, Shaodi You, Theo Gevers
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7036831
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Hyperspectral imaging (HSI) captures rich spectral information beyond conventional RGB images, enabling detailed material understanding and offering significant potential for computer vision (CV). However, existing studies mainly focus on remote sensing (RS) or isolated applications, while the fundamental differences between remote sensing HSI and computer vision HSI remain insufficiently explored. In this survey, we present the first systematic review of HSI from a computer vision perspective. We establish a unified framework connecting imaging physics, sensing systems, datasets, and downstream vision tasks. Specifically, we analyze the differences among spectroscopy, remote sensing, and computer vision in terms of illumination, imaging models, sensor noise, and spectral characteristics. Based on this analysis, we organize existing literature into low-level vision, high-level vision, and AI-driven paradigms, and summarize representative methods under a unified taxonomy. We further review commonly used computer vision HSI datasets and benchmark representative spectral foundation models on computer vision hyperspectral datasets. Experimental results reveal substantial domain gaps and limited cross-domain generalization capability of current spectral foundation models. Finally, we discuss key challenges and future directions involving imaging physics, datasets, sensing systems, and learning architectures. We hope this survey provides useful insights for future research toward robust and scalable hyperspectral computer vision systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6901539`

- title: The Cognitive Windfall: Where does Human Time, Attention, and Effort Go when AI Lowers the Cost of Cognition?
- authors: Walter Guevara, Cristina Atomi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6901539
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence is most often analyzed as a labor-market technology: which tasks will be automated, which workers complemented, and what will happen to wages and employment? This framing, while important, is incomplete. If AI lowers the cost of useful cognition across work and everyday life, the central social question is not only whether people keep their jobs but where human time, attention, and effort go when cognitive assistance becomes cheaper. Productivity gains are not welfare gains until we know where the saved time and effort are reallocated. This paper introduces the cognitive windfall, the stock of time, attention, and mental effort potentially freed when AI reduces the cost of cognition, and the human activity frontier: the full set of market and non- market activities into which people can allocate time, from paid work and caregiving to learning, serious leisure (Stebbins, 1992), passive consumption, and civic participation. Drawing on Becker's theory of time allocation, task-based models of automation, and self-determination theory, the paper identifies four regimes through which the cognitive windfall may flow: employer and institutional capture, passive absorption, purposeful reallocation, and capability trap. It proposes a measurement agenda anchored in time-use surveys, AI adoption data, and platform-level usage evidence. The paper presents no original empirical results. Its central argument is conditional: the welfare consequences of AI depend on how the cognitive windfall is allocated across the human activity frontier, and that allocation is shaped by institutions, social capital (Putnam, 2000), autonomy, skill, attention markets, and inequality.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6906459`

- title: Blitzscale or Bust: Is There a Pricing Problem in the Generative AI Industry?
- authors: Stuart Mills, Richard Whittle
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6906459
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

We argue the generative AI industry is pursuing a blitzscaling strategy. Using venture capital (VC) funding, generative AI companies have charged substantially below cost-plus-margin (C+M) prices to gain market share. However, consumers may not be willing to pay higher prices for these technologies, raising questions about the long-term strategy of the generative AI industry. Where blitzscaling benefits platform businesses which achieve dependencies through large network effects, the quality of an AI model does not substantially improve the more people who use it. Furthermore, blitzscaling benefits weightless businesses with near-zero variable costs, while generative AI firms face substantial variable costs in the form of inference. This strategic mismatch underpins this pricing problem. Given this strategic mismatch, we argue generative AI companies are likely to a) raise prices; b) promote features like personalization which encourage consumer dependencies; and c) seek government support in terms of financing, regulation, and revenue generation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7036735`

- title: An Integrated Natural Language Processing and Generative Artificial Intelligence Framework for Translating Latent User Needs into Product Design Solutions​
- authors: jie Luo, Yue Qiu, Yunhuan Deng, Linghao Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7036735
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Abstract: This study proposes a generalizable data-driven product design framework that integrates online review mining, natural language processing, generative artificial intelligence, and multi-criteria decision-making methods to support efficient engineering design.The key innovation of this work lies in the creative integration of artificial intelligence techniques into the early stages of industrial product design, particularly in user requirement acquisition and concept generation, enabling a significantly accelerated and structured design workflow. Firstly, online user reviews are analyzed using a Biterm Topic Modeling-based natural language processing method to extract latent user needs. These needs are then prioritized through a hybrid approach combining a fuzzy analytic hierarchy process with a criterion importance-based correlation weighting method. Subsequently, a Low-Rank Adaptation-based generative model is employed to rapidly generate diverse product form alternatives from learned design representations. Finally, a multi-criteria compromise ranking method is used to evaluate and select the optimal design solution.The effectiveness of the framework is demonstrated through a case study of a home elliptical training machine. The results show that the proposed approach enables an efficient and structured design workflow covering requirement extraction, concept generation, evaluation, and optimization within a unified pipeline. Cross-category validation further confirms the robustness and generalizability of the method. Overall, the study provides a systematic and scalable solution for accelerating product design processes while improving decision quality in engineering applications with the help of artificial intelligence methods.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7036093`

- title: A Hypothesis-Driven AI-Assisted Research Pipeline for Modeling Compliant Parallelogram Mechanisms'
- authors: Hai-Jun Su, Benjamin Servey
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7036093
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

This paper presents a hypothesis-driven artificial-intelligence (AI)-assisted research pipeline for modeling a compliant parallelogram mechanism, using parasitic-rotation prediction as a bounded case study. Domain experts propose and adjudicate physically motivated pseudo-rigid-body model (PRBM) variants, while large-language-model (LLM) coding agents implement models, run finite-element-analysis (FEA) benchmarks, train machine-learning (ML) surrogates, and preserve an auditable hypothesis log. The pipeline tested PRBM variants against a 1547-case FEA-3D dataset and a separate 24-case FEA evaluation set at a fixed normalized half-separation ratio. The examined PRBM extensions could not predict all three outputs across small, medium, and large deflection ranges with one physically interpretable parameterization; the final Gaussian-process (GP) model is therefore presented as a data-driven, geometry-specific surrogate rather than a general analytical reduced-order model. On the evaluation dataset, the GP surrogate gave the best accuracy--speed tradeoff among the evaluated models, winning seven of nine range/output root-mean-square-error cells and predicting in sub microsecond per case, approximately 75 times faster than representative PRBM Newton-solve variants. Code, data, and logs are released for audit.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.00856v1`

- title: Shapley in Context: Explaining Financial Language with Domain Expertise
- authors: Dangxing Chen, Pengzhan Guo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00856v1
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

In recent years, large language models have achieved remarkable success and have seen growing adoption in financial applications. At the same time, explainability remains critical in finance, a domain characterized by high stakes and strict regulatory requirements. Although numerous methods have been proposed to explain black box machine learning models, the majority of these approaches are designed for general purpose tasks and do not incorporate domain specific knowledge. In this work, we study the explainability of financial textual data modeled by large language models through the lens of the Shapley value. Specifically, we investigate whether Shapley based attributions align with established financial domain knowledge. Through rigorous theoretical analysis and extensive empirical evaluations, we demonstrate that Shapley values can yield explanations that are consistent with financial reasoning and can offer meaningful insights into the model's behavior in text based financial applications.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.01510v1`

- title: Janus: a Playground for User-Involved Agentic Permission Management
- authors: Natalie Grace Brigham, Eugene Bagdasarian, Tadayoshi Kohno, Franziska Roesner
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01510v1
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

AI agents that autonomously execute tool calls on a user's behalf raise pressing questions about permission management: what role could users play, and what role should they play? Despite many proposed approaches, the user's role in agentic permission management remains under explored. We introduce Janus, a playground system for implementing and evaluating user-involved agentic permission management designs. Janus consists of two components: Janus-Core, a modular agentic system supporting a diverse spectrum of permission management designs, and Janus-Harness, an automated evaluation framework. Grounded in a conceptual model that identifies key design axes for user involvement, we implement six permission assistants spanning the design space and evaluate them across three scenarios and three synthetic responders. We demonstrate that user input is critical and can significantly strengthen privacy and security, that AI augmentation of user decisions can help reduce cognitive load, and that realistic user behavior including permission fatigue must be accounted for in system design. No single design performs optimally across all contexts, motivating a more principled and context-sensitive approach to deploying permission assistants in agentic systems. Janus is publicly available to support future investigation into this dimension of agentic system design.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.01470v1`

- title: World Feedback for Clinical Agents: Diagnosing RL in FHIR Environments
- authors: Ananya Mantravadi, Harshit Rajgarhia, Prasanna Desikan, Abhishek Mukherji
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-01
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01470v1
- keyword hits: qwen

### abstract

Clinical protocol-execution tasks -- checking a lab value, applying a threshold, placing a correctly structured FHIR order -- are natural candidates for RL from world feedback: once clinical SMEs encode decision logic into a verifier, that verifier grades unlimited rollouts without per-episode annotation. But applying RL requires a sound feedback channel and sufficient base capability. We audit MedAgentBench v1/v2, find a 41.7\% silent-finish ceiling that makes inaction the RL dominant strategy, and construct \textbf{MedAgentBench-v3 (MAB-v3)} (508 tasks, 8.9\% ceiling). Training Qwen3-8B exposes two structural barriers: a \emph{capability ceiling} (10/20 task types have 0\% base performance, zero gradient) and a \emph{format-knowledge barrier} (3/20 types require exact clinical codes undiscoverable by exploration). Pure RL reaches 18.2\% pass@1 vs.\ 34.1\% for rule-based SFT; the 15.9~pp gap is attributable entirely to these barriers. A decision/format-knowledge/lookup taxonomy predicts RL learnability and prescribes the fix: SFT to inject codes, RL to learn conditionals.
