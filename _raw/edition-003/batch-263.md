## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6476021`

- title: The SEO-to-GEO Gap: Quantifying Ranking Factor Divergence Between Traditional and Generative Search
- authors: Dmitrii Kargaev
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6476021
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

The emergence of generative AI search engines, including Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT search, and Perplexity, has created a new optimization paradigm: Generative Engine Optimization (GEO). Although practitioners increasingly recognize this shift, the literature still lacks a careful cross-paradigm comparison that separates direct factor evidence from adjacent visibility, overlap, and volatility studies. This paper presents an exploratory comparative synthesis built from a triaged evidence base: a small core quantitative corpus of direct SEO and GEO studies, plus a contextual layer of market, overlap, and citation-behavior sources. The extracted evidence supports three provisional findings. First, GEO-side visibility appears more tightly associated with entity and brand prominence than with classic link-proxy measures. Second, source-credibility interventions such as adding citations and statistics produce substantial gains in the GEO benchmark, suggesting that generative systems reward evidence-bearing and citation-ready content more directly than traditional SEO studies capture. Third, the current overlap literature indicates that GEO still operates on top of an organic search substrate rather than replacing SEO outright. A provisional Divergence Index framework is introduced and applied to the currently matched factor families. Under this framework, authority appears broadly persistent across paradigms, while content-quality divergence remains highly sensitive to construct mapping on the SEO side. The paper therefore argues for a reweighting model rather than a replacement model: familiar factor families persist, new generative-facing signals emerge, and some headline claims about SEO-to-GEO disruption remain stronger than the current evidence base can support.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6658238`

- title: Melgo Sequence: A Price Structure Classification Framework Built on the Behavioral Properties of HL2, HLC3, and OHLC4 as Structural Market Levels
- authors: Jeff Rey Melgo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6658238
- keyword hits: claude

### abstract

This paper presents the Melgo Sequence, an original price structure classification framework derived from the relative ordering of three standard derived price levels within any completed higher-timeframe candle: HL2 designated the Neutral Anchor, HLC3 designated the Structural Core, and OHLC4 designated the FakeOut Threshold. These levels were identified independently through direct market observation as behaviorally distinct structural zones — HL2 as a price equilibrium and stabilization zone, HLC3 as the most actively defended price within any candle, and OHLC4 as the zone where fakeout activity concentrates due to its sensitivity to candle origin. Their relative ordering produces six classification states with defined market character. The framework was validated through eight phases of empirical backtesting across four instruments — BTCUSDT spot, SPY, EURUSD, and NASDAQ — covering 18,904 higher-timeframe candles. All findings are reported with binomial significance tests and Bonferroni-corrected confidence intervals. Single-timeframe classification produces no actionable directional edge after multiple comparison correction across any instrument or state. Multi-timeframe confluence produces statistically confirmed edge on two states: Accumulation on BTCUSDT at 62.3% bull directional accuracy (p=0.0021, n=162, 95% CI 54.4% to 69.8%) and Bear Trend on SPY at 60.0% bull (p=0.0041, n=215, 95% CI 53.1% to 66.6%), both surviving Bonferroni correction. Zone break at the primary structural level is the most statistically robust finding in the research: SPY Bull Rejection zone breaks produce 83.7% bearish outcomes (p<0.001, n=153, 95% CI 76.8% to 89.1%) and SPY Bear Trend zone breaks produce 90.5% bullish outcomes (p<0.001, n=63, 95% CI 80.4% to 96.4%). A structural crossover exit system warns before the hard stop fires in 82 to 89 percent of trades across all four instruments, all confirmed at p<0.001. An original touch-only wick finding reported in an earlier version is retracted following peer review — corrected measurement produces results statistically indistinguishable from a coin flip. The dominant variable in level pierce behavior is the timeframe that produced the level, not the specific level type, with 4H levels producing 74 to 85 percent fakeout rates on 15-minute bars compared to 61 to 65 percent for 1H levels. A statistically confirmed asset-class dependency is documented — the same structural state carries materially different directional implications on risk assets versus the EURUSD forex pair, confirmed by two-proportion z-test at z=3.044, p=0.0023. The conceptual framework was developed by Jeff Rey Melgo. Research architecture, statistical analysis, and manuscript preparation were conducted with substantial assistance from Claude, an AI language model developed by Anthropic. Full AI disclosure is included in the paper. The public classifier implementation is available on GitHub at: https://github.com/jeffreymelgo/melgo-sequence

### abstract

Monitoring and maintenance have become fundamental requirements with the recent expansion of photovoltaic plants. Serial number recognition is essential to ensure module-level traceability, efficient maintenance, warranty management and accurate fault localization in future operations. However, traditional approaches for mapping are often cost-prohibitive and time-consuming using manual operations. This study proposes an end-to-end pipeline for extracting serial numbers from images captured by Unmanned Aerial Vehicles, integrating state-of-the-art object detectors for label localization, preprocessing algorithms (such as Text Image Super-Resolution and orientation correction), and Optical Character Recognition models.,Real measurements from operating photovoltaic plants were acquired to train and evaluate the models, combining images with different quality and orientations during aerial operations with several altitudes and orientations. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6473079`

- title: AI-Assisted Development of a Regulated Brokerage Platform: Governance Failures, Incident-Driven Learning, and a Preliminary Taxonomy of AI Failure Modes
- authors: Atif Neuman Jamil
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6473079
- keyword hits: claude

### abstract

This paper presents a single-case, hypothesis-generating study of AI-assisted software development in a regulated capital markets context. A single senior engineer, using AI coding agents (Claude 4.6 Opus, high-thinking mode, via Cursor IDE) as the primary code generation tool, built a production-intended brokerage Order Management System over 21 active development sessions. The platform spans 23 phases across two milestones, encompassing 151 coarse-grained requirements, 190+ database migrations, 73 domain model files, and 251 service implementation files covering order lifecycle, multi-market execution (Saudi Exchange and US equities), post-trade settlement, 14-way reconciliation, double-entry general ledger accounting, and regulatory compliance. The central contribution is not the platform itself but the empirical documentation of AI governance failures and the incident-driven evolution of a Three-Layer Quality Model. In the most significant failure, all 32 AI review agents including 5 hard-veto domain specialists unanimously returned PASS on code containing 7 runtime-breaking SQL defects. The root cause was structural: agents granted review authority lacked the capability to cross-reference SQL against migration schemas. This led to the Authority-Capability Gap principle and the finding that a single automated schemavalidation test outperformed the entire 33-agent review committee for structural defect classes. The study contributes: (1) an empirical account derived from 30+ formally logged incidents, 116+ prevention rules, and 52+ architecture lessons; (2) a multi-agent governance framework ("Committee1") with domain-specialized hard-veto authority; (3) a layered quality model separating mechanical testing, evidence-based AI review, and human strategic oversight; and (4) a preliminary taxonomy of 6 AI failure mode categories grounded in structured incident analysis. The taxonomy identifies wiring gaps (locally correct components never integrated), discarded error returns, and domain boundary blindspots as the three systemic failure clusters. Limitations: This is a single-developer, single-model, pre-production study. The platform has not processed production/live transactions. No independent third-party audit has been conducted. All findings should be interpreted as hypothesis-generating for future controlled studies.

- title: When Language Meets Visual Design Can Large Language Models Improve Automated Image Composition
- authors: Anshu Raj
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6465739
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Automated image generation systems have achieved remarkable progress in producing visually appealing images. However, these systems often lack an explicit understanding of fundamental visual design principles, such as balance, hierarchy, contrast, and spatial harmony. As a result, the generated compositions may appear visually plausible but frequently exhibit inconsistencies in aesthetic structure and layout organization. Addressing this limitation requires integrating higher-level reasoning about design principles into the image generation process. In this study, we explore whether large language models can support automated image composition by reasoning about visual design concepts expressed through natural language. We demonstrate that incorporating languagedriven reasoning into image composition pipelines can substantially enhance both the interpretability and aesthetic quality of automatically generated designs.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6589479`

- title: AgentForensics: Exploring the Real-Time Prompt Injection Detection and Forensics Threats in LLM Agents
- authors: Aparnaa Mahalaxmi Arulljothi, Theepan Kumar Gandhi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6589479
- keyword hits: claude, llm

### abstract

With LLM agents increasingly deployed to autonomously processed external content like web pages, emails, documents, and API responses become targets of indirect prompt injection attacks where malicious instructions embedded in external content hijack the agent's behavior. Existing defenses focus primarily on filtering user-layer inputs, leaving the wider attack surface unaddressed. In this paper, we present AgentForensics, an open-source security framework that monitors entire LLM agent sessions in real time and detects prompt injection attacks wherever they originate. Our system implements a five-stage detection pipeline combining seven heuristic regex rules and one Unicode script-ratio check, a pre-trained DistilBERT-based ML classifier, instruction boundary pattern matching, semantic drift analysis, and sliding-window multi-turn detection. We evaluate AgentForensics across two independent benchmarks ARPI bench (7,560 payloads) and deepset/prompt-injections and achieve 100% detection on 7,763 injection payloads with zero false positives across 343 benign samples. F1 score of 1.0 is reported for the deepset benchmark, which contains both injection and benign classes. Our framework requires minimal-integration support to existing agent logic and integrates natively with OpenAI, Anthropic, LangChain, AutoGen, and Claude Desktop via MCP.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6670582`

- title: Assessing systemic risks of generative AI in media: A socio-technical framework based on hazard, exposure, and vulnerability
- authors: Ahmad haidar, christine BALAGUE, Nessrine Omrani
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6670582
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This study examines the systemic risks associated with generative artificial intelligence (GAI) in the media sector by adapting the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) risk framework. Specifically, it conceptualizes risk as the interaction between hazard, exposure, and vulnerability, and empirically validates this framework using 507 real-world incidents from the OECD AI Incident Monitoring database. The findings reveal that GAI-related risks are not isolated but emerge from the interaction of interdependent socio-technical drivers. In particular, opacity and inadequate validity checks are the most prevalent hazards, while information and content integrity represent the dominant exposure pathways. These drivers significantly interact with vulnerabilities across key stakeholders, especially businesses and consumers, leading to economic, reputational, and public interest harms. The study demonstrates that existing atomistic risk frameworks fail to capture these relational dynamics. By contrast, the adapted IPCC framework provides a robust systemic approach to understanding how risks co-occur and amplify across the media ecosystem. These results offer important implications for policymakers and organizations by providing a structured methodology for assessing and governing GAI risks, emphasizing the need for integrated and context-sensitive regulatory approaches.
