## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6835100`

- title: Reducing the Cost of Invoice Processing in Small and Medium Enterprises: A Reference Architecture and Economic Model for LLM-Powered Accounts Payable Automation
- authors: Gururaj Veershetty
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6835100
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Small and medium enterprises (SMEs) bear disproportionately high accounts payable (AP) processing costs relative to invoice volume, with manual per-invoice costs widely benchmarked between $8 and $22. Despite two decades of automation tools, adoption among SMEs has lagged because legacy solutions, template-driven optical character recognition (OCR) and rules-based robotic process automation (RPA), depend on supplier-format stability and lack contextual judgment. The emergence of large language models (LLMs) with strong zero-shot information-extraction capability alters this calculus. This paper proposes a five-stage reference architecture for LLM-enabled AP automation, presents a unit-economics model demonstrating per-invoice costs below $1.30 at SME scale, and outlines an implementation pathway scoped to a single-quarter pilot. The paper also examines the security, compliance, and governance considerations specific to small finance teams operating without dedicated IT staff. The analysis indicates that the binding constraint on SME adoption has shifted from cost and technical complexity to organizational readiness, and that a tiered model-routing strategy combined with confidence-based human-in-the-loop review can deliver enterprise-grade throughput at SME-appropriate cost.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.13812v1`

- title: CFOs Meet LLMs
- authors: John R. Graham, Campbell R. Harvey, Manish Jha
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13812v1
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Business sentiment is a closely watched economic signal, but measuring it is slow and costly: surveys reach only a few hundred firms, arrive periodically, and take time to compile. We show that large language models hold the potential to address these shortcomings. We prompt an LLM to role-play as the CFO of a specific company at a specific date and focus on the economic-optimism question on the Duke-Federal Reserve CFO Survey over 2002-2025. We find that the LLM reproduces individual human responses: the predicted optimism score significantly forecasts the CFO's actual answer, surviving firm and year-quarter fixed effects and a control for the most recent prior response. Predictive accuracy increases with the amount of information supplied, as both respondent history and firm characteristics improve fit, and the relationship persists under quarterly aggregation. With appropriate conditioning, LLMs may be able to serve as credible digital twins of executives, offering scalable, high-frequency expectations data for financial research and policy.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6934199`

- title: Prediction Model of Motivators and Demotivators of Integrating Large Language Models in Software Engineering Education: An Empirical Study
- authors: Maryam Khan, Muhammad  Azeem Akbar, Jussi Kasurinen, Estefanía Martín-Barroso
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6934199
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models are increasingly influencing software engineering practices and education. While previous studies examine their technical performance and classroom use, limited research provides cost-aware and empirically grounded models to guide systematic institutional integration. This study develops and validates a prediction model to identify cost-efficient strategies for integrating LLMs into software engineering education based on motivating and demotivating factors. Drawing on our previously developed literature survey-based taxonomies [1], we operationalized 19 validated factors (9 motivators and 10 demotivators) into a structured survey completed by 126 stakeholders in software engineering education across multiple countries. Likert-scale responses were encoded and used to train probabilistic models (Naïve Bayes and Logistic Regression) to estimate the likelihood of high LLM familiarity. The aggregated probability estimates were integrated into a Genetic Algorithm (GA)-based optimization framework to model trade-offs between predicted familiarity and implementation cost. Optimization was performed at both global and category levels. Survey respondents perceive strong benefits in Programming Assistance and Debugging Support and Personalized and Adaptive Learning. However, major concerns relate to Plagiarism and Intellectual Property Concerns, Over-Reliance on AI in Learning, and Reduced Critical Thinking and Problem-Solving. Optimization results indicate that governance related mechanisms, particularly integrity and ethical safeguards should be prioritized under cost constraints. Pedagogically oriented domains yield higher marginal efficiency compared to structurally intensive reforms. The study introduces a hierarchical, optimization informed decision support framework that links stakeholder perceptions with probabilistic modeling and cost–effort analysis. The model supports staged, cost-aware LLM integration grounded in governance stability and cognitively meaningful pedagogical development.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6846804`

- title: Stop Detecting AI Authorship, Start Verifying Logical Alignment: A Methodological and Governance Framework for Scientific Research in the Generative AI Era
- authors: Mingdong He
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6846804
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence, large language model, large language models

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence has driven the marginal cost of producing scholarly text toward zero, while leaving the cost of verifying scholarly claims essentially unchanged. This asymmetry, not the existence of AI tools as such, is the structural problem confronting contemporary science. The dominant institutional response-mandating disclosure of AI use and deploying AI-text detectors-fails on three counts: detection is technically unstable and demonstrably biased; disclosure encodes too little information to inform either editorial decisions or epistemic trust; and both rest on a category error, treating the provenance of text as a proxy for the validity of an argument. We argue that the locus of scientific quality control must shift along two axes simultaneously: from authorship provenance to logical structure, and from post-hoc peer review to pre-submission verification. We develop an integrated, three-layer framework. (i) As an evaluative standard, we define Logical Alignment: a manuscript is logically aligned when its premises are transparent, its inference is traceable, its predictions are falsifiable and discriminatory, its boundary conditions are explicit, and its epistemic increment is identifiable. (ii) As a methodological pipeline, we introduce the Minimum Viable Idea (MVI) → Minimum Viable Theory (MVT) workflow, preceded by an Explanation-Depth Test that prevents premature termination of inquiry at proximate explanations, and validated through Heterogeneous Multi-Model Cross-Examination (HMCE), which treats the alignment differences across large language models as epistemic probes rather than as noise. (iii) As a governance interface, we introduce Cognitive Auditability as scholarly metadata-distinguishing cognitive amplification from cognitive substitution-and a lightweight Logic Alignment Report (LAR) that reduces the marginal cost of expert evaluation for editors, reviewers, supervisors, and funders. We provide formal definitions, multiple worked examples spanning evolutionary biology, the social sciences, the formal sciences, and the physical sciences, a full HMCE protocol with annotated transcripts, completed LAR templates, an explicit analysis of failure modes (including model homogeneity and the suppression of genuine novelty, for which we propose a High-Divergence Preservation Principle), and a voluntary, additive implementation pathway requiring no change to copyright law or existing authorship norms. The framework does not legitimize careless AI use; it makes such use structurally visible while routing rigorous use through transparent, inspectable verification channels. It is released as a preprint to establish methodological priority for its constructs and to invite open replication and critique.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6958931`

- title: FINTAGGING: Benchmarking Large Language Models for Extracting and Structuring Financial Information
- authors: Yan Wang, Xueqing Peng, Yang Ren, Yi Han, Keyi Wang, Dongji Feng, Linhai Ma, Xuguang Ai
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6958931
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Corporate filings contain large volumes of numerical information that must be organized into standardized, machine-interpretable records for search, comparison, auditing, and decision-making. Although XBRL (eXtensible Business Reporting Language) provides the reporting infrastructure, assigning numerical facts to appropriate US Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (US-GAAP) concepts remains difficult. Evidence for a correct tag is often distributed across narrative disclosures, tables, and accounting definitions, while the full taxonomy contains more than 17,000 semantically related concepts. Many financial NLP benchmarks and XBRL tagging datasets reduce this problem to flat classification over limited label sets, underrepresenting the structural cues that determine how facts should be interpreted.We introduce FINTAGGING, a benchmark for evaluating large language models (LLMs) on taxonomy-grounded financial information extraction from corporate filings. Rather than treating tagging as a single prediction problem, FINTAGGING decomposes it into two stages. FinNI (Financial Numeric Identification) identifies numerical facts and assigns value types from mixed text-table inputs. FinCL (Financial Concept Linking) links each fact to the most appropriate US-GAAP concept. This formulation separates local fact extraction from global taxonomy alignment and exposes model failures.Zero-shot experiments show that LLMs recover many numerical facts but remain weaker at concept linking, revealing a persistent gap between numerical extraction and taxonomy-grounded semantic alignment. The AI contribution of FINTAGGING is a staged benchmark that disentangles fact extraction from concept alignment to expose LLM capability boundaries. Its engineering application is automated financial disclosure processing for auditing, regulatory compliance, and investment decision-making.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6929398`

- title: LLMs and Childhood Safety Identifying Risks and Proposing a Protection Framework for Safe Child-LLM Interaction
- authors: Junfeng Jiao, Saleh Afroogh, Kevin Chen, Abhejay Murali, David Atkinson, Amit Dhurandhar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6929398
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly embedded in child-facing contexts such as education, companionship, creative tools, but their deployment raises safety, privacy, developmental, and security risks. We conduct a systematic literature review of child-LLM interaction risks and organize findings into a structured map that separates (i) parent-reported concerns, (ii) empirically documented harms, and (iii) gaps between perceived and observed risk. Moving beyond descriptive listing, we compare how different evidence streams in surveys, incident reports, youth interaction logs, and governance guidance operationalize “harm,” where they conflict, and what mitigations they imply. Based on this synthesis, we propose a protection framework that couples child-specific content safety and developmental sensitivity with security-grade controls for adversarial misuse, including prompt injection and multimodal jailbreak pathways. The framework specifies measurable evaluation targets (e.g., harmful-content avoidance, age-calibrated readability, bias parity checks, prompt-injection robustness, and monitoring transparency) to support developers, educators, and policymakers in assessing and improving child-safe LLM deployments.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.20041v1`

- title: AI Economist Agent: An Agentic Framework for Model-Grounded Economic Analysis with RAG, Knowledge Graphs, and Large Language Models
- authors: Masahiro Kato
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-18
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20041v1
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

We propose a model-grounded RAG-based AI economist with an agentic framework for economic scenario analysis using large language models (LLMs) and knowledge graphs. While LLMs can generate fluent economic narratives, economists are often required to make economic claims grounded by economic theory and real-world data. Based on this motivation, this study proposes an RAG-based AI economist, which utilizes knowledge graphs including economic data and theory and LLM-based agents to plan the analysis, retrieve relevant evidence, select appropriate models, and generate reports. In our framework, we do not produce quantitative claims directly with the language model alone; instead, we generate narratives grounded in explicit model-based computations and linked to the retrieved evidence via AI agents. We refer to our framework as an AI economist agent. We evaluate the AI economist agent in two applications: economist report generation for U.S. inflation persistence and Federal Reserve policy, and bank stress-test narrative generation for U.S. commercial real estate refinancing stress. The results illustrate how grounding the generated reports improves their economic coherence and traceability.
