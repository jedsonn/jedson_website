## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6662143`

- title: The Governance of Abundance: Generative AI, Selective Permeability, and Complementor Strategy
- authors: Thomas Jungbauer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6662143
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI changes platform ecosystems not only by expanding complement supply, but also by making surface plausibility cheap to manufacture. I develop a theory in which a platform allocates scarce exposure between a verified trusted lane and a protected exploration lane, while complementors choose substantive effort, AI-enabled polish, and verification. When AI raises the return to polish faster than the return to substance, complementors privately overinvest in mimicry and underuse verification. The platform's optimal response is often neither openness nor closure but selective permeability: more trusted exposure combined with a protected exploratory frontier. A theory-guided computational application shows that this hybrid architecture becomes optimal in most post-AI categories and materially outperforms openness and closure in discovery-rich environments.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6668103`

- title: Energy Storage System-Based Demand Response with Time-Varying Uncertainties, Cyber-Degraded Observability, and Risk-Aware Settlement in Renewable-Rich Integrated Energy Systems
- authors: Reza Sepehrzad, HOSSEIN HOSSEINALIBEIKI, Hadi Vatankhah Ghadim, Nima Khosravi, Ahmed Al durra, Mahdieh S. Sadabadi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6668103
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

Energy storage systems (ESSs) are becoming critical flexibility resources in renewable-rich integrated energy systems (IESs), where demand response (DR) must support renewable energy source (RES) variability, network feasibility, and market-oriented operation. However, ESS-supported DR faces a practical but underexplored inconsistency: the flexibility physically delivered by storage assets may differ from the delivery observed, measured, and settled by the market when uncertainty and cyber-communication disturbances occur together. RES forecast errors, load and price deviations, false data injection, denial-of-service events, packet loss, and latency can jointly distort ESS dispatch, metered DR delivery, imbalance exposure, and settlement reliability. To address this problem, this paper proposes a storage-centric, uncertainty-aware, and cyber-resilient DR framework for RES-rich IESs. The proposed model connects ESS dynamics, multi-carrier operational constraints, DR commitments, cyber-degraded observability, metered delivery, and risk-aware settlement within a unified operational chain. A large language model-guided hierarchical multi-agent deep reinforcement learning architecture coordinates distributed ESS-backed DR decisions, while a constrained action repair and projection layer converts raw control actions into feasible dispatch commands satisfying storage, network, comfort, ramping, and power-quality limits before execution. The framework is evaluated on the IEEE 69-bus system, the Caracas 141-bus feeder, and the IEEE 342-node system under nominal, uncertainty-only, attack-only, and combined uncertainty-cyber regimes. In the combined regime, the proposed method maintains 93.8–96.4% DR delivery with only 1.2–2.3% violation rates and consistently outperforms the benchmark methods in profit, constraint satisfaction, and CVaR0.95, confirming its ability to preserve feasible and economically reliable ESS-supported DR under degraded real-time conditions.

- title: Comparative evaluation of AlphaEarth annual embeddings vs. seasonal remote sensing Layers with U-Net segmentation for California crop and land-use mapping
- authors: Gabriel Díaz-Ireland, Derya Gülçin, Aida López-Sánchez, Mario García Peces, Diego Prieto-Herráez, Javier Velázquez
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6667132
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Foundation models for Earth observation encode images from multiple sensors and time periods into dense per-pixel embeddings that can be used for different tasks. However, it is still not clear whether these annual embeddings have enough temporal information for fine-grained, regional mapping of crops and land use compared to seasonal inputs. The present study evaluates this question by directly comparing Google’s AlphaEarth annual satellite embeddings with Sentinel-1/2 seasonal composites for statewide crop and land-use segmentation in California. The analysis employs the same U-Net-based decoder, loss, and experimental design. As ground truth, Land IQ’s CalCrops 2022 field-level polygon dataset is used to regroup the original legend into a 6-class agricultural taxonomy. Under 5-fold spatialcross-validation, the embedding track achieves 82.6% ± 6.8% overall accuracy, 0.662 ± 0.102 mIoU, and 0.776 ± 0.085 macro-F1. The tuned seasonal track achieves 73.3% OA, 0.480 mIoU, and 0.625 macro-F1; the naive seasonal baseline reaches 71.1% OA, 0.466 mIoU, and 0.610 macro-F1. The largest per-class margins in favor of embeddings are observed for Alfalfa(+0.459 IoU) and Mixed/Native Pasture (+0.292 IoU). AlphaEarth embeddings outperform seasonal in all classes, including phenologically dynamic crops. Loss–metric trajectories show continued learning despite stable validation loss, highlighting the importance of metric-based early stopping. The study discusses the pros and cons of using foundation-model embeddingsversus task-specific seasonal representations in different label and transfer regimes. It provides empirical evidence that foundation-model embeddings retain sufficient phenological information to surpass task-specific seasonal inputs even in a phenology-driven application.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6512098`

- title: Pre-Execution Control Planes for AI and Software Supply Chains A Deterministic Approach to Admissibility Enforcement
- authors: Skip Middleton
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6512098
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Modern systems increasingly convert external input into execution. Large language models generate actions, software supply chains deliver code, and automation frameworks translate both into system changes. Current defenses focus on detection, trust models, and post-execution containment. These approaches do not address the core failure mode: execution is granted implicitly. This paper presents a control-plane architecture that enforces admissibility before execution. Using IVD-ACP (Invariant Vector Defense, Admissibility Control Plane) as a reference implementation, it demonstrates how all inputs, including AI-generated actions and software dependencies, can be intercepted, evaluated, and assigned bounded authority prior to execution. A validation using a live agent environment is presented, along with direct application to recent supply chain security events including the Axios npm compromise.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6665242`

- title: Two Pathways at Once: How Reactance and Ethical Engagement Shape Student Learning with AI
- authors: Steven Hyde, Andrew Hanna, Gundars Kaupins
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6665242
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI tools promise to expand student agency in management education, yet the same tool can help and hurt learning inside a single classroom. We argue this contradiction is the signature of two psychological pathways running in parallel. Drawing on the cognitive-affective-conative framework, we develop a dual-pathway serial mediation model in which a cognitive pathway runs from psychological reactance through autonomy perceptions to effectiveness judgments, while an affective pathway runs from ethical perceptions through emotional engagement to effectiveness judgments. Ninety-seven undergraduate management students completed two AI-enhanced activities with different chatbot tools across one semester. Both pathways operated as predicted. More surprising was how the cognitive pathway changed across exposures. At first use, reactance traveled through autonomy, consistent with self-determination theory. By the second activity, reactance bypassed autonomy and suppressed effectiveness directly. The affective pathway remained stable across exposures. For management educators, ethical framing before a student’s first AI encounter may do more work than redesigning the tools themselves to preserve student choice.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6669317`

- title: GenAI-Assisted Revision Materials in Source-Based English for Academic Purposes Writing: A Learner-Side Materials Evaluation Study
- authors: Huican Huo, Yanfan Cheng
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6669317
- keyword hits: deepseek, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This study examines how generative artificial intelligence (GenAI)-assisted revision materials functioned in a source-based English for Academic Purposes (EAP) writing course. Although GenAI is increasingly used for EAP materials development and writing feedback, less is known about how students evaluate generated revision support after it enters their own writing processes. This classroom-based materials evaluation study analyzed a bounded Draft 1–DeepSeek–Draft 2 revision cycle in an undergraduate EAP course at a Chinese university. Data included task materials, rubrics, AI-use guidance, 160 student drafts, DeepSeek interaction records, and 80 learner reflections from 40 students. The analysis identified 793 revision episodes linking draft issues, DeepSeek-generated suggestions, D2 revisions, and, where available, learner rationales. Findings show that GenAI-assisted revision materials served functions beyond language polishing, including source-use support, clarification, rhetorical organization, evidence and claim support, stance and voice support, and verification guidance. These functions were shaped by task demands: the summary-and-paraphrase task elicited more source-use and verification-oriented support, whereas the business report task elicited more organization-, evidence-, recommendation-, and stance-related support. Learners did not simply accept generated suggestions; they adapted, verified, queried, consulted on, rejected, or left them unresolved. Useful use emerged when learners treated suggestions as revisable resources and checked them against source meaning, evidence, attribution, task requirements, and authorial stance. Risk-prone use occurred when fluent but weakly verified suggestions were incorporated. The study contributes a learner-side account of GenAI-assisted EAP materials evaluation and offers design implications for accountable GenAI-mediated revision.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6666661`

- title: Can GenAI fill banks’ emissions data gaps?
- authors: Cristina Angelico, Enrico Bernardini
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6666661
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This paper presents new evidence highlighting significant limitations in emissions data for major listed banks in the euro area, sourced from four leading providers. Notable issues are data gaps, inconsistencies across providers and high volatility over time in scope 3 emissions, which are often inexplicably lower than scope 2 emissions and do not correlate with the banks’ exposure to high-emitting sectors. The paper then examines whether Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI), which relies on broader and diverse information sets, can help bridge the existing data gaps by comparing the outcomes from three GenAI tools. We find that GenAI-based emissions data, either estimated or retrieved, are correlated with data from traditional sources and may therefore help to partially fill current gaps and identify anomalies in the available data. Nevertheless, they suffer from similar issues in terms of quality and consistency to those documented for the data supplied by professional providers. Additional concerns regard replicability and transparency, underscoring the need for initial caution in their use. Despite the current limitations, looking forward, GenAI may become a valuable complementary data source as models that are fine-tuned for this task are developed. Future improvements will also depend on the availability of more reliable underlying information, which in turn requires parallel progress in defining simple, clear and actionable measurement standards as well as regulatory developments to promote climate disclosure by non-financial corporations within banks’ portfolios.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6657278`

- title: Adaptive Cross-Scale Fusion of Foundation Models for Expert-Level Eyelid Malignancy Diagnosis
- authors: Linyan Wang, Zihao Zhao, Yanyan Huang, Haixia Bai, Fengbo Huang, Bangxun Mao, Lequan Yu, Cheng Zhuo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6657278
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Background: Eyelid malignancies present a significant diagnostic challenge in computational pathology due to their diverse histological subtypes and the scarcity of large-scale annotated datasets. Current pathology foundation models often lack dedicated representation for these specific ocular tumors, limiting their ability to capture both global tissue architecture and local cellular pleomorphism simultaneously. Methods: In this multicenter retrospective study, we analyzed hematoxylin and eosin (H\&E)-stained whole-slide images (WSIs) of 1,054 patients with eyelid tumors. We developed ELSiP (Eyelid malignancies diagnosis with Slide and Patch integration), a deep learning framework designed to perform binary classification (benign versus malignant) and fine-grained subtyping across nine clinically distinct subtypes. The model was trained and internally validated on a primary cohort comprising 819 patients (1,061 WSIs) from The Second Affiliated Hospital of Zhejiang University. ELSiP utilizes a Slide-Guided Attention (SGA) mechanism to synergistically fuse cross-scale representations—integrating global slide-level context with local cellular details—and incorporates patient clinical metadata (age, sex, and specimen site) to calibrate predictions. Beyond internal validation, the framework was externally evaluated on an independent retrospective cohort of 235 patients (369 WSIs) from the Lishui Hospital of Traditional Chinese Medicine. Model interpretability was enhanced using spatially-resolved attention heatmaps, and its diagnostic capabilities were directly benchmarked against a panel of ophthalmic pathologists. Findings: ELSiP achieved an area under the curve (AUC) of 0.998 and an accuracy of 0.977 for the binary classification of benign versus malignant lesions, alongside a macro-average AUC of 0.982 for the comprehensive nine-class subtyping task in the internal cohort. In the independent external validation cohort, the model maintained robust generalisability, yielding AUCs of 0.943 and 0.930 for the binary and eight-class tasks, respectively. Furthermore, in a head-to-head comparative study, ELSiP demonstrated expert-level precision in the complex nine-class diagnostic challenge (accuracy 0.865), surpassing all five participating board-certified pathologists and outperforming the most experienced senior pathologist by a margin of 0.025. Interpretability analysis using attention heatmaps confirmed that the model's predictions were grounded in established morphological criteria, accurately highlighting critical diagnostic features. Interpretation: The ELSiP framework effectively addresses the dual challenges of data scarcity and subtype complexity in eyelid malignancy diagnosis. By fusing multi-scale morphological features with clinical context, it provides a high-precision, scalable tool for computational pathology. This approach highlights the potential for specializing foundation models in rare or complex disease domains to support clinical decision-making.Funding.
