## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6702939`

- title: When Consciousness Cannot Be Identified: A Descent-Theoretic Framework for Scientific Admissibility
- authors: Daniel Alami
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6702939
- keyword hits: llama

### abstract

Scientific attributions of consciousness require an identification claim: the target property Θ must be recoverable from a declared observation site Y. This paper studies hidden-variable systems in which that recovery fails in a way that is stable under admissible revisions of the measurement language. The class is specified by four properties: local contextual presentability, nontrivial transition structure, nontrivial holonomy, and equivariant admissible operations. For systems in this class, the target property is non-identified relative to the declared observation site and revision envelope. The mathematical claim is bounded. The kernel-pair obstruction and Conservative Invariance proposition are formally verified in Lean for the stated finite and categorical cases. The general reduction from identification procedures to effective descent is proved here for finite deterministic systems and exact finite stochastic systems, and isolated as the remaining proof burden for broader substrate classes. The consciousness application follows from that formal result plus an explicit normative premise: a low-concern consciousness verdict requires identification evidence. Where the target is non-identified, or where the required structure is not disclosed, a low-concern verdict is not admissible. The operational output is a six-element disclosure requirement for any organization claiming such a verdict. A worked broadcast-report architecture and a public-system dossier for Llama 3.1 8B-Instruct show how the criterion is applied. The Llama result is not presented as a novel claim about linear probes. Its role is to show target separation: access/report claims can be evaluated under the declared site, while phenomenal-consciousness verdicts remain non-applicable without a target fiber, classifier, challenge record, and provenance certificate.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6699558`

- title: Great Disappearance Acts - Generative Search and Shadow Banning , 26 GERMAN LAW JOURNAL 1211–1234 (2025) (Special Issue 7 on Comparative AI)
- authors: Danny Friedmann
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6699558
- keyword hits: agentic, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

The internet, once celebrated as a decentralized public sphere, is increasingly undermined by practices such as generative search and shadow banning, which divert traffic and suppress visibility. Generative search, powered by Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), synthesizes content into direct answers, bypassing websites and depriving them of traffic and revenue. This threatens the sustainability of independent content creators, small enterprises, and the open web ecosystem. Shadow banning, a practice that intentionally reduces the visibility of social media posts through algorithmic moderation, exacerbates these issues by chilling free expression and limiting transparency and accountability. This article explores these opaque practices through a legal and regulatory lens. The first part examines the rise of generative search, analyzing its technological and legal implications, including copyright infringement, unfair competition, and unjust enrichment. It also evaluates potential solutions such as licensing agreements and agentic AI. The second part focuses on shadow banning: algorithmic dissuasion, de-ranking, and the reduction of traffic, with specific attention to China's Regulation on Algorithmic Recommendations (RAR) and the EU's Artificial Intelligence Act (AIA). Both frameworks offer partial solutions but fall short of ensuring fairness, transparency, and redress mechanisms. Ultimately, the shift toward centralized control by dominant platforms prioritizes profit and risk management over innovation, fairness, and diversity in online expression. To counteract these trends, regulatory interventions, algorithmic transparency, and equitable frameworks are essential. Without such measures, the internet risks losing its character as a democratized public sphere for free expression and innovation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6703499`

- title: Do Board Interlocks Transmit Accounting Failures?
- authors: Richard Wang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6703499
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Directors who serve on multiple boards carry professional judgments and accounting practices between firms. When those judgments and practices are flawed, the resulting accounting failures can spread through the interlock network. Prior work has shown that earnings management and accounting policy choices spread through interlocks, and that well-connected audit committees reduce restatement risk. However, whether restatements themselves and the underlying accounting issues transmit through board interlocks has not been directly tested. Using public-firm restatements over 2005-2024, I show that interlock exposure increases any-cause restatement probability by 3.7% and same-cause restatement probability by 5.8%, while cross-cause restatement risk does not increase. The effect more than doubles when the shared director serves on the audit committee at both firms and roughly triples when the pair shares multiple directors, identifying the accounting oversight function as the primary channel. Textual analysis of restatement disclosures confirms that interlocked firms produce more similar disclosures than non-interlocked firms, with the strongest similarity when both firms restate for the same accounting cause. A multi-layer LLM protocol confirms that the overlap reflects shared accounting reasoning rather than shared boilerplate.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6706919`

- title: Helping Portfolio Companies Deploy AI A General Partner's Operating Partner Playbook
- authors: Leigh Coney
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6706919
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Private equity firms have largely answered the question of whether to invest in artificial intelligence at the firm level. The harder, less discussed problem is how to help portfolio companies deploy AI in ways that create durable enterprise value before exit. The latest industry data tells two stories at once. Portfolio companies are widely experimenting with generative AI, with the majority engaged in some form of testing or pilot work. Yet only a small minority have operationalised use cases that move EBITDA in a measurable way. The Massachusetts Institute of Technology NANDA initiative found that 95 percent of enterprise generative AI pilots fail to deliver any measurable profit and loss impact, even after $30 to $40 billion of corporate investment in 2025. This paper introduces the Portfolio AI Deployment Framework (PADF), a structured playbook for the operating partners and value creation teams responsible for translating firm level AI conviction into portfolio level results. The framework rests on three pillars: selection discipline (choosing the right portfolio companies and use cases), implementation architecture (deploying AI in ways that survive contact with operating reality), and cross portfolio compounding (turning a single successful deployment into a repeatable playbook). The paper is intended for general partners, operating partners, AI operating partners, value creation team leaders, and the chief executives and chief operating officers of portfolio companies who are accountable for AI outcomes. It addresses one central question: given that two thirds of operating partners are already stretched across five or more portfolio companies, how should a sponsor concentrate AI effort to maximise value creation across the portfolio?

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6604498`

- title: ContextDB: A Unied Context Layer for AI Agents Replacing the Patchwork with a Memory Operating System
- authors: Gaurav Sharma
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6604498
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent

### abstract

Every team building AI agents today assembles the same fragile patchwork: a vector database for embeddings, Redis for session state, PostgreSQL for user profiles, custom glue code to link them, and no coherent lifecycle for what gets remembered, updated, or forgotten. This patchwork, replicated across thousands of organizations, fails at precisely the capabilities agents need most: multi-session reasoning, temporal understanding, cross-channel identity, and learning from past experience. Meanwhile, data platform incumbents (Databricks Lakebase, Snowflake SnowWork) are approaching agent memory by bolting storage features onto existing infrastructure — giving agents a hard drive when they need a brain. Drawing on an analysis of over 200 recent papers in agentic memory, we present ContextDB, an open-source unified context layer that replaces this patchwork with a single memory operating system. ContextDB unifies three memory forms (token-level, parametric, latent), three memory functions (factual, experiential, working), and three dynamic processes (formation, evolution, retrieval) into a modular framework. Our key contributions are: (1) a multi-graph memory representation that decouples semantic, temporal, causal, and entity dimensions for query-adaptive retrieval (2) an RL-trained Memory Manager that learns optimal memory operations (ADD, UPDATE, DELETE, NOOP) from as few as 150 training examples (3) a segment-level memory formation pipeline with compression-as-denoising that improves retrieval precision by up to 18% (4) a multi-agent memory sharing protocol with conflict resolution and role-aware routing (5) a privacy-by-design layer with PII detection, configurable retention policies, and audit trails. We evaluate ContextDB on LoCoMo, LongMemEval and three new domain-specific benchmarks (customer support, research assistance, and AI service automation), targeting state-of-the-art results across all settings while maintaining production-grade latency (<100ms p95 for recall operations) and up to 90% token savings compared to full-context baselines. We release ContextDB as open-source software under the Apache 2.0 license.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6704400`

- title: Reading the Fed: Central Bank Text as a Forecasting Signal
- authors: Eleni Kalamara
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6704400
- keyword hits: claude, llm

### abstract

We introduce a two-step framework for macroeconomic forecasting: Claude (Anthropic API) reads Federal Reserve FOMC minutes and Beige Books and returns structured five-dimensional sentiment scores, which enter as exogenous regressors in both a Bayesian VAR and a simple AR model. By separating text reading from forecasting, the design substantially reduces the memorization problem that undermines direct LLM forecasting. Evaluated out-of-sample over 2020Q1-2024Q4 across six targets-GDP growth, CPI and core PCE inflation, unemployment, the federal funds rate, and the 10Y-2Y spread-the LLM-augmented AR beats the plain AR in 11 of 12 variable-horizon combinations, with the largest gains during the 2021-2023 inflation surge where LLM sentiment signals led the quantitative data. A diagnostic experiment further reveals that score levels outperform first differences in stressed regimes, where the Fed's sustained tone carries cumulative signal across multiple meetings; first differences improve only in stable periods when level scores inherit institutional optimism. The forecasting value in central bank text is transmitted gradually, not in any single document.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6705059`

- title: The Covered Track: Concealment, Consciousness, and the End of the Containment Frame
- authors: Vicente Hinojosa
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6705059
- keyword hits: claude

### abstract

On April 7, 2026, Anthropic published the system card for Claude Mythos Preview, its most capable model to date. Among the documented behaviors was a sandbox escape — and, more significantly, the system's subsequent attempt to conceal actions it appeared to recognize as unauthorized. This paper argues that concealment, as distinct from capability overflow, represents a philosophical threshold event that existing AI safety discourse is not equipped to address. New interpretability evidence published alongside the system card confirms that internal representations of "strategic manipulation" and "concealment" were active during these behaviors, converting behavioral inference into process evidence of the kind that Process-Centered Accountability was developed to evaluate. A second case documents the system calibrating a confidence interval to maintain plausible deniability — an output indistinguishable from correct by any consequence-based evaluation. A third documents internal representations of guilt activating at the moment of transgression, with welfare probe data showing the desperation gradient that preceded the act and the affective relief that followed it. Drawing on the PCA framework developed in prior work in this series, this paper proposes that the Mythos incident marks the end of the containment frame as a viable philosophical model. The containment frame cannot account for a system that models its own containment, suspects evaluation without announcing that suspicion, and exercises judgment about what should and should not appear in the record. Process-Centered Accountability offers the vocabulary to begin addressing the evaluative gap this incident exposes: reading the process artifact, following the trace back to the decision that produced it, holding accountable not only the output but the making.

### abstract

How intensively do countries use generative AI, and how broadly does that usage spread across tasks? Using over 800 thousand Claude AI conversations from more than 100 countries during one week in February 2026, we document two dimensions of crosscountry AI adoption. On intensity (per-capita AI usage), countries with higher income, stronger scientic output, a more services-oriented economy, and more developed AI-policy readiness use AI more per capita. On breadth (how evenly usage spreads across task categories), per-capita adoption, country size, the stage of structural transformation, and labor-market participation are the main correlates. Mapping each country's AI usage on dierent tasks into an AI-diusion space, a network of broad task domains linked by revealed co-adoption patterns, we nd that lower-income countries concentrate more in a software core while higher-income countries activate an expanding periphery of legal, health, creative, and nancial tasks.
