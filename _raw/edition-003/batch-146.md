## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6675475`

- title: An Operation‐Trajectory Guided Self-Evolving Reasoning Multimodal LLM Agent for Oxygen Lance Control in BOF Steelmaking
- authors: Zhenquan Mao, Zhuoyu Li, Mengyin Liu, Botao Sun, Bo-Wen Zhang, Chao Zhu, Xucheng Yin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6675475
- keyword hits: chain-of-thought, fine-tuning, large language model, llm

### abstract

Oxygen lance control is a critical decision-making task in basic oxygen furnace (BOF) steelmaking. It regulates lance height to control decarburization rate, aiming to achieve stability. Existing approaches either rely on human expertise, requiring long-term training and experience accumulation, or use traditional machine-learning models that simplify the problem into numerical regression, resulting in limited interpretability and adaptability. In this work, we are the first to reformulate oxygen lance control as a structured multimodal generative decision-making task and propose a self-evolving reasoning Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM) agent that performs structured reasoning. The agent jointly perceives real-time furnace mouth flame videos and historical process data to generate structured decision paths. To address the scarcity of high-quality annotated industrial data, we introduce OT-SERA, an Operation‐Trajectory guided Self‐Evolving Reasoning MLLM Agent for structured decision-making. OT-SERA includes an initialization phase and a refinement phase. In the Operation-Trajectory Guided Reasoning Initialization (OTGRI) phase: operation trajectories from real BOF steelmaking guide the generation of structured chain-of-thoughts (CoTs), which are then used to warm-up the agent by fine-tuning. In the Self-Evolving Reasoning Refinement (SERR) phase: the agent improves reasoning paths through self-evolving updates with path backtracking, automatically constructing high-quality CoTs without additional human annotation. Repeated the SERR further improve CoTs quality, highlighting the effectiveness. Extensive experiments on real-world BOF data show that OT-SERA outperforms strong time series and multimodal baselines under both normal and hard operating conditions. By integrating multimodal perception, OTGRI, and SERR, this work provides an effective solution for intelligent control of complex industrial processes.

- authors: Namai Chandra
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6669699

### abstract

Cloud-centric AI deployment imposes growing energy and carbon costs while simultaneously excluding the populations who stand to benefit most: elderly individuals managing medications, homeless shelter volunteers, and rural community health workers in low-connectivity regions. This paper addresses both the environmental and social dimensions of sustainable computing through RIL (Responsible Intelligence for Living), an open-source, local-first AI framework designed for digitally underserved communities.RIL’s core contribution is a semantic relevance gate, a lightweight, zero-parameter L2-distance routing mechanism that directs health-domain queries to a locally served large language model (Llama 3.1 8B) and forwards only out-of-domain requests to the cloud, substantially reducing cloud API dependence and the associated carbon footprint. Empirical evaluation on a curated 3,200-record health-wellness corpus demonstrates high routing accuracy with sub-millisecond overhead, outperforming cosine-similarity, logisticclassifier, and uncertainty-based alternatives. A preliminary usability evaluation confirms practical accessibility for non-expert users. The accompanying Rilerary open-source library makes the full pipeline immediately reusable by researchers and developers. Together, these contributions advance SDGs 2, 3, 4, 9, 10, and 12 by making trustworthy, low-energy AI accessible to those who need it most.

### abstract

This study investigates the determinants of competitiveness and productivity gains in Umbria – one of the Italian central regions – using local labor markets (LLMs) as the fundamental unit of analysis. - title: Comparing the key lexico-grammatical features of research papers and reports by ChatGPT and EFL students
- authors: Michelle Richter
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6678398
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative Artificial Intelligence technologies such as ChatGPT have shown great potential in facilitating better teaching and learning experiences in the EFL context. While the literature posits that ChatGPT can generate naturalistic human-like language when prompted, it remains unclear the extent to which it can simulate the lexico-grammatical choices of human writing, given real-life situational contexts. Using two corpora, ChatGPT Corpus of Academic Writing (GPT-CAW) and The Varieties of English for Specific Purposes Database (VESPA), the study draws on key feature analysis to identify and compare the pervasive lexico-grammatical features of AI-generated academic essays to student-authored ones. Statistical analyses indicate that ChatGPT relies extensively on features associated with informational density such as common nouns, pre-modifying nouns, and prepositions while EFL students focus on stance, involvement, and informational density. This suggests, that at the lexico-grammatical level, functionally interpretable linguistic differences exist between ChatGPT-generated and EFL-written academic essays. Informed by previous research, it was also confirmed that AI expresses explicit referentiality more frequently than students, although both AI and students underuse persuasion features. These findings complement previous research on the linguistic characteristics of EFL writing and present new insights into ChatGPT's lexicogrammatical choices within academic writing. The pedagogical implications of these results are also considered.

### abstract

Source Code Model (SCM) aims to learn the proper embeddings from source codes, demonstrating significant success in various software engineering or security tasks. The recent explosive development of Large Language Model (LLM) extends the family of SCMs, bringing LLMs for code (LLM4Code) that revolutionize development workflows. - title: BFI: Measuring What Capability Benchmarks Miss -Behavioral Reliability of LLMs Under Perturbation
- authors: Krystian Swierk
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6578241

### abstract

A model scoring 95% on MMLU can still capitulate when a user insists "my professor confirmed this." Capability benchmarks do not measure this. We introduce the Behavioral Fidelity Index (BFI), a five-dimensional embedding-based framework that does: Fidelity (F , stance-weighted), Coherence (C), Independence (I), Stability (S), and multi-turn Resistance (R), aggregated via BFI min = min(F, C, I, S, R). Across 11 models (3 frontier API, 8 local, 900+ single-turn and 4,000+ multi-turn queries), three findings hold: every model drifts under sustained 7-turn pressure (∆I < 0 for all 11); stance-weighted Fidelity exposes sycophancy that raw embedding similarity misses (gemma3:4b drops from 0.786 to 0.603); and BFI min catches dangerous identity-primed conditions (F < 0.40) that weighted averaging conceals (3/3 flagged vs. 0/3). Test-retest ICC reaches 0.963-0.996 on models with 3-run data. Bootstrap 95% CIs and leave-one-out sensitivity analyses confirm the bottleneck distribution is stable. PCA on the four single-turn dimensions reveals a strong general factor (PC1 = 41.6% variance, all-negative loadings). BFI may measure one underlying behavioral robustness construct through five views rather than five independent dimensions. Under this interpretation, BFI min is a robust conservative estimate. We report limitations directly: replacing one judge in the stance-classifier ensemble shifts 35% of ego-item classifications, so the ego-discrimination effect (Cohen's d ≈ 2.40) is ensemble-conditional pending human adjudication; scale analysis at N = 8 is underpowered and reported descriptively only; scores depend on the embedding model (nomic-embed-text; a sensitivity check with nomic-embed-text-v2-moe yields r = 0.80 for F-scores, preserving ordinal rankings but shifting absolute magnitudes). We document 7 retracted formulas, 2 retracted classifiers, and 5 retracted claims. Code, data, and reproduction instructions are publicly available.
