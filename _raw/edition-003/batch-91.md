## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6804000`

- title: Generative Equilibrium for Biomedical IoMT: A CPST-Informed Integrative Review and Conceptual Framework for Security, Privacy, and Clinical Usability
- authors: Tharcisse Ndayipfukamiye, Jianguo Ding, Huansheng Ning
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6804000
- keyword hits: agentic, generative ai, large language model, large language models, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

The Internet of Medical Things (IoMT) promises data-driven healthcare, yet its clinical adoption remains constrained by a persistent security-privacy-usability trilemma. Stronger security controls can increase alert fatigue and workflow disruption; stronger privacy mechanisms may reduce clinical utility; and usability-driven simplification can expose systems to avoidable risks. Prior surveys examine these dimensions separately, but rarely explain how they interact in clinically sensitive IoMT environments. We conducted an integrative review of 162 peer-reviewed studies published between 2021 and April 2026. Using the Cyber-Physical-Social-Thinking (CPST) lens, we categorized each study by the dimensions it substantively addresses and synthesized evidence across generative AI (GenAI) techniques, including GANs, transformers, large language models, retrieval-augmented generation, federated learning, agentic IoT, and digital twins. Our findings show that current GenAIenabled IoMT research is technically strong but clinically incomplete: the Cyber dimension appears in 71.6% of reviewed studies in any CPST combination, whereas the Social/usability dimension appears in only 11.1%, and no reviewed study addresses all four CPST dimensions simultaneously. To address this gap, we propose the Generative Equilibrium Framework (GEF), a testable CPSTinformed conceptual architecture for coordinating security, privacy, and usability. GEF provides normalized candidate metrics, a closed-loop control algorithm, and an implementation roadmap for empirical validation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6818764`

- title: Large Language Model as a Code Generator in Simplified Domain-Specific Modelling
- authors: Adam Bochenek, Jarosław Protasiewicz, Witold Pedrycz
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6818764
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Context: The increasing complexity of modern software systems poses challenges for traditional development methods. Manual conversion of specifications into source code often leads to inefficiencies, inconsistencies, and higher maintenance costs. Model-Driven Engineering (MDE) aims to address these issues by elevating the level of abstraction. However, it still faces adopting barriers, methodological gaps, and limitations in tools, by one of which is Domain-Specific Modelling (DSM). Objectives: This study introduces Simplified Domain-Specific Modelling (SDSM), a streamlined variant of DSM. SDSM manages without domain-specific languages, relying instead on simple data structures and a clearly defined implementation procedure to reduce implementation complexity and improve reusability in business application development, which makes it a better choice compared to DSM. Methods: We integrate Large Language Models (LLMs) as formal code generators within the SDSM framework. This integration transforms LLMs from ad-hoc programming assistants into structured, repeatable components of the software engineering process. To the best of our knowledge, this proposal is the first to simplify DSM by eliminating a Domain-Specific Language (DSL) and utilizing LLMs as code generators in this process. Results: Two real-world experiments compared LLM-based generators to traditional Template-Based Code Generation and programmatic approaches. The evaluation concerned code quality and productivity. Results indicate that, in specific contexts, LLMs can effectively replace conventional generators, achieving competitive code quality and productivity improvements while reducing repetitive, non-creative coding tasks. Conclusion: Our findings provide empirical evidence for the underexplored intersection of MDE and AI, highlighting both the potential and current limitations of LLM-driven model-based software engineering. We provide practical implication that the SDSM method and LLMs form an integrated software development framework that can be utilised in real-world software.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6783159`

- title: Interpreting Sensory Experience in Customer-generated Text: A Large Language Model (LLM) Approach
- authors: Tianyi Zhang, Jonas Schöne, Zhongtian Ji, Carlos Velasco, Charles Spence
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6783159
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Sensory dimension is a central component of how consumers evaluate products and services, and online reviews offer a rich record of how these experiences are described. Prior research has used lexicon-based text analysis to extract sensory cues from customer language by mapping words to predefined sensory dictionaries and examining their effects on ratings. However, such approaches rely on fixed word–sense mappings and struggle to capture the context-dependent nature of sensory language. A context-sensitive approach using large language models (LLMs) to interpret sensory language in customer reviews is introduced. Validated against human coders and lexicon-based methods across 82,350 restaurant reviews, the LLM achieves substantially higher reliability by capturing implicit and context-dependent sensory references. While lexicon-based approaches infer senses from predefined word categories, LLMs infer which sensory modalities are most likely activated given the linguistic context. Leveraging this approach, this research moves beyond identifying sensory cues to examine how sensory experiences are used in evaluation. The findings suggest that sensory modalities exhibit markedly different associations with ratings when considered in isolation, but these differences are largely explained by the evaluative contexts in which sensory experiences are expressed. Different sensory modalities are disproportionately associated with positive versus negative evaluations, with some primarily used to express hedonic assessments and others more frequently invoked as diagnostic cues in negative evaluations. This study demonstrates how LLMs can be used to interpret customer experience from consumer text and provides a framework for uncovering the evaluative roles of sensory attributes that can be generalised across product types.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6782581`

- title: AI-Agent Framework for Sales Risk Identification and Response in Live-Commerce Environments
- authors: Jiho Kim, Sangmin Park, Yooshin Kwon, Heongi Kim, Hyunjae Cheon
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6782581
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llama

### abstract

Conventional rule-based approaches are widely used for analyzing customer evaluations in e-commerce and live-commerce environments. However, these approaches often show limitations in detecting newly emerging product issues and prioritizing business-critical risks. To address these limitations, this study proposes an intelligent AI-agent framework for proactive product-risk identification and sales-response support. First, semantic embeddings generated using Sentence-Transformers are integrated with the HDBSCAN clustering algorithm to identify latent issue patterns and remove noisy evaluations. Subsequently, class-based term frequency-inverse document frequency is employed to extract representative keywords from clustered customer feedback. Based on the extracted information, the Llama large language model automatically generates product defect categories and issue descriptions. In addition, this study introduces a risk-scoring mechanism incorporating vote-related metadata to identify defect issues with high customer churn potential. The most influential defect category is further analyzed using aspect-level sentiment analysis to derive fine-grained customer sentiment patterns. Finally, the proposed framework automatically generates objection-response guidelines that can be directly utilized in live-commerce broadcasting and customer-response operations. The proposed framework demonstrates how large language models can be integrated into an automated decision-support architecture for proactive risk management in e-commerce environments.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6819391`

- title: Runtime generative editing of web interfaces using grammar-constrained JSON-DSLs and LLMs
- authors: Enrique Chavarriaga, Francy  D. Rodríguez, Luis  Rojas Pino, Francisco Jurado
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6819391
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Runtime generative editing of web interfaces raises important challenges related to structural reliability, renderability, maintainability, and control over LLM-generated transformations. This study introduces RhoArchitecture, a declarative–generative client–server framework that combines grammar-constrained JSON-based Domain-Specific Languages (DSL-JSON) with Large Language Models (LLMs) to support controlled runtime adaptation of data-driven web interfaces. RhoArchitecture integrates three core components: RestRho, which defines RESTful services and orchestrates LLM-driven template transformations; RhoPortable, a lightweight frontend DSL-JSON engine for managing views, events, navigation, and REST bindings; and RhoLLM, a conversational component that enables natural-language editing of web interfaces within predefined grammar and template constraints. The framework was evaluated through the PTGenAI proof of concept using static software analysis and a comparative implementation study against React, Vue, and Svelte. The static analysis considered Halstead complexity, cyclomatic complexity, and the Maintainability Index, while the comparative study assessed structural compactness using effective Lines of Code (LOC). The results show favorable maintainability indicators and suggest that RhoArchitecture achieves functional equivalence with approximately 75–80% fewer effective LOC than imperative frontend implementations. In addition, the evaluation distinguishes syntactic validity, renderability, semantic acceptability, traceability, and reversibility of generative editing outcomes. These findings provide initial evidence that grammar-constrained DSL-JSON artifacts can reduce structurally invalid or non-renderable transformations while supporting controlled runtime adaptation. Further empirical studies are required to assess semantic correctness, developer effort, scalability, privacy, and robustness under repeated generative transformations.

- posted: 2026-05-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6739060

### abstract

According to global estimates 41% of all code is now AI-generated, with over 97% of developers adopting AI use even ahead of company policies or mandates [1] and even top engineers at Anthropic and OpenAI, such as Boris Cherny, the head of Claude Code, saying AI now writes 100% of their personal code [2]. As agentic AI automates entry-level programming tasks, a “Junior Gap” paradox is emerging: the traditional on-ramps that once built deep engineering judgment are disappearing, threatening long‑term skill development and the talent pipeline. Human oversight remains essential because LLM-based agents suffer from fundamental limitations—including hallucinations [3] [4] and contextual misunderstandings [5]—that make fully autonomous software development unreliable. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6826582`

- title: AdaScheduler: an SLO-aware LLM inference scheduling algorithm without output length prediction
- authors: Ling Liu, Shasha Zhou, Wang Xu, Pan Zhou, Xiaoqiong Xu, Liu Ling, Qiang Wang, Hongfang Yu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6826582
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

With the widespread adoption of Large Language Models (LLMs), a new generation of interactive artificial intelligence applications has emerged, such as code generation and online classification. These applications demand different job completion times (JCTs) for LLM inference (referred to as latency-sensitive (LS) requests), while some other applications such as document summary, may only require the LLM inference system to provide best-effort services (referred as best-effort (BE) requests). To enhance the user experience, the LLM scheduling for hybrid scenarios with hybrid LS and BE requests should be carefully considered. Most LLM scheduling algorithms for hybrid requests require a pre-estimation of the output token length of each request, which is extremely challenging in achieving accurate predictions. Therefore, this paper studies the scheduling algorithms that do not require predicting the output length and proposes AdaScheduler, an adaptive hybrid LLM serving system that optimizes the JCT SLO attainment ratio and system throughput. Specifically, AdaScheduler mainly consists of two parts, one is the priority-based scheduling that sets priorities for the hybrid requests, which determines the scheduling order in a coarse-grained manner. The other is lookahead dynamic batching, which calculates GPU memory usage in subsequent iterations based on task information and resource status, and determines the batch size and scheduling order in a more fine-grained manner. Besides, to reduce the computational overhead and the overhead of KV cache movement, the priorities are recalculated and the batch is formed only after each batch has executed a predetermined number of iterations. The evaluation results show that AdaScheduler significantly outperforms the baselines, achieving up to a 154.5% increase in SLO attainment ratio and a 53.04% improvement in system throughput.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6744998`

- title: The Dyslexic Indexic Encounters Structural Hallucination A Diagnosis of Epistemic Pathology and Legal Exposure in the Knowledge Industry
- authors: William C. Houze
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6744998
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

The global knowledge system presents itself as a comprehensive catalog of human inquiry, but its index is scrambled. Bibliographic databases segregate scholarship into mutually inaccessible silos; paywalls and authentication protocols restrict access along lines of institutional privilege; and large language models, increasingly the new mediators of discovery, train on corpora that constitute only a partial alphabet of recorded thought. This essay diagnoses an encounter between two pathologies of the contemporary research ecosystem. The dyslexic indexic — the scrambled, biased, and incomplete catalog by which the system reads itself — encounters structural hallucination, the resulting condition in which the system confidently asserts a coherent picture of the field on the basis of fragmentary evidence. Drawing on recent scientometric work on independent researchers, classical sociology of science, and analysis of the publisher oligopoly, the essay argues that the barriers to a unified knowledge system are political and economic rather than technological, and that the canonical telos-bearing corpora of the world’s civilizations function as the available counterweight to the smoothed, decontextualized output of the contemporary indexing apparatus. The essay further examines why the firms producing large language models have not publicly engaged with this critique, despite its growing technical and scholarly literature, and identifies the legal exposure their position creates. The same architectural feature that makes the structural-hallucination thesis intellectually powerful — its systemic rather than incidental character — establishes the foreseeability, common-question, and failure-to-warn predicates required for substantial tort liability under negligent-misrepresentation, products-liability, and class-action doctrines. The Infernal Engine — the symbolic downstream LLM, framed within Turing’s Imitation Game as a machine whose core function is the production of convincing simulation drawn from a structurally hallucinated corpus — is therefore positioned for a litigation horizon that the industry’s silence cannot indefinitely defer.
