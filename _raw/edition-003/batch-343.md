## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7115195`

- title: Can Generative Artificial Intelligence Enhance Firms' Export Resilience? — A Difference-in-Differences Analysis Based on Chinese Data
- authors: Mengjun Xie
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7115195
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This paper investigates whether and how generative artificial intelligence (GAI) enhances firms' export resilience. Drawing on organizational information processing theory and exploiting the staggered rollout of China's GAI service registration system as a quasi-natural experiment, we employ a staggered difference-in-differences approach with firm-level data. We find that GAI significantly enhances export resilience, primarily through three interconnected pathways: stabilizing import supply chains, securing production chains, and expanding export sales chains. Heterogeneity analysis shows that technology-intensive firms, capital-intensive firms, firms oriented toward developed markets, and those in growth and maturity stages benefit more from this effect. Threshold effect tests indicate that the enabling effect becomes significantly stronger only when a firm's capital intensity, export market complexity, and age cross specific thresholds. Spatial spillover tests demonstrate that GAI adoption by other firms positively affects a focal firm's export resilience, confirming a synergistic enabling effect along industrial chains. This study offers new theoretical insights into the microeconomic effects of GAI and provides important policy implications for enhancing the resilience of China's foreign trade development.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7119690`

- title: From ”blue” and ”red” to ”purple”: skill composition and dynamics in AI jobs in Australia and New Zealand
- authors: Alexandra Bratanova, David Evans, Claire Mason, Sarah Hartman, Haohui Chen, Hien Pham, Einat Grimberg, Stefan Hajkowicz
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7119690
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) is reshaping labour markets, yet evidence remains limited on how AI-related skill demand is evolving in smaller advanced economies. Using Lightcast job postings data, this study analyses AI-related hiring in Australia and New Zealand between 2015 and 2024. It examines where AI demand is concentrated and whether these roles are primarily technical (”blue”), human-centred (”red”), or hybrid (”purple”).,AI-related hiring increased substantially over the decade, rising from 1.3% to 4.2% of all job postings in Australia and from 0.1% to 3.2% in New Zealand. Demand remained strongest in Information Media and Telecommunications and Professional, Scientific and Technical Services, with Education and Training prominent in Australia and Financial and Insurance Services in New Zealand, but expanded into a wider set of occupations. Information and Communications Technology professionals remained a core group, while demand grew in education, management, creative and sector-specific occupations. In 2024, AI-related requirements appeared in 31% of postings for Authors and Book and Script Editors (level 4) in Australia and 10.4% of Arts and Media Professional (level 2) postings in New Zealand, indicating new generative-AI demand for language and editorial expertise.,The skill composition of AI-related roles also shifted. Technical capabilities such as machine learning, programming, data science and generative AI were consistently bundled with communication, research, problem-solving, planning, leadership, writing, management and detail orientation. Several of these human-centred skills accelerated after 2022, especially writing, leadership and detail orientation. The findings show that AI-related work in Australia and New Zealand is increasingly defined by hybrid ”purple” skill profiles, although the composition of these profiles differ across the two countries.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6960140`

- title: AI and Digital Tool Adoption in SMEs: A Structured, Theory-Building Review and a Prospective Supply-Chain Account, 2015-2025
- authors: Nahid Hasan Siam
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6960140
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) make up roughly nine in ten firms in most economies, yet they are the slowest part of the digital supply chain. This review asks what ten years of research (2015-2025) have shown about why SMEs adopt, or do not adopt, artificial intelligence (AI) and related digital technologies, and what happens when they do. It is a structured, theory-building review, and it takes clear positions. Most of the evidence covers general SME and Industry 4.0 digitalisation; work that is strictly about supply chains is a minority, so the supply-chain account offered here is a forward-looking contribution rather than a settled finding, and AI is treated as the newest step on a longer digital-adoption path. I argue five things. First, the field borrowed its main frameworks from research on large firms, and these fit SMEs poorly. Second, explanation has built up three logics that now sit side by side (cost, capability, and managerial cognition) rather than replacing one another, and the strongest cognitive claim holds mainly in higher-income settings. Third, the account does not travel well to emerging economies, where weak institutions make barriers lock together instead of simply adding up. Fourth, pressure from regulators and large buyers is joining digital and sustainability goals, which changes the reason to adopt in the first place. Fifth, the almost uniformly positive performance findings owe at least as much to weak research designs (one-off surveys, self-reported outcomes, surviving firms only) as to the technology itself. On this base I build an SME-native account in which adoption arrives through three channels: autonomous (the owner-manager's own judgement), mimetic (copying peers), and imposed (buyer or regulator demands). All three pass through the same gate, the firm's absorptive capacity, inside a wider institutional field, and they follow the logic of improvising effectuation rather than fixed stages. The account is stated as testable propositions, ranked by the strength of the evidence behind them. This review closes with a research agenda shaped by generative AI.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6959363`

- title: Data Integration and the Accounting Labor Market Effects of Generative AI
- authors: Charles Downing
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6959363
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This paper examines how generative AI (GAI) is reshaping demand for corporate accountants. GAI is a technological shock that requires complementary investments to realize productivity gains. I show that within-industry variation in prior investment in data integration-the extent to which information can flow across a firm's multiple internal systems automatically-predicts GAI adoption in the accounting function. I find that firms with one-standard-deviation-greater prior investment in data integration reduce accountant employment by 2.7% and job postings by 13% post-GAI. The declines are concentrated in junior positions, leaving demand for manager-level accountants stable, and are driven by attrition rather than layoffs. I then use the text of job postings and résumés to examine changes in the task content and skill requirements of remaining accounting jobs. I document a reduction in routine information processing tasks and a shift toward analytic and strategic tasks, along with wage premia for manager-level roles performing more expert work. Data-integrated firms increasingly seek candidates with CPAs, technical backgrounds, and advanced degrees. Taken together, the evidence is consistent with GAI reducing the demand for junior accountants and pushing the profession toward higher-skill roles requiring greater accounting expertise.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7105638`

- title: The Right Question, the Wrong Design: A Critique of How Do Judges Use Large Language Models? Evidence from Shenzhen
- authors: Luis Alejandro Moya
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7105638
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

In 2025, Liu and Li asked whether AI amplifies judges' biases. Their study of one of China's largest courts combined a case study with a randomised controlled trial. The findings proved inconclusive. This critique examines their research design and quantitative methods. It argues that the study falls short of its promise. A self-selected sample, a probable Hawthorne effect, an underpowered design, and a misapplied linear regression undermine both internal and external validity. These flaws stem from fixable design choices. A difference-indifferences evaluation of real Shenzhen decisions is proposed as a stronger alternative.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6962978`

- title: Delegating to AI: Beliefs and the Organization of Work at New Ventures
- authors: Innessa Colaiacovo, Rembrand Koning
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6962978
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

When a technology emerges that may enhance productivity, managers must decide whether and how to apply it to their firms. We recruit founders of North American tech startups willing to share indepth information about their experiences with generative AI and their perceptions of its impacts on their ability to scale. We first document substantial heterogeneity among the AI users in our sample: while founders in our sample estimate that in the absence of AI they would need 55% more employees, the median is 17% and nearly a third estimate no additional employees would be needed. Founders who formally integrate AI into their workflows estimate their required headcount increases would be nearly four times higher than those of informal users, and are more likely to report that AI has changed hiring and management. We then show that founders can have different preferences for delegating to AI, holding constant their beliefs about how well it performs a given task relative to humans. We randomly expose founders to optimistic or pessimistic information about AI performance and show that even when these task performance beliefs and delegation preferences update on average, substantial managerial heterogeneity persists in willingness to delegate to AI. These results underscore that, as with prior transformative technologies, the impact of AI on work and organizations is not only related to what the technology can do but also what managers choose to do with it.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7116980`

- title: Decision Support at the Edge of the Firm: Artificial Intelligence, Human and Agent Collaboration, and the Resilience of Microbusiness Venture Creation Across the United States
- authors: Md Raihanul Islam
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7116980
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Decision support systems are increasingly framed as instruments through which entrepreneurs interpret uncertainty and sustain ventures under disruption, and generative artificial intelligence has rapidly become the most widely adopted form of such support among small firms. Whether the manner in which entrepreneurs collaborate with these systems translates into more resilient venture creation remains untested with large-scale observational data. This study examines that question across the United States. It links a recent measure of how people in each state divide their work with an artificial intelligence assistant, expressed as the share of usage that is augmentative rather than automative and drawn from the Anthropic Economic Index for April and May 2026, to four resilience outcomes constructed from the United States Census Bureau Business Formation Statistics covering 2004 to 2026. Resilience is operationalized as recovery relative to the baseline before the pandemic, deviation from the pre-shock trajectory, depth of the pandemic trough, and volatility of new business applications. Three complementary estimators are applied to fifty states and the District of Columbia, namely ordinary least squares regression with robust standard errors, a gradient boosting model with attribution analysis, and partial least squares structural equation modelling, and three further analyses decompose the mechanism through mediation, dominance, and spatial diagnostics. The raw association between augmentative collaboration and formation recovery is negative and modest, with correlations between minus 0.25 and minus 0.34. Once state unemployment, population growth, net domestic migration, and the baseline level of formation are controlled, the association disappears for every outcome, with all p values above 0.17, and it does not return under region or division fixed effects. Net domestic migration emerges as the consistent driver, accounting through dominance analysis for the largest share of explained variance and channeling about half of the raw association in the confounding decomposition. The contribution is an explained association rather than a bare null, in which an apparent technology effect is shown to be the footprint of the pandemic-era reshuffling of population toward lower-cost states. The design has power to rule out a large or moderate aggregate effect but not a small one, so the conclusion is that there is no evidence of a substantial effect of collaboration style on aggregate venture creation, while a small effect cannot be excluded. The finding cautions against attributing resilience outcomes to artificial intelligence adoption when economic geography offers a more parsimonious explanation, and it reframes the behavioral contribution of decision support toward stability and process rather than aggregate venture counts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6960860`

- title: AI Adoption, Workplace Collaboration, and Mean Field Games
- authors: Christos Makridis
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6960860
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This paper studies generative AI adoption inside firms as a finite-type network mean field game with adaptive expectations. Workers are heterogeneous agents whose states-AI capability and task-specific human capital-evolve through own AI use, learning, and collaboration with AIcapable coworkers. Types summarize positions in the workplace collaboration structure, such as degree, team role, or exposure to AI-capable peers, and a row-stochastic mixing matrix maps type-level distributions into local peer environments. Managerial clarity enters through the perceived return to AI-enabled output and the speed of belief updating. The model yields a backward Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation for each worker type, a forward Fokker-Planck equation for the type-specific distributions, and consistency conditions linking optimal policies to aggregate AI capability, collaboration, and perceived returns. A numerical exercise provides an illustrative finite-population network approximation designed to isolate the mechanism implied by the model. Managerial clarity can operate like a control parameter, shifting collaboration as an order parameter and accelerating convergence to the AI-capability frontier.
