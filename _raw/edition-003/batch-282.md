- title: AI-Driven Autonomous Enterprises and the Future of Work: Impact, Ethics, and Value Creation by 2026
- authors: Abhinav Mahajan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6704078
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) systems are moving from assistive tools into enterprise workflows where software agents retrieve information, call tools, draft artifacts, execute bounded actions, and ask for human intervention when risk rises. Written from a practitioner-scholar perspective, this paper reviews empirical evidence, technical design patterns, governance standards, and labor-market research relevant to AI-driven autonomous enterprises and the future of work by 2026. The paper's central argument is uncomfortable for firms that want a clean maturity roadmap: there is no smooth path from copilot to autonomous enterprise, because AI capability is jagged rather than uniform, and organizations that ignore this tend to automate confidently into exactly the tasks where AI fails. Evidence from randomized experiments and field studies shows substantial task-level productivity gains in writing, customer support, consulting, and programming; it also shows performance degradation outside validated task boundaries and heavy dependence on workflow redesign. Labor-market evidence indicates high exposure of occupations and tasks to generative AI, while macroeconomic effects remain uncertain and contingent on whether AI complements workers, substitutes for tasks, or enables new work. The review synthesizes this evidence into the concept of frontier-conditioned autonomy, a non-linear maturity model, an enterprise control architecture, an ethics and risk taxonomy, a value-accounting framework, and a research agenda. The highest-value autonomous enterprises by 2026 will not be those that maximize automation alone, but those that map AI's jagged frontier, preserve human responsibility for consequential decisions, measure business and human outcomes, and embed governance into enterprise platforms.

- title: From Specificity to Universalization: The Underlying Logic and Economic Implications of AI-Driven Government Automation
- authors: Ping Xu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6689518
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Governments worldwide have pursued "one-stop" service integration for decades. Yet a more ambitious goal-"one-stop completion" (yizhan daowei), in which a single interaction fully resolves a citizen's cross-departmental needs-remains elusive. This paper argues that the bottleneck lies not in technology but in an overlooked economic logic. Traditional government automation follows a specificity logic: every service is treated as a special case requiring bespoke processes, making each incremental crossdepartmental application costly. AI-particularly large language models and intelligent agentsenables a fundamental shift to a universalization logic: learning the abstract rules and intentions underlying specific services, then applying them to unlimited contexts at near-zero marginal cost. We formalize this distinction, develop a mathematical model of marginal costs, describe three enabling mechanisms (abstraction, intent recognition, and dynamic orchestration), and illustrate with empirical signals from China's recent AI government pilots-including Jiangxi's "Dingding Qiangdan" system (approximately 40% labor cost savings, covering 387 service types) and Beijing's "Feng Xiaozhi" intelligent agent (spanning 11 departments, processing 2,300 anomaly alerts daily). We also identify hard constraints and engage seriously with counterarguments from public administration theory-including accountability risks, discretion erosion, and legal adaptability-and offer responses. For government administrators, the central implication is to invest in shared AI infrastructure capable of learning the "universal logic" of services, rather than isolated point solutions. We conclude that universalization capacity will become the next stage of governmental efficiency and competitive advantage.

- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6725082
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Natural hazards cause hundreds of billions of dollars in losses annually, with impacts that disproportionately affect vulnerable populations. Earth observation (EO) provides comprehensive, repeatable measurements that are essential for assessing disaster impacts when ground-based systems fail. Over the past two decades, methods for post-disaster mapping impacts from EO data have evolved rapidly, from manual interpretation and spectral indices to deep learning and, most recently, foundation models. Yet this methodological diversity has outpaced systematic evaluation: most existing studies focus on individual hazards, single tasks, or specific method families, and lack reproducible cross-method comparison. In this paper, we provide a unified review and benchmark for EO-based disaster mapping. We define a taskdriven taxonomy distinguishing disaster extent mapping from infrastructure damage mapping, and systematically review methods across both tasks, spanning traditional, deep learning, and foundation model paradigms. We then benchmark over 30 methods under a standardized protocol across 13 datasets covering diverse hazards, sensors, and operational settings. Our studies reveal that architectural complexity yields diminishing returns for extent mapping, that change detection architectures benefit damage mapping but not extent mapping, and that foundation models do not yet consistently outperform task-specific baselines, particularly on non-RGB modalities. Based on these findings, we identify key priorities for advancing operational disaster mapping. Code and benchmark resources are publicly available at https://github.com/ChenHongruixuan/AnyDisasterMapping

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6750101`

- title: Examining Patent Disclosures
- authors: Lisa Larrimore Ouellette, Jason Reinecke
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6750101
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Patents are premised on a fundamental bargain: in exchange for exclusive rights, inventors must disclose how to make and use their inventions. But the patent bargain is eroding. This Article shows that patent examination and litigation overwhelmingly concentrate on whether an invention is new, while largely overlooking whether the patent’s teachings are practically usable or scientifically credible. When disclosure issues arise, they typically focus on what we call “scope-evidence mismatch,” which occurs when an applicant fails to provide any support for part of the claimed invention. In contrast, scientific peer review—the other significant context in which technical disclosures are scrutinized—routinely surfaces disclosure problems with the support itself, like flawed methodology or data analysis. It would be surprising if these defects in scientific manuscripts were absent from patent applications. This Article surfaces the disclosure issues that peer review focuses on and that patent law neglects. Drawing from doctrine and scientific practice, we develop a taxonomy of disclosure breakdowns beyond the “scope-evidence mismatch” frame—aspirational disclosures, defective teaching, and unreliable support—and show how each maps onto existing but underused doctrinal hooks. We then present new mixed-methods evidence from interviews, public records, and examination and litigation decisions to explore why the patent system under-polices these problems. Examiners face severe time pressure, limited training, and weak incentives; litigation rarely corrects these errors because technical disputes are hard to resolve pretrial and difficult to integrate into a coherent trial narrative. The stakes of these disclosure failures are rising. Modern innovation depends on data- and biology-driven techniques that require complex and validation-sensitive disclosures. And generative artificial intelligence tools are making it feasible to flood the system with plausible-sounding but erroneous patents. The lack of a robust disclosure screen means not only that examiners mistakenly grant patents that fail to deliver the promised teaching, but also that these poorly supported patents can improperly block later inventors who do meaningful inventive work. The result is a patent literature that often fails to educate patent readers or ensure that patent rights are limited to what the inventors actually contributed, increasing costs for follow-on researchers and the public. Drawing from our diagnoses, the Article concludes with concrete reforms to strengthen patent disclosure and restore the patent bargain.

- title: The impact of ChatGPT on developing creative thinking and writing in students
- authors: Binbin Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6724724
- keyword hits: chatgpt, large language model

### abstract

Since the release of the large language model ChatGPT in 2022, research on its impact on creative thinking and writing has been growing. However, most studies examined ChatGPT's impact on a single dimension of students' creative thinking or writing in isolation, with few examining its impact on both dimensions simultaneously. This raised questions regarding the nature of ChatGPT's impact on students' creative thinking and writing, as well as how teachers can utilize ChatGPT to enhance these skills. To address these questions, this study employed a narrative literature review method to analyze 30 research articles examining ChatGPT's impact on students' creative thinking and writing. The analysis revealed that ChatGPT's interactive dialogue features can encourage students to ask questions proactively and engage in independent exploration of knowledge, thereby fostering creative thinking. However, if students passively accept information without questioning its authenticity or exploring alternative perspectives, this may hinder the development of creative thinking. Furthermore, ChatGPT's real-time feedback feature can help students expand their writing ideas and spark creative inspiration, thereby enhancing their creative writing abilities. However, when students over-rely on answers generated by ChatGPT, there may be a risk of homogenization in creative writing. Therefore, teachers can encourage students to use ChatGPT as a supplementary tool rather than relying on it to complete all learning tasks. In sum, this study addressed the limitations of existing research, which examined ChatGPT's impact on either creative thinking or writing in isolation, by providing a comprehensive analytical framework that integrates both dimensions. Furthermore, the study adopted a balanced perspective, acknowledging both the potential benefits and the possible drawbacks of ChatGPT. Moreover, the study provided actionable guidelines for teachers on how to effectively utilize ChatGPT in the classroom to enhance students' creative thinking and writing skills.
