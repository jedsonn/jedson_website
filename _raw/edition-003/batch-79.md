## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6637619`

- title: The Compression Wave: Digital Maturity and the Evolutionary Fate of Modern Culture
- authors: J.M. Bailey
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6637619
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Global fertility rates are currently converging toward unprecedented lows, with digitally mature nations exhibiting Total Fertility Rates (TFR) far below the replacement threshold. While standard economic models attribute this decline to the "quality-quantity" trade-off, they fail to explain why fertility remains suppressed despite significant pro-natalist financial interventions. This paper proposes a novel theoretical framework-the "Compression Wave"-to explain this demographic-evolutionary paradox. Synthesizing longitudinal demographic data with cultural evolutionary theory, I argue that digital maturity acts as an environmental mismatch that hijacks evolved status-seeking heuristics. Drawing on Richerson and Boyd's "Big Mistake" hypothesis and Henrich's "Collective Brain," I demonstrate how the shift from vertical (parent-to-child) to oblique (peer/teacherdriven) cultural transmission creates a "Status Trap." In this trap, individuals are evolutionarily seduced into a runaway positional arms race for digital prestige, modeling their lives after childless "teachers" rather than fertile parents. This process results in the systematic "unlearning" of the domestic social operating system-the specific cultural heuristics required for low-friction child-rearing. The paper concludes by proposing a "Reflexive Turn," suggesting that the same digital maturity that induced the collapse-specifically via Large Language Models (LLMs)-may offer the only viable mechanism for re-synthesizing the lost cultural buffers necessary for demographic sustainability. A companion paper, "The Eighth Atheism," develops the philosophical corollary of this argument; a subsequent paper extends the framework to aesthetic transmission, developing the concept of "compression without the wave" to diagnose cultural artifacts that replicate form without inheriting the social chain that produced them.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6631579`

- title: LLM Measurement of Open-Ended Reasoning
- authors: Pedro Simon, Jing  Cynthia Wu, Jin Xi, Shihan Xie
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6631579
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

We propose a new framework for measuring open-ended reasoning using large language models (LLMs), consisting of four stages: embedding and clustering, LLM assisted category definition, probabilistic scoring of category membership, and validation against human-coded benchmarks. We demonstrate the validity and usefulness of the framework through human survey responses and LLM-generated reasoning. We first validate the framework and show a naïve LLM fails to deliver stable and interpretable categories. We then demonstrate that continuous probability measures capture economically meaningful information that binary classification misses, including subjective uncertainty and cross-category structure. Finally, we apply the framework to LLM-generated reasoning to study variation across question formats, when reasoning data are no longer a binding constraint. In this application, the framework recovers both similarities and economically meaningful differences in reasoning across question formats.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6640618`

- title: The Double-Edged Mind: How LLMs Expand Stock Market Participation Yet Strengthen Confirmation-Seeking
- authors: Cara Damm, Kevin Bauer, Florian Hett, Loriana Pelizzon
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6640618
- keyword hits: generative ai, llm, llms

### abstract

The shift from information retrieval (keyword-based search engines) to information synthesis (generative AI) constitutes a fundamental change in how people inform themselves online. We investigate how this shift impacts investment behavior using an incentivized online experiment (N = 374), in which we vary whether participants have access to keyword-based search engines, an LLM-based chatbot, or no additional information source. We find that LLMs facilitate participation in the stock market. Participants with access to an LLM when making investment decisions are significantly more likely to enter the stock market and to remain invested compared to those with access to keyword-based search engines or no further information. Our experiment suggests that perceived difficulty of stock market participation decreases and confidence in these choices increases when using an LLM. However, we also document a substantial risk. Access to LLMs enables individuals to confirm and strengthen experimentally induced beliefs. Even when the chatbot itself is not biased, users can prompt the model to validate beliefs they want to hold. Overall, our findings suggest that while LLMs can reduce participation frictions and encourage stock market investments, their effectiveness in confirmation-seeking can also have detrimental consequences. Consequently, these results highlight the critical need for consumer protection frameworks and financial literacy programs that specifically address the unique dynamics of human-AI interaction in modern retail investing.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6646721`

- title: Authorship-Aware Fake News Detection Using Hierarchical Meta-Learning with Transformer-Based Text Features
- authors: Subrina  Jahan Meem, Md Samsuddoha, Salman  Jahan Momo, Md Erfan, Md  Manjur Ahmed
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6646721
- keyword hits: claude, deepseek, gemini, gpt-4

### abstract

The spread of misinformation and the rapid evolution of AI-generated content pose an unprecedented dual threat to information integrity. The existing approaches to detecting fake news and AI-generated text treat these two as independent issues, which does not account for the fact that AI-generated text may be factual or deliberately misleading. This study introduces a four-way hierarchical classification framework that can measure simultaneously content authorship as human or AI and veracity as real or fake of political statements. The framework uses a two-stage architecture in which the first stage is used to detect authorship to route content and the second stage applies specialized veracity classifiers on each type of content. The method uses only textual features and has no external knowledge sources, speaker metadata, or social context. The AI-generated versions of four language models including GPT-4o-mini, Gemini 1.5 Flash, DeepSeek-V3, and Claude-3-Haiku were used to construct a dataset of 20,276 political statements augmented to the LIAR benchmark. The classification model combines engineered linguistic features and fine-tuned transformer representations using DeBERTa-v3-base and RoBERTa-base through a meta-learning fusion method. Experimental analysis shows high performance on all the pipeline stages. Authorship detection has 91.72% accuracy, classification of veracity shows notable asymmetry with 83.51% accuracy on AI-generated content and 70.34% on human-written content. The integrated pipeline has four-way accuracy and partial accuracy of 70.36% and 92.90% respectively. This study demonstrates the feasibility of unified authorship-veracity classification and proves that effective content authentication is possible by linguistic analysis only, and human-generated false information is more difficult to detect than false information generated by AI.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6449799`

- title: Cognitive State Architecture: A Conceptual Model of Integration and Control in Human and Hybrid Cognition
- authors: Thomas A. Blüm
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6449799
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Human cognition operates under varying levels of complexity, requiring adaptive shifts between different processing modes. Existing concepts such as flow states, task switching, multitasking, and cognitive overload describe specific aspects of these dynamics but rarely provide a unified structural perspective on how such modes relate to one another. This paper introduces Cognitive State Architecture (CSA) , a conceptual framework that integrates multiple cognitive processing modes within a shared structural model based on two functional dimensions: integration density and control stability. Within this functional state space, several typical processing patterns can be observed, including linear sequential processing, coordinated pseudoparallel switching, high-density relational integration ("hyperconnected" processing), fragmentation under reduced control, and overload conditions in which integration demands exceed available cognitive control. Rather than proposing discrete psychological categories or new neurobiological states, the model interprets these modes as dynamic regions within a continuous state space. Transitions between modes are interpreted as adaptive responses to changing integration demands and fluctuations in control stability. This perspective allows previously distinct cognitive phenomena - including flow states, task switching, multitasking, fragmentation, and overload - to be interpreted within a shared functional architecture. The paper also explores implications for hybrid human–AI cognition, suggesting that dialogic interaction with large language models (LLMs) may influence cognitive state dynamics by reducing articulation latency and enabling recursive cycles of conceptual integration. While the present work is conceptual and empirical validation remains limited, the proposed architecture provides a parsimonious theoretical framework for discussing cognitive state transitions in both individual cognition and hybrid cognitive systems.

### abstract

Fact-checking aims to assign truth values to claims within a specified context. Unimodal fact-checking methods, due to modality fragmentation and inadequate adaptiveness, struggle in complex scenarios where multimodal misinformation is interconnected. The recent rapid advancement of generative large language models (LLMs) has provided a novel technological paradigm for multimodal fact-checking. With their capabilities in deep semantic understanding and cross-modal alignment, LLMs can significantly enhance the accuracy, interpretability, and generalizability of misinformation detection. This paper systematically surveys the subtask arrangements, LLM-based methodologies, and relevant datasets for both unimodal and multimodal fact-checking tasks.Following the standard fact-checking pipeline, this paper systematically reviews existing methods from four key stages: Claim Detection and Extraction, Evidence Retrieval, Veracity Prediction, and Justification Production, providing a structured perspective for method comparison and evaluation.This paper provides a systematic survey of fact-checking research and offers insights into the development of next-generation fact-checking systems that are expected to be more robust, scalable, and adaptable to diverse information.

- title: Fluid Relational Semiotic: A Model of Meaning Construction Beyond Static Vector Representation
- authors: Jae Hoon Jung

- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6471100
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper analyzes the structural limitations of how current AI systems handle meaning, and proposes a new framework to address them. While large language models (LLMs) demonstrate capabilities in natural language processing, they remain structurally limited in capturing the habitual patterns and fluid dynamics through which meaning emerges in collective interaction. This paper redefines meaning as a semiotic process, and proposes the Fluid Relational Semiotic (FRS) model on the basis that this process is constituted through relational structures and temporal change. Meaning is not defined-it is formed. Because it evolves, it cannot be fixed; because it cannot be fixed, it cannot be monopolized.
