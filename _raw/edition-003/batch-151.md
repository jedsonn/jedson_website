## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6664738`

- title: In Defense of Niche Benchmarks: Why the Long Tail of LLM Evaluation Matters
- authors: Solomon Shalom Lijo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6664738
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

General-purpose large language model (LLM) benchmarks such as MMLU, HumanEval, and GPQA Diamond have entered a regime in which the leading models cluster within two to four percentage points of one another, and in which substantial evidence of training-data contamination undermines the interpretability of the remaining gaps. In response, the evaluation ecosystem has produced a rapidly growing tail of niche benchmarks: domainvertical evaluations such as MedQA, LegalBench, FinBen, BridgeBench, and PIF-Bench; and indie/informal evaluations such as SkateBench and SnitchBench. The dominant framing in the recent literature treats this proliferation as fragmentation to be cleaned up. We argue the opposite: niche benchmarks are a structurally necessary correction to the saturation, contamination, and Goodhart-style overfitting of general-capability leaderboards, and they generate value in two directions at once. For practitioners, they make model selection a tractable, evidence-based decision tied to a specific deployment context. For frontier laboratories, they provide a discriminating signal precisely where general benchmarks no longer separate models, and they surface failure modes (geographic recall gaps, temporal displacement of facts, edge-case adaptation, sensitive-topic handling) that aggregate accuracy metrics obscure. We propose a three-tier taxonomy of niche benchmarks (academic-formal, industry-vertical, and indie-informal), articulate seven design principles for constructing one, and present three short vignettes-SkateBench, SnitchBench, and PIF-Bench-that illustrate how benchmarks across radically different scopes and methodological styles each surface failure modes invisible to general evaluation. We address the standard objections to benchmark proliferation (fragmentation, quality variance, evaluator conflicts, cultural bias) and argue that the appropriate response is not consolidation but better infrastructure for documentation (e.g., benchmark cards), portfolio-level evaluation, and meta-analysis across the tail.

- title: Ex Machina: financial stability in the age of artificial intelligence
- authors: Kartik Anand, Sophia Kazinnik, Agnese Leonello, Ettore Panetti
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6728539
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Does artificial intelligence (AI) pose a threat to financial stability? We study AI investor behavior, specifically Q-learning and large language model (LLM) investors, in a mutual fund redemption problem with economic and strategic uncertainty. Different AI architectures generate systematically different outcomes. Q-learning investors coordinate well but under default risk exhibit excessive redemption that amplifies fragility. LLM investors internalize equilibrium structure but display belief heterogeneity, weakening coordination and predictability. Our findings show that AI architecture is a first-order determinant of financial stability.

- title: Resilience-Oriented Market-to-Delivery Demand Response in Integrated Energy Systems under Dynamic Uncertainty and Cyber-Physical Disruptions: A Constrained Deep Reinforcement Learning Framework
- authors: Reza Sepehrzad, HOSSEIN HOSSEINALIBEIKI, Nima Khosravi, Ahmed Al durra
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6727499
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Cyber-physical integrated energy systems (IESs) require demand response (DR) strategies that can preserve real-time service deliverability under dynamic uncertainty and cyber-physical disruptions. Existing studies often decouple market clearing, multi-storage scheduling, AI-enabled control, and resilient operation, leaving unclear how cleared DR commitments can be tracked, repaired, verified, or safely degraded during disturbed real-time execution. This paper proposes an AI-enabled resilience-oriented market-to-delivery DR framework that connects day-ahead commitment formation with real-time operational capability assessment and disturbance-aware recourse. In the day-ahead layer, a hierarchical leader–follower mechanism clears storage-supported DR commitments while considering distribution feasibility, settlement consistency, and the available capability of heterogeneous storage assets. At the IES layer, constrained deep reinforcement learning (DRL) supports multi-carrier scheduling through calibrated rewards and storage-capability-aware action repair, with large language model (LLM) assistance restricted to bounded calibration and repair-template selection rather than direct dispatch. In real time, cleared schedules are lifted into a fast execution layer where disturbance-aware state estimation, safety filtering, fallback switching, corrective recourse, and service verification determine whether committed DR can be delivered, partially restored, or conservatively degraded. The framework is validated on the IEEE 69-bus, Caracas 141-bus, and IEEE 342-bus systems under normal, uncertainty-only, and compound-disturbance scenarios combining dynamic operational uncertainty and cyber disruptions. Results show preserved operational headroom, coherent scaling across test systems, real-time DR delivery above 88% under compound disturbances, and bounded constraint excursions below 3%. Ablation, sensitivity, stability, and baseline comparison analyses confirm that performance gains arise from the integrated resilience chain rather than DRL alone.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6479841`

- title: Information Aggregation with AI Agents
- authors: Spyros Galanis
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6479841
- keyword hits: ai agent, large language model, large language models, prompting

### abstract

Can Large Language Models (AI agents) aggregate dispersed private information through trading and reason about the knowledge of others by observing price move ments? We conduct a controlled experiment where AI agents trade in a prediction market after receiving private signals, measuring information aggregation by the log error of the last price. We find that although the median market is effective at aggre gating information in the easy information structures, increasing the complexity has a significant and negative impact, suggesting that AI agents may suffer from similar limitations as humans when reasoning about others. Consistent with our theoretical predictions, information aggregation remains unaffected by allowing cheap talk commu nication, changing the duration of the market or initial price, and strategic prompting, thus demonstrating that prediction markets are robust. We establish that “smarter” AI agents perform better at aggregation and they are more profitable. Surprisingly, giving them feedback about past performance has no impact on aggregation.

### abstract

The integration of foundation models (FMs) into transportation recommendation systemsranging from ride-hailing and route planning to multimodal transit-has brought unprecedented personalization and efficiency. However, these large-scale generative models are susceptible to learning and amplifying historical and systemic social biases, leading to discriminatory outcomes based on protected attributes such as race, gender, socioeconomic status, or disability. This paper presents a novel data mining framework to detect, quantify, and mitigate social discrimination in FM-based transportation recommendations. First, we propose bias-aware auditing methodologies that leverage counterfactual fairness testing and association rule mining to uncover hidden disparities in recommended travel times, surge pricing, ride acceptance rates, and accessibility options. Second, we introduce a discrimination pattern mining approach using sequential rule mining and graph neural networks to trace how biased representations propagate from training data through FM embeddings to final recommendations. Finally, we develop an adversarial debiasing mechanism integrated with prompt tuning to reduce disparate impact while preserving recommendation utility. Experiments on real-world ride-hailing and urban mobility datasets demonstrate that our approach successfully identifies discriminatory patterns overlooked by traditional fairness metrics and reduces group-level disparities by up to 40% without substantially degrading accuracy. This research underscores the critical need for fairness-aware data mining in the lifecycle of foundation models, offering practical tools for regulators, platform designers, and auditing bodies to ensure equitable transportation access in the age of generative AI.

### abstract

The proliferation of foundational Large Language Models has reduced the marginal cost of information retrieval and synthesis to zero. When every capital allocator operates from the same omniscient information baseline, information asymmetry ceases to generate alpha. This paper argues that the competitive frontier in capital allocation has fundamentally shifted: the premium is no longer on accessing data, but on the rigor with which logic derived from that data is stress-tested. We define this transition as the emergence of AI Judgment Infrastructure-a dedicated architecture for evaluating the structural soundness of investment theses, rather than merely retrieving and summarizing the information they contain. Drawing on a corpus of 60,000+ Clarity Scores benchmarked through the RUNE Protocol (patent-pending), we identify seven empirically derived archetypes of investment thesis failure-the Grammar of Failure-and introduce the Judgment Graph as a proprietary data structure mapping relationships between claims, evidence, and historical failure patterns. We further propose the Defensible Audit Log as the canonical output artifact of this infrastructure: an immutable, machine-verifiable proof of analytical rigor for institutional stakeholders. The Clarity Framework and The Rigor Protocol are presented as the applied methodology and organizational standard required to operationalize AI Judgment Infrastructure at scale. Our central thesis: judgment is the last unscalable asset, and the infrastructure we build today will determine who commands the next decade of capital deployment.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6734908`

- title: Regime-Aware Multi-Scale Attention-RNN for Financial Time-Series Forecasting: Bridging Hybrid Architectures and Foundation Models
- authors: Vishnu Vardhan Reddy Yeruva
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6734908
- keyword hits: foundation model, llama

### abstract

Financial time-series forecasting remains one of the most challenging applications of deep learning due to non-stationarity, regime shifts, and low signal-to-noise ratios. While hybrid attention-RNN architectures have demonstrated superiority over pure Transformers on financial benchmarks, existing models apply uniform attention mechanisms regardless of prevailing market conditions, failing to adapt to the fundamentally different dynamics of bull, bear, and crisis regimes. Simultaneously, recent time-series foundation models (TimesFM, Chronos, Lag-Llama) have shown poor zero-shot performance on financial data, with R2 values as low as −2.80% and directional accuracy below 50%. This paper introduces RAMAR (Regime-Aware Multi-scale Attention-RNN), a novel architecture that addresses both limitations through four innovations: (1) a regime detection module using Hidden Markov Models to classify market states in real-time; (2) regime-conditioned attention whose scoring functions, temperature, and weight distributions explicitly adapt to detected market regimes; (3) cross-frequency fusion attention that dynamically aligns daily technical indicators with monthly macroeconomic releases and quarterly fundamental data from Crunchbase; and (4) multi-horizon output heads producing simultaneous 1-day, 5-day, and 20-day forecasts with regime-conditioned temporal scale selection. Evaluated on S&P 500 constituents (2010–2025), RAMAR achieves RMSE of 0.0128, R2 of 0.962, and directional accuracy of 62.4%—reducing RMSE by 17.9% over TFT [1] and 16.9% over MCI-GRU [5], while outperforming zero-shot TimesFM by 58.9%. Regime-specific analysis reveals RAMAR’s largest advantage during bear markets and crisis periods, where standard models degrade most severely. Ablation studies confirm that regime-conditioned attention alone accounts for 37.2% of total improvement, while cross-frequency macro fusion contributes an additional 18.4%.
