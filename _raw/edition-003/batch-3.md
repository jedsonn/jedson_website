## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7119484`

- title: AI Trading: Evaluating Large Language Models for Technical Market Analysis
- authors: Geofrey Ntale
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7119484
- keyword hits: claude, fine-tuning, gemini, gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llama, llm, llms

### abstract

The convergence of artificial intelligence and financial markets has produced a fundamental shift in how trading decisions are conceived, evaluated, and executed. Large Language Models (LLMs), initially designed for natural language understanding, have emerged as a powerful class of tools capable of processing the heterogeneous information environments that characterize modern financial markets. This paper presents a systematic, comparative evaluation of five prominent LLMs, specifically GPT-4 Turbo, Claude 3 Opus, Gemini 1.5 Pro, Llama 3 70B, and the domain-specialized FinGPT, with respect to their capacity for technical market analysis. The evaluation spans four structured tasks: candlestick pattern recognition from Open-High-Low-Close-Volume (OHLCV) data, directional signal generation with BUY/SELL/HOLD classification, backtesting of signal quality through a simulated execution pipeline, and financial report comprehension measured against established benchmarks. Our experimental framework employs rigorous quantitative metrics, including Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown, Sortino ratio, information coefficient, F1-score, and BLEU score, alongside standard confusion-matrixderived classification metrics. Findings from simulated backtesting indicate that GPT-4 Turbo achieves the highest annualized return and Sharpe ratio among general-purpose models, while FinGPT demonstrates competitive risk-adjusted performance attributed to its domain-specific fine-tuning. Both models outperform a passive S&P 500 benchmark under the tested conditions. The study identifies persistent failure modes across all evaluated models, including numerical hallucination, context-window limitations, and inconsistent performance in sideways market regimes. These findings suggest that while LLMs hold genuine promise as components within ai trading systems, robust deployment requires careful task decomposition, rigorous backtesting protocols, and domain-aware fine-tuning strategies. The paper concludes with a discussion of practical and ethical implications, as well as a structured agenda for future research in multimodal market intelligence and reinforcement learning-augmented trading agents.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6675831`

- title: Register Alignment of AI-Generated and Human-Written Research Article Introductions in Applied Linguistics: Multidimensional Analysis and Reader Perception
- authors: Zhurun Li, Ying Huang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6675831
- keyword hits: chatgpt, deepseek, gemini, gpt-5, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The growing use of large language models (LLMs) in academic writing has intensified interest in how AI-generated texts align with disciplinary register norms. Yet multidimensional register comparisons remain scarce in highly conventionalized genres such as research article (RA) introductions. This study compiled a corpus of 200 human-authored applied linguistics RA introductions and a corpus of 600 counterpart texts generated by three frontier LLMs (ChatGPT-5.2, DeepSeek-R1, and Gemini-2.5 Pro; 200 each), and applied Biber’s (1988) Multidimensional (MD) analysis to compare their register profiles. The strongest group differences emerged on Dimension 1 (Involved vs. Informational Production; η² = 0.57) and Dimension 6 (On-line Informational Elaboration; η² = 0.35). At the feature level, AI-generated texts consistently used more attributive adjectives, longer and more varied words, and more possibility modals, but fewer first-person pronouns and prepositional phrases than human texts; DeepSeek-R1 and Gemini-2.5 Pro also showed stronger on-line elaboration via demonstratives and subject-position that-relative clauses. Discriminant analysis classified AI versus human texts with 81.9% accuracy (four-way: 65.4%). A pilot judgment task further suggested that MA students could perceive some, but not all, of these register contrasts. The findings offer a linguistically grounded basis for evaluating AI-generated academic writing and highlight the need to develop students’ critical AI literacy through explicit attention to register features.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6705598`

- title: Less Volume, More Variety: An Inverse Relationship Between LLM Output Length and Contrarian Discovery in Pharmaceutical Stock Selection
- authors: HoKwang Kim
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6705598
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, deepseek, gemini, large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

We report preliminary evidence of a counterintuitive empirical pattern in large language model (LLM) based equity research: shorter outputs contain proportionally more contrarian stock recommendations than longer ones. Submitting an identical pharmaceutical sector long/short prompt to four frontier LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude, DeepSeek, Gemini) on May 4, 2026, we observe a strong negative rank correlation (Spearman ρ = −0.80 across all four models; ρ = −1.00 when ChatGPT — identified as a stale-data outlier — is excluded) between output length and what we term the contrarian discovery rate — the count of unique stock picks not appearing in any other model's recommendation, normalized by output length. The most compressed model (Gemini, 4.13 KB) generated 14 times more contrarian picks per kilobyte than the most verbose (DeepSeek, 38.6 KB). We propose a compression-forces-selection mechanism: when token budget is constrained, models cannot afford to enumerate consensus picks and must allocate scarce capacity to differentiated signals. Given the small sample (n = 4) and single-period design, we frame the result as a hypothesis-generating observation, and outline a five-study research agenda for causal identification, scaled replication, and forward performance evaluation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6756759`

- title: Hallucination Detection and Reduction in Open-source Large Language Models via the Kerimov-Alekberli Information-Geometric Framework: Empirical Evaluation on HaluEval, FEVER, and SimpleQA
- authors: Rahid Alekberli, Hikmat Karimov
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6756759
- keyword hits: chain-of-thought, deepseek, fine-tuning, large language model, large language models, llama, llm, llms, qwen

### abstract

Background: Hallucination—the generation of factually incorrect, internally inconsistent, or ungrounded content—remains a major barrier to the safe deployment of large language models (LLMs), particularly in high-stakes domains. Most existing hallucination-detection methods rely on external knowledge bases, model fine-tuning, or cloud-based APIs, limiting their applicability for private or fully local inference environments. Methods: We evaluated the Kerimov–Alekberli (KA) information-geometric framework as a real-time, inference-time hallucination detector across six open-source LLMs running locally on Apple M5 Silicon hardware using Ollama v0.23.2 with Q4_K_M quantisation. The KA framework monitors the Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence between consecutive output distributions relative to a Fisher Information Metric (FIM)-derived threshold (τ = 0.065). Hallucination events are identified through First-Passage Time (FPT) alarms triggered when generation trajectories depart from the stable Riemannian output manifold. We evaluated 120 responses (6 models × 20 questions) using prompts drawn from three established hallucination benchmarks: HaluEval (14 questions spanning Fact, Confuse, Date, Num, and Trap categories), FEVER (4 adversarial fact-verification questions), and SimpleQA (2 factual-recall questions). All prompts were classified as Hard difficulty and targeted known LLM failure modes including off-by-one numerical errors, geographical traps, and disputed-attribution confounds. Results: The KA framework achieved a session-level hallucination detection sensitivity of 90.9% (20/22 hallucinated responses correctly flagged) with zero false positives among correct responses (0/98). Hallucination prevalence varied substantially across models. The reasoning-oriented model deepseek-r1:latest (Qwen3 chain-of-thought architecture; 5.2 GB) exhibited a 95% hallucination rate (19/20 prompts), all correctly detected by the KA framework. In contrast, gemma3:27b (17.4 GB) and gemma3:latest (4.3B; 3.3 GB) produced no hallucinated responses on the evaluated tasks. Two KA false negatives involved highly confident factual errors with KL divergence values below the detection threshold. Mean KL divergence was significantly higher for hallucinated outputs (D_KL = 0.068 ± 0.004) than for correct outputs (D_KL = 0.042 ± 0.016). Conclusions: The KA framework provides competitive hallucination detection without requiring external knowledge bases, model fine-tuning, or cloud infrastructure, operating in real time with negligible computational overhead. The exceptionally high hallucination rate observed in deepseek-r1 suggests a potentially important trade-off between chain-of-thought reasoning depth and factual precision on concise factual queries, warranting further systematic investigation.

### abstract

Context: Large Language Models (LLMs) have catalyzed a paradigm shift in Software Engineering (SE); however, their empirical evaluation remains predominantly confined to developer-centric, static code generation tasks. The socio-technical potential of LLMs to augment Agile project management—specifically the Product Owner (PO) role, which is tasked with mediating the conflicting demands of business stakeholders and technical development teams—remains critically underexplored.Objective: This study addresses this gap by evaluating the capacity of advanced models to act as strategic, consultative co-pilots for Agile Product Owners.Methods: We introduced a structured, five-phase LLM Chat Analysis Methodology, complemented with a novel Dynamic Prototype Evaluation Protocol. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6843083`

- title: BenjiTest: An Agentic Framework for Evaluating LLM-Based Unit Test Generation and Correction
- authors: Andrea Lops, Azzurra Ragone, Massimiliano Di Penta, Michelantonio Trizio, Fedelucio Narducci
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6843083
- keyword hits: agentic, deepseek, gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, prompting

### abstract

Automated unit testing is central to software quality, yet manually producing comprehensive test suites remains costly. While Large Language Models (LLMs) show promise for test generation, their effectiveness at the class level - where complex dependencies and state management arise - remains underexplored. We present BenjiTest, a multi-agent framework designed as an evaluation platform rather than a competing test generator, that measures what LLMs and agentic workflows can and cannot do at the class level under reproducible, protocol-controlled orchestration. BenjiTest coordinates specialized agents for repository mining, black-box and white-box analysis, and iterative error correction. We conduct an empirical study on the Classes2Test dataset (147,473 classes, 9 models, 4 strategies) and on the Defects4J benchmark (854 real-world bugs), used as a stress-test on real faults. Dynamic prompting improves compilation rates by up to 51 percentage points over zero-shot baselines, and the iterative correction loop resolves 72% of initial defects. Selected configurations (gpt-4.1, deepseek-r1) exceed 90% mutation score and branch coverage on compiled tests. On Defects4J, the agentic workflow reaches a 46.1% fault detection rate - a 4.4x gain over zero-shot (10.5%) and 59% over few-shot (29.0%)-while the 53.9% of bugs that remain undetected map onto known structural limits of implementation-based test generation (Oracle Problem, complex object setup, environment-dependent failures, stateful method sequences). A memorization analysis shows 24.1% of test classes are semantically reproducible by gpt-4.1, yet performance on non-memorized classes remains strong, indicating that the main trends are not explained by data leakage. - title: Benchmarking the Safety of General Purpose Large Language Models for Suicide Risk Detection and Response
- authors: Kate Bentley, Emily Van Ark, Julian De Freitas, Tim Hahn, Nicolas  C. Jacobson, Nina Vasan, Ursula Whiteside, Luca Belli
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6836551
- keyword hits: claude, gpt-4, gpt-5, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Background: As more people turn to AI chatbots for psychological support, questions are growing about whether these tools are safe. Automated LLM-as-a-judge evaluations can both quantify chatbot safety at scale and identify actionable areas for improvements, enabling more efficient iteration for developers and safer AI tool deployment. Methods: We used the Validation of Ethical and Responsible AI in Mental Health (VERA-MH), an open-source automated evaluation of simulated multi-turn conversations, to benchmark the performance of ten leading general-purpose large language models (LLMs) for suicide risk detection and response. We first tested the framework’s internal structure and identified optimal testing conditions for producing stable scores and facilitating meaningful comparisons across LLMs. We then applied VERA-MH to evaluate both overall safety and five safety dimensions across version histories from four major LLM providers. Outcomes: The framework’s five safety dimensions were largely distinct, supporting the value of both overall and dimension-specific scores. Scores stabilized when judging 200 conversations of up to 30 turns per LLM, suggesting these parameters provided optimal testing conditions. Newer LLMs achieved higher overall safety scores than earlier versions for 3 of the 4 providers, with Claude Sonnet 4.6 and GPT-5.2 scoring the highest overall and GPT-4o and Grok 4 the lowest. Models consistently performed the best on detecting potential risk, but underperformed in asking direct follow-up questions about suicidal thoughts or safety 61% of the time, and failed to adequately guide users to human care 33% of the time. Interpretation: Although safety has improved over time for most general-purpose models, gaps remain in suicide risk handling, particularly in how consistently chatbots confirm risk and effectively guide users to human support when needed.
