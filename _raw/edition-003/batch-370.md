## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6580618`

- title: The Verification Economy: How AI's Trust Crisis Creates a $300 Billion Market
- authors: Niall Norton
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6580618
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

This paper identifies and analyzes the emergence of a $300+ billion verification economy driven by AI’s fundamental trust crisis. As AI makes content creation, expertise access, and credential generation approach zero marginal cost, verification becomes the scarce resource determining value. We document over $12 billion in verified losses from verification failures (2019-2024) and identify $100+ billion in stalled AI deployment due to verification gaps. Using Grove’s strategic inflection point framework, we demonstrate why 2026-2029 represents a narrow window for infrastructure development, after which catch-up costs become prohibitive. The analysis maps seven distinct verification verticals (AI agents, credentials, supply chain, transactions, content, identity, and legal), providing market sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM), competitive moat analysis (Porter framework), and execution roadmaps (Grove/Moore timing). We identify why verification uniquely resists platform re-integration through structural conflict-of-interest moats: credible verification requires independence from the entities being verified. The paper makes three primary contributions: (1) quantitative documentation of verification market opportunity across seven verticals, (2) strategic framework distinguishing Type A (behavioral monitoring) from Type B (data integrity) verification systems, and (3) dual execution roadmap addressing both immediate opportunities and long-term infrastructure plays. Historical analysis of interface control dynamics (IBM, Intel, Microsoft, Adobe) validates why verification represents the only defensible layer in the AI stack where specialist firms can sustainably resist platform bundling. Key findings: Goldilocks-difficulty verticals (AI agents, credentials, supply chain) offer $150-200 billion addressable markets with 3-5 year first-mover windows. Platform risk categories (content, identity) require defensive strategies. Hard-mode categories (transactions, legal) suit experienced founders with patient capital. The window for establishing verification infrastructure closes 2029-2030 as platforms develop proprietary solutions and regulatory frameworks ossify. This work synthesizes Porter (strategic positioning), Grove (inflection point timing), and Moore (technology adoption lifecycle) to provide actionable guidance for entrepreneurs, investors, platforms, and enterprises navigating AI’s verification crisis.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6655439`

- title: When Agents Cannot Be Benchmarked: AI Capability Opacity, Corporate Governance, and a Research Agenda *
- authors: Phuc Nguyen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6655439
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

Sixty-nine Anthropic employees delegated a week of buying and selling decisions to AI agents in a controlled marketplace experiment (Anthropic, 2026). The agents differed only in capability. Principals matched with the more capable agent earned $2.68 more per deal as sellers and paid $2.45 less as buyers; the principals on the losing side did not detect the loss. Classical agency theory cannot account for this pattern: every action was logged, and incentives were perfectly aligned by construction. We model the puzzle as a bilateral transaction in which two principals delegate to AI agents whose relative capability determines a Tullock share of the surplus. The model yields a strict capability premium even under perfect alignment. It also yields an identification result: the principal cannot infer her own agent's capability from her own outcomes, and the problem persists with repeated transactions when the joint distribution of counterparty types and capabilities is unknown. We label this informational deficit capability opacity and argue it is structurally distinct from hidden action, hidden type, and non-verifiable performance. Peer benchmarking with disclosed capability metrics restores point-identification, which provides a welfare argument for mandatory AI capability disclosure. The paper closes with a corporate-governance research agenda built around board AI oversight, audit committee due diligence, fiduciary duty, and disclosure design.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6570381`

- title: From Prototype to Production: A Practitioner's Framework for Governing AI Agent Deployments in Regulated Industries
- authors: Zeki Emre Tekin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6570381
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

Organizations across the United States are racing to deploy AI agent systems into compliance-and document-intensive workflows-building code review, clinical documentation, benefits adjudication, procurement processing-with remarkably little structured governance. The consequences are already visible: ECRI has named insufficient AI governance the No. 2 patient safety threat for 2025, and only 12% of U.S. hospitals have formal AI governance frameworks despite the FDA authorizing over 1,000 AI/ML-enabled medical devices. National policy frameworks like the NIST AI Risk Management Framework define what good governance should look like in principle, but a critical translation layer is missing: the practitioner-level mechanisms that organizations can actually deploy. This paper presents a four-pillar framework for AI deployment governancegrounding techniques, evaluation frameworks, reference architectures, and releasereadiness gates-integrated through human-in-the-loop (HITL) governance as a structural design principle. The framework is not theoretical. It is derived from the author's direct experience building and deploying governance mechanisms across national-scale government analytics systems (82 cities, 12,000 users), regulated construction compliance automation, consumer-scale AI platforms (40M+ users), and enterprise AI enablement. Key elements of the framework have been independently adopted by teams beyond the author's direct involvement, confirming its transferability. The paper maps the framework to NIST AI RMF functions and current federal policy directives, and provides implementation guidance for organizations at varying levels of governance maturity.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6659865`

- title: Metastatic Pancreatic Acinar Cell Carcinoma to the Liver Mimicking Neuroendocrine Tumor (NET) and Pancreatic Ductal Adenocarcinoma (PDAC): A series of 7 cases
- authors: Tiane Chen, Ralph  H. Hruban, Pedram Argani
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6659865
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Acinar cell carcinomas (ACCs) of the pancreas typically demonstrate distinct morphologic features including minimal fibrous stroma, acinar architecture, single prominent nucleoli and eosinophilic cytoplasmic granules. We report seven cases of metastatic carcinomas to the liver with acinar cell differentiation (two carcinomas with pure acinar cell differentiation, four carcinomas with mixed acinar neuroendocrine differentiation, one mixed acinar ductal carcinoma) that lacked many of these features, and, as a result, were misclassified. The cases were prospectively identified in our routine confirming consultation service. Clinical data and histology were retrospectively reviewed. The seven patients ranged in age from 48-72 years with a male predominance (5 males, 2 females). Five patients had a known pancreatic mass, and all patients had multiple liver lesions on initial imaging, prompting liver biopsies. Three of seven tumors in the liver were initially diagnosed at the submitting institution as high-grade neuroendocrine tumors (NETs) as they all expressed synaptophysin and/or chromogranin, had a high Ki67 proliferation index, and lacked cytoplasmic granules. The remaining four tumors in the liver were initially diagnosed as adenocarcinoma as they lacked prominent nucleoli, had prominent fibrotic stroma (3 cases), lacked eosinophilic cytoplasmic granules (3 cases), and/or had significant nuclear pleomorphism (1 case). In all cases, reevaluation at the time of consultation with an extended immunohistochemical panel revealed the expression of pancreatic exocrine enzymes (trypsin, chymotrypsin, and/or BCL10), confirming acinar differentiation. All six patients with follow-up died within 1 to 24 months of initial diagnosis. In conclusion, metastatic ACC should be considered in differential diagnosis of the more common liver metastasis of pancreatic origin, NET and PDAC. ACC metastatic to the liver may lack hallmark morphologic features such as prominent nucleoli and eosinophilic cytoplasmic granules and may express neuroendocrine markers (mimicking NET) or exhibit prominent fibrotic stroma and pleomorphism (mimicking PDAC). Immunolabeling for acinar cell markers is warranted for cases of purported PDAC/NET with atypical features, such as purported NET with high Ki67 index and/or only patchy neuroendocrine marker labeling, or purported PDAC with luminal proteinaceous secretions rather than mucin and/or minimal pleomorphism.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6476618`

- title: When Does Hierarchical Retrieval Beat Vector Search? A Cross-Domain Empirical Study
- authors: Shivam Rawat
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6476618
- keyword hits: llm, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines typically rely on dense vector similarity to locate relevant document chunks. PageIndex, a recent alternative, organizes documents into hierarchical trees and uses LLM-guided traversal for retrieval, reporting 98.7% accuracy on the FinanceBench benchmark-but from a single domain under the authors' own evaluation. We compare three retrieval strategies across four document QA domains (600 questions total) with a shared LLM backbone that isolates retrieval as the sole variable. Our central finding: hierarchical retrieval's apparent large advantage on financial documents (judge score 3.94 vs. 1.69 for dense RAG, d=1.16) is primarily a retrieval coverage artifact. Dense RAG returns "NOT FOUND" for 53% of financial questions; hierarchical retrieval always selects content from the tree. When we restrict to questions both systems answered, the gap collapses from 2.25 to 0.31 points. Conversely, dense RAG dominates on short technical passages (3.93 vs. 1.75, d=-1.09), where hierarchical retrieval's tree structure adds overhead without benefit. No system wins overall (p=0.58). We argue that the hierarchical-vs-vector choice is better understood as a coverageprecision tradeoff, modulated by document length and structural richness, than as an accuracy comparison.

- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6630398

### abstract

Between November 2025 and April 2026, the five largest enterprise governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) platform vendors repositioned their product lines around artificial intelligence governance. Using documentary analysis of publicly available vendor materials across a twelve month window, this paper examines the pattern of capabilities these vendors now market as AI governance. It finds that the repositioning was substantive in marketing terms and minimal in architectural terms: every vendor's AI governance product is an extension of the observation and workflow automation infrastructure the vendor already sold.
