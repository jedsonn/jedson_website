- title: The Law and Economics of the Ai Excuse: Liability, Perception Bias, and the Case for Epistemic Calibration
- authors: Massimiliano Caruso
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6958684
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Large language models err persuasively, distorting users' beliefs about AI reliability through an architecturally induced perception bias. We model the interaction between a developer, an LLM, and a boundedly rational human decision-maker, and prove three results. First, Liability Separation Failure (Theorems 1 and 1′): no pure liability regime-strict human liability, strict developer liability, or negligence-can implement the first-best across the full range of AI capabilities. Second, Paradox of Human Oversight (Section 5): a reasonableness standard collapses into the biased decision rule, rendering mandated oversight structurally hollow. Third, Restoration Theorems (Theorems 2 and 3): a Calibrated Reliability Score mandate, paired with conditional liability, restores the first-best. Embedded in a Grossman-Hart-Moore framework, perception bias shifts optimal asset ownership toward the AI developer-a new verticalintegration prediction-and a CRS mandate substitutes for ownership reallocation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7113773`

- title: GARDRec: Decision-Level Graph Grounding for Large Language Model Recommendation
- authors: Yongchao Wang, Hongliang Sun, Jinlan Liu, Hua Zhang, Dianbo Sui, Dianhui Chu, Zhiying Tu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7113773
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) offer new opportunities for recommendation by interpreting item descriptions, user instructions, and external knowledge through natural-language prompts. However, existing graph-augmented LLM recommenders often use knowledge graphs mainly as prompt-level evidence, leaving ranking decisions weakly constrained by structured user--item relations. This is problematic for next-item recommendation, where the model must compare candidates under the same user context while preserving temporal preference, collaborative signals, and attribute matches. To address this issue, we propose \emph{GARDRec}, a Graph-grounded Adaptive Reasoning and Decision-aware Recommendation framework for LLM-based next-item ranking. GARDRec constructs semantic-structural item representations from textual node features and graph propagation, derives personalized graph contexts from temporally weighted histories and first-order neighborhoods, and aligns graph-derived representations with a frozen LLM through continuous multimodal prompts. Explicit interaction and matching features are injected through late-stage decision branches, while inter-candidate attention and restricted generative likelihood support final ranking. Experiments on three public benchmarks with multiple LLM backbones show that GARDRec generally improves candidate-ranking performance over representative baselines. Ablation and diagnostic analyses verify the contributions of graph projection, neighborhood retrieval, explicit decision features, ranking loss, and generative calibration.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7115108`

- title: Corporate physical climate risk, trade credit occupation and supply chain risk contagion: A study based on large language models
- authors: Feifan Chen, Yueshu Zhou
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-13
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7115108
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

This study employs FinBERT2, a large language model (LLM), to construct a novel firm-level indicator of physical climate risk. It examines whether firms rely on trade credit as a liquidity buffer against physical climate shocks and whether this behavior triggers supply chain risk contagion. The results show that exposure to physical climate risk significantly increases trade credit occupation, with a clear asymmetry: the effect is particularly pronounced for credit obtained from upstream suppliers. Firms primarily resort to trade credit occupation for liquidity management when facing such shocks. Physical climate shocks trigger supply chain contagion, deteriorating upstream suppliers’ operational performance and reducing supply chain efficiency and resilience. Higher supplier concentration helps curb trade credit occupation. This study deepens the understanding of firms’ financing decisions under physical climate shocks through the lens of trade credit occupation, and offers implications for strengthening climate adaptation and preventing the erosion of supply chain funds.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7093339`

- title: MedPlain: Local, Bilingual Plain-Language Simplification of Clinical Text with a Faithfulness Check
- authors: Awais Ur Rehman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7093339
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Patients frequently cannot understand their own clinical documents, a health-literacy gap associated with poorer outcomes. Large language models (LLMs) can rewrite clinical text into plain language, but their fluency masks a safety-critical weakness: they can silently alter clinically material facts. We present MedPlain, an open-source system that simplifies clinical text at a selectable reading level, in English and Arabic, entirely on the user's device, and pairs each simplification with a two-layer faithfulness check (a deterministic dose/number verifier and an LLM semantic verifier). MedPlain further ingests printed prescriptions from PDF or image and applies an OCR confidence gate that refuses low-confidence reads to prevent an OCR-to-hallucination failure mode. We evaluate the system on a gold-standard set of five prescriptions spanning oncology, cardiology, and general practice, with documented "known facts" and "known traps." Readability improved from a mean Flesch-Kincaid grade of ~21.5 to ~7.4, and 95% of numeric facts were preserved. However, the semantic verifier detected only 1 of 24 subtle meaning-level distortions, and on a handwritten document the pipeline produced a fluent but fabricated prescription. We report these negative results transparently: numeric fidelity and fluency create false confidence, and robust semantic faithfulness for medical simplification remains unsolved. MedPlain is positioned as a research and educational prototype-not a medical device-and its design centers on privacy, honest safety framing, and mandatory human review. Code and data are openly available.

### abstract

A retail-executable FOMC calendar rule — long SPY from the prior close into the statement-day close, inspired by but not replicating the pre-FOMC announcement drift of Lucca and Moench (2015) — looked compelling in training: across 112 scheduled FOMC events (2002–2015) it earned +$3.561 per event after frozen costs (daily t-statistic 3.20, profit factor 2.33), positive in thirteen of fourteen years, surviving a doubled-cost stress test. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7118513`

- title: Select What Matters: Key-State Relabeling for Offline Goal-conditioned Reinforcement Learning
- authors: Xule Gao, Rui Wang, Changwen Zheng, Yuquan Wu, Chuxiong Sun
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7118513
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Offline goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (GCRL) provides a practical framework for learning policies from fixed offline datasets to accomplish diverse goals. Existing methods commonly construct goal-conditioned learning signals through goal relabeling, reusing achieved states in offline trajectories as training goals. However, conventional relabeling methods typically treat these states uniformly, failing to prioritize informative, task-advancing goals. In this work, we propose Key-State Relabeling (KSR), a plug-and-play goal relabeling strategy that prioritizes task-relevant key states as relabeled goals. To efficiently identify key states in complex long-horizon offline trajectories, we leverage the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) to generate executable discriminators from task specifications. For trustworthy and controllable relabeling, we further introduce a two-stage Self-Check mechanism to mitigate hallucinations in LLM-generated discriminators: compile-time code validation and TD-error-guided relabel-time refinement. Theoretically, we show that KSR yields a tighter sample-complexity bound by reducing the covering complexity of the relabeled goal set and lowering the effective GOAL-BE dimension. Empirically, we show that KSR, as a plug-and-play goal relabeling strategy, consistently improves multiple offline GCRL baselines across diverse OGBench navigation and manipulation tasks, while remaining effective on noisy datasets and introducing only moderate computational overhead. Overall, key-state relabeling provides an effective way to construct more informative training transitions for goal-conditioned policy learning.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.13285v1`

- title: Harness Handbook: Making Evolving Agent Harnesses Readable,Navigable, and Editable
- authors: Ruhan Wang, Yucheng Shi, Zongxia Li, Zhongzhi Li, Yue Yu, Junyao Yang, Kishan Panaganti, Haitao Mi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13285v1
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent, foundation model, llm

### abstract

The capability of a modern AI agent depends not only on its foundation model but also on its harness, which constructs prompts, manages state, invokes tools, and coordinates execution. As models, APIs, environments, and requirements evolve, the harness must be continually modified. Before such a change can be made, a developer or coding agent must identify all code locations that implement the target behavior. This is difficult because production harnesses are large, tightly coupled, and behaviorally distributed, while modification requests describe what the system should do and repositories are organized by files and modules. Code search, repository indexing, and long-context processing ease inspection, but still leave this behavior-to-code mapping to be recovered by hand. Behavior localization is therefore a central bottleneck in harness evolution. We introduce the Harness Handbook, a behavior-centric representation synthesized automatically from a harness codebase via static analysis and LLM-assisted structuring, linking each behavior to its corresponding source. We also introduce Behavior-Guided Progressive Disclosure (BGPD), which guides agents from high-level behaviors to relevant implementation details and verifies candidate locations against the current source. On diverse modification requests from two open-source harnesses, Handbook-Assisted planning improves behavior localization and edit-plan quality while using fewer planner tokens, with the largest gains on scattered sites, rarely executed paths, and cross-module interactions. Evolving complex agentic systems thus depends not only on generating edits, but also on determining where those edits should be made.

### abstract

This position paper explores how Agentic AI and Model Context Protocol (MCP) can support power-grid studies in a Transmission System Operator (TSO) context. We focus on integrating Large Language Models with numerical simulation tools, structured workflows, and human supervision. We identify key industrial requirements for agent assisted grid studies and introduce pypowsybl-mcp, an MCP-based interface exposing selected capabilities of our simulation tool, pypowsybl to AI agents. This first step provides a testbed to study how agents can setup simulations, execute analyses, retrieve results, and interact with power-system simulators through standardized tool calls. We also discuss principles for human-in-the-loop, multi-agent workflows and outline an evaluation strategy combining technical metrics and practitioner feedback. The paper positions MCP-based tool integration as a step toward more interactive, auditable, and scalable grid-study environments.
