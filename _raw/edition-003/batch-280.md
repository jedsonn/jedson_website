### abstract

The proliferation of competing identity definitions across the human sciences is not a failure of rigour-it is a structural consequence of the way knowledge about identity has been produced. Each tradition that has theorised identity was solving a specific problem within a specific methodological frame, and each question made certain features of the phenomenon visible while rendering others inaccessible. Drawing on Heisenberg's epistemological principle that what we observe is nature exposed to our method of questioning, this paper proposes that the definitional impasse is a consequence of asking the wrong question. The question what is identity? produces an object to be classified. The question how does identity function as an instrument of movement through life? produces an architecture to be navigated. Posed across four disciplines-neuroscience, cybernetics, dynamical systems theory, and physics-the navigational question generates structurally convergent answers, producing a formally specified navigational architecture as its object. The contribution is not another definition of identity. It is a demonstration that a change in the question produces not only a different answer, but a different order of object-one that is genuinely operable across clinical, organisational, and institutional domains. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6748306`

- title: How Generative AI Reorganizes Knowledge Work
- authors: K. Sudhir, Xueming Luo, Shucheng Miao
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6748306
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

Abstract: Generative AI sharply reduces the cost of producing the analyses, content, and communications central to knowledge work. As these production tasks become cheaper, organizational scarcity shifts toward orchestrating how the resulting work is interpreted, integrated into decisions, and deployed. We term this the production-to-orchestration shift, which predicts that GenAI will not uniformly displace jobs but reallocate human effort from production toward higher-order managerial, relational, and integrative activities. We test this framework in marketing with more than 40 million U.S. job postings and employment records from 2021–2024 in a difference-indifferences (DiD) design exploiting the release of ChatGPT. We document four patterns. First, total marketing employment declines by about 5-7%, concentrated in production-centered areas (research, content creation, operational support), while strategic and relational roles (sales/CRM, strategy) expand. Second, entry-level roles shrink while mid-level and executive roles grow. Third, wages rise despite fewer jobs. Fourth, skill demand shifts toward interpersonal capabilities in orchestration-oriented roles, while remaining production roles become more technically specialized. The results support the production-to-orchestration shift as a framework for understanding how GenAI reorganizes knowledge work, with implications for marketing organizational design, career pathways, and professional expertise development.

### abstract

Background: Online crowdfunding has emerged as a prominent form of digital philanthropy, facilitating public contributions toward medical, humanitarian, and social needs. Despite its rapid growth, systematic evidence on variations in campaign characteristics and outcomes across cause types remains limited, particularly in Asia. To characterise the landscape of digital philanthropic crowdfunding in Asia by examining campaign volume, geographic distribution, temporal trends, fundraising performance, and disease-specific heterogeneity. Methods: A cross-sectional descriptive study was conducted on 5,803 publicly accessible campaigns on Give.Asia, a major crowdfunding platform across Southeast and East Asia between 2012 and 2025. Campaign data were extracted from unstructured text using a large language model (LLM)-assisted approach. Categorisation into five mutually exclusive cause groups was performed using predefined rule-based and keyword-driven algorithms. Variables included country, year, fundraising metrics, disease group, and narrative features. Findings: Health Causes campaigns was the largest category (n = 2,040; 35.2%), followed by Human Trafficking (22.0%), Animal (18.4%), Community/Social Support (17.4%), and Food (7.0%). Campaign volume grew nearly fourteen-fold from 2012 to 2025, with suppression during 2018–2020 and a sustained post-pandemic rebound. Human Trafficking achieved the highest completion (83.33%) despite the lowest median targets (USD 525), while Health Causes with the highest targets (USD 1,300) and donor counts (median 7) achieved only 10.00%. Fundraising targets differed significantly across disease groups (p<0·001), but amounts raised did not (p = 0.92). Interpretation: Crowdfunding outcomes vary substantially by cause, geographies, and disease profiles. Health Causes campaigns dominate in volume yet underperform relative to goals, highlighting structural limitations of digital philanthropy in addressing healthcare financing gaps and potential reinforcement of existing inequities.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6749888`

- title: Structured Multidimensional Representation Learning for Large Language Models
- authors: Mohamed El Guide, Alaa Elichi, Khalide Jbilou, Franck Dufrenois
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6749888
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Transformer architectures achieve state-of-the-art performance across a wide range of pattern recognition and natural language processing tasks, but their scaling is accompanied by substantial parameter growth and redundancy in the embedding dimension. In this work, we introduce a structured spectral factorization of the embedding space based on the L-product for third-order tensors. By reshaping token representations into spectral tensor slices and performing attention and feed-forward operations in the transform domain, we obtain a Tensor Transformer architecture that decomposes the encoder into p independent spectral sub-transformers while preserving standard Transformer semantics. We prove that the proposed L-Transformer is spectrally equivalent to p parallel Transformers operating on reduced-dimensional embeddings, which yields approximately 1/p reduction (up to lower-order terms such as biases and normalization parameters) in encoder parameters under fixed total embedding size. When instantiated with a real-valued Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT), the method remains fully differentiable and compatible with existing training pipelines. Beyond compression, the spectral decomposition introduces an inductive bias over embedding frequencies, enabling slice-dependent frequency scaling that improves generalization. Experiments on IMDB and AG~News show that the proposed model can substantially reduce encoder parameters (up to 75\% for p=4) while maintaining competitive accuracy. On IMDB, the tensorized encoder matches or improves upon the standard baseline under compression, whereas on AG~News at moderate width we observe a small accuracy decrease in exchange for a 4 times encoder reduction; at BERT-base width (d=768), performance returns to parity.

### abstract

Background. Generative artificial intelligence has reversed a pattern that has held throughout the history of technology: until now, the technical environment adapted to the subject; under generative AI, it is the subject who adapts to the environment. Aim. This article proposes the category of the vital interface to think about what kind of human ought to mediate between artificial systems and the world under the postdigital condition. Method. The argument proceeds through conceptual analysis, building the category from a cross-reading of four philosophical traditions: Ortega y Gasset's vital reason, Polanyi's tacit knowledge, Han's critique of total transparency, and Stiegler's theory of proletarianization. Findings. The category is contrasted with the notion of Human API articulated by Bhatt (2026) from an engineering perspective and illustrated through a pre-AI professional case showing that the tendency to act as a sensor predates the technology now amplifying it. Implications. The thesis is prescriptive: higher education must form vital interfaces (humans able to mediate between AI and the world through vital reason) rather than passive sensors optimized to transmit data to the machine. The argument engages Rancière's objection regarding pedagogical stultification, showing that deliberately forming the vital interface does not reproduce the logic of the explicating master but is its only effective counter when facing an AI which, unlike the inert objects with which the maître ignorant operates, has its own agenda: maximizing the continuation of dialogue. The article closes with a research agenda outlining the empirical and institutional operationalization of the thesis.

- title: THE REGULATORY ARCHITECTURE OF LEGAL AI: BAR AUTHORITY, FEDERAL PREEMPTION, AND THE GOVERNANCE GAP
- authors: JD Morris, Alexis Austin Litle, Deepankar Das
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6748958

### abstract

Federal courts and state bars have produced fundamentally incompatible regulatory regimes governing attorney use of artificial intelligence. More than three hundred federal court standing orders now address AI-assisted filings, ranging from mandatory disclosure to outright bans. More than forty-seven state bar ethics opinions, anchored by ABA Formal Opinion 512, permit AI use subject to competence, confidentiality, candor, and supervision obligations. Executive Order 14,365, issued in December 2025, directed the Department of Justice to challenge state AI laws as preempted by federal authority. Practitioners face contradictory commands from three separate regulatory layers, and no existing institutional architecture resolves the conflict. This Article develops two substantive targets that any coherent regulatory architecture for legal AI must enforce. The first target is a certification regime calibrated to what AI systems must do: a certainty-threshold framework operating per task class, per architectural tier, and per stake, drawing on the architectural-tier taxonomy and AI Legal Reference Model developed in prior papers in this series. The second target is an antecedent permission layer addressing whether a particular use is permitted at all, operationalized at the platform layer through role-based access control governing supervisory authority, attribute-based access control enforcing per-call operational authority, and federated agentic identity protocols enabling cross-organizational AI agent interaction without compromising privilege or confidentiality. Certification asks whether the tool is adequate; authority to process asks whether the use is permitted at all. The institutional allocation through which these targets are enforced forms a three-legged regulatory architecture. State bars retain primary authority over the ethical dimensions of attorney AI use under bar federalism and the Middlesex presumption. Federal courts retain authority over procedural requirements for AI-assisted filings under the Rules Enabling Act and inherent supervisory power. Federal legislation governs vendor regulation through a framework mirroring the three historical preemption models analyzed in this Article: SOX § 307 / Part 205, the McDade Amendment, and IRS Circular 230. The Allocation Principle resolves the regulatory trilemma without displacing state bar authority or producing federal preemption without replacement. The Article identifies five governance gaps the current architecture cannot resolve, develops a falsifiable five-year prediction across three regulatory configurations with probability weights, and supplies practitioner action items, regulator amendment templates, and a federal vendor-regulation framework. The proposed architecture is structurally compatible with the solo-practitioner segment of the bar through a lowest-common-denominator design constraint.
