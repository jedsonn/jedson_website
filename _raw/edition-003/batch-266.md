## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6549580`

- title: Distant Writing Journalism: Epistemology, Method, and Accountability of Journalistic Writing in the Age of Generative Artificial Intelligence
- authors: Fabio Mascagna
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6549580
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The growing adoption of generative artificial intelligence (AI) in journalism has so far been examined mainly in terms of automation, ethical concerns, and audience perceptions. While these perspectives are important, they remain insufficient to account for a more fundamental transformation: the epistemological status of journalistic writing when textual production is mediated by automated processes. The central issue is not simply who writes, but how journalistic claims are justified, verified, and rendered accountable in the public sphere. Building on Luciano Floridi's concept of distant writing and integrating scholarship from journalism studies, epistemology of news, and computational journalism, this article introduces the paradigm of Distant Writing Journalism (DWJ). DWJ reconceptualizes journalistic writing as a design-oriented practice in which journalists are responsible for the processes that lead to the production of texts, rather than for the mechanical act of writing itself. The article makes three main contributions. It provides a theoretical foundation for DWJ by rethinking authorship, intention, and operational truth in the context of AI-mediated writing. It formalizes an operational method based on claim mapping, verification-by-design, and process disclosure, conceived as replicable and open to empirical assessment. It examines the implications of DWJ for newsroom organization, editorial governance, and public trust, arguing that transparency of method can strengthen journalistic accountability. The article concludes that Distant Writing Journalism is not a marginal option but a necessary condition for preserving journalism's role as a practice of responsible public knowledge production in the age of generative artificial intelligence.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6672878`

- title: A Digital Twin and Procedural-DAO Architecture for EUDR-Specific Deforestation Monitoring under Evolving Regulatory Requirements
- authors: Jüri Sildam
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6672878
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), Regulation (EU) 2023/1115, requires operators and traders placing cattle, cocoa, coffee, palm oil, soya, wood, rubber, charcoal and their derived products on the EU market to demonstrate that the underlying commodities are deforestation-free after 31 December 2020, are legally produced, and are covered by a due diligence statement. While deforestation detection is often treated as the technically dominant requirement, EUDR compliance is broader: it also requires traceability across supply chains, provenance of production, country-specific legal context, and auditable due diligence processes. Translating these obligations into an inspectable, reproducible, software-supported workflow raises three coupled problems: (i) the regulation itself is a moving artefact whose definitions, country risk classifications and implementing acts evolve; (ii) the geospatial evidence used to satisfy Article 3(a) depends on upstream datasets-primarily the Hansen Global Forest Change product-whose versions, tile schemes and methodological conventions also change; and (iii) the resulting compliance interpretations cannot be ethically delegated to a fully autonomous agent because they affect market access, livelihoods and the legal exposure of operators. This paper describes an architecture that addresses these problems jointly through a closed feedback loop linking regulation, data dependencies, implementation, validation, and governance. We separate authoritative deterministic generation of evidence from a public, non-authoritative Digital Twin portal that exposes system state, dependencies, and example outputs for inspection. A procedural Decentralized Autonomous Organization (DAO)implemented in this work as a file-grounded YAML proposal workflow, but compatible with optional blockchain anchoring of evidence digests and proposal records-closes the governance loop between stakeholders, developers, and evolving regulatory interpretation. The procedural design is deliberate: as we discuss below, the kind of DAO appropriate for governing truth claims about the physical world differs in object, voting subject and failure mode from the protocol-governance DAOs commonly associated with the term, and the choice to run the governance layer off-chain reflects that difference rather than a rejection of distributed-ledger technology as such. An LLM-based Digital Twin Engineer (DTE) agent supports inspection and proposal drafting under strict grounding rules, but never executes code or makes compliance determinations. We describe the multi-repository implementation, the deterministic evidence bundle contract, the public/private trust-zone separation that protects per-operator plot data while allowing example reports to be inspected publicly, and the regulation-as-dependency feedback loop that forces reruns of impacted methods when upstream artefacts change. Although the current implementation focuses primarily on geospatial deforestation evidence, the proposed pipeline is intended as a practical starting point for progressive enrichment as new forms of land intelligence, supply-chain transparency, legal provenance data, and business-network evidence become available. We argue that this design is a generalisable pattern for compliance domains in which regulatory requirements evolve over time and implementations must remain inspectable, reproducible, extensible, and corrigible by humans.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6671075`

- title: GeoReg: Weight-Constrained Few-Shot Regression for Socio-Economic Estimation using LLM
- authors: Kyeongjin Ahn, Sungwon Han, Seungeon Lee, Donghyun Ahn, Hyoshin Kim, Jungwon Kim, Jihee Kim, Sangyoon Park
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6671075
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Socio-economic indicators like regional gross domestic product, population, and education levels are fundamental to evidence-based policy and sustainable development. This research introduces GeoReg, a regression model that integrates multi-modal data, including satellite imagery and web-based geospatial information, to estimate these indicators in data-scarce regions such as developing countries. Our approach leverages the prior knowledge of large language model to mitigate label scarcity. Within this framework, the language model functions as a "data engineer", extracting informative features to enable effective estimation in few-shot settings. Specifically, our model identifies contextual relationships between data features and target indicator, categorizing correlations as positive, negative, mixed, or irrelevant. These features are then processed by a linear estimator with category-specific weight constraints. To capture nonlinear patterns, the model also identifies meaningful feature interactions and integrates them, along with nonlinear transformations. Experiments in three countries at varying developmental stages demonstrate that our model consistently outperforms baselines in estimating socio-economic indicators, particularly in data-limited, low-income contexts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6591698`

- title: The Economic Dimension of Power for a Small Open Economy in the Age of Machine Knowledge Capital
- authors: Dan Ciuriak
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6591698
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This paper examines the evolving economic dimension of national power for a small open economy, focusing on Canada’s position in the transition to the age of machine knowledge capital (MKC). The analysis situates current dynamics within a broader historical sequence of technological epochs, emphasizing the shift from the data-driven economy to MKC following the 2022 inflection point marked by generative AI. Building on the traditional Mundell-Fleming framework, which demonstrates that small open economies respond differently to policy measures than do large, quasi-closed economies, it argues that small open economies face constraints in exercising geoeconomic power due to their greater reliance on trade and ipso facto their greater vulnerability to retaliation for a geoeconomic play. Accordingly, small open economies are drawn to rules-based systems and collective action rather than geoeconomic adventures. The paper develops a “theory of the case” to explain Canada’s relative decline in the data-driven era, highlighting the failure to capture data rents, the outflow of high-potential firms, and the resulting erosion of domestic value-added along the “smile curve.” It argues that in the MKC era, which is characterized by capital intensity and enormous appetite for data and compute, these weaknesses translate into diminished economic and geopolitical power, including a diminished capacity to benefit economically from the imperatives of building enhanced defence capabilities. The central conclusion is that, for small open economies, economic power in the MKC age hinges less on traditional factor endowments and more on the ability to retain and scale firms, capture data rents, and embed these capabilities in dual-use industrial strategies. Without these, conventional strengths in material resources, human capital and institutional quality do not translate into effective national power.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6677989`

- title: Less is More: Interpretable Prefix-Based Jailbreaking of MoE Language Models
- authors: Qingsong Xie, Yuwei Li, Shiwen Ou, Qiangpu Chen, Taiyan Wang, Yu Chen, Yi Shen, Zulie Pan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6677989
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures have become the dominant paradigm for scaling open-source large language models, yet their security implications remain insufficiently understood. Recent studies reveal that MoE safety alignment is concentrated in specific ``safety experts'' and ``safety neurons'', but existing methods require modifying model internals, limiting practical applicability. We identify that a small number of critical tokens in harmful prompts disproportionately activate safety experts at specific layers. Motivated by this insight, we propose ClearCut, a prefix-based jailbreak method that exploits this architectural vulnerability through input manipulation alone while remaining mechanistically interpretable. Specifically, ClearCut optimizes a short, semantically natural prefix with a hybrid contrastive-suppression loss that redirects these tokens away from safety experts, bypassing safety alignment without any modification to model weights. Across six mainstream MoE models, ClearCut achieves an average success rate of 64.6\%, outperforming GCG (45.1\%) and AutoDAN (51.2\%), while approaching GateBreaker (61.5\%) which requires model modification. The prefix averages only 5.77 tokens, substantially shorter than existing approaches. By identifying critical tokens and tracking routing changes, ClearCut provides interpretable pathways that reveal how MoE safety mechanisms function. This work exposes a fundamental security risk in MoE architectures, that safety experts can be disproportionately activated and manipulated by a small number of critical tokens. Beyond identifying this vulnerability, we provide ClearCut as a practical tool for security evaluation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6598018`

- title: From Tool Use to Evaluative Judgment: A Readiness Framework for Generative AI in Higher Education
- authors: Margeret Hall, Daniel Hawkins
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6598018
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI (GenAI) tools are rapidly reshaping higher education, yet institutional responses often oscillate between prohibition and permissiveness without a clear theory of student readiness. This white paper advances a multidimensional framework of GenAI readiness grounded in established readiness scholarship and empirical student data collected through focus groups and survey responses at a large public university. Rather than defining readiness as tool familiarity or prompt proficiency, the report conceptualizes GenAI readiness as a composite capacity spanning cognitive strategies (evaluative judgment under uncertainty), academic skills (disciplinary competence independent of automation), academic behaviors (self-regulation and delegation literacy), and contextual awareness (policy literacy and ethical norms). Student findings reveal that their dominant workflow is verification and that they routinely triangulate AI outputs against authoritative sources, adjust use based on task stakes, and express concern about dependency and skill erosion. At the same time, ambiguity around disclosure, paraphrase boundaries, and detectability creates anxiety and inconsistent behavior. These findings align with contemporary literature identifying both the pedagogical promise of AI-supported learning and the “performance paradox,” wherein short-term output gains may not translate into durable competence. The paper argues that the central institutional question is not whether GenAI should be allowed or banned, but under what design conditions it strengthens learning rather than substitutes for it. It proposes a readiness-centered institutional strategy that includes standardized policy vocabulary, explicit verification literacies, assessment redesign that makes reasoning visible, and structured professional development for faculty and staff. By reframing GenAI integration as a readiness problem rather than a compliance problem, the report offers a coherent path for aligning academic integrity, instructional design, and workforce preparation in AI-mediated learning environments.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6537638`

- title: A Proposed Model for Test Coverage Optimisation Using AI
- authors: Lakshmi Sravanya Thirukandiyur
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6537638
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Software testing is one of the most resource intensive phases of the software development lifecycle, often consuming up to forty to sixty percent of total project effort. A central challenge lies in determining which tests to create, execute, and evaluate to ensure that the system is meaningfully exercised. Traditional coverage metrics such as line, branch, and path coverage provide structural indicators of test adequacy, but high coverage does not necessarily guarantee fault detection. This paper proposes the Test Coverage Optimisation Using AI (TCOAI) framework, an intelligence driven approach that enhances test generation and prioritisation through adaptive learning techniques. The framework integrates three components: an AI driven Test Case Generation Engine using large language models and search based optimisation, a Coverage Intelligence Module that combines structural and mutation based signals into a Unified Adequacy Score (UAS), and a Reinforcement Learning Prioritisation Layer for selecting high value tests. The paper also presents a review of fifteen key studies, architectural diagrams, and a pseudoalgorithm describing the optimisation process, while outlining limitations and future research directions.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6673078`

- title: AI-Enabled Internship Learning: Measuring the Impact of PT Vidio Dot Com's Magang Berdampak Program on Work Readiness and Digital Competency Development
- authors: Alvien Khairullah, Amelia Wardani, Hermawan Sutanto
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6673078
- keyword hits: gemini, generative ai

### abstract

As the digital economy expands, the gap between higher education and industry requirements remains a critical challenge, particularly in emerging markets such as Indonesia. This study is conducted within the Magang Berdampak 2025 program, a national internship initiative funded by Kementerian Pendidikan Tinggi, Sains, dan Teknologi (Kemdiktisaintek), which aims to strengthen the link between education and industry through structured, impact-driven learning. Within this context, PT Vidio Dot Com implemented an AI-enabled internship model by integrating generative AI (Gemini) into daily workflows. Using data from 91 interns, this study examines the relationship between AI enablement, measured through usage frequency, depth of use, and perceived impact, work readiness , including digital skills, communication, and professional competencies. The findings show statistically significant positive relationships between AI enablement and all dimensions of work readiness, with the strongest association observed in digital readiness . Qualitative insights further indicate that AI functions as “scaffolded expertise,” accelerating learning, improving output quality, and enhancing professional behavior. These results highlight the role of AI in workforce development and suggest that integrating AI literacy into internship programs is a critical strategy for accelerating digital talent development and bridging the education–industry gap.
