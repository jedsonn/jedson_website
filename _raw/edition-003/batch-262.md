## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6658261`

- title: A tale of two cities: Bangalore's stagnation in the shadow of the California AI Boom
- authors: Satyam Sovasaria
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6658261
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

For decades, the Indian IT sector has been viewed as a leveraged proxy for US technology growth. This paper re-evaluates this relationship in the wake of the Generative AI revolution. Using daily data from March 2021 to February 2026, I apply a timezone-adjusted (lagged) asymmetric beta model to account for the market transmission delay between New York and Mumbai. I find that the structural relationship between the Nasdaq 100 and Nifty IT remains statistically stable. The sector continues to exhibit a low but significant upside beta (≈0.29) and a slightly higher downside beta (≈0.36), with no statistically significant structural break post-ChatGPT. However, a divergence emerges in risk-adjusted performance. While the Nasdaq 100's Sharpe Ratio surged from a crisis-level-0.57 to a hyper-efficient 1.14, the Nifty IT sector's efficiency remained stagnant (0.16 → 0.17). These findings suggest that Indian IT functions as a stable "utility-like" stakeholder in the tech ecosystem, insulated from the direct volatility of the AI boom but also excluded from its efficiency premium.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6662078`

- title: Overrated Judgment, Underrated Power: Gen AI at Work
- authors: Dora Moscato
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6662078
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI is reshaping cognitive work by compressing productivity gaps between top and low performers and improving the relative position of senior workers over junior entrants, because it still cannot adequately exercise human judgment in nuanced contexts. This paper argues that human judgment is less robust than the rhetoric of human oversight assumes-it is biased, noisy, weakly introspectable, and reliably narratable rather than reliably explainable-and that the only human function not replaceable by machines is, hopefully, goalsetting. Unfortunately, that function is exercised primarily by the firms that build and own the most capable models, not by users. The resulting axis of inequality runs between a small number of producing firms and user populations worldwide. Inclusion and accountability therefore require participation in defining the objectives these systems optimise for. The paper points out the necessity of reclassifying AI producers under the editorial obligations of transparency and pluralism that other communicative powers carry, and argues for close collaboration among academia, engineers, and policymakers to determine how shared participation in goal-setting can be secured. It acknowledges that no institutional response presently on offer is adequate to the asymmetry it confronts, and that mere regulation or sovereign AI alone does not resolve the problem.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6465719`

- title: Can Large Language Models Understand Visual Aesthetics for Intelligent Image Editing
- authors: Anshu Raj
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6465719
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Recent progress in multimodal AI has significantly expanded the capabilities of automated image editing systems. Contemporary models can perform a variety of complex editing operations, including object manipulation, style transformation, and lighting adjustment. However, despite these advancements, many existing systems still struggle to capture subjective aspects of visual aesthetics, such as compositional quality, visual balance, and stylistic harmony. These elements play a crucial role in determining how viewers perceive and evaluate visual content, yet they remain difficult to model computationally. In this work, we investigate whether large language models can reason about visual aesthetics and provide meaningful guidance for intelligent image editing. We propose a framework in which a language model analyzes an input image and generates structured aesthetic critiques together with actionable editing recommendations. These recommendations are then used to guide an image editing module that performs targeted modifications, including lighting enhancement, object repositioning, background simplification, and color adjustment.

- title: The Algorithmic Principalship: A Bibliometric Mapping of Artificial Intelligence in Educational Leadership
- authors: Aurelio Aguite Jr
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6660540
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The integration of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) demands a structural reassessment of educational governance and school management. This study applies a formal bibliometric methodology to analyze scholarly works derived from 45 uploaded datasets (representing 38 unique sources) to map the cognitive structure of the emerging "algorithmic principalship." By tracing publication trends across primary scholarly outlets and institutional databases, the research maps source productivity and synthesizes the literature into five core thematic clusters: leadership styles and administrative competencies, educator well-being and perceptions, policy and ethical governance, global innovation and pedagogy, and methodological shifts in academic research. Results indicate a field rapidly shifting from surface-level technology adoption to profound qualitative inquiries regarding human-AI interaction, ethical frameworks, and the institutional re-engineering of educational systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6582039`

- title: The Expansion Horizon: Generative AI Beyond the Reach of Current Economic Foresight
- authors: Ante Prodan, Jo-An Occhipinti, Pawel Swieboda, Roy Green, John Buchanan, Marcel Tanner
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6582039
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Between 2022 and 2025, the four largest AI-adopting technology firms increased revenue per employee by 38-89% while headcounts remained flat or declined. This productivity-headcount decoupling is now replicating across knowledge-work sectors with structurally different employment outcomes, yet the most influential economic model of AI's macroeconomic effects projects a total factor productivity increase of only 0.53-0.66 percentage points over ten years. We identify this divergence as the expansion horizon problem and argue that it arises because generative AI (genAI) constitutes the irruption of a sixth technological revolution in Carlota Perez's historical periodisation, not a phase within the fifth. Synthesising Perez's macro-historical framework with W. Brian Arthur's theory of combinatorial evolution and Weitzman's formalisation of recombinant growth, we show that genAI introduces a qualitatively new input factor (near-zero-marginal-cost cognitive labour) and a defining organisational principle (human capital compression). Every technological revolution decouples output from workforce scale to some degree, but genAI's version is distinguished by its domain generality and its reflexivity: it operates simultaneously across cognitive domains that the ICT paradigm left structurally intact, and it compresses the human capital required for innovation itself. We demonstrate that the dominant economic models of AI embed assumptions, fixed capability frontiers, sequential task substitution, exogenous technological change, that are systematically violated by a combinatorially self-expanding technology, producing underestimates that are not empirical gaps but category errors. A three-dimensional framework mapping deployment staging, demand elasticity, and implementation maturity explains why the decoupling dynamic produces employment recomposition in elastic-demand sectors and permanent contraction in inelastic-demand sectors. Economic models calibrated to static capability snapshots will systematically misguide policy during the critical institutional transition ahead, and the window for effective institutional response is narrowing as rapidly as the technology's capability frontier is expanding.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6465158`

- title: Tiered Asynchronous Queue Architecture for Decoupled Document Scoring and Large Language Model Analysis Pipelines
- authors: Triveni Gopala Krishna Ganta
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6465158
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

We describe a tiered asynchronous queue architecture that decouples deterministic batch document scoring (Tier 1) from large language model (LLM) gap analysis (Tier 2). The architecture's defining property is the absence of a failed terminal state: LLM failure reverts records to the SCORED state, ensuring Tier 1 deterministic scores remain valid and operator-accessible regardless of LLM availability. A five-state status machine (PENDING, SCORING, SCORED, ANALYZING, COMPLETE) governs record lifecycle, with SCORED as a valid terminal state for below-threshold documents. Tier 1 dispatches only above-threshold records to Tier 2 via a configurable composite score gate. Tier 2 routes through a multi-provider LLM fallback chain. A per-record operator override enables manual Tier 2 dispatch. We present the complete state machine, task pseudocode, database schema, failure handling, worked examples, and comparison to prior art.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6661419`

- title: cairnsearch: A privacy-first hybrid search and local RAG system for personal document collections
- authors: Muhammad Waqas
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6661419
- keyword hits: llama, llm

### abstract

cairnsearch is an open-source document search and question-answering system that runs entirely on the user’s machine. It indexes PDFs (including scanned ones via OCR), Word and Excel files, CSV, plain text, Markdown, HTML, and images into a single SQLite FTS5 index for lexical search and a NumPy-backed vector index for dense retrieval. The two signals are fused with a weighted sum; an optional cross-encoder reranks the top candidates. Questions are answered by a local LLM served through Ollama, with citations pointing back to the source chunks. The same core engine is exposed as a FastAPI web service, a single-page browser UI, and a Typer CLI. The intended users are people who need to search or query documents they cannot send to a cloud service: lawyers, clinicians, journalists, researchers, and anyone managing a personal archive. This paper describes the system’s architecture, its ingestion and retrieval pipeline, the built-in safeguards (PII detection, extraction guardrails, failed-file quarantine), and how it is deployed in practice.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6661422`

- title: MSMGOptimizer: An Open-source R package and Web Application for Streamlining Life Cycle Inventory Data Automation in Life Cycle Assessment
- authors: PHILIP DUAH, Kwame Awuah-Offei
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6661422
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) compilation is indispensable in any Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) study, yet the preparation of inventory data for import into LCA software remains one of the most time-consuming and error-prone steps in the LCA workflow. LCI datasets frequently originate from multiple data sources and formats, necessitating automation tools that can optimize inventory data for direct use in LCA modeling platforms. SimaPro, the world's leading LCA modeling platform, for example, requires inventory data to be structured according to a proprietary CSV specification that is labour-intensive to prepare manually. Existing tools remain largely inaccessible to most researchers due to their proprietary nature and high cost. or require a programming background that limits their adoption among practitioners unfamiliar with scripting languages. Approaches based on large language model-based (LLM) are prone to hallucinations and constrained by token capacity limits, affecting the accuracy and reliability of LCA studies. Here we present MSMGOptimizer, an open-source R package and interactive web application that automates the conversion of Excel-based LCI data into SimaPro-compatible CSV format with no programming knowledge. MSMGOptimizer utilizes the Shiny web framework and a structured Excel setup template system that supports single-product, multi-product, and large-scale inventory modeling strategies. We present the software architecture, core functionalities, and two case studies demonstrating the conversion, impact assessment, and sensitivity analysis of 1 kg of onshore crude oil extraction and 1 kg of alumina processing in the United States. The usefulness of MSMGOptimizer as a practical tool for accelerating LCA inventory automation is demonstrated.
