- title: Heterogeneous Energy Storage-Supported Demand Response in Integrated Energy Systems: A Resilient Market-to-Delivery Framework under Compound Disturbances
- authors: Reza Sepehrzad, HOSSEIN HOSSEINALIBEIKI, Nima Khosravi, Ahmed Al durra, Mahdieh S. Sadabadi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6811358
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Heterogeneous energy storage systems are central to preserving flexibility delivery in cyber-physical integrated energy systems (IES) when market-cleared commitments must remain feasible under uncertainty, cyber disturbances, communication degradation, and real-time operational deviations. This paper proposes a resilience-oriented market-to-delivery framework for storage-supported demand response (DR) in IES. The proposed model connects day-ahead flexibility clearing, multi-storage scheduling, learning-based decision support, real-time capability assessment, safety-filtered recourse, and verified service delivery within a unified hierarchical structure. In the day-ahead stage, a leader–follower market mechanism clears storage-supported load-to-grid commitments while considering distribution feasibility, settlement consistency, scenario-based risk exposure, and available storage capability. At the integrated energy system level, heterogeneous storage assets, conversion units, and comfort-aware flexible loads are coordinated to transform cleared commitments into device-feasible multi-carrier schedules. A constrained deep reinforcement learning (DRL) mechanism is used to solve the high-dimensional scheduling problem, while large language model (LLM) assistance is restricted to bounded reward calibration and storage-capability-aware repair-template selection rather than direct dispatch. During real-time execution, the cleared schedule is lifted into a fast delivery layer in which disturbance-aware estimation, storage-aware safety filtering, corrective recourse, fallback switching, and service verification determine whether the committed flexibility can be delivered, partially restored, or conservatively degraded. The framework therefore treats storage-supported DR not as a static cleared quantity, but as a physically constrained service whose credibility must be maintained from market commitment to real-time delivery. Numerical results demonstrate coherent scalability across benchmark, real-world, and large-scale systems, preserved storage-supported operational headroom, real-time delivery above 88% under compound disturbances, and bounded constraint excursions below 3%. Ablation, stability, sensitivity, and comparative analyses confirm that performance gains arise from the integrated storage-supported resilience chain rather than from isolated scheduling or control components.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6814012`

- title: Generative Artificial Intelligence and Household Risky Financial Market Participation: Evidence from China's Frontier Regions
- authors: Wucheng Chi, Runze Gong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6814012
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This study explores the impact of generative artificial intelligence (AI) on household participation in risky financial markets. Using micro-level data from the 2025 Frontier Economics Database Household Survey, we find that generative AI significantly increases both the probability of participating in risky financial markets and the proportion of risky financial assets held. Mechanism analysis reveals that generative AI influences participation primarily by alleviating information and borrowing constraints. Heterogeneity analysis shows that the impact of generative AI is more pronounced for households with lower human and physical capital, as well as those residing in regions characterized by either high cultural diversity or border proximity. Furthermore, generative AI and digital inclusive finance exhibit a complementary relationship, working synergistically to improve household financial decision-making. These findings enrich the existing literature on the role of Generative AI in household asset allocation decisions.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6819385`

- title: A Unified Fuzzy Logic Controller for Adaptive Text Generation ​in Large Language Models
- authors: Maxim Bobyr, Natalya Milostnaya, Svetlana Belskaia
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6819385
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

This paper proposes a unified fuzzy logic controller for adaptive text generation in large language models. The controller integrates three fuzzy inference mechanisms based on a sliding window parameter regulator for data preparation, an attention scaling coefficient regulator for the multi-head attention mechanism, and a decoding selectivity coefficient regulator for autoregressive generation. Each regulator is implemented using the Mamdani algorithm with centre-of-gravity defuzzification. The key architectural contribution is a joint fuzzification block in which membership degrees of input variables are computed once and transmitted in parallel to all regulators, reducing computational cost by a factor of two. Closed-loop feedback via the probability distribution entropy forms a dynamic control system. A protection mechanism against looping in autoregressive generation is developed by combining a repeated subsequence detector and a repetition penalty for recurring tokens. Three theorems are proved covering the computational efficiency of joint fuzzification, the reproduction of standard values under neutral context characteristics, and the stability of the anti-looping mechanism. Experimental evaluation on the pretrained GPT-2~(124M) model confirmed a 3.1-fold reduction in mean decoding entropy without modifying model weights. The final controller configuration demonstrated the best generation results across all three test contexts, outperforming standard GPT-2 decoding, nucleus sampling~($p=0.9$), and contrastive decoding.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6746523`

- title: Bias Mitigation in Generative Chatbots through Adversarial Debiasing
- authors: Parveen Kumar, Shanmugaraja Krishnasamy Venugopal, Dr. Savya Sachi, Sunjhla Handa, Sarvesh Kumar Gupta, Arpit Jain
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6746523
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Generative chatbots based on large language models (LLM) can learn and reproduce bias that occurs in training data and can lead to biased or unsafe behavior. Adversarial debiasing in this paper addresses such bias by adding an adversarial element in chatbot training. Specifically, we train an adversarial network to learn sensitive features (gender and race) given the output of the chatbot, and the core model to generate informative answers eliminating the footprint of these features. Based on this min-max game, the generator is compelled to produce responses that rely less on attributes that are protected by more attributes, and thus they are less biased. We also experimentally analyze benchmark conversational datasets, which demonstrate that our approach does not reduce quantifiable bias according to conventional fairness measures at the expense of quantifiable response fluency or relevance. Semantic analysis demonstrates that information is equally relevant and less stereotypical on this new data. We further examine trade-offs between fairness, utility and computational cost, and how the proposed settings can make useful deployment in practice. The contribution of this work to the mitigation of undesired AI behaviors is the presentation of a scalable and model-agnostic debiasing framework, which can ensure fairer generative chatbots in the application domains.

- title: From APT Reports to Interactive Attack Investigation: Intent-Aware Reasoning over Temporal Evidence Graphs
- authors: yiren chen, yunxiang wang, wentao li, Qiyuan Liu, weiwei ye, yan zhu, Bo Jiang, Zhigang Lu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6826531
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs) pose severe risks to network security and require timely, effective investigation. Existing APT investigation systems typically reconstruct attack summary graphs or generate APT reports. However, such static outputs offer limited support for analysts' follow-up queries, and errors or omissions in the reconstructed outputs can propagate, potentially leading to incomplete or incorrect conclusions.To address these limitations, we present \textsc{Weaver}, an intent-aware interactive APT investigation framework. \textsc{Weaver} constructs temporal evidence graphs (TEGs) that aggregate suspicious evidence over time while preserving intermediate investigation states. The large language model (LLM) then refines anomaly labels, reconstructs attacks, and generates evidence-grounded answers conditioned on analyst queries, iteratively updating intermediate evidence according to feedback. We evaluate \textsc{Weaver} on DARPA E3 and E5 datasets. The system achieves competitive detection and reconstruction performance. Given 20 analyst queries from E3-CADETS and E3-THEIA, \textsc{Weaver} improves 25.3\% \emph{Answer Recall} rate and reduces 64.3\% \emph{Hallucination Rate} on average, substantially outperforming baselines. Ablation analysis further validates the contributions of TEGs, intent parsing, and structured evidence processing, while overhead measurements confirm efficiency under practical computational budgets.Overall, \textsc{Weaver} bridges automated APT reconstruction with human-in-the-loop reasoning, enabling adaptive, accurate, and interactive investigation.

## uid: `arxiv:2605.26074v1`

- title: StakeBench: Evaluating Language Understanding Grounded in Market Commitment
- authors: Yunhua Pei, Jingyu Hu, Yiwei Shi, Hongnan Ma, Weiru Liu, John Cartlidge
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-25
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26074v1
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

Existing financial NLP benchmarks often rely on labels supplied by outside observers, measuring how language is perceived rather than what speakers have committed to in the market. We introduce StakeBench, an evaluation framework for language understanding grounded in market commitment. StakeBench links 560,876 comments from 2,261 resolved markets to verified position, action, and market-odds records across Polymarket and Manifold. Supervision is derived from observable market behavior. Position sides, post-comment trading actions, and market-odds trajectories replace human annotation. Four diagnostic tasks test whether models detect market commitment, identify the revealed side, anticipate future action, and perform collective odds projection. Three commitment-aware metrics measure alignment with revealed preferences rather than perceived sentiment. Validity audits and explicit interpretation boundaries help distinguish observable commitment signals from latent belief and causal market-odds impact. Across 15 LLMs and 18 topics and platform settings, models partially recover position-side signals, with Directed Accuracy from 0.506 to 0.599, but show structural failures on later tasks. Ten of the fifteen models collapse to one or two action labels in future action anticipation, and no model consistently improves on the naive odds-direction baseline in collective odds projection. Model scale is not correlated with performance, finance-domain tuning does not improve revealed-side identification, and platform incentives strongly shape higher-order results. StakeBench is packaged with evaluation code and dataset under CC-BY 4.0.

- title: From Assessment Integrity to Institutional Capacity: How Business Schools Are Adapting Management Education to Generative AI
- authors: Chander Carvalho, Luis Henrique Pereira
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6831684
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) is challenging business schools to reconsider not only what they teach, but also how learning is assessed, governed, and aligned with evolving professional expectations. This article examines how global business schools are adapting management education in response to generative AI. Drawing on qualitative evidence from 12 semi-structured interviews with academic directors and program managers from CEMS member schools across multiple regions, the study analyzes emerging institutional responses to AI-related disruption in curriculum, assessment, and governance. The findings show that adaptation occurs through a layered process. First, schools are redesigning assessment and academic integrity practices to preserve credible evidence of learning, using formats such as oral examinations, in-class assessments, presentations, and reinforced project defenses. Second, they are integrating AI into curricula through electives, short modules, AI labs, and embedded course activities. Third, they are developing institutional capacity through committees, guidelines, faculty development, and pedagogical innovation structures. The study proposes a Three-Layer Adaptation Framework that conceptualizes generative AI integration as a multidimensional process involving assessment integrity, curriculum integration, and institutional capacity, shaped by transversal concerns such as ethics, AI literacy, employability, infrastructure, and operational readiness.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6832338`

- title: Intent-Driven CPU Heterogeneity Management in the Cloud-Edge Continuum
- authors: Djawida Dib, Thijs Metsch
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6832338
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

The Cloud-Edge Continuum introduces significant CPU heterogeneity within individual deployment sites due to incremental hardware upgrades, diverse processor generations, and specialized configuration requirements. This heterogeneity creates performance variations that critically impact Service Level Objective (SLO) compliance for latency-sensitive workloads such as Large Language Model (LLM) inference. While container orchestration platforms like Kubernetes provide resource isolation, they treat CPUs as scalar quantities based on core counts, remaining agnostic to fine-grained architectural differences that can create 2-3× performance variations under equivalent resource allocations. This paper presents a specialized CPU profile management actuator for Intent-Driven Orchestration (IDO) frameworks. We formalize the CPU profile selection problem as multi-objective optimization under uncertainty and develop an ML-enhanced actuator that predicts workload performance across heterogeneous CPU profiles; with configurations combining hardware characteristics, allocation policies, and system settings. The actuator integrates into IDO’s planner-actuator architecture, providing specialized analysis capabilities that enable the orchestration planner to dynamically select optimal profiles based on declared intents and current system conditions. We evaluate this approach on a representative four-node heterogeneous Kubernetes cluster using latency-sensitive LLM inference workloads (Phi 3.5). Our comprehensive evaluation methodology assesses both SLO satisfaction and resource efficiency. Experimental results demonstrate that the ML-enhanced actuator achieves 78.8% SLO satisfaction while maintaining 0.33 idle CPU cores; outperforming static single-profile approaches (39.8-50.4% satisfaction) and achieving 50.7% resource overhead reduction compared to conservative over-provisioning. The system achieves 72.73% decision accuracy against optimal offline selection. These findings validate that CPU heterogeneity awareness substantially improves both SLO compliance and resource efficiency in edge-cloud continuum deployments, while establishing a reusable methodology for evaluating dynamic resource management systems.
