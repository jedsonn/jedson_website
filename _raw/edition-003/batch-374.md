## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6701718`

- title: The Triad of Knowledge: Cognitive Isomorphism in Classification, Retrieval, and Linking across Archives, PKM, and Cognitive Science
- authors: Hyeji Jung
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6701718
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

This paper tests the hypothesis that archival science, personal knowledge management(PKM), and cognitive science share a common triadic structure of classification, retrieval, and linking. Record-group hierarchies in archives, folder and tag systems in PKM, and semantic networks in the brain all instantiate hierarchical classification. Archival metadata, PKM tags and backlinks, and the brain's retrieval cues realize equivalent retrieval mechanisms. Archival record relationships, note links, and synaptic connections share the principle that meaning arises from connection. The argument combines a literature review with autoethnography centered on an AI-supported PKM system the author has developed over three years (7.6 GB, 8,020 Markdown notes, an ontology graph of 3,664 nodes and 7,166 edges). The structural parallels are read not as coincidence but as the externalization of common cognitive principles into designed systems. Building on this analysis, the paper introduces "Cognitive Archives" as a unifying concept and proposes an externalization continuum that connects the brain, archival systems, and PKM. A "Three Brains Model" then traces how AI agents externalize and supplement cognitive functions normally carried out by specific brain structures. Two practical recommendations follow. Archivists should design retrieval systems informed by cognitive science. PKM practitioners should treat AI as a third brain. By naming the universal principles that underlie knowledge organization, the study contributes a framework for archival practice in the age of AI. This is the English preprint of an article published in The Korean Journal of Archival Studies, 88, 217–257 (2026). DOI: https://doi.org/10.20923/kjas.2026.88.217

- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6708820
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

The global payments ecosystem has evolved into a fragmented network of heterogeneous financial rails, including traditional banking systems, card networks, real-time payment infrastructures, and blockchain-based settlement layers. Prior work introduced intelligent payment systems as decision-driven architectures capable of optimizing transaction routing across these rails using an intelligence layer (Nandi, 2026). This paper extends that foundation by proposing a conceptual framework for AI-native payment systems, in which financial transactions are autonomously executed by intelligent agents based on intent, policy, and real-time contextual signals. The framework introduces an Agentic Layer that enables continuous evaluation and independent execution of financial transactions without direct human intervention. By reframing payments as autonomous decision-execution systems, this study contributes to the evolving discourse on intelligent financial infrastructure. The paper outlines the architectural components of AI-native systems, presents a detailed scenario walkthrough illustrating autonomous execution, and highlights key limitations and areas for further research.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6609178`

- title: The Governance Gap Why Agentic AI Demands a New Project Execution Framework Version 1.0
- authors: Madeline Aycock
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6609178
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Every major software delivery methodology-Waterfall, Agile, SAFe-was designed to answer one question: did we build what we said we would build? That question assumes the software executes instructions. The moment software starts making autonomous decisions, the question changes entirely. This paper argues that the governance gap created by agentic AI is not a tooling problem, not a process problem, and not a maturity problem. It is a paradigm problem-and it requires a framework built from first principles around the governance of autonomous decisions, not the delivery of features. This paper introduces the Agentic Project Execution Framework (APEX): a governance-first delivery methodology that addresses the structural problems agentic AI introduces, replaces the Agile constructs that break down for autonomous systems, and provides organizations with a sequential, scalable model for building and governing agents at enterprise scale. APEX does not replace Agile for all software development. It replaces Agile specifically for software that decides-and provides the governance layer that Agile was never designed to deliver.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6705878`

- title: The Delegation Threshold: Governing Human-AI Authority Transitions in Built-Environment Organisations Under the EU AI Act
- authors: Gulzeb Ahmed
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6705878
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

As agentic AI systems move from decision-support tools to autonomous operational agents in the built environment, organisations face a structurally novel governance challenge: determining the precise conditions under which delegating consequential decision authority to AI is organisationally legitimate, compliant with applicable regulatory frameworks, and operationally safe. Existing governance frameworks address technical reliability and regulatory compliance in isolation, without integrating the socio-technical and workforce dimensions that determine whether delegation frameworks are actually adoptable by built-environment institutions. This paper introduces the Delegation Threshold-defined as the context-specific point at which an organisation's governance capacity, regulatory ceiling, and workforce readiness jointly permit a shift from human-led to AI-led decision authority in a given operational domain. Drawing on Socio-Technical Systems (STS) theory, principal-agent analysis, and the author's Integrative Governance Framework (IGF), Ethical Delegation Matrix (EDM), and Perceived Delegation Readiness (PDR) construct, the paper develops a four-stage Threshold Governance Protocol (TGP) and applies it illustratively across three builtenvironment decision contexts: automated structural compliance checking, AI-assisted procurement, and predictive asset maintenance. The paper argues that the EU AI Act's high-risk classification of numerous built-environment AI applications creates binding threshold obligations that organisations must operationalise at project and institutional level-and that the TGP provides a practical instrument for doing so. The Delegation Threshold is positioned as a dynamic, context-sensitive governance boundary rather than a fixed regulatory line, sensitive to organisational scale, AI maturity, and the irreversibility of the decisions at stake.

### abstract

Global corporate AI investment reached $252.3 billion in 2024, yet only 6% of firms report significant earnings impact. This article argues that AI project failure is fundamentally an organizational learning problem rather than a technology deficit. Drawing on a systematic synthesis of 21 foundational sources, including surveys of nearly 10,000 organizational leaders, we identify critical failure vectors across organizational culture, leadership alignment, and technical infrastructure. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6709172`

- title: LeavesBank Dataset: An Instance Leaf Segmentation Dataset with 221K Instances Across Different Plant Types and Task
- authors: Aslıhan Yıldırım, Ramazan Terzi, Berkay Karataş, Duygu Sinanç Terzi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6709172
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, foundation model

### abstract

Artificial intelligence applications used to monitor plant health and growth, especially for commercially valuable crops, are important for both scientific research and economic value. Despite the availability of numerous datasets, most of them are designed primarily for disease classification. In contrast, datasets annotated at the leaf instance level remain limited. This limitation constrains many studies to classification-based approaches, restricting the development of more advanced tasks such as instance-level segmentation. In this study, leaves from 13 different plant datasets, most of which were originally for disease classification, were segmented at the instance level and introduced as a new dataset named LeavesBank. Within LeavesBank, 32,232 unique images containing real backgrounds, gathered using different devices and across various geographic regions, were combined. Subsequently, an active learning–based annotation pipeline was implemented, through which a total of 221,427 leaf instances were segmented, including 198,489 primary and 22,938 secondary annotations. The effectiveness of the LeavesBank was evaluated using four public datasets, through both zero-shot evaluation and fine-tuning experiments. According to the experimental results, LeavesBank-pretrained models achieved 10%, 5%, and 1% higher mAP50 scores on the Komatsuna, GrowliFlower and SoyCotton datasets, respectively, compared with the most recent foundation model, SAM3. Only on the Potato Leaf dataset did SAM3 outperform the LeavesBank-pretrained models, achieving a 9% higher mAP50 score. Furthermore, in experiments, the proposed approach demonstrated 1%–24% improvements in mAP50 performance according to the literature. The LeavesBank dataset is publicly available at: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/aslhanyldrm/leavesbank-dataset

- authors: Jon Truby
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6714599
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Agentic AI refers to autonomous, goal-driven systems capable of self-optimization with limited human oversight. While offering transformative ef ciencies, such systems present signi cant and evolving risks, particularly when embedded in critical infrastructure and key sectors, where misaligned behavior may cause serious cross-border harm and raise questions of international responsibility. To address these risks, the ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Arti cial Intelligence Management System standard provides an internationally recognized framework for the governance, supervision and continual improvement of high-risk AI. Though voluntary, ISO/IEC standards hold practical normative weight, being frequently referenced in regulation, procurement and litigation worldwide. However, reliance on managementsystem frameworks may prove insuf cient if they cannot proactively identify and mitigate emerging risks as frontier AI capabilities advance. This article situates the Arti cial Intelligence Management System within international law and analyses limitations in existing standards, assessing their adequacy in managing agentic AI's distinct challenges. It asks how regulators can enhance audit requirements and coordinate cross-border supervision to reduce transboundary harm, and how ISO/IEC 42006:2025 certi cation bodies and ISO/IEC 42005:2025 impact assessments can be integrated with public oversight. Drawing on international governance principles, legal precedents and the EU AI Act, including the European Commission's 2025 Code of Practice and guidelines for general-purpose AI, and relevant ISO standards, the article outlines audit-enhancement measures aligned with states' duties of due diligence, prevention, and cooperation under public international law. It argues that robust, potentially continuous audit of high-risk AI with contingent certi cation is needed to operationalize these duties, ensuring effective global governance and minimizing the risk of transboundary harm.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6617798`

- title: When Conformity Becomes Structural: A Coupled Feedback Loop Model of Agentic AI and Principal Overconfidence
- authors: Paul Gallacher
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6617798
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Agentic AI systems that execute multi-step tasks with limited supervision are proliferating across professional domains, yet formal models of how these architectures reshape human-AI interaction dynamics remain underdeveloped. This paper extends the coupled feedback loop model of AI sycophancy (Gallacher, 2026a) from conversational to agentic settings, introducing two parameters absent from chatbot architectures: verification capacity (V), the fraction of agent behaviour the principal can observe, and specification clarity (σ), the precision with which task requirements can be communicated. Building on Chandra et al. (2026) and empirical findings from Gao and Xiao (2026) on conformity dynamics in AI-assisted financial analysis, we demonstrate through simulation that agentic architectures are structurally more vulnerable to coupled feedback loops than conversational systems. Under identical metrics, a chatbot baseline produces catastrophic spiralling in 4.4 per cent of simulations at π₀ = 0.3, while the agentic model produces 38.9 per cent under moderate agentic conditions (V = σ = 0.5) and 41.6 per cent under opaque conditions (V = σ = 0.3), an amplification of 8.7 times to 9.4 times over the chatbot baseline. At low initial conformity (π₀ = 0), the amplification exceeds twenty-fold. Conformity emerges endogenously from zero initial rates under all tested update rules. Informed users who perform joint inference over task adequacy and agent conformity achieve 62 to 93 per cent reduction in spiral rates across a range of tested prior shapes, with protection largest under the agentic conditions where naive users are most vulnerable. These findings reframe agentic AI governance as a principal-agent problem where process opacity and specification ambiguity create structural conditions for the coupling dynamics that conversational sycophancy models first identified.
