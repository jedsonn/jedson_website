- title: Seeing Without Understanding: Large Language Model Evaluation of Mobile User Interface Quality, Failure Taxonomy, and Architectural Explanation
- authors: Md. Rejaul Korim Sadi, Golam Mostofa Naeem, Toufiqur Rahman Tasin, Syed Mostofa Moosa, Mahmudul Hasan Emon, Mahmudur Rashid, Ferdus Ahmed
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6771787
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Evaluating mobile user interface quality at scale remains a persistent challenge in software engineering and human computer interaction. Rule-based heuristic methods offer structural reliability but demand significant engineering effort, while human annotation does not scale to the volume of applications produced annually. Large language models present a promising alternative, yet their reliability for structured UI judgment has not been systematically examined, and the patterns behind their failures remain insufficiently characterized. This paper addresses both gaps. We begin with the complete RICO dataset of 66,261 real-world mobile application screens, from which we derive a refined evaluation corpus of 15,000 screens through a rigorous, literature-guided selection process. Each screen is assessed across seven criteria grounded in UI research: structural JSON validity, minimum visible element count, clickable component presence, non-zero layout bounds, image integrity, and perceptual duplicate removal. Against this curated corpus, we apply a heuristic baseline scorer built from severity-weighted usability signals, normalized layout metrics, and pixel-ratio complexity measures drawn from established interface quality literature, calibrated to real user sentiment rather than abstract design rules. Multiple language models then independently rate each screen across usability, layout quality, and visual complexity, primarily from structured JSON descriptions and secondarily from raw mobile application screens. We conduct dimension-level statistical comparison between model ratings and heuristic ground truth using agreement rates, Cohen's Kappa, and confidence calibration, examining where models converge with the baseline and where they diverge. Recurring divergence patterns are organized into a failure taxonomy and interpreted through transformer architectural signatures: MLE plausibility bias, attention misgrounding, and autoregressive over-commitment.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6766598`

- title: The Agentic Law Firm: Competence, Supervision, Confidentiality, and Conflicts Across Six Levels of AI Autonomy
- authors: Alexis Austin Litle, JD Morris, Deepankar Das
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6766598
- keyword hits: agentic, generative ai, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

In March 2026, an Orange County Superior Court sanctioned an attorney whose AI system autonomously generated and filed a motion containing fabricated citations without the attorney’s review. The AI did not hallucinate a single case in a research query. It drafted a pleading, selected authorities, assembled arguments, signed the attorney’s name to the filing, and submitted it to the court. The attorney learned of the fabrications when opposing counsel raised them. This is not a hallucination problem. It is a supervision problem. And it is not a problem the Model Rules of Professional Conduct were designed to address. Existing scholarship on artificial intelligence (AI) in legal practice treats AI tools as nonlawyers under Model Rule 5.3, supervised under the same framework applied to paralegals, contract attorneys, and other human assistants. American Bar Association (ABA) Formal Opinion 512 adopts this uniform treatment. That framework absorbed the typewriter, word processing, electronic research, and even retrieval-augmented generative AI. But agentic AI systematically inverts four assumptions embedded in the supervision doctrine: that nonlawyer assistance operates at human speed under continuous attorney direction, within observable scope, with ex-ante conflict identification. When those assumptions fail, the doctrine’s gap-filling mechanisms fail with them. This Article proposes a framework that calibrates Model Rules compliance to AI autonomy level through two analytical axes. First, a six-level autonomy spectrum (Level 0 through Level 5) adapted from the Society of Automotive Engineers J3016 taxonomy and the emerging AI governance consensus of DeepMind’s Levels of AGI and the Cloud Security Alliance’s Agentic Trust Framework, calibrated to the doctrinal task of Model Rules compliance. Second, a dual-vector framework that distinguishes substantive legal practice (Vector A: competence, communication, candor, conflicts) from firm operations (Vector B: confidentiality infrastructure, trust accounting, marketing). The two vectors carry different ceilings: Vector A permits autonomy through Level 2 with limited Level 3 deployment for non-judgment components; Vector B permits autonomy through Level 4 under bounded-autopilot architecture. The framework produces five categorical bright lines the Rules assign to named attorneys by text and that no level of AI autonomy can cross: rendering legal advice under Rule 2.1; Rule 11 and Rule 26(g) certification; privilege calls under Rule 1.6; substantive communication judgment under Rule 1.4(b); and Rule 1.7 and 1.9 conflict clearance. The Article identifies a substance-touch rule for mixed-vector communications: any communication that transmits, summarizes, or characterizes substantive legal analysis is Vector A regardless of the channel through which it is delivered. The Article identifies a combinatorial compounding problem that produces the Level 5 categorical impossibility for substantive practice and that limits Level 4 deployment to operationally bounded tasks. The Article identifies six architectural boundaries that define bounded autopilot operation. The prediction is eighteen months: at least one state bar will adopt autonomy-level calibration in a formal opinion, and at least one reported sanctions decision will cite the bright-line framework. If either condition fails to occur within the window, the framework is falsified.

### abstract

This paper presents a semi-automatic methodology to construct VietTravelVQA, a localized, multi-difficulty visual question answering (VQA [1]) dataset of 1,406 openly licensed images [2] and 7,030 annotated question-answer pairs. It serves as a foundation for multimodal AI research in culturally specific domains. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6800169`

- title: Skills, Not Scale: GenAI and Technology Adoption
- authors: Gianmarco Ottaviano, Nuriye Melisa Bilgin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6800169
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Do the determinants of technology adoption depend on technological architecture? Using administrative data on Turkish firms from 2021 to 2024, we compare the adoption of traditional and generative artificial intelligence (GenAI). We show that GenAI adoption is driven by workforce skill intensity and is not positively associated with firm size, whereas traditional AI depends on both scale and skills. Firms that adopt both technologies are distinct and represent the most persistent adoption mode. Conditional on adoption, the skill-to-size ratio governs technology choice, and transition dynamics indicate a sequential process in which firms adopt GenAI before expanding to hybrid use. Exploiting the release of ChatGPT as a quasi-experimental reduction in access costs, we find that high-skill firms differentially increased GenAI adoption, while firm size played a limited role. These results suggest that the canonical size-based diffusion pattern is not universal but depends on the cost structure of technologies, with implications for innovation policy and productivity dispersion.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6806084`

- title: Trust in Generative AI Companions: Care and Stratified Prosocial Pathways among Chinese Youth
- authors: Brianu Goldfarb, Chen Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6806084
- keyword hits: deepseek, generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI chatbots are increasingly used as everyday companions, yet their role in shaping prosocial intentions remains poorly understood, especially outside Western contexts. Drawing on survey data from 1,025 Chinese youth who use ERNIE Bot, DeepSeek Chat, or Doubao, this study examines how trust in generative AI shapes prosocial intentions through distinct cognitive and affective pathways. Structural equation modeling and moderated mediation analyses reveal two differentiated mechanisms: a robust satisfaction pathway that is largely independent of trust, and an emotional dependency pathway that emerges primarily under high trust. We argue that these asymmetrical pathways constitute an emerging AI relational divide, in which users vary not only in access to functional benefits but also in access to emotionally mediated prosocial gains. By situating these findings in China’s platform and policy environment, the study contributes to research on the social impacts of AI companionship and offers implications for prosocial-by-design governance and platform development.

### abstract

An AI-powered smart advisory system for Precision Agriculture leveraging Large Language Models and Retrieval-Augmented Generation is presented in this paper. The proposed framework generates personalized, contextual, and explainable agricultural recommendations based on farmers' queries as well as soil characteristics, weather conditions, crop information and agricultural domain-specific knowledge. A retrieval augmented generation approach is employed to gather the relevant agricultural information prior to generating advisory responses, thus preventing any hallucinations or out-of-date recommendations. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6786098`

- title: SCOPE: Submodular Context Optimisation with Pandora's-Box Exploration
- authors: John Christiansen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6786098
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Token cost is now the dominant marginal expense of large language model (LLM) deployment in production. Existing token-reduction methods-information-theoretic prompt compression (LLMLingua, SelectiveContext), similarity-based top-k retrieval, reranking, and recent adaptive-depth methods (CAR, Stop-RAG) and bandit-based retrieval (PURPLE)-are heuristic in nature or address one aspect of the problem in isolation, and lack composable approximation and regret guarantees. We propose SCOPE (Submodular Context Optimisation with Pandora's-Box Exploration), an operations research framework whose central thesis is that token-efficient retrieval is most naturally posed as the composition of three classical OR sub-problems, each carrying its own guarantee that survives the composition: (i) which context chunks to include is cast as monotone submodular maximisation under a knapsack constraint, solved by lazy greedy with a (1-1/e-ε) approximation guarantee; (ii) how deep to retrieve before stopping is cast as Weitzman's Pandora's Box problem with a closed-form reservation-value index; and (iii) how to learn chunk values from observed reward is cast as a combinatorial contextual bandit, yielding sublinear O(d√(T log T)) regret. Three contributions distinguish this work from the now-substantial literature on each individual module: a compositional argument that all three guarantees hold jointly; a hidden-metadata benchmark protocol that exposes a quality ceiling for cosine-only retrievers and demonstrates measurable bandit-driven improvement; and a regulated-finance application framing addressing FCA Consumer Duty, PRA SS1/23 model risk, and EU AI Act auditability requirements. Three case studies-consumer-credit regulatory question answering, SME lending decision support, and customer-service query triage demonstrating online learning-show 3× token reduction at +0.15 quality improvement relative to top-k retrieval, with empirical cumulative-regret exponent 0.60 tracking the theoretical √(T log T) bound. A fourth section develops in detail the application to software engineering workflows, where the metadata signal is unusually rich (git, CI, issue trackers) and the reward signal is unusually dense (developer accept/reject, test pass/fail, PR merge/revert).

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6788620`

- title: Cognitive Delegation in Childhood: A Theory of Skill Formation under Generative AI
- authors: Siqi Wei, Naying Zhou
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6788620
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

We develop a dynamic theory of childhood human-capital formation under generative artificial intelligence. The technology separates observable school output from the cognitive process that historically produced it: a child can submit a finished answer, essay, or analysis without performing the active cognitive work through which durable capacity is built. We call this class of uses cognitive delegation, and we distinguish process-preserving delegation, in which the child's active engagement remains above the threshold required for capacity formation, from process-replacing delegation, in which it does not. Treating the active-engagement distinction from cognitive science as a primitive of dynamic skill formation, our main result decomposes the long-run capacity wedge under process-replacing delegation into a contemporaneous discipline-channel cost and a cascade cost from downstream knowledge complementarities. Two implications follow. First, the marginal welfare cost is concentrated in early childhood. Second, under sufficient conditions on the household and institutional environment, the welfare effect of AI varies with governance capacity-a composite of observable features such as parental supervision time, teacher process-monitoring, process-based assignments, and household digital literacy. The developmental effect of generative AI therefore depends not on access alone, but on whether governance preserves or replaces the cognitive process through which childhood capacity is formed.
