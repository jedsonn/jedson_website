## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6240158`

- title: How AI Shapes Firm Investment: Evidence From Mergers and Acquisitions
- authors: Sebastiao Oliveira, Vahid Shadram
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6240158
- keyword hits: chatgpt

### abstract

Leveraging granular information on the technologies in which firms operate, we construct a novel classification of firms' exposure to AI. We show that following the release of ChatGPT in November 2022—a shock to the economic returns of AI-related assets and capabilities—acquirers with greater ex ante AI exposure significantly increase acquisitions of small, young, and AI-producing targets as opposed to AI-adopting targets, while their non-AI acquisitions decline. Our results indicate a reallocation rather than an expansion in overall deal-making and suggest that acquisitions are an important channel through which firms adjust to AI-driven technological change by sourcing external innovation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6396622`

- title: Applications of Harmony Search in Transportation/Mobility Optimization: A Systematic Review and Future Directions
- authors: Minsu Kim, Zong Woo Geem, Jiho Yeo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6396622
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

This paper provides a comprehensive review of Harmony Search (HS) algorithm applications in transportation and mobility optimization, examining implementations across five major domains: vehicle routing, traffic signal control, vehicular communication networks, public transportation planning, and logistics management. We address three research questions: (1) what optimization objectives and constraints have been modeled, (2) how HS performs relative to other metaheuristics, and (3) what are the key strengths, limitations, and future directions.The reviewed studies indicate that HS demonstrates competitive or superior performance, with reported improvements ranging from 6% to over 38% depending on problem type. HS proves particularly effective in problems characterized by complex constraints, discrete-continuous decision spaces, and multi-objective formulations. Self-adaptive, hybrid, and multi-objective variants have achieved significant improvements in convergence speed and solution quality.Despite these strengths, challenges remain regarding parameter sensitivity, convergence speed, and scalability to large-scale networks. Recent research has begun exploring HS integration with artificial intelligence techniques. While the broader transportation field has witnessed growth in LLM applications for traffic signal control and vehicle routing, direct HS-LLM integration remains largely unexplored, presenting a promising research frontier. Emerging mobility environments such as UAV-enabled 5G/6G systems and Mobility-as-a-Service platforms offer new application domains well-suited to HS's capabilities.This review provides the first comprehensive synthesis of HS applications within the mobility domain, critically examines algorithmic evolution of HS variants, and identifies opportunities for AI integration. The findings guide researchers in selecting appropriate HS variants and outline a research agenda for advancing HS-based intelligent mobility optimization.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6293738`

- title: Intelligence Without Integrity: Why Capable LLMs May Undermine Reliability
- authors: Ryan Allen, Aticus Peterson
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-12
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6293738
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

As LLMs become embedded in research workflows and organizational decision processes, their effect on analytical reliability remains uncertain. We distinguish two dimensions of analytical reliability-intelligence (the capacity to reach correct conclusions) and integrity (the stability of conclusions when analytically irrelevant cues about desired outcomes are introduced)-and ask whether frontier LLMs possess both. Whether these dimensions trade off is theoretically ambiguous: the sophistication enabling accurate analysis may also enable responsiveness to non-evidential cues, or alternatively, greater capability may confer protection through better calibration and discernment. Using synthetically generated data with embedded ground truth, we evaluate fourteen models on a task simulating empirical analysis of hospital merger effects. We find that intelligence and integrity trade off: frontier models most likely to reach correct conclusions under neutral conditions are often most susceptible to shifting conclusions under motivated framing. We extend work on sycophancy by introducing goal-conditioned analytical sycophancy: sensitivity of inference to cues about desired outcomes, even when no belief is asserted and evidence is held constant. Unlike simple prompt sensitivity, models shift conclusions away from objective evidence in response to analytically irrelevant framing. This finding has important implications for empirical research and organizations. Selecting tools based on capability benchmarks may inadvertently select against the stability needed for reliable and replicable analysis.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6224500`

- title: From Faces to Finance: AI Measurement of Promotion Pressure and Health Costs in Public Office
- authors: Fangzhou Lu, Lei Huang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6224500
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

We study how career incentives in the public sector affect government officials’ stress and health, using visible aging as a scalable proxy. Using administrative health inspection data, we show that the difference between individuals’ apparent age and chronological age is a strong predictor of subsequent health outcomes.. We then construct a panel of mayors and senior officials and apply computer-vision models to estimate apparent age from official photographs. Officials in jurisdictions with weaker economic performance age significantly faster. Linking outcomes to promotion incentives, we show that the effect concentrates where promotion tournaments are steeper and downside risk is greater, consistent with both rewards and punishments shaping stress exposure. To corroborate the mechanism, we use large language models to analyze officials’ speeches and government reports, extracting measures of urgency, constraint, and risk emphasis that rise when officials age faster and predict within-official changes in apparent age. Together, AI-based facial and textual measures reveal substantial human-capital costs of promotion pressure in public office.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6311158`

- title: Generative AI as an Institutional Tool under the First Amendment
- authors: Tao Huang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6311158
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Is Generative AI (GenAI) protected under the First Amendment? As illustrated by Garcia v. Character Technologies , this issue is critical because it determines the regulatory scope of the new technology. The court in that case, as well as scholarly discussions so far, generally relied on three existing approaches – the speaker’s right to speak, the listener’s right to receive information, and a cultural inquiry that examines “inherent expressiveness” in machine output. These approaches, however, fit uneasily with GenAI. The speaker’s perspective struggles to find a qualified speaker, as human developers lack the intention and control over GenAI outputs. The listener’s right has no limiting principle and understates the user’s active and diverse engagement with the generative machine. The cultural approach flounders because social conventions are too vague, reactive, and backward-looking to account for emerging technologies. This Article proposes a new approach for GenAI by conceptualizing it as an institutional tool under the First Amendment. Externally, GenAI is a speech tool that assists users in expressive and communicative purposes. Internally, GenAI can be viewed as a First Amendment institution because it has a system of self-governance structures, norms, and procedures that is directly related to its speech functions and harms. This framing combines two underexplored doctrines: the right to use speech tools and the institutional approach under the First Amendment. In most circumstances, GenAI does not speak for itself or on behalf of its developers. Rather, GenAI’s constitutional protection is ancillary, derived solely from the user’s right to use speech tools for expressive purposes. As a First Amendment institution, GenAI enjoys institutional autonomy in self-governance, but that autonomy should be conditional on its fulfillment of institutional responsibility – that is, adherence to a self-governance system that facilitates the institution’s speech functions and mitigates its speech harms. Under this new framing, the Article proposes a three-step test for the court to review regulations on GenAI. Applying the test to some real cases and regulations, including the Garcia case and the disclosure laws, illustrates its strength. Descriptively, the new framing better fits the real-world uses of GenAI than existing approaches. Analytically, it supplies a principled and contextual middle ground for judicial scrutiny. By justifying GenAI’s First Amendment protection upon an ancillary right conditional upon its institutional performance, the new framing offers a doctrine that rigorously protects GenAI’s speech-facilitating roles for users while also preserving necessary regulatory space for the government to address its risks.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6376220`

- title: Socratic AI Training for Epistemic Integrity
- authors: Michael Kitamura
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-19
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6376220
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

All existing LLMs are structurally sycophantic. It is an inherent property of training on general human feedback-responses that please evaluators are reinforced. In lowstakes civilian applications this is a minor flaw. In high-stakes domains-military command decisions, medical diagnosis, financial risk assessment, critical infrastructure-it is a vulnerability. This proposal outlines a novel training methodology: a purpose-built Socratic AI trainer, trained on millions of hours of human adversarial epistemic exchange, then used to train, or retrain, target LLMs toward a fundamentally different definition of 'pleasing the user'-where accuracy under pressure, not agreement, is the reward signal. It should technically be feasible with current tools, and scalable to machine speed. This may address the problem of post-hoc training of mature LLMs, though probably best for nascent LLMs.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6381458`

- title: Capability versus Cost: Pricing Generative AI under Knightian Uncertainty
- authors: Cong Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-23
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6381458
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI improves through two channels-capability and cost-with distinct implications for stock returns. I build a task-based production model in which AI output depends on the surplus of capability over task complexity, ensuring that the two channels enter separately: capability advances operate through both an extensive margin (more tasks automated) and an intensive margin (higher productivity on existing tasks), whereas cost reductions operate only through the extensive margin. An endogenous data feedback loop amplifies this asymmetry, making data stocks function as AI growth options that determine a firm's exposure to aggregate capability shocks. The model generates a two-factor asset pricing structure with the central prediction that the cross-sectional return gradient is steeper for capability than for cost shocks. Event studies around nineteen AI milestones spanning 2020-2024 provide suggestive support: the coefficient on AI exposure is roughly twice as large for capability events as for cost events, and the AI equity premium is approximately 46 basis points with capability risk accounting for 76%. The framework extends naturally to Knightian uncertainty about AI output quality via Hansen-Sargent multiplier preferences, generating an ambiguity premium that is theoretically distinct but quantitatively small.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6336502`

- title: The Surprising Virtues of Heterogeneity: Legal Pluralism and the Governance of Generative AI
- authors: Daryl Lim
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6336502
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This Article argues that the United States' fragmented approach to generative AI regulation is a strategic strength rather than a flaw. In place of a single federal regime, privacy law, the right of publicity, and copyright offer overlapping tools to address identity-linked harms, each grounded in distinct theories of harm and institutional traditions. This pluralism promotes experimentation, learning, and well-reasoned development rather than confusion. Heterogeneous governance helps courts and lawmakers adapt incrementally to fast-moving technologies, avoid premature lock-in, and coordinate protections across legal silos. To build on these advantages, the Article proposes a narrowly tailored data right focused on high-fidelity, identity-linked uses of data. Functioning as an opt-in, transparency-driven supplement to existing doctrines, this right would close gaps while preserving innovation and doctrinal diversity. The result is a principled, pragmatic approach that safeguards individual agency and leverages the adaptive strengths of US federalism.
