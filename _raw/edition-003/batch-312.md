## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6900479`

- title: A Tool for Rapid Assessment of the Quantitative Attributes Required for Emerging Occupations
- authors: Wilson Martinez Diaz, Christophe Combemale, Erica Renee Fuchs
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6900479
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

Technological change, trade, and industrial policy can reshape the nature and organization of work. U.S. federal and state agencies rely on the Department of Labor's and Employment and Training Administration's O*NET taxonomy to characterize occupations in the Office of Management and Budget's list and manage interventions. Unfortunately, the nature of work is changing faster than the rate with which the OMB's list of occupations is revised. We evaluate the potential of Natural Language Processing (NLP) to close this gap by translating qualitative descriptions of occupational requirements (such as job postings) into quantitative representations within O*NET, which can then be compared systematically to other occupations. We develop an algorithm built on Sentence-BERT and historical O*NET data. We evaluate our model for coherence and congruence and find that while our model's occupation requirement vectors have fewer total attributes, they capture most of the important ones and assign sufficient level. We also generate quantitative O*NET occupation vectors for workers in the Permanent Labor Certification (PERM) forms and find that most (~71%) of our model-generated attributes are within one standard deviation of the expert-based ones. Our results suggest that it may be possible to create meaningful quantitative occupation attribute vectors in the O*NET framework from publicly available text. Thus, our model may offer a fast "initial" assessment of occupational attributes when occupation-specific survey results do not yet exist. The model produces similar results to a large language model, with less user knowledge, and less computational time and costs. Significance Statement Technological change and industrial policy are altering job requirements faster than official occupation taxonomies can be updated, limiting the timely information on workforce capabilities and gaps needed to inform government policymaker, firm, and worker decisions. We show that natural language processing of job descriptions combined with historical O*NET data can provide fast, meaningful, quantitative measures of the attributes required for new occupations not yet in federal databases. Our model produces similar results to a large language model, with less user knowledge, computational time, and costs.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6853581`

- title: FSAIM: Spec-driven AI as a Governance Layer for Empirical Research A Position Note
- authors: Grzegorz Wojarnik
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6853581
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Generative AI now drafts fluent prose, writes analysis code, and assembles entire manuscripts, yet fluent text is not a credible result. The distance between the two does not shrink as models improve; it grows. This note proposes treating spec-driven development (SDD)-specifications as the source of truth, deterministic builds, and quality gates-not as a writing accelerator but as a governance layer over generative AI in empirical research. We describe an engine for the empirical paper lifecycle in economics and finance in which the manuscript is built from frozen artifacts (design, data, results) rather than written alongside results: numbers enter the text only from analysis outputs, exploration is walled off from confirmation by an explicit design freeze (no-look-ahead), claims are bounded to their evidential status, and every AI action is logged as part of the method (an AI bill of materials). A three-layer survival architecture-epistemic invariants, role-and-gate contracts, and an interchangeable substrate-keeps the method independent of any model or vendor. We name this methodology FSAIM, state precisely what is and is not novel about it, position it against the closest 2025-2026 work, and report its first empirical instance together with that instance's honest shortfalls.

### abstract

The Cognitive Tri-State Dynamics Architecture (CTDA) is a cognitive control layer designed to operate above foundation models, knowledge graphs, tool invocations, and experimental environments . Rather than forcing models to directly output breakthroughs, CTDA orchestrates an executable engineering closed loop encompassing anomaly preservation, premise tracing, shadow-layer deconstruction, candidate recombination, testability compilation, probabilistic verification, and audit rollbacks . Emphasizing the capacity to "preserve high-value uncertainty" , the system mandates that candidate hypotheses pass rigorous testability compilation, hard constraint checks, and soft evidence evaluations before reaching a confirmation state . Serving as a pre-MVP specification and Proof-of-Concept (PoC) blueprint , this whitepaper outlines the state models and core operators—including Minimum Description Length (MDL) coding criteria and typed semantic tracing —necessary to reliably generate verifiable candidate packages and auditable research workflows in AI for Science .

### abstract

This paper outlines a learning system to govern generative AI in ways that advance human flourishing. Multimodal reflexive governance offers a framework to evolve multiple modes of governance across multiple institutional, spatial, and time dimensions. It preserves the ability of developers and users to explore the tremendous potential of AI while mitigating undesirable outcomes. The analysis starts by examining the role and limitations of current proposals to respond to the challenges of AI governance by increasing the agility and adaptivity of governance. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6956699`

- title: Remote Sensing Products in Climate Change Studies: Recent Developments, Scenarios and Future Possibilities
- authors: Pranjal Dubey, Ghanshyam  T. Patle
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-17
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6956699
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

Remote sensing (RS) has emerged as a vital foundation of climate science, providing unparalleled synoptic, repeat-cycle and multi-decadal measurements of Earth’s interconnected environmental systems. This review synthesises the state of the art in RS applications to climate change research, based on a curated corpus of peer-reviewed publications from 2021 to 2025. We review the main RS platforms including passive optical sensors (Landsat, MODIS, Sentinel-2/3), active microwave systems (SAR, InSAR, Sentinel-1), laser altimeters (ICESat-2, ATLAS), gravimetric satellites (GRACE-FO) and hyperspectral imagers covering three thematic domains: (1) terrestrial biosphere (land use/land cover change, deforestation, above-ground biomass and carbon stocks), (2) atmosphere (greenhouse gas concentrations, aerosols and cloud feedbacks) and (3) agricultural yield, food production and climate-driven crop stress. We outline the rapidly evolving role of machine learning and deep learning in automating data processing, enhancing spatial resolution, and producing predictive risk assessments from RS data streams. The review also considers the institutional architecture supporting long-term, consistent climate data records, including the Global Climate Observing System (GCOS) Essential Climate Variables (ECVs) framework, the European Space Agency’s Climate Change Initiative (ESA-CCI), and the NASA constellation of Earth-observing missions. Finally, we describe the path to the future of next-generation RS capabilities, including constellation architectures, digital twin Earth initiatives, and foundation models, and highlight the scientific, technical, and governance challenges that must be overcome to realise their full potential for climate monitoring and policy support.

- title: EU AI Act Article 50 Compliance for Generative AI Systems: Six Open Implementation Questions from a Developer's Formal Submission to the European Commission (June 2026)
- authors: Vinita Silaparasetty
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6933339
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The EU AI Act's Article 50 transparency obligations apply from 2 August 2026. Most published guidance addresses single-model chatbots. It does not address the dominant commercial architecture for generative AI: API-based deployment where a provider generates outputs that downstream developers consume, transform, and surface to end users through interfaces the original provider does not control. This paper identifies six engineering questions that current Article 50 guidance leaves unanswered for generative AI API providers and proposes six concrete architectural approaches implementable before the August deadline. Questions cover the downstream marking preservation problem, the contradiction between paragraphs permitting single techniques and requiring combined solutions, Article 50(4) liability allocation across the API stack, and the threshold at which embedded sentiment analysis triggers Article 50(3) emotion recognition obligations. Submitted as a formal practitioner contribution to the European Commission's Article 50 consultation (closed 3 June 2026) from the perspective of a generative AI systems developer and AI governance engineer.

- title: Beyond the Training Peak: An Analysis of the Sustained Environmental Costs of Inference and Data Life cycle in Deployed AI Systems
- authors: Shreya Gupta, Khushboo Bhatia, Nagesh Gulkotwar, Rahul  Rishi Sharma, Amanpreet Kaur
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-18
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6960803
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

The discourse on Green AI has been overwhelmingly dominated by the energy ”peak” of pre-training large-scale models, such as large language and foundation models. This analysis proposes a critical shift in perspective toward the total life cycle environmental cost, which is often dominated by two other phases: continuous inference and data life cycle management. This paper provides a specialized and quantitative survey of the cumulative energy and carbon footprint associated with these sustained operations. For inference, we analyze the cumulative cost of running a deployed model potentially billions of times in production, exploring efficiency techniques specific to deployment, such as batching, model caching, and edge-offloading strategies. For the data life cycle, we investigate the ”hidden” environmental costs of data collection, storage, cleaning, and continuous ETL (Extract, Transform, Load) pipelines that feed production models. By quantifying these often historically neglected factors and reviewing mitiga- tion strategies for both inference and data operations, this work provides a more holistic and pragmatic framework for assessing and improving the sustainability of real-world, operational AI systems. This framework is essential for accurately assessing, optimizing and improving the overall sustainability and environmental responsibility.
