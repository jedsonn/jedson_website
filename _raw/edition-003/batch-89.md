## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6805805`

- title: AI Herding in Financial Market: An Experiment with Large Language Models
- authors: Leping Zhang, Guangyou Zhou
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6805805
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

In a sequential trading market experiment, LLM traders exhibit a higher degree of rationality than human participants, engaging less frequently in irrational cascade behavior while demonstrating the ability to undertake rational herding. Increasing the reasoning level of LLMs further improves their performance. A decomposition of irrational behavior shows that it arises from errors in belief formation and inconsistencies between actions and beliefs. Across treatments, LLM performance is not fully robust: changes in role and context lead to behavioral shifts of up to 4% and 7.2%, respectively. These findings provide new insights into the application of LLMs in financial decision-making.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6806500`

- title: Multimodal Large Language Model Agents Across Transportation Systems Lifecycle: Early-Stage Progress Review and Policy Implications
- authors: Jiangbo Yu, Ali Eslami, Jiyao Wang, Yunbiao Wang, Chengbo Zhang, Simon Paquette-Greenbaum
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6806500
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent, large language model, large language models

### abstract

Society is witnessing rapid advances and deployments of intelligent systems exhibiting varying degrees of agency, enabled in part by multimodal large language models. These AI agents demonstrate continuously improving capabilities such as contextual intention formation and interpretation, goal-directed planning and action, purposeful social engagement, external tool use, and adaptation through memory and learning. These agentic capabilities present significant opportunities as well as substantial risks. This paper synthesizes the concepts of agentic AI and AgTS and provides a systematic review of relevant transportation literature. Existing developments show that early-stage AI agents and agentic workflows have been developed across stakeholders and lifecycle phases, including (i) real-time traffic prediction and control, (ii) vehicle operation and fleet management, (iii) asset monitoring and maintenance, and (iv) systems data analysis, demand forecasting, and strategic planning. The review further indicates that current implementations understandably only exhibit low and a subset of agentic capabilities. A more critical concern is the predominantly siloed development, with nearly no attention to their strategic integration in environments where these agents will inevitably interact—directly or indirectly—with humans and with one another. If unaddressed proactively, this fragmentation may become too late to adjust, risking the reproduction and amplification of chronical challenges that arose in part from poor coordination and conflicts among historical human stakeholders that also had their agency. The paper concludes with policy and practice recommendations for the responsible and sustainable development for the continuous development of AgTS in different socioeconomic, regulatory, and environmental contexts.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6800890`

- title: The Cognitive Lean Loop: Accelerating Entrepreneurial Learning and Development with Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence (HAI)
- authors: Carlton L. Robinson, Taylor  Frances Drury
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6800890
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The cognitive industrial revolution catalyzes a necessary evolution in entrepreneurial innovation, as traditional Lean Startup frameworks reach the boundaries of human cognitive capacity. In this new paradigm, Large Language Models (LLMs) function as the steam engine of innovation, providing the mechanical power to avoid pervasive constraints such as pivot-hells, failing to pivot, or analysis paralysis. We introduce the Cognitive Lean Loop, a novel framework that integrates coevolutionary intelligence into standard value and growth hypotheses via a Human-to-AI-to-Human (H2A2H) feedback loop. Central to this system is a human-centered AI pipeline utilizing an Interviewer LLM, trained on over 1,000 historical coaching interactions to conduct semi-structured intake, and a Translator LLM that synthesizes transcriptions into standardized deliverables (e.g., Viability-Rated Business Model Canvases) and actionable business artifacts (e.g., infographics). This process facilitates the 8-4-2 Funnel, where entrepreneurs and coaches systematically filter eight identified Jobs-to-be-Done down to two top-priority, time-bound goals targeted for follow-up. By delegating diagnostic synthesis to domain-specific AI, scarce human capital is reserved for higher-order reasoning. A case study of 11 entrepreneurs from Jax Bridges demonstrates a 95% acceleration in learning velocity compared to the 75-hour human-only intake process. Participants reported perceiving great value for their time and leaving the focus group feeling very encouraged. Facilitator reflections parallel these findings: the Cognitive Lean Loop closes perceptual gaps between coaches and entrepreneurs, ensures participants feel deeply seen (not mechanically processed), and fosters resilience and self-efficacy among entrepreneurs, all while maintaining quality coaching in a community setting.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6731099`

- title: The Expansion-Compression Trap: How AI Broke the Link Between Writing and Thinking in Modern Organizations
- authors: Deepak Babu Piskala
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6731099
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Large language models have severed a quiet but load-bearing link in how modern organizations communicate: the relationship between the length of a document and the depth of thinking behind it. Documents now move through workflows via repeated cycles of LLM-driven expansion followed by LLM-driven compression, each translation lossy, each pass favoring fluency over fidelity. We name this dynamic the expansion-compression trap, locate it within a collapsing economy of writing whose only remaining scarce input is human judgment, connect it to a parallel collapse in reader attention shaped by a decade of short-form media consumption, and document the resulting signature problem in which the strongest human writing is now mistaken for machine output. We propose a three-principle framework for AI-native authorship (separating the canonical spine of a document from its rendered surface, using AI as critic before author, and attaching an explicit reader contract), and argue that the organizations that adapt to these new economics will out-decide those that do not.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6805344`

- title: AI Exposure and Housing Markets
- authors: Cayman Seagraves, Stace Sirmans
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6805344
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai, llm

### abstract

Generative AI arrived just as the pandemic had pushed housing demand away from expensive, supply constrained metros. We ask whether the period after ChatGPT extended that dispersion or reversed it. Using a quarterly panel of 211 U.S. metros from 2017Q1 to 2025Q4, we relate Zillow and FHFA house price growth to AI Geography Exposure weighted by 2019 population, with the 2020 remote work break timed separately. AI exposed metros grow faster after 2023: a one standard deviation increase in AIGE is associated with 3.6% higher cumulative ZHVI growth, about $9,400 at the sample median 2023 home value. The premium also appears in FHFA prices and rents, concentrates in supply inelastic metros, and is reproduced by a predetermined LLM task suitability index. A pandemic WFH placebo has the opposite sign. IRS migration data show reduced outmigration from exposed metros, and Revelio LinkedIn data show rising AI hiring there. The early generative AI period appears to have reinforced the residential value of dense, supply constrained high skill labor markets, reversing the spatial direction of the pandemic.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6732658`

- title: A Semantic Reinterpretation of the Language Generation Mechanism of Large Language Models - Focusing on the Dual Structure of Mathematical Language States and the Translation into Human Language -
- authors: Deokju Jegal
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-21
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6732658
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This study reinterprets the language generation process of large language models (LLMs) not as a direct production of human language, but as a process of translating a mathematically stabilized internal language state into human language. Whereas prior discussions have either explained artificial intelligence ’ s linguistic capability as a simulation of human language understanding or, conversely, reduced it to pure probabilistic computation, this study focuses on the dual linguistic strata inherent in the language generation process. Specifically, the vector operations and probability distribution formation performed within an LLM constitute an “ AI-specific linguistic state ” that is prior to human language, and only at the final token output stage does a form interpretable as human language emerge. To demonstrate this, the study analyzes the generation process step by step — from tokens and embedding space through the inference stages (attention and feed-forward operations) to the output stage — while juxtaposing engineering explanations with semantic interpretations at each stage. By arguing that meaning is not a fixed unit residing within language itself but a phenomenon formed in the transitional process between pre-linguistic computational states and linguistic interpretation, this study proposes the theoretical possibility of extending the scope of traditional semantics beyond human language to encompass language generation structures in general.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6774982`

- title: Biases and Debiasing Approaches in Visual Question Answering: A Comprehensive Survey
- authors: Yuhao Tian, Quanxing Xu, Songyu Li, Ling Zhou, Xian Zhong, Feifei Zhang, Rubing Huang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6774982
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Visual Question Answering (VQA) is a dynamic multimodal research field focused on generating accurate textual answers from visual and question information. Despite recent architectural advancements, balanced reasoning remains a challenge. But models still do not combine this linguistic prior with visual heuristics in a useful way. VQA systems, including recent Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), often rely on spurious shortcut correlations rather than genuine cross-modal evidence. This biased learning leads to fragile performance on out-of-distribution data and undermines system trustworthiness in real-world applications. This survey provides a comprehensive review of bias and debiasing methodologies in VQA. It proposes a unifying debiasing taxonomy that includes Data-centric, Model-centric, Learning-centric, and LLM-era approaches. In addition to traditional language bias, we consider visual, distributional, and sociocultural biases. This study summarizes existing evaluation metrics and diagnostic benchmarks for bias-aware assessment. Finally, we discuss existing limitations and outline promising future research directions. We expect this survey to provide valuable insights to help develop reliable and fair VQA systems in the future.
