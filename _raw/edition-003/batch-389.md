## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6810542`

- title: The Future Orientation of Languages and Stock Price Timeliness: International Evidence
- authors: Hanyu Zhang, Qiyu Zhang, Rong Ding, Hang Zhou
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6810542
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Strong future time reference (FTR) languages grammatically mark the future, prompting speakers to mentally separate future events from the present. Using 183,797 firm-year observations collected from 47 countries between 1992 and 2022, we examine the relationship between language FTR and intra-year price discovery. We find that the use of strong-FTR languages is associated with slower incorporation of earnings-relevant information into share prices. The effect is more pronounced for firms with lower analyst coverage and weaker corporate governance. Our findings, which remain robust across extensive sensitivity analyses, have important implications for investors and policymakers.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6811738`

- title: Redrawing the AI Map: A Theory of Accountability Boundaries in Agentic Ecosystems
- authors: Muhammad Zia Hydari, Farooq Muzaffar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6811738
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

Agentic AI orchestrators reduce the interface and assembly costs of composing information systems capabilities across organizational boundaries, apparently accelerating the modular forms that digital innovation theory leads us to expect. Yet AI-enabled capabilities whose outputs require evidence, review, signoff, or assignable responsibility may retain integrated accountability boundaries even when their technical interfaces become modular. We introduce accountability assets: complementary assets whose strategic value derives from making AI-supported outputs legitimate, auditable, reviewable, and assignable to a responsible party. When verification is costly and responsibility is difficult to transfer, accountability assets limit the boundary-shifting effects of agentic orchestration, even when technical decomposability is high. We further introduce rule debt: the latent governance burden that accrues when organizational decision rules migrate from formal information systems into ungoverned agentic execution environments, producing unmanaged policy artifacts that must eventually be inventoried, versioned, tested, and reconciled. The theory integrates digital innovation, transaction cost, complementary-assets, digital platform governance, and IS control perspectives to develop seven propositions explaining how agentic reductions in interface and assembly costs, verification cost, responsibility transferability, and accountability-asset cospecialization shape three capability-level boundary strategies: component, integrated, and dualtrack. The propositions further explain how appropriability conditions accountability-asset value capture, how orchestrator intent capture enables value appropriation, and how boundary misconfiguration produces rule debt. Structured theoretical illustrations across document processing, legal services, audit, clinical decision support, and procurement discipline the boundary logic. The theory advances IS scholarship by specifying when digital modularization extends to organizational disaggregation and when it does not, by extending IS control theory to agentic settings in which decision rules execute outside governed information systems, and by identifying accountability-asset cospecialization as a structural determinant of digital platform governance asymmetries.

- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN

- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

This research paper explores the potential emergence of new organizational forms enabled by emerging information technologies including artificial intelligence (AI). Classically, firms are seen to exist to optimize transaction cost minimization along with employee monitoring and coordination. We argue that new technologies are reducing the potential benefits of all these outcomes, especially in knowledge-based organizations. Given these changes, we introduce here a model of an agentic networked knowledge enterprise (ANKE). This is a conditional ideal type defined by five interdependent features, namely primary use of cloud-based infrastructure, AI-driven employee coordination and monitoring, protocol-mediated participation in decision making, collective rule-based governance, and distributed residual claims. We identify six potential failure modes and discuss mitigation mechanisms grounded in existing theoretical frameworks. We also develop an empirical research agenda to test the boundary conditions of ANKE viability.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6865759`

- title: Spec-SSM: A Spectral-Enhanced State Space Model for Long-term Time Series Forecasting
- authors: Xinghao Wang, ChunYu Qiu, Shan Ma
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6865759
- keyword hits: transformer model

### abstract

Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) plays an essential role in various industrial fields, including energy management, weather forecasting, and traffic planning. As mainstream Transformer models have made significant progress in accurately capturing correlations between variables, they remain constrained by the computational bottleneck of quadratic complexity, performing poorly in long sequence prediction. The recently proposed Mamba model successfully reduces computational complexity to linear through a selective mechanism, yet overlooks the contribution of frequency-domain information to prediction accuracy. In this paper, we propose the Spectral-enhanced State Space Model (Spec-SSM), a novel and highly efficient dual-branch architecture that integrates state space models with spectral analysis. This architecture innovatively combines the Mamba network with a learnable frequency-domain filter in parallel, where Mamba efficiently captures temporal dependencies while the spectral stream performs global filtering in the frequency domain. The subsequent adaptive gating enables the model to handle data with complex periodicity and noise. We validated our approach across eight publicly available benchmark datasets. Experimental results demonstrate that Spec-SSM achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on datasets with strong periodicity and seasonality. Furthermore, it exhibits high memory efficiency while maintaining high precision.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6867324`

- title: An Agentic AI Framework for E-Scooter Mobility Hub Planning Across German Cities
- authors: Meng Jin, Melanie Handrich, Simone Martinenz, Nicholas Hoeser, Ziyue Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6867324
- keyword hits: agentic, llm

### abstract

Existing e-scooter hub planning relies on correlational demand models built on proprietary data, without distinguishing causal drivers across urban typologies. This paper presents a three-phase agentic AI framework that constructs a Causal Template Library from public GBFS data across 29 German cities, encoding which environmental features causally drive hotspot demand for each city-type (large, university, industrial, hilly) and cluster-type (core, peripheral) combination. An LLM-orchestrated causal discovery pipeline adapts algorithm selection to local data conditions across 57 city-cluster units. The library reveals systematic variation: core demand is driven by activity access and transit proximity, while peripheral demand responds to built form. A planning tool built on the library scores candidate sites, calibrates infrastructure recommendations to local demographics, and generates practitioner-ready reports. In Heilbronn, two hub sites informed by the framework's causal evidence are currently under construction, illustrating real-world applicability.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6866877`

- title: Satisficing in the age of GenAI? The Moderating Role of Technical Background and Domain Expertise on Human-Algorithm Interaction based decision-making in Public Policy
- authors: Anshumaan Goel, Sudip Patra
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6866877
- keyword hits: generative ai, prompting

### abstract

The proliferation of Generative AI (GenAI) in Public policy marks a shift toward AI-partnered decision-making, introducing critical cognitive risks, such as Human-Algorithm Interaction (HAI) bias. Grounded in Dual Process Theory, Bounded Rationality, and Cognitive Load Theory, this study utilizes a Quasi-Experimental Non-Equivalent Groups Design to analyze how technical and domain expertise moderate HAI bias among public policy professionals. Findings reveal that GenAI serves primarily as a heuristic for efficiency. While non-expertise exacerbates bias, domain and technical expertise mitigate it. Notably, technical experts face increased cognitive load from "over-prompting," a constraint reduced by domain expertise, which fosters higher human intervention. The data indicates simultaneous System 1 and System 2 activation among technocratic experts. Consequently, the paper advocates for "Cognitive-Aware" HAI literacy and adversarial interaction design. Finally, it proposes Quantum-Like Modeling (QLM) as a robust framework for capturing the contextual nuances of HAI bias in complex policy environments.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6810085`

- title: Beyond the Algorithm: Scaffolding Strategies and AI-Transformed Student Support in Online Higher Education
- authors: Hany Zaky
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6810085
- keyword hits: generative ai, prompting

### abstract

Purpose: The rapid integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into online higher education has fundamentally transformed instructional scaffolding, prompting educators and researchers to reconceptualize how temporary, adaptive support structures are designed, delivered, and withdrawn. This integrative literature review synthesizes 38 peer-reviewed empirical studies and theoretical analyses published between January 2021 and February 2026 to examine the intersection of scaffolding theory and AI-mediated online learning environments. Guided by Vygotsky's (1978) Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) as its organizing framework, the review traces the evolution of scaffolding from human-led, socially embedded interactions to data-driven, algorithmically personalized support systems. Its original contribution lies in identifying five theoretical tensions that current AI scaffolding systems must resolve, most critically, the persistent failure of AI tools to implement calibrated fading, the mechanism that theoretically distinguishes scaffolding from dependency-inducing support. Method: A PRISMA-adapted systematic search across seven databases identified 4,088 records. After screening, 38 studies were retained for thematic synthesis and quality assessment using the Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT). Results: Five key thematic areas emerged: (a) AI serving as a digital scaffold and More Knowledgeable Other; (b) Intelligent Tutoring Systems and adaptive platforms; (c) generative AI as tools for interactive scaffolding; (d) supports for metacognitive and self-regulated learning; and (e) challenges such as overreliance, academic honesty issues, and equity concerns. Evidence consistently indicates that proactive, dialogue-based AI scaffolding outperforms passive strategies, and the lack of properly calibrated fading remains the most significant flaw in the field. Conclusions: Effective AI scaffolding should be rooted in pedagogical theory, human-centered values, and fading mechanisms that promote true learner independence. Future research ought to focus on longitudinal studies, analyzing different demographic subgroups, and empirically testing calibrated fading strategies.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6873994`

- title: Toward a foundational thermal model for residential buildings
- authors: Ting-Yu Dai, Kingsley Nweye, Dev Niyogi, Zoltan Nagy
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6873994
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, foundation model

### abstract

The building energy community lacks a foundational thermal model, i.e., a single pretrained model capable of generalizing across diverse buildings, climates, and control strategies without building-specific calibration. Achieving this vision requires architectural principles that capture universal thermal dynamics rather than memorizing building-specific patterns. We take a step toward this goal by presenting a physics-informed transformer architecture that embeds domain knowledge, e.g., derivative enrichment and Euler-based numerical integration, into a decoder-only framework. We incorporate static building features extracted from simulation models and employ Rotary Position Embedding attention to capture temporal dependencies. Evaluated on the CityLearn dataset spanning 247 residential buildings across three climate zones, our model achieves one-step prediction accuracy (RMSE of 0.30$^\circ$C in Texas, 0.29$^\circ$C in Vermont) while outperforming both traditional baselines and fine-tuned Time-Series Foundation Models. We also demonstrate zero-shot transferability: models trained on as few as two buildings generalize to unseen buildings and climate zones without fine-tuning. Despite the limitation of simulated residential buildings, our results establish physics-informed architectural principles as a promising foundation for universal building thermal models.
