- title: Bug-Miner: A Gen-AI Powered Software Optimizer
- authors: J K Periasamy, Shanthakumar S, Gunoutham M, Karthick B
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6725358
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

Modern software development requires more than functional correctness; it must be high-performant, maintainable, and efficient. While LLMs excel in code generation and understanding, their direct application in automated code optimization often lacks the contextual awareness and nuanced judgment of a human developer. Fully automated tools may introduce subtle bugs or stylistic changes that are not aligned with standards of the projects they target. In this work, we propose Bug-Miner, an AI-powered framework for facilitating interactive, suggestion-based code optimization. At the core of our approach is placing the developer at the center of this workflow by using an LLM to create optimization suggestions a user can review, modify, and approve. Such architecture is orchestrated by a Model Communication Protocol (MCP), which manages the information flow between a user interface, the source code of the project, a library of prompts, and the core LLM. The resulting tool will be collaborative, marrying the powerful analytics of AI with the strategic oversight of a developer for more reliable and effective code improvements.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6739621`

- title: DPI ODA as a Conditional Vanguard: Evidence on Trade and FDI from Korea
- authors: Soomin PARK, Sojeong LEE, Seungwoo YOO
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6739621
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

This study analyzes the conditional relationship between Korea’s Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) Official Development Assistance (ODA) and Korea’s bilateral commercial linkages—exports, imports, and outward FDI—using country-year panel data covering 146 recipient countries from 1987 to 2024. We identify 5,523 DPI-related projects from 83,083 OECD CRS projects through LLM-based text classification and construct a novel country-year DPI ODA panel variable. Using PPML estimation with country and year fixed effects, we document three main patterns. First, DPI ODA is positively associated with Korea’s exports and outward FDI with a time lag, while no consistent relationship appears in imports. This asymmetric pattern contrasts with lead-value tests in which all three channels are significant, suggesting a directional commercial spillover not easily explained by a purely symmetric strategic allocation story. Second, the relationship is conditionally stronger in recipient countries with higher digital readiness, measured by internet users, and stronger logistics performance, measured by the Logistics Performance Index, whereas broad governance indicators do not operate as significant conditional channels. Third, we find evidence of a post-2015 structural break in the FDI channel, consistent with a substantive change in Korea’s DPI ODA–commercial linkage structure following the strengthening of Korea’s DPI ODA policy. These findings suggest that DPI ODA may reduce transaction execution costs for donor-country firms, generating asymmetric and conditionally amplified commercial spillovers through recipient-country execution infrastructure.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6739939`

- title: The Transformative Impact of Student Innovation Competitions and Programs on Innovation Mindset
- authors: Abdullah Konak
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6739939
- keyword hits: chatgpt

### abstract

Innovation competitions and programs (ICPs) have emerged as pivotal elements in STEM education, providing students with experiential learning experiences that cultivate technical skills, networking opportunities, and an innovation mindset. However, the literature does not explore ICPs' transformative impact on students' entrepreneurial mindsets, which involves profound changes in students' values and abilities. The study posits that participation in these programs significantly enhances students' self-awareness, open-mindedness, and innovation capabilities, which are critical components of an innovation mindset. Semi-structured interviews were conducted with 36 students who participated in various ICPs, using a qualitative inductive approach. Thematic analysis was performed using both human coders and ChatGPT 4.0 to identify the strengths and weaknesses realized by students and how their assumptions and beliefs changed during ICPs. The results showed some agreement between humangenerated and AI-generated codes, highlighting the role of ICPs in enhancing self-awareness and open-mindedness. Key strengths discovered included communication, leadership, problem-solving, organizational, tenacity, and interpersonal skills. At the same time, common weaknesses included time management issues, communication challenges, a lack of self-advocacy, trust issues, inflexibility, difficulties with conflict resolution, and stress and anxiety. The results also showed that the students became more receptive to careers in innovation and entrepreneurship, leading to a shift in their perceptions of their innovation capabilities.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6740239`

- title: Will Fair Use Save AI from Common Sense?
- authors: Benjamin Hardman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6740239
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

This essay argues that the artificial intelligence fair use debate has become too technical and too detached from economic common sense. Modern AI systems depend on massive volumes of human-created expressive works. Those works are not incidental. They help supply the fluency, style, cultural competence, and expressive range that make generative AI valuable. Yet AI developers seek to use those works without payment while paying for every other major input, including engineers, chips, cloud computing, energy, and capital. The central question is not whether AI is useful. It is whether fair use should allow a commercial technology to treat creative works as a free industrial input. Even if traditional market-harm doctrine struggles to capture diffuse harm to creative labor markets, the underlying asymmetry remains: AI systems extract value from expressive works and then compete with the markets that produced them. The innovation defense is serious. Uncompensated use may sometimes accelerate general-purpose technologies and produce broad public gains. But if fair use is being used to shift the cost of AI’s expressive inputs onto creators, that trade-off should be named clearly. Fair use may answer the legal question, but it should not obscure the common-sense one: who should bear the cost of the expressive works that make generative AI valuable?

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6736917`

- title: Blind Model Checking of Workflows in Agentic AI with Zero-knowledge Proof
- authors: Subhasis Thakur
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6736917
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent, large language model

### abstract

An AI agent's workflow in response to a task given by the end user in natural language may lead to undesirable outcomes due to probabilistic error from its large language model. Hence, it is necesary to ensure the workflow to be executed by an agent is safe. In many cases, the end user may not have access to the internal model of the agent and hence its intended workflow. In such cases, it is necessary to perform verification of the safety of an agent's workflow without the knowledge of the workflow. In this paper, we address this problem using a zero-knowledge proof for model checking the agent's workflow where the verifer(the end user) does not have access to the workflow. We have developed such verification protocols to check whether the workflow leads to undesirable states or cycles. We prove that these verificaiton protocols are correct and sound. We also present an experimental evaluation to estimate the time it takes to perform such verification.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6737540`

- title: LHEMO: LLM-Based Heuristic Evolution for Multi-Objective Sensor-Weapon-Target Assignment
- authors: Yihao Zhong, Changsheng Yin, Ruopeng Yang, Yuantao Yang, Yiwei Lu, Yongqi Wen, Yongqi Shi, Bo Huang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6737540
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

The multi-objective sensor--weapon--target assignment (SWTA) problem requires the coordinated allocation of heterogeneous sensing and action resources under conflicting objectives such as mission effectiveness, resource consumption, and response time. Existing SWTA solvers, including classical multi-objective evolutionary algorithms and recent large language model (LLM)-based automated heuristic design (AHD) methods, either rely heavily on hand-crafted search components or transfer poorly to SWTA because of heterogeneous entities, strict feasibility requirements, and the need for Pareto-oriented feedback. To address these challenges, we propose LHEMO, an LLM-based heuristic evolution framework for multi-objective SWTA. LHEMO adopts a bi-level architecture in which the outer level evolves executable heuristic programs through LLM-guided operators, while the inner level evaluates each candidate within a unified multi-objective solver. Within this architecture, two reconstruction operators expand exploration through line-level and module-level code regeneration, a validation-and-repair mechanism recovers invalid candidates, a cross-generation reflection mechanism reuses evolutionary experience, and a structured seed heuristic stabilizes early-stage search. A Pareto-oriented fitness based on hypervolume and a feasibility penalty further guides the evolution toward high-quality trade-off solutions. Experiments on a benchmark ranging from 8 to 320 targets show that LHEMO consistently outperforms both classical multi-objective baselines and representative LLM-based AHD baselines, achieving up to 191% hypervolume improvement on the largest-scale instances. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of LLM-driven heuristic evolution as a practical paradigm for structured, constraint-intensive multi-objective resource allocation problems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6736926`

- title: A knowledge graph-constrained multi-agent workflow for constructing a benchmark for large language model evaluation in traditional Chinese medicine
- authors: Shuqi Xia, Jingyao Chen, Jinghua Li, Wei Jiang, Yang Wu, Tong Yu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6736926
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Background and Objective: Generic benchmarks rarely capture specialized clinical reasoning that requires heterogeneous evidence and multistep inference. We developed a knowledge graph-constrained multi-agent workflow, named MAC-TCM, for constructing benchmarks in traditional Chinese medicine, and instantiated it as ZBN-Bench for the Zhao Bingnan academic school of dermatology.Methods: A domain knowledge base was assembled from over 1000 curated clinical cases, 54 books, over 1000 full-text articles, and over 3000 indexed records, and encoded as a graph of 3300 nodes and 12,370 edges along the symptom-syndrome-disease-treatment-formula-herb chain. Candidate items were produced under graph-path and question-direction constraints, filtered by answer-reachability validation and four-dimensional soft scoring. An external sample of 300 items was independently rated by four domain experts, and fourteen large language models were evaluated under a prespecified zero-shot protocol.Results: Entity extraction against manual review reached 92.4% precision and 89.6% sensitivity (Cohen’s kappa 0.85). The automatic quality filter showed high specificity against low-quality candidates, reducing factual errors from 27.2% under unconstrained generation to 5.4%. The final benchmark contains 1500 items (750 single-choice, 450 multiple-choice, 300 chained-choice), with an expert consensus score of 3.802 out of 4.0 and inter-rater Fleiss’ kappa 0.78. Automatic scoring agreed closely with expert consensus (mean absolute error 0.035). The best-performing model reached 62.30% overall accuracy, with degradation on high-difficulty (26.54%) and chained-choice (18.62%) items.Conclusions: The workflow provides a traceable software pipeline and a reproducibility-oriented protocol for constructing specialty benchmarks in traditional Chinese medicine. ZBN-Bench offers a school-specific evaluation resource for model benchmarking and methodological research, although broader external validation remains necessary before downstream clinical interpretation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6745038`

- title: Stated-Preference Primacy as a Value-Alignment Guardrail in AI Career Recommender Systems A Three-Tier Signal Hierarchy for Workforce-Equity Recommendation Architectures
- authors: Jason Chapman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6745038
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

Some of you might have grown up with the book series "Choose your own Adventure." Well, PROSPER is the adult version. Most contemporary recommender systems blend multiple input signals into a single objective function. Stated user preferences become one feature among many. This paper describes a different architecture, deployed in the PROSPER career intelligence platform, in which user-provided signals are organized into a strict hierarchy. Stated context governs which stated preferences are allowed to constrain the output. Stated preferences always override inferred preferences. The architecture introduces what I call a value-alignment guardrail, meaning a deterministic gating layer interposed between an inference engine and a downstream large language model that enforces the primacy of user agency over algorithmic adjacency. The system was implemented as part of a workforce-displacement recommendation for serving displaced and transitioning workers in Michigan. It closes a class of failure modes in which inferred adjacencies override the user's declared situation. The clearest example is a displaced worker who has stated they need to remain in their current industry, but who receives recommendations to change industries entirely. I position this approach within the literature on contestable AI, stated-preference primacy in welfare economics, and recent alignment work on user-controlled recommendation. I argue that for high-stakes decision-support systems in workforce, healthcare, and public-benefit domains, the dominant blended-signal paradigm can be improved. Hierarchical signal governance is both technically tractable and ethically warranted.
