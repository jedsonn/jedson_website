## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6627678`

- title: Openmythos: Breaking The Parameter-depth Trade-off Via LTI-stable Recurrent Trans-formers
- authors: Fahril Irkham
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6627678
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Scaling the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models typically requires increasing model depth, which imposes a linear penalty on parameter count and memory usage. In this paper, we introduce OpenMythos, a Recurrent-Depth Transformer architecture that decouples computational depth from parameter overhead by iteratively looping a single transformer block. To overcome the numerical instability and signal drift inherent in deep recurrence, we propose a Linear Time-Invariant (LTI) stable injection mechanism that guarantees a spectral radius ρ(A) < 1, ensuring stability across arbitrary loop depths. We further optimize the architecture using Adaptive Computation Time (ACT) for dynamic halting and a hybrid learnableharmonic loop-index embedding to distinguish between iterative refinement stages. Our experiments on the shakespeare_char dataset demonstrate that the learnable depth embedding significantly enhances generalization, reducing the best validation loss from 1.691 in the static baseline to 1.641 while accelerating convergence. These results suggest that recurrent architectures can effectively emulate the depth of traditional transformers, providing a scalable path toward deep reasoning with a compact parameter footprint.

### abstract

Abstract Modern academia is highly specialized, and research that crosses disciplinary boundaries faces structural obstacles to recognition and evaluation. Conventional responses to this problem — establishing interdisciplinary centers or relying on rare researchers who command multiple fields — have not resolved the underlying issue. This paper first draws on the work of Kuhn, Prigogine, Polanyi, and Dewey to clarify the epistemic foundations of disciplinary silos, and then demonstrates, through concrete examples from everyday food preparation, what kinds of knowledge interdisciplinary inquiry can actually generate. Three categories of food transformation — Mechanical (bread dough, noodles, mochi), Diffusion (curry left overnight, cheese aging), and Biological (bread fermentation, miso, sake) — can each be described by the same mathematical skeleton of exponential convergence, despite being studied in isolation by rheology, food physics, culinary science, and biochemistry. This skeleton connects directly to thermodynamic concepts of entropy and order generation, and unfolds further into the epistemological question of what it means to say that a 'system' exists at all. The central contribution of this paper is to draw on Polanyi's concept of tacit knowledge to theoretically locate the human capacity to ask questions, position it as an irreplaceable human contribution, and formalize the collaboration between this capacity and AI's cross-domain connecting ability as a new theoretical unit of interdisciplinary knowledge generation. An appendix reflexively applies this model to the genesis of the paper itself, demonstrating the collaborative process in action. - authors: Dora Moscato
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6712619

### abstract

In the last three years, generative AI was everywhere in firms' narratives but barely visible in official labour statistics. In Europe, this may be changing. Combining EU Labour Force Survey data with the Eurostat enterprise survey on AI adoption, it is now possible to identify some first crosscountry EU evidence, drawn from harmonised official statistics, of post-GenAI movements in the labour force linked to the actual adoption of, and not merely exposure to, generative AI. One of the strongest patterns emerging from the statistics confirms the so-called seniority bias: the data show a clear post-2022 divergence in the age composition of High-AI sectors. In the NACE sectors with the highest AI adoption rates, the youngest workers lose relative ground while senior workers continue to gain, a divergence that does not appear in the economy as a whole, let alone in low-adoption sectors. The pattern cannot be reduced to demographic ageing, as it is not driven by a contraction of the young population at large.

### abstract

Generative AI models extract protected expression at ingestion, yet traditional infringement tests under the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 remain outputcentric. The High Court in Getty Images (US) Inc v Stability AI Ltd accepted that an intangible object may constitute an "article" for the purposes of secondary infringement, but held on the evidence that the specific model weights did not store or reproduce the claimant's works and therefore were not statutory "infringing copies" under sections 22, 23 and 27. To address this transparency vacuum, the judiciary should urgently adapt civil procedure. Rather than awaiting legislative reform, courts should utilise the issue-based disclosure rules under Practice Direction 57AD (PD 57AD), distinguishing the pre-existing but structurally insufficient pre-action disclosure mechanism under CPR r.31.16, before establishing the Post-Report Provenance Procedure (PRPP). Under this framework, where a claimant establishes a prima facie likelihood of ingestion on the Disclosure Review Document (DRD), the court may order Model C Extended Disclosure, compelling the developer to produce evidence of data provenance (such as cryptographic hash manifests) within a confidentiality ring; unreasonable non-compliance may result in an adverse inference on ingestion. This framework offers courts and litigants a procedurally defensible solution under existing UK law, requiring only standard Pt 35 expert evidence where necessary, to resolve the enforcement vacuum created by the March 2026 Report.
