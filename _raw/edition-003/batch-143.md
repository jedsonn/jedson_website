## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6652059`

- title: How AI Platforms Search: Fan-Out Query Behavior Across Intent Types, Verticals, and Platforms
- authors: Anthony Lee
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6652059
- keyword hits: chatgpt, gemini, gpt-5

### abstract

When users submit queries to AI search platforms, the platforms do not pass the user's text to web search verbatim. They decompose each prompt into multiple internal "fan-out queries" — the actual strings sent to retrieval engines. These fan-out queries determine which pages get fetched, which enter the AI's context window, and which get cited in the response. Despite their centrality to AI search discoverability, fan-out queries have not been studied at scale. This study classifies 1,323 fan-out queries generated by 540 parent queries across three AI platforms (ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity), ten commercial verticals, and five intent types. We capture fan-out queries via the OpenAI Responses API, Google GenAI grounding metadata, and Perplexity browser-level SSE interception. Nine findings emerge. First, user intent is a significant predictor of fan-out composition (X2=299.6, p<0.001, V=0.24): discovery queries trigger 3.3x the entity injection rate of informational queries. Second, platforms exhibit distinct retrieval personalities — ChatGPT injects entities from training data on 32% of fan-outs, Gemini casts a wide net with 27% expansion queries, and Perplexity leads in evidence-seeking at 21%. Third, ChatGPT's search trigger rate varies dramatically by model tier: gpt-5.4 searches on only 29% of queries while gpt-5.4-nano searches on 100%, suggesting larger models are more confident in answering from training data alone. Fourth, platform-intent interaction effects explain fan-out variation better than either factor alone (two-way AIC=192 vs main-effects AIC=937). Fifth, situation-first query phrasing produces significantly different fan-out distributions than standard phrasing (V=0.35, p<0.001). Sixth, no significant vertical effect was detected at this sample size (H=6.26, p=0.71, 18 queries per vertical), suggesting intent and platform are the dominant factors. Seventh, a three-part replicate analysis establishes that the search trigger decision is highly deterministic across platforms (Gemini 98.9%, ChatGPT gpt-5.4-mini 91.7%), while fan-out string consistency varies by platform and capture method: gpt-5.4-mini via the OpenAI API shows near-total stochasticity (Mean Jaccard 0.012), while Gemini (0.167), Perplexity (0.157), and ChatGPT UI (0.213) all reveal meaningful canonical cores with top-5 coverage ranging from 59% to 76%. Fan-out *types* remain moderately stable across all regimes (55-65% top-type agreement), supporting type-level rather than string-level optimisation. These findings establish that AI search operates a two-layer retrieval system: a model-confidence layer that decides whether to search at all, and a query-decomposition layer that determines what to search for. Optimising for AI citation requires understanding both layers.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6653878`

- title: YYA Glossary | Adaptive Policy Communication
- authors: Yuen Yuen Ang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6653878
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

Adaptive Policy Communication is a theory of governance under complexity, introduced by Yuen Yuen Ang (2016), which explains how leaders steer policy implementation by mixing clear and ambiguous directions to selectively enforce discipline and grant flexibility. Ang theorizes ambiguity not as a failure of communication, but as a mechanism for enabling flexibility and policy feedback. This system of communication is an example of a Meta-Institution (higher-order structures that supports adaptive policymaking rather than offering a fixed solution.) Ang’s later work ( 2023 ; 2026 ) operationalizes this logic using automated text analysis and LLMs on large volumes of Chinese policy directives, translating the theory into measurable patterns and datasets. This project demonstrates that Ang's Adaptive Political Economy (APE) — as a paradigm, not just a framework — can generate new lines of inquiry (how leaders communicate under complexity), theory (adaptive policy communication), typology (five policy signals), measurement (theory-driven, human-LLM annotation), and novel data (CAPC-CG), which can inspire more data collection.

- title: LLMs Are Not Polymaths and Reconciliation at the Seams Still Needs Humans
- authors: Philip Obazee
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6659418
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper studies two epistemic actors: the large language model 𝑀 and the Expert 𝐸. The question is not whether 𝑀 can produce plausible interdisciplinary language; it plainly can. The question is whether 𝑀 can replace 𝐸 as a terminal arbiter at the seams where one domain's verified claims are translated into another domain's admissible judgments. The answer developed here is no, except in the degenerate case in which the model's internal information partition is already fine enough to separate the expert's decisive target classes.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6472898`

- title: Conversation History Shapes Semantic State: EPA (Evaluation, Potency, Activity) Trajectories in Transformer Residual Streams
- authors: Juan Jacobo Jimenez Sanchez
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6472898
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

We present a method for tracking the internal semantic state of a large language model across conversation turns, using Kozlowski et al.'s (2025) technique for extracting semantic axes from word embeddings and recovering Osgood's EPA structure in LLMs (arXiv:2508.10003), applied here to transformer residual streams. Working with Gemma 3 27B-IT, we project the last-token hidden state at each assistant turn onto three classical EPA dimensions, Evaluation, Potency, and Activity, derived from 24 antonym-pair axes via PCA. We demonstrate that the Evaluation dimension at layer 36 (of 62) reliably tracks conversational depth turn-by-turn.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6662138`

- title: Beyond the Score: A Hybrid Framework Combining Ridge Regression with Large Language Models for Actionable Net Promoter Score Analysis
- authors: Fabio Grandi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6662138
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

The Net Promoter Score (NPS) remains one of the most widely adopted customer experience metrics, yet organizations consistently struggle to translate NPS data into actionable operational improvements. This paper presents a hybrid analytical framework that combines ridge regression with large language model (LLM) interpretation to bridge the gap between customer satisfaction measurement and operational decision-making. The framework operates on two complementary axes-quantitative and qualitative, external (survey) and internal (business context)-to produce segment-level insights that identify both which customer segments and operational levers drive NPS variation and why, using customers' own verbatim responses as evidence. Ridge regression is selected over tree-based alternatives for its ability to produce signed, interpretable coefficients for each feature, which then serve as directional hypotheses for LLM-guided qualitative analysis. A multi-stage feature selection process employing recursive cross-validated elimination, statistical significance filtering, and hyperparameter optimization ensures coefficient reliability. The framework was deployed on a B2B customer satisfaction survey (n = 775, 88 features), reducing analysis time from approximately one week of manual effort to under 40 minutes while producing a comprehensive 20-page analytical report. The ridge regression model achieved an R² of 0.49, explaining approximately half of NPS variance through segment-level drivers. Operational experience during development indicates that statistically directed LLM analysis produces markedly more specific and actionable insights than either method in isolation. The framework generalizes to any domain combining quantitative outcomes with unstructured text, including CSAT, Customer Effort Score, employee engagement, and churn prediction.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6656018`

- title: An AI-Assisted Computational Analysis of Landlord Concerns in Online Housing Communities
- authors: Cheng Ren, Saketh Reddy Voodem, Zhi Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6656018
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

In the U.S., approximately 35% of people live in rental housing, and the majority of rental properties are owned by individual investors. Despite this, most research on housing stability, eviction, and tenant rights focuses on tenants, the demand side of the rental market, while giving limited attention to landlords, who represent the supply side. This study aims to explore the most common topics landlords raise in housing-related discussions and examine how these topics vary over time and across states. This study collected data from the subreddit r/Landlord, and the sample included 20,826 posts from 2013 to 2023. We applied advanced AI methods, such as large language models, to create an automated pipeline that extracts location data and summarizes topics from posts. The AI achieved 98% accuracy in extracting locations and 90% accuracy in summarizing topics, as validated by human reviewers. The five most common topics were Eviction, Termination, and Possession; Tenant Conduct, House Rules, and Neighbor Issues; Repair, Maintenance, and Building Systems; Lease Terms and Agreements; and Tenant Screening, Application, and Onboarding. Eviction, Termination, and Possession emerged as the dominant growth category. California, New York, Texas, and New Jersey were the four states with the highest posting volume. The findings reveal prominent concerns expressed in an online landlord community and provide insights into how landlord-related discussions may inform housing stability interventions.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6656119`

- title: The Aden Effect: Why Existing Frameworks Cannot Account for the Current Moment
- authors: Johan van Rooyen, Ruban Ganeshu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6656119
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

This paper introduces the Aden Effect: the condition in which the simultaneous convergence of geopolitical restructuring, artificial intelligence integration, and institutional reconstitution produces analytical failures across disciplines, not sequentially but together, rendering frameworks built for the post-Cold War order systematically inadequate at precisely the moment they are most needed. The argument is that the disciplines best positioned to interpret the current moment, international relations, macroeconomics, education theory, technology governance, are each producing partial accounts of phenomena that cannot be understood except as integrated. The 2026 closure of the Strait of Hormuz, the operational integration of large language models into military targeting systems, and the emergence of parallel institutional stacks operating outside the post-1945 settlement are not three separate stories. They are one story, and the inability of any single discipline to tell it is itself a finding. The reconstitution is itself fragmented: the institutions emerging in response to the convergence are being built within the same disciplinary silos that produced the analytical failures the paper diagnoses, raising the prospect that the new order will inherit the blindness of the old. Drawing on three contemporary cases, the paper develops a framework that does two things. First, it specifies the mechanism by which disciplinary specialization, which served the post-Cold War order well, now actively obstructs the analysis the present moment requires. Second, it offers a set of integration tools, concepts that translate across disciplines without requiring practitioners to abandon their training, that allow economists, IR scholars, educators, and decision-makers to incorporate forces their existing frameworks cannot accommodate. The contribution is not a new discipline but a new analytical posture: the recognition that the convergence is the phenomenon, and that any account that decomposes it into its disciplinary components has already misunderstood it.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6666660`

- title: The Epistemic Gap: A Formal Framework for Detecting Strategic Misalignment
- authors: Etai Fiedelman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6666660
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

This paper introduces a framework for detecting strategic misalignment between narratives and underlying realities in political, regulatory, and business environments. It argues that reliance on headlines, speeches, and summaries can distort judgment by oversimplifying complex conditions, causing either overreaction to visible but low-substance signals or delayed recognition of quiet yet consequential developments. To address this, the paper proposes decomposing informational artifacts into a Marketing Vector (rhetoric, framing, intended interpretation) and a Reality Vector (incentives, enforcement capacity, dependencies, and material consequences), whose divergence can be systematically assessed through a four-quadrant matrix. To operationalize the model at scale, the paper proposes a constrained Large Language Model (LLM) architecture in which LLMs are used as analytical tools for structured extraction, domain-guided inference grounded in codified expert knowledge, and deterministic gap-scoring rather than autonomous interpreters. The framework offers a practical method for improving strategic judgment and resource allocation under uncertainty.
