## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.5775428`

- title: The ECB's Green Put: From Cheap Talk to Priced Action
- authors: Tristan Jourde, Urszula Szczerbowicz, Floris van Dijk
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2025-11-22
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.5775428
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

Standard asset pricing theory predicts a carbon premium for high-emission firms, yet recent realized returns have shown the opposite. We show that the European Central Bank's (ECB) communication acts as a green put, a systematic policy signal that has persistently penalized brown assets and limited the return premium they would otherwise have earned. To capture these shocks, we construct the Central Bank Climate and Nature Communication (CB-CNC) index, a novel high-frequency measure of ECB's sustainability involvement from 1997 to 2025. Using a Large Language Model, the index distinguishes action-oriented communications from materiality-oriented ones and incorporates nature alongside climate. We find that only action-oriented climate communication, not materiality rhetoric, drive the repricing of brown firms in both equity and bond markets. A counterfactual analysis shows that the cumulative impact of this action-oriented discourse has effectively eliminated the brown premium, preventing high-emission companies from achieving a cumulative outperformance of approximately 25% relative to green assets since 2018.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.5776803`

- title: La llamada «new value exception» en los planes de reestructuración ( The So-Called “New Value Exception” in Reorganization Plans)
- authors: Enrique Gandía
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2025-11-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.5776803
- keyword hits: llama

### abstract

Spanish Abstract: La aplicación de la regla de prioridad absoluta (art. 655.2.4.º LC) implica que si un plan de reestructuración no consensual contempla una quita a los acreedores —aunque sea de un solo céntimo—, los socios no pueden recibir o mantener ninguna participación en la sociedad reestructurada. El excesivo rigor de esta regla puede frustrar la reestructuración de empresas económicamente viables. Por eso, la jurisprudencia norteamericana reconoce desde los años treinta del siglo pasado una «excepción» a la misma, en virtud de la cual los socios pueden conservar sus acciones o participaciones siempre que realicen una aportación nueva, sustancial, en dinero o bienes de valor equivalente, necesaria y razonablemente proporcional al valor recibido. El presente trabajo examina si esta llamada «new value exception» resulta aplicable en Derecho español y, en tal caso, con qué requisitos. A tal fin, se empieza exponiendo los orígenes de esta «excepción», tanto en el Derecho norteamericano como en el Derecho alemán (I); a continuación, se ensaya una interpretación teleológica del artículo 655.2.4.º de la Ley Concursal para terminar afirmando que, en efecto, la «excepción de valor nuevo» tiene cabida en nuestro ordenamiento, puesto que no se trata de una verdadera excepción, sino de una consecuencia o corolario de la regla de prioridad absoluta (II). A partir de ahí, se analizan críticamente los distintos requisitos exigidos por la jurisprudencia norteamericana (III), con especial atención a la razonable equivalencia de lo aportado con el valor de las acciones o participaciones mantenidas (IV). El trabajo concluye con una síntesis de los principales resultados alcanzados (V). English Abstract: The application of the absolute priority rule (Art. 655.2.4 of the Spanish Insolvency Act) means that if a non-consensual restructuring plan contemplates a write-down for creditors —even of a single cent— the shareholders cannot receive or retain any interest in the restructured company. The excessive rigidity of this rule may frustrate the restructuring of economically viable businesses. For this reason, U.S. case law has recognized since the 1930s an “exception” to it, whereby shareholders may retain their shares or equity interests provided that they make a new, substantial contribution, in money or money's worth, that is necessary and reasonably equivalent to the value received. This paper examines whether this so-called “new value exception” is applicable under Spanish law and, if so, under what requirements. To this end, it first sets out the origins of this “exception” in both U.S. and German law (I); it then undertakes a teleological interpretation of Article 655.2.4 of the Insolvency Act and ultimately argues that the “new value exception” indeed fits within the Spanish legal system, since it is not a true exception but rather a consequence or corollary of the absolute priority rule (II). From there, the various requirements imposed by U.S. case law are critically analyzed (III), with particular attention to the reasonable equivalence between the contribution made and the value of the shares or equity interests retained (IV). The paper concludes with a synthesis of the main findings (V).
