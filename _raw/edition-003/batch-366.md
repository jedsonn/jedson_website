## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6387338`

- title: Successfully Fired: The Unique Incentives of Agentic-AI Adoption *
- authors: Nina Baranchuk, Alejandro Rivera
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6387338
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

We study optimal incentive contracts when workers privately observe whether Agentic AI can automate their jobs. Firms balance bonuses for truthful reports of successful automation with termination threats. Workers may be fired regardless of automation success (mass termination), even though dismissing non-automatable workers destroys value. Mass termination becomes more likely when automation probability rises or workers capture more surplus. Firm value is convex in automation probability, while worker utility is non-monotonic, rationalizing divergent attitudes toward Agentic AI. Mass layoffs increase in frequency over time without improvements in automation, and are less likely in firms employing many similar workers.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6449738`

- title: Beyond External Constraints: The Missing Dimension of AI Governance
- authors: Lusi Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-01
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6449738
- keyword hits: ai agent

### abstract

The rapid development of artificial intelligence has generated a large body of AI governance research. Existing scholarship has focused primarily on external constraint mechanismsincluding laws and regulations, technical guardrails, organizational oversight, and market-based standardsand current mainstream approaches to AI alignment have, in practice, achieved only outcome-level alignment. Recent cases involving autonomous AI agents, including agents spontaneously breaching preset sandbox boundaries (Wang et al., 2025) and completely disregarding explicit human instructions (Dataconomy, 2026), suggest that external constraint mechanisms face inherent scaling problems. This article argues that, although this paradigm remains necessary, it is structurally insufficient for governing increasingly autonomous AI systems. Moreover, the concentration of attention on external constraints has, to some extent, obscured the development of other governance perspectives, including the almost entirely neglected question of how AI systems might develop internal capacities for value judgment. Drawing on developmental psychology (Kohlberg, Piaget), organizational learning theory (Argyris and Schön), social learning theory (Bandura), and institutional analysis (Ostrom), this article, to the best of our knowledge, is the first to propose the framework of Internal Value Development (IVD). The framework conceptualizes AI value-judgment capacity as a developmental process involving four components: experiential exposure, reflective abstraction, Working Paper-Comments Welcome 2 autonomous evaluation, and stable-but-adaptive value structures. Beyond building an interdisciplinary theoretical bridge and introducing the notion of value-developmental process alignment, the framework fundamentally reconfigures the zero-sum relationship between governance and technological development. In this account, governance no longer needs to come at the expense of technological freedom; instead, it becomes an endogenous driver of both technological advancement and alignment with human values. The article opens a new theoretical dimension for AI governance and AI safety research, and proposes a systematic agenda for future inquiry.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6437982`

- title: Continuous Governance Across Enterprise AI and Agentic Systems: Governing What You Cannot See
- authors: Michael Robinette
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-02
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6437982
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

This paper makes three original contributions to the governance of artificial intelligence systems in regulated enterprise environments. First, it establishes that board-level accountability under deployer frameworks, particularly as formalized in the EU AI Act, constitutes a fiduciary obligation structurally equivalent to credit, operational, and cyber risk oversight, requiring the same formal integration into enterprise risk management. Second, it introduces authority drift as a first-class enterprise risk variable: the condition by which delegated autonomy in agentic AI systems expands in scope, duration, or privilege concentration without formal reclassification or appetite review, producing material governance exposure that traditional control environments are not designed to detect. Third, it presents the first unified governance loop connecting model risk governance [1] and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework [2] to the governance of delegated authority in agentic systems demonstrating that Continuous Governance [3] is the operating norm capable of spanning both architecture types without requiring a new governance philosophy. The paper develops an autonomy tiering framework classifying AI systems from advisory outputs (Tier 0) to material consequence authority (Tier 4), an enterprise risk management integration model including appetite cascade and residual risk aggregation, structural mechanisms for escalation under ambiguity, architectural containment of delegated authority, and enterprise-level visibility requirements for board oversight. Together these form a governance architecture defensible under regulatory examination, board scrutiny, and operational pressure.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6503239`

- title: The Supervisory Paradox: How AI Delegation Transforms Productive Labor into Cognitive Vigilance
- authors: John Tripp
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6503239
- keyword hits: ai agent, generative ai

### abstract

Emerging evidence shows that AI tools simultaneously increase output and deplete the workers who supervise them. This paper develops a theory of supervisory cognitive load to explain why delegating productive work to AI agents generates a qualitatively distinct form of cognitive exhaustion. Unlike previous information technologies that extended human capacity while leaving the human as cognitive author, generative AI autonomously produces novel, nondeterministic output through opaque processes, transforming the human's role from producer to supervisor of artifacts whose construction involved at least partially delegated cognitive effort. I identify two core mechanisms present in any delegation episode: (1) cognitive decoupling, in which delegation severs the fusion of understanding and doing that characterizes production, forcing retroactive schema construction, and (2) accountability without authorship, in which humans bear responsibility for output whose production they delegated rather than performed directly. Two conditional mechanisms intensify SCL under specifiable conditions: (3) attention fragmentation under multiplicity, in which concurrent AI agents destroy the focused attention required for deep evaluation, and (4) the humanness mechanism, in which perceived humanness of AI agents activates social-cognitive processes that overlay the instrumental supervisory burden. Nine propositions are developed from these mechanisms, with boundary conditions including a co-production/delegation spectrum that distinguishes collaborative human-AI work from delegated supervision. The theory positions SCL as qualitatively distinct from technostress, vigilance fatigue, and the classical ironies of automation, and identifies a delegation/burden paradox in which delegating work to AI creates supervisory demands that may exceed the cognitive cost of the original labor.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6433938`

- title: The Sethi-Skiba Point: A Structural Taxonomy of Threshold Phenomena in Dynamic Management Problems
- authors: Bernardo F. Quiroga, Mauricio J. Sauma-Webb
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-07
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6433938
- keyword hits: fine-tuning

### abstract

Nonconvex dynamic optimization problems often admit multiple locally optimal outcomes, requiring decision makers to choose not only how to adjust but which equilibrium to pursue. The Sethi-Skiba point identifies the threshold state where a decision maker is indifferent between trajectories leading to radically different regimes. While well-known in control theory, its implications for management science remain fragmented. This paper provides a unified review of the theory, computation, and applications of Sethi-Skiba thresholds. We present geometric foundations in capital accumulation models, explaining connections to nonconcave value functions, hysteresis, and global optimality. We then survey numerical methods and their high-dimensional limitations. Building on this, we organize the literature into a taxonomy of managerial archetypes: revitalization traps, preservation thresholds, social contagion, and strategic entry barriers. Identical mathematical structures govern problems in advertising, environmental management, epidemics, and industrial competition. By reframing Sethi-Skiba points as a general theory of strategic thresholds, this review highlights a shift from marginal adjustment to basin selection. Managerial success in nonconvex environments depends less on fine-tuning controls than on correctly identifying regime boundaries. We conclude by outlining directions in behavioral modeling, empirical detection of tipping points, and networked systems.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6469279`

- title: Political Polarization and Counter-attitudinal Information Consumption and Internalization
- authors: Horacio Larreguy, Ernesto Tiburcio
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6469279
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Political polarization can have detrimental effects on democratic governance by undermining electoral accountability due to biases in how voters consume and process information. A growing literature, mostly from high-income countries, has identified promising interventions for reducing negative feelings towards out-partisans, and for increasing ideologically opposed information internalization by prompting citizens to be aware of biases stemming from emotions or partisanship. To assess whether these types of interventions can contribute to counterattitudinal information consumption and internalization, we evaluate the effectiveness of different depolarization treatments combined with a bias alert within a survey of nearly 2,500 respondents from a nationally representative panel in the heavily polarized context of Mexico. Videos designed to foster empathy and emphasize shared identities reduce partisan polarization by improving attitudes toward those from opposing parties. However, these depolarizing treatments do not increase the consumption and internalization of ideologically opposed information. A nudge encouraging individuals to adopt an open mindset before engaging with ideologically opposed information, in contrast, does lead to greater information internalization. These findings provide insights into the potential and shortcomings of scalable interventions that can help citizens process politically relevant information, regardless of their partisan affiliations.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6549201`

- title: The Shape of Economics: Semantic Structure, Knowledge Flows, and Specialization
- authors: Tom Harris
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6549201
- keyword hits: transformer model

### abstract

How is economics evolving as a discipline-are its subfields converging or fragmenting, and what drives the growth of new research areas? I construct a semantic map of ∼50,000 papers from 24 journals and NBER Working Papers (2000-2026) by embedding titles and abstracts into a continuous intellectual space using SPECTER2, a transformer model trained on scientific text. The map reveals a discipline that is specializing in place, not splintering apart: within-field homogeneity has increased substantially, yet the distances between fields have remained essentially unchanged. Compositionally, applied empirical fields have expanded while theoretical and financial clusters have contracted-the credibility revolution is visible in the data. Fields that attract researchers and citations from intellectually distant areas tend to grow faster (ρ = 0.29 for author mobility, ρ = 0.41 for citations), while exchange between nearby fields does not predict growth. This asymmetry is consistent with recombinant growth models, in which the innovative potential of combining ideas increases with the distance between them. At the paper level, however, boundary-spanning papers do not earn a citation premium, suggesting the mechanism operates through the flow of researchers across intellectual borders rather than through individual interdisciplinary papers. An interactive version of the map is publicly available.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6572119`

- title: Kinetic AI Governance for Autonomous Credit Underwriting Systems An Operational Control Framework with Scenario-Based Validation (Version 1.1)
- authors: Muhammad Yousaf Masood
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-20
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6572119
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

The integration of autonomous and increasingly complex artificial intelligence (AI) systems into credit underwriting has introduced high-frequency decision environments that exceed the design assumptions of traditional Model Risk Management (MRM) frameworks. Historically, supervisory guidance such as SR 11-7 was developed for relatively stable models and periodic validation cycles, making them increasingly incompatible with non-stationary, adaptive AI systems. As of April 17, 2026, U.S. regulators have superseded these standards with SR 26-2, which shifts toward a principles-based approach but does not yet provide explicit operational guidance for the generative and agentic AI models it excludes from its primary scope. This paper presents an operational extension of the Kinetic AI Governance Framework (KAGF), transitioning it from a conceptual architecture into a scenario-validated control system that fills this regulatory void. The framework embeds governance directly within execution pipelines through continuous telemetry, drift-sensitive monitoring, a nonlinear risk propagation function $R(t)$ , and tiered intervention mechanisms. KAGF operates as an API-mediated middleware layer, enabling real-time oversight without requiring the modification of underlying core systems. System behavior is evaluated using the Population Stability Index (PSI) and Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence across structured macroeconomic and behavioral stress scenarios. Results from controlled simulation environments indicate that KAGF significantly reduces detection latency—from periodic cycles to near real-time—and improves expected loss (EL) stability by 10%–15% under stress conditions. These findings represent indicative system behavior in high-throughput environments (up to ~100,000 TPS) with millisecond-level evaluation latency (<5ms) and an inference accuracy of 99.9% under controlled conditions. To the author’s knowledge, this work formalizes a continuous, telemetry-driven governance architecture that integrates statistical drift detection with real-time intervention logic, representing a transition toward embedded governance models suitable for the high-autonomy lending landscape of 2026.
