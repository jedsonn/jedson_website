- authors: Ian Bann
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6708259
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, gemini

### abstract

AI-generated answers are changing how prospective students discover and evaluate universities because systems interpret queries, select sources, and construct responses before a user visits a website. This shifts visibility from ranking position alone to observable behaviour within generated outputs, where inclusion, attribution, and recommendation determine which institutions are considered. This paper presents an applied framework for evaluating university AI search visibility using four outcome layers: AI search inclusion, AI brand mentions, AI recommendation behaviour, and AI referral traffic. The study applies this framework to a controlled dataset of Computer Science queries executed across multiple AI systems, including ChatGPT, Google AI Overview, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, and Grok. The results show that visibility is concentrated among a limited set of universities, with repeated inclusion and recommendation driven by content structure, entity clarity, and external reinforcement rather than traditional ranking position alone. Institutions that provide structured, topic-aligned content and maintain consistent representation across external sources are more likely to be selected and recommended within generated answers. The findings demonstrate that university visibility is increasingly determined at the point of answer construction. This requires a shift from ranking-focused optimisation to content and entity strategies aligned with how AI systems interpret, synthesise, and present information during student discovery.

### abstract

We introduce EAR-AI , a novel scalar measure constructed using chronologically consistent large language models, to extract predictive information from earnings call text over 2009-2024. Portfolios formed on EAR-AI deliver sizable Sharpe ratios with robust out-of-sample performance. We find that EAR-AI is closely linked to firm fundamentals: it predicts earnings and revenue surprises, R&D growth, capital expenditure growth, and changes in leverage. Its predictive content subsumes classical post-earnings-announcement drift measures, and its return predictability is concentrated among firms whose subsequent earnings realizations confirm its forecasts. In addition, EAR-AI shapes the expectation formation of equity analysts, and this effect is not driven by sentiment. Finally, EAR-AI captures a distinct source of return predictability relative to a comprehensive set of 125 characteristics, and combining EAR-AI with these characteristics substantially improves the mean-variance efficiency of characteristic-managed portfolios.

### abstract

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) holds significant promise for inclusive human development across Sub-Saharan Africa, yet it also risks reinforcing digital inequalities. This comparative study examines how Angola, Kenya, Rwanda, and Nigeria are integrating GenAI into national development agendas, focusing on socio-technical enablers and barriers shaping equitable outcomes. - title: Towards agricultural specialty industries with scarce data and experts: from automatic data collection to intelligent ‘brain’-machine realization
- authors: Yan Zhe, Xiangnan He, Rui Yan, Beilei Fan, Chenxue Yang, Zhemin Li, Yuting Wang, Xian Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-04
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6709171
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Agricultural specialty industries, exemplified by the high-value Cymbidium goeringii sector, are severely hindered by scarce data and a reliance on rare domain experts for tasks like variety identification. To overcome these dual bottlenecks, this study proposes an end-to-end framework advancing from automatic data collection to intelligent 'brain'-machine realization. First, addressing the data scarcity, a large language model (LLM)-driven pipeline automatically constructed a standardized dataset of 84,477 images across 20 Cymbidium goeringii varieties. Second, to distill expert knowledge into an intelligent "brain", an improved lightweight detection model, YOLO-AgriLite, was developed based on YOLOv5s by incorporating Depthwise Separable Convolutions, ContextAggregation, a Bidirectional Feature Pyramid Network, and the SW-MPDIoU loss function . Finally, for physical "machine" realization, the model was deployed on an RK3588S edge computing device using INT8 quantization. Experimental results demonstrate that YOLO-AgriLite achieved a mean average precision (mAP@0.5) of 98.6%, significantly outperforming newer baselines like YOLOv8s and YOLOv11s. On the edge device, it delivered a high-throughput inference speed of 80 FPS with a full-core power consumption of only 9W. Field validation at a national orchid exhibition confirmed its robustness, achieving a 90.6% real-time accuracy rate .This research provides a highly generalizable 'brain'-machine paradigm that successfully resolves the scarcity of data and experts, enabling low-cost, intelligent decision-making in regional specialty agriculture.

- title: FraudRAG: Knowledge Graph-Augmented Retrieval for Real-Time Financial Statement Fraud Detection Using a Domain-Driven Medallion Architecture
- authors: Akshay Sharma
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6714178
- keyword hits: claude, llm

### abstract

Financial statement fraud-encompassing document forgery, balance manipulation, amount substitution, and entity fabrication-imposes losses exceeding $5.38 trillion annually on global financial institutions. Existing detection systems analyze documents in isolation, failing to leverage the rich relational context inherent in customer-institution networks and prior fraud histories. This paper presents FraudRAG, a novel knowledge-engineering architecture that addresses this limitation through three original contributions: (1) the first Graph-RAG pipeline for financial document forensics, combining Neo4j knowledge graph multi-hop traversal with ChromaDB dense vector retrieval to provide Claude 3.5 Sonnet with dynamically retrieved customer-specific context at inference time; (2) a Domain-Driven Medallion architecture with formally specified Bronze, Silver, and Gold data quality contracts, including a deterministic accounting arithmetic validator at the Silver layer that constitutes a high-precision, submillisecond fraud pre-filter; and (3) a risk propagation mechanism enabling passive fraud ring detection through graph neighborhood scoring without explicit ring labels. Experiments on a reproducible synthetic benchmark of 2,400 annotated financial statements spanning six document types demonstrate AUC=0.943, Precision=0.891, Recall=0.876, and F1=0.883, outperforming rule-based, LLM-only, RAG-only, and graph-only baselines by 18-24 percentage points. Ablation studies confirm the independent contribution of each pipeline component. The system achieves sub-3-second end-to-end latency and scales to petabyte workloads via Apache Spark and AWS EMR. An open-source implementation is publicly available.

- title: How a Non-Theorist and Two AIs Proved a Theorem: Anatomy of a Human-AI Collaboration in Mathematical Economics
- authors: Diana Weinhold
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-05
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6591059
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

In April 2026, a two-week collaboration between one applied economist with no training in formal mathematics, two large language models, and a third AI entering later as hostile auditor, produced what the participants believe to be valid formal proofs of two asymptotic theorems in mathematical economics - one under independent values, one extending the result to the empirically relevant case of positive correlation. The proofs have survived multiple rounds of hostile AI auditing but have not yet been reviewed by a human mathematician. This paper documents that collaboration as a case study in human-AI joint production of formal theory. The paper identifies five generalisable themes: comparative advantage (the human's economic intuitions repeatedly selected the mathematical objects the proofs were built on); complementary error modes (multiple AI systems caught each other's characteristic mistakes); dead ends as construction (failed proof strategies narrowed the search space when properly documented); context management as infrastructure (handover documents and fresh-session resets were load-bearing components); and the AI-assisted discovery chain (the entire research arc, from computational finding through formal proof, was feasible only through human-AI collaboration). The paper also presents an error taxonomy based on the documented record, identifying five distinct AI error types including a novel pattern termed computational dissociation, in which an AI's reasoning, code execution, and text presentation operate on independent tracks. The collaboration's core methodological lesson is that multi-AI collaboration was most productive not when models agreed, but when the workflow repeatedly forced synchronisation between prose, mathematics, and executable objects.
