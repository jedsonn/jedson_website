## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6481200`

- title: The Biological Prerequisite for Artificial General Intelligence: Why Probabilistic Computation Cannot Produce Cognition
- authors: Murat Atilgan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6481200
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

This paper argues that Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) and its theoretical successor, Artificial Superintelligence (ASI), are fundamentally unachievable through probabilistic computation alone, regardless of model scale, architectural innovation, or computational investment. We establish that all current AI systems-including large language models, diffusion models, and reinforcement learning agents-operate through statistical pattern matching over structured data representations. While these systems produce outputs that superficially resemble cognitive behaviour, they lack the defining properties of cognition: embodied experience, temporal continuity, homeostatic self-regulation, and phenomenal consciousness. We argue that these properties are not emergent features of sufficient computational complexity but are intrinsic to biological neural substrates operating through electrochemical processes that cannot be replicated through digital simulation. The paper presents two logically exhaustive paths to AGI: (1) direct bidirectional integration between artificial systems and biological neural tissue, or (2) complete replication of human neural architecture at biological fidelity. Both paths require breakthroughs in neuroscience, bioengineering, and materials science-not in software or computational scaling. We conclude that the prevailing industry narrative of achieving AGI through larger models and more compute represents a category error of historic proportions, and that genuine progress toward AGI requires redirecting research investment toward neurotechnology and biological-artificial integration.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6521001`

- title: What Crisis Narratives Hide
- authors: Dennis Bennett
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6521001
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This essay applies John Trimbur's framework for analyzing literacy crisis discourse to contemporary debates about generative artificial intelligence in higher education. Drawing on personal narrative, Trimbur's identification of competing "decline" and "progress" narratives, and Fredric Jameson's observations about ideological foreclosure, the essay argues that responses to generative AI in writing instruction reproduce the same discursive patterns that have characterized every major literacy crisis in American educational history. Both the alarm over AI's threat to student writing and the enthusiasm for AI integration as pedagogical progress perform identical ideological work: focusing attention on technological and pedagogical questions while systematically deflecting examination of the economic and political conditions-decades of defunding, privatization, and wealth concentration-that determine educational outcomes. The essay concludes by calling on writing studies professionals, particularly graduate teaching assistants entering the field, to ground their responses to technological change in disciplinary scholarship rather than crisis-driven frameworks that prevent meaningful engagement with the structural forces shaping literacy education.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6665244`

- title: Structured AI-Integrated Learning (SAIL) in Business and Management Education forDeveloping AI and Prompt Literacy
- authors: Antigoni Papadimitriou, Zilong Pan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6665244
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

​The rapid integration of generative artificial intelligence (GAI) into organizational practice has created an urgent need for business and management education to move beyond tool exposure toward meaningful skill development. This study develops and implements a Structured AI-Integrated Learning (SAIL) approach that embeds GAI within active learning environments to support students’ AI literacy and prompt literacy. A central feature of the approach is the use of structured scaffolds, particularly the RACE framework (Role–Action–Context–Explanation), which guides students in designing prompts, evaluating AI outputs, and linking responses to theoretical concepts. The approach was implemented in an undergraduate Organizational Behavior course (n = 93). Quantitative results indicate significant improvements in both AI literacy and prompt literacy, including gains in knowledge, application, and ethical awareness. Qualitative findings show that students engaged with AI iteratively and reflectively, using it as a “co-thinker” to refine prompts, assess output quality, and connect AI-generated insights with course theories. The findings demonstrate that structured, pedagogically guided AI integration transforms AI from a passive information source into an active learning scaffold, offering a practical pathway for preparing students to navigate AI-augmented professional environments.

## uid: `arxiv:2604.25224v2`

- title: ValueBlindBench: Agreement-Gated Stress Testing of LLM-Judged Investment Rationales Before Returns Are Observable
- authors: Sidi Chang, Peiying Zhu, Yuxiao Chen
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-28
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25224v2
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

LLM-based financial agents increasingly produce investment rationales before the outcomes needed to evaluate them are observable. This creates a delayed-ground-truth evaluation problem: realized returns remain the eventual arbiter of investment quality, but they arrive too late and are too noisy to guide many model-development and governance decisions. LLM judges offer a tempting shortcut for pre-deployment evaluation of AI-finance systems, but unvalidated judges may reward verbosity, confidence, or rubric mimicry rather than financial judgment. This paper introduces ValueBlindBench, a preregistered agreement-gated stress-test protocol for deciding when LLM-judged investment-rationale claims are publishable, qualified, or invalid. In a controlled market-state capital-allocation prototype with 1,000 honest decision cycles and 100 preregistered adversarial controls (1,100 trajectories, 5,500 judge calls), ValueBlindBench clears the aggregate agreement gate at \(\barκ_w = 0.7168\) but prevents several overclaims. Lower-rank systems collapse into a tie-class, one rubric dimension fails the per-dimension gate (\texttt{constraint\_awareness}, \(\barκ_w = 0.2022\)), single-judge rankings are family-dependent, and terse-correct rationales receive a \(Δ= -2.81\) rubric-point penalty relative to honest rationales. A targeted anchor-specificity probe further shows that financial constructs such as constraint awareness are operationally load-bearing. The scientific object is therefore not a leaderboard and not a claim to measure true investment skill. ValueBlindBench is a pre-calibration metrology layer for AI-finance evaluation: it governs whether a proposed LLM-judge-based investment-rationale claim is stable enough, agreed enough, and uncontaminated enough to be reported at all.

### abstract

The Solo Decision Architecture (SDA) framework operationalizes the foundational technique documented in the Methodological Layer of the Decision Architecture discipline (Hernandez and Montero 2026b) at the individual scale, where a single Decision Architect is the unit of accountability for a consequential commitment. The framework is currently implemented on the Convoking4 platform, and this paper documents that implementation. The Diagnostic Layer of the discipline (Hernandez and Montero 2026a) establishes that consequential decisions are distorted by the compounding interaction of human cognitive bias, organizational dynamics, and the technical properties of generative artificial intelligence, with distortion forming inside one person's thinking before it ever reaches a team. The Methodological Layer establishes the technique that responds to the diagnostic, backcasting and the dual lens with a decision architecture artifact as its output, and the five constraints any framework operationalizing the technique must meet. The implementation captures the practitioner's frame in a Brain Dump phase conducted human-only, extracts four coordinates under the Honest Extraction rule, and proceeds through a nine-phase session that holds aspiration and constraint together through the dual lens. Structural mechanisms enforce the methodology's conditions at specific gates: the Camera Test on milestone inspectability, the ADICE Matrix on stakeholder assumptions, the Flinch Test on commitment acceptance, and the bidirectional Verification on artifact convergence. The paper documents the implementation's structural answers to the methodology's five constraints, with platform-design enforcement of the four AI participation conditions producing the methodology's required conditions during the practitioner's normal use of the system rather than relying on the practitioner to maintain the conditions through self-discipline. The implementation does not eliminate the diagnostic's failure modes; it bounds their reach into the artifact through structural mechanisms documented across the paper. Practitioner discipline at specific points where the platform structures but does not strictly enforce remains essential, and the paper names honestly the failure modes the implementation can exhibit when applied dishonestly and the failure modes the implementation does not address. The SDA on Convoking4 is one of two platform-anchored implementations of the technique on a shared methodological foundation. The Business Decision Architecture (BDA) framework, forthcoming as the companion publication, operationalizes the same technique at the organizational scale. The two implementations interlock through practitioners who move between scales: the SDA produces audited individual frames that the BDA process integrates, and the BDA produces organizational decisions that individuals execute through their own SDA practice. The paper closes with empirical research questions specific to the SDA's claims, offered to the broader research community as a structured agenda for testing what the framework asserts about artifact examinability, the Stage 1 / Stage 2 boundary, milestone inspectability, commitment accountability, and the implementation's bounded scope.

- posted: 2026-04-29

- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6595359

### abstract

Captivating and taking the lives of young, vulnerable populations is a form of AI powered by LLMs referred to as AI chatbots. While some AI chatbots are placed in the digital market under the guise of a confidant and others a helpful assistant to make one’s daily life more efficient, they come accompanied by features lurking below the surface that, for many users, blur the line between fiction and reality, fostering dependency and even leading to suicide. Especially susceptible to this consequence are youth, the majority of which have indulged in chatbot usage, as revealed by the deaths of Adam Raine and Sewell Setzer III. This comes as a result of a limited regulatory environment for AI chatbot companions that left platforms on their own to decide how to approach platform safeguards. Although a federal AI framework is nonexistent at current in part due to gridlock and the inherent barrier posed by the United Staes two party system, executive efforts show an interest in preempting state AI laws in furtherance of innovation and in particular opposition to the patchwork regulation in the states. This patchwork design, however, led to California’s Companions Chatbots Law, the first law of its kind to regulate AI chatbot based on capability, installing both proactive and reactive measures, with tailored youth protections. The solution, this comment suggests, is not what national framework should be implemented, but instead, to allow states to model their AI companion chatbot laws off of California, which they have begun to do, acting as laboratories in AI legislative efforts, and potentially providing evidence backed policies that influence a future national framework.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6675400`

- title: The Narrative Cost of Efficiency: What Gets Lost when AI Summarizes Legal Briefs?
- authors: Abigail Patthoff
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6675400
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

Judicial policies and advisory bodies are now authorizing courts to use AI tools for summarizing legal briefs, with guidelines endorsing the use of generative AI to help judges manage overwhelming caseloads. This technological shift threatens to fundamentally alter legal advocacy: AI processing often strips away narrative techniques—character development, theme, strategic organization, and vivid description—that empirical research shows persuade human judges. This Article undertakes the first exploratory study of how AI summarization transforms legal storytelling, examining what happens when an algorithmic intermediary stands between advocate and judge. Prior scholarship examines AI as a producer of legal work, yet researchers have largely ignored how AI summarization mediates human advocacy during judicial review. Through a case study of how ChatGPT processed competing legal narratives, this Article demonstrates that AI summarization can strip significant narrative architecture from advocacy in ways that existing judicial AI frameworks, which regulate for accuracy and bias, do not address. The narrative cost is not limited to judges who might rely entirely on AI summaries. Psychological research on anchoring suggests that even judges who read both a summary and the original briefs may find their understanding shaped by which aspects the AI preserved or discarded. The Article identifies open questions—from how AI summaries are actually being used in chambers to whether narrative costs fall disproportionately on certain types of litigants—and calls for the legal profession to study AI summarization’s role in judicial decision-making before it becomes an unintended barrier between the stories lawyers tell and the judges who need to hear them.
