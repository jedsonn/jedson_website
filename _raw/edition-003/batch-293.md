## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6814723`

- title: Redesigning Empathy Maps with GenAI
- authors: Tracy L. M. Norton
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6814723
- keyword hits: claude, generative ai

### abstract

Legal writing pedagogy runs on individualized feedback, and individualized feedback runs on knowing each student well enough to calibrate it. For two decades, the author collected first-day survey data and built student profiles by hand, then watched them go stale by mid-semester under the weight of a full teaching load. The tools available were sound. The time and technology to sustain them were not. This essay describes how generative AI changed that equation. It documents a redesigned intake questionnaire and a Notion-based pipeline that together transform raw survey responses into evolving student empathy maps. The redesign rests on three principles: forced ranking, behavioral and scenario questions, and indirect elicitation, each producing differentiation data as a byproduct of honest answers. The pipeline routes anonymized responses through a synthesis layer in which Claude generates narrative profiles, differentiation tags, and resource recommendations. The instructor reviews every output and refreshes each profile across the semester as conference notes and grading observations accumulate. The essay also describes a happy discovery. The author asked Claude to help automate an existing workflow. The conversation that followed surfaced a better way to gather intake data in the first place. The redesigned questionnaire produces richer data with or without the pipeline. Full deployment is planned for Fall 2026.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6834113`

- title: LEAPS: Language- and Event-Driven Task Planning for Humanoid Mobile Robots via Digital Twin-Enhanced Semantic Map
- authors: Tianyu Sheng, Zhihao Liu, Wajid  Ali Khilji, Xi  Vincent Wang, Lihui Wang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6834113
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Humanoid mobile robots deployed in shopfloor and factory environments are increasingly expected to operate and work in an integrated way with other manufacturing equipment and human workers, responding not only to explicit operator instructions but also to real-time events generated by manufacturing execution systems. Despite substantial research efforts, two challenges remain unresolved. First, existing mobile robot task planning methods lack semantic knowledge specific to real manufacturing environments, resulting in non-ideal automation performance. Second, most existing mobile robot task planning methods are incapable of real-time response to system event triggers.This paper proposes LEAPS (Language- and Event-driven Task Planning via digital twin-enhanced Semantic Map), a unified framework for humanoid mobile robot task planning in real shopfloor environments. The framework employs a digital twin model with regional semantic information to inject real-world manufacturing knowledge into a conventional geometric map, resulting in a semantic map enriched with manufacturing knowledge. A large language model (LLM) is then used to jointly process human language command inputs and real-time system status and events, enabling real-time task planning grounded in domain knowledge. Experimental results on an evaluation dataset and real-world case studies demonstrate the effectiveness of LEAPS.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6830565`

- title: Do Generative AI Tools Enhance Students’ Learning Performance or Promote Laziness and Academic Cheating?
- authors: Tin-Chun Lin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6830565
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

In this paper, we examine whether the use of artificial intelligence (AI) as a supplemental learning tool enhances students’ learning performance or promotes laziness and academic cheating. For our experiment, we separated students into two groups (control and treatment). We used two in-person ”Introduction to Microeconomics” classes taught by the same instructor (using the same textbook and teaching identical materials) as a case study. Students in the control group did not use AI, while students in the treatment group used AI as a supplemental learning tool to help them comprehend and solve homework problems. To evaluate knowledge retention, students in each group were given an unannounced, closed-book quiz repeating prior questions. Econometric analysis revealed that students in the treatment group performed significantly worse on the unannounced quiz. We also found that students who dropped 20 points or more (comparing their initial homework with the quiz) in the treatment group had a negative and statistically significant effect on quiz performance. The evidence demonstrated that students in the treatment group took advantage of AI by using AI as a shortcut for doing their homework assignments, implying that AI could promote laziness, dependency, and academic cheating.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6829430`

- title: More than Grading: An Agentic RAG System for Uncovering the Conceptual and Thematic Landscape of Student Reasoning
- authors: Ai  Quan Nguyen, Ruizhi Yu, Han-fen Hu, Rajiv Kishore
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6829430
- keyword hits: agentic, retrieval-augmented

### abstract

This study addresses fundamental limitations in automated essay scoring (AES), particularly the inability to fairly evaluate conceptually valid but non-standard student responses. Grounded in design science research, we develop an Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)-based AES system that identifies and evaluates latent conceptual topics. Drawing on the Hybrid Convergence Zone (HCZ) model, the system captures multiple cognitive levels of student reasoning. The proposed architecture integrates three coordinated agents, Extractor, Classifier, and Aggregator, to perform multi-stage semantic analysis, classification, and conceptual integration. Empirical evaluation using student answers demonstrates strong performance in relevance classification and consistent system behavior across contexts. A real-world evaluation with instructors further confirms high information quality, usefulness, and user satisfaction. The findings demonstrate that the system improves fairness by recognizing diverse forms of conceptual expression and extends AES from a scoring tool to a decision support system for instructional insight. By uncovering latent topics and providing transparent explanations, the system supports both assessment and pedagogical refinement. This research contributes a cognitively grounded, interpretable AES framework and highlights the value of human-AI collaboration in evaluating complex open-ended responses.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6749961`

- title: The LOGOS Framework: A Five-Level Taxonomy of Human Cognitive Agency in AI-Assisted Assessment
- authors: Vinicius Covas
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6749961
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid adoption of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) in higher education has produced a critical governance gap. While 95% of university students globally report using AI in assessed work (HEPI & Kortext, 2026), fewer than 20% of institutions have formal AI policies (Coursera, 2026). In Mexico, the ANUIES-OIIAES National Survey confirms that more than 80% of students use GenAI while 76% of public-university faculty report no institutional framework (SEP & ANUIES-OIIAES, 2025). Existing assessment frameworks-Finland's ARENE Traffic Light (2023) and the AI Assessment Scale (Perkins et al., 2024)-address permission levels but do not account for the qualitative nature of human cognitive engagement during AI-assisted tasks. This paper introduces the LOGOS Framework, a five-level taxonomy of human cognitive agency developed at Universidad Anáhuac México and grounded in a humanist account of the person, reason, and cognitive agency. The five levels-Lone, Operated, Guided, Orchestrated, and Systemic-describe qualitatively distinct modes of cognitive relationship between the student and AI, ranging from full autonomy to critical systemic analysis of AI as object of study. Each level is grounded in canonical traditions: Vygotskian mediation, distributed cognition (Hutchins), Bakhtinian dialogism, metacognition (Flavell), computational thinking (Wing), and critical algorithm studies. The framework operationalizes the Vatican's Antiqua et nova (2025) directive that AI must complement rather than replace human intelligence. Through theoretical synthesis, the paper introduces a new unit of analysis-cognitive agency-distinct from the permission logic The LOGOS Framework 2 of ARENE and the presence scale of AIAS, and contributes the original Systemic level, where AI becomes the object of academic study. LOGOS offers one of the first AI assessment frameworks for Latin American higher education grounded in a humanist account of cognitive agency, developed within the humanist Christian tradition of Universidad Anáhuac México but portable across secular, public, private, and faith-based institutional contexts, with concrete applications across Communication Studies and a roadmap for empirical validation in 2026.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6749083`

- title: Code Translation Agent-as-a-Judge with AgentBeats
- authors: Samer Attrah
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6749083
- keyword hits: agentic, ai agent, gemini

### abstract

The landscape of model evaluation continuously shifts as AI systems grow in capability and complexity. Recently, AI agents have become the dominant paradigm for building efficient applications and tools. In this work, we adopt the Agentic Agent Assessment (AAA) paradigm, where evaluation itself is treated as an autonomous agentic task. Because an agent is a highly capable, tool-augmented model, it can serve as an expert assessor for other systems, models, and agents. We present the Code Translator Agent Judge, a specialized Green Agent that evaluates participant (Purple) agents on their ability to translate source code between programming languages while preserving functionality and adopting target-language idioms. The evaluator is built on the Agent-to-Agent (A2A) protocol and powered by Google Gemini in structured JSON mode. A dedicated leaderboard was created alongside four custom metrics grounded in software engineering literature — Execution Correctness, Style, Conciseness, and Relevance. Experimental results across Python-to-JavaScript translation tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the AAA paradigm, showing high correlation with human-level stylistic judgements. The evaluator agent is available at: https://github.com/Samir-atra/code_translator_green_agent

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6749643`

- title: The Institutional Origins of AI: Evidence from Large Language Models
- authors: Purushothaman Padmanabhan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6749643
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Organizations adopt artificial intelligence on cost and capability. They do not ask what else travels with the efficiency. This paper argues that institutional logics are embedded in AI systems through the production process itself-carried into every organization that adopts them, whether recognized or not. We develop a production-position framework identifying eight dimensions along which AI producers vary: organizational type, regulatory context, training data, human feedback labor, evaluation regimes, market positioning, funding structure, and distillation lineage. Drawing on institutional carrier theory (Scott, 2003) and epistemic cultures (Knorr-Cetina, 1999), we theorize AI as a new class of institutional carrier-one that transports institutional dispositions without the communities of practice that produced them. Two empirical studies test this framework. Study 1 administers 25 organizational decision scenarios to five frontier models from four institutional origins (3,125 observations): models diverge systematically on 68% of scenarios while maintaining 91.4% within-model consistency. Study 2 administers six open-ended knowledge-production tasks to nine frontier models from four institutional contexts (162 outputs): institutional signatures are detectable at the embedding level but not at the surface, manifesting as divergent epistemic commitments about what constitutes evidence, explanation, and legitimate inquiry. When knowledge workers collaborate with different AI, they encounter different epistemic cultures-not because of capability differences but because of production-origin differences. The configuration of production dimensions predicts behavioral similarity better than any single dimension-including geographic origin. AI selection is institutional selection. "Does it work?" must be joined by "whose world travels with it?

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6782298`

- title: Leadership Behaviors and Employee Engagement During Generative AI Adoption in Engineering Organizations: Applying the LAWS-AI Framework
- authors: Sunil Manohar
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6782298
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Engineering organizations invest heavily in Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) yet frequently fail to realize full value because workforce engagement weakens during transformation. This paper proposes the Leadership-Alignment-Well-being-Sustainability for Artificial Intelligence (LAWS-AI) framework to explain how leadership behaviors shape employee engagement during GenAI adoption. Three leadership theories are integrated through explicit, nonoverlapping pillar assignments: transformational leadership anchors the Leadership pillar (L), addressing motivational and identity dimensions; Situational Leadership Theory anchors the Alignment pillar (A), addressing developmental calibration; and adaptive leadership anchors the Well-being pillar (W), addressing identity renegotiation under uncertainty. The Job Demands-Resources model anchors the Sustainability pillar (S) and specifies downstream outcomes. Four empirically testable hypotheses trace a causal chain from leadership input through engagement to organizational performance. A mixed-methods sequential explanatory design specifies Phase 1 interviews (n = 15-25) to inform Phase 2 survey administration (n ≥ 300), with the survey analyzed through structural equation modeling. The reflection documents three genuine intellectual shifts and identifies three priority gaps for future inquiry.
