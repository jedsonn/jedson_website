## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6827658`

- title: Evolving and Detecting Multi-Turn Deception using Geometric Signatures
- authors: Surender Suresh Kumar, Mary  L. Cummings
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6827658
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Safety defenses for large language models (LLMs) are typically trained and evaluated on single-turn prompts, yet real attacks often unfold as indirect, multi-turn probing. To defend against this more nuanced form of deception, we present a unified pipeline that generates realistic multi-turn deceptive question sets via multi-objective genetic prompt optimization with co-evolving mutation operators. We validate this dataset through a human study, which also revealed that early generations yielded the most convincing deception and practical constraints such as adherence filtering and ordering effects. Using this data, we were able to detect deceptive attempts to access prohibited information using simple, explainable geometric signals in embedding space coupled with a lightweight feed-forward classifier. Three geometric features (angular coverage, distance ratio, and linearity) augmented with pairwise similarity statistics led to a compact predictive model that achieved consistently high recall (0.89) across base, reworded, and truncated (three-turn) scenarios, with test-time F1 ranging from 0.74–0.86. The results support a central hypothesis that multi-turn deceptive intent leaves a stable geometric footprint that enables lightweight, transparent screening without expensive end-to-end training. We further discuss responsible uses, limitations, and paths toward larger, more diverse human-evaluated datasets. The primary contribution to artificial intelligence is the multi-objective evolutionary framework for prompt generation, and the engineering application is the deployment of a lightweight geometric detection system for LLM safety infrastructure.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6740818`

- title: Why LLMs Can Read Milton but Not Weep The Categorical Agency Wall in Frontier Language Model Systems
- authors: William C. Houze
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-25
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6740818
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

This essay argues for the categorical agency wall: the foundational ontological boundary in every operational large language model that separates the engineered substrate of voltage states and binary logic from the apparent agentive surface of textual output. Drawing on the architectural lineage of project-management discipline, philosophical anatomy in the Aristotelian tradition, and literary witness from Mary Shelley's Frankenstein, Milton's Paradise Lost, and the Icarian myth, the essay demonstrates that no engineered ingredient between the substrate and the surface introduces agency, intentionality, or telos. The wall is exhibited through a compressed dissection of the LLM Machina Corpus, a no-remainder argument applied to a single LLM-generated specimen sentence, and a structural comparison between the biological alpha — the union of ovum and spermatozoon at conception — and the machine alpha — the binary pair {0, 1} as instantiated in voltage states across logic gates. The no-remainder analysis is further extended to the characteristic pathologies of each substrate — off-distribution generation (loosely called hallucination) on the silicon side, cellular abnormality (paradigmatically cancer) on the biological side — exhibiting that the wall determines not only proper functioning but also the remainder-status of failure: the engineered pathology is no-remainder, traceable end-to-end in the manner of Ishikawa fishbone analytics; the biological pathology is remainder, retaining even after centuries of inquiry a residue that medical research has not exhausted. The essay engages the strongest philosophical objections to the wall: substrate-independence, functionalism, and emergence-at-scale. It then addresses the cultural mythology that obstructs recognition of the wall — the contemporary Shelleyan myth-making that grants frontier LLMs the inner life Shelley grants her creature in the world of her novel, and the Kubrickian cinematic dramatization of the same projection in HAL 9000 — and the Icarian mythology of soaring and falling that animates both the AI-utopian and AI-doomer narratives. The essay closes with the Aristotelian recovery: phronesis, telos, and eudaimonia are categories that exist above the wall and have no purchase below it. The artifact is a utility infielder of immense practical value, not a home-run hitter of cognitive agency. The wall holds. The argument distilled in this essay is developed at full scale in the author's book in progress, Everyman's LLM Baedeker.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6750700`

- title: From Erasure to Obfuscation: A Paradigm Shift in Privacy in the Age of Generative AI
- authors: Yutong Wu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6750700
- keyword hits: generative ai, large language model, large language models

### abstract

Privacy governance after the internet has been organised around erasure—Article 17 of the GDPR, Article 47 of China's PIPL, and parallel provisions elsewhere. Generative AI disturbs the premise on which that paradigm rests. As large language models increasingly mediate how individuals are publicly known, the operative threat shifts from data being recorded to data being automatically integrated into stable profiles. I argue that erasure, while still important, no longer exhausts the field of privacy protection: a second strategy is needed, one that weakens profile stability through the deliberate production of internal inconsistency in self-representation. I call this strategy narrative autonomy and distinguish it from five adjacent concepts—Goffmanian self-presentation, context collapse, online obscurity, contextual integrity, and obfuscation in the Brunton–Nissenbaum sense. What it adds is a target: not data collection or audience management, but the integration step itself. The essay closes with a speculative observation that model collapse may unintentionally support this kind of privacy interest. The contribution is conceptual.

### abstract

Background: Artificial intelligence (AI), particularly natural language processing (NLP) and deep learning, is increasingly used in education. However, its application to educational content generation in low-resource contexts remains insufficiently studied, especially in settings characterized by data scarcity, lowresource languages, and limited computational infrastructure, such as Madagascar. Objective: This systematic review analyzes existing research on AI-based educational content in low-resource contexts, with a focus on Madagascar, in order to identify dominant approaches, application domains, limitations, and research gaps under realworld constraints. Methods: Following PRISMA guidelines, studies published between 2015 and 2025 were retrieved from major scientific databases. including IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, SpringerLink, Elsevier, and arXiv. Selected works focus on AIdriven educational content creation, adaptation, or personalization in low-resource environments. Lightweight models, prompt-based learning, black-box LLMs, and human-in-theloop strategies emerge as promising directions. Conclusions: This review reveals a clear gap in AI solutions tailored to low-resource educational contexts. It highlights the need for frugal, adaptable, and pedagogically grounded AI approaches, positioning Madagascar as a representative case for future research on inclusive and scalable AI-driven education.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6837689`

- title: Addressing Data Imbalance in Low-resource Multilingual Large Language Models via Personalized Curriculum Learning
- authors: Yu Weng, jiaxing zhang, Jiu Sha, Xingyu Wang, Zheng Liu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6837689
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

The equitable deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) is severely constrained by the imbalanced distribution of multilingual corpora. While existing mitigation strategies attempt to address data scarcity, they primarily increase the sampling probability or loss weighting of low-resource languages, which often induces cross-lingual interference and leads to catastrophic forgetting. To address these limitations, we propose Personalized Curriculum Learning (PCL), a unified curriculum-driven training framework that explicitly decouples multilingual alignment from downstream task learning. PCL comprises two key components: a Dynamic Loss Scheduler (DLS) and an Adaptive Sampling Scheduler (ASS). The DLS aligns each language within the embedding space via contrastive learning, enhancing cross-lingual representation disentanglement while progressively shifting the optimization objective toward downstream tasks. To further mitigate the interference of high-resource languages on low-resource ones, the ASS gradually reduces the sampling proportion of high-resource languages, thereby allocating more training capacity to low-resource languages. Extensive experiments demonstrate that PCL can be broadly applied to multilingual training in extremely low-resource scenarios, consistently outperforming state-of-the-art methods.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6837695`

- title: An Agentic Artificial Intelligence Framework for Weekly Stock Portfolio Selection in the United States Technology Sector
- authors: Kunakorn Pruksakorn, Ekarat Rattagan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6837695
- keyword hits: agentic, foundation model, llama, prompting

### abstract

Automating weekly portfolio selection in fast-moving equity markets remains difficult because signals from price action, news flow, liquidity conditions, and risk must be converted into disciplined allocation decisions under tight time constraints. This study proposes an agentic artificial intelligence (AI) framework for weekly portfolio selection in United States (U.S.) technology equities. The framework coordinates five role-specialized agents—Momentum, Catalyst, Technical, Liquidity, and Risk—using zero-shot prompting with a single foundation model, Llama 3.3 70B, to evaluate a fixed universe of 90 highly liquid, predominantly NASDAQ-listed U.S. technology stocks within a recurring one-week trading cycle. Recent AI-based studies in finance often rely primarily on backtesting, use communication-heavy multi-agent debate protocols, provide limited interpretability, or stop at stock ranking rather than generating fully specified portfolios for short-horizon trading. To address these limitations, the proposed framework uses LangGraph to orchestrate a transparent score-based synthesis process that converts independent agent assessments into an actionable weekly portfolio recommendation under live market conditions. Over a nine-week live evaluation period from August to October 2025, the framework achieved an average weekly return of 3.28%, a cumulative return of 32.99%, and an annualized Sharpe ratio of 6.05. Over the same window, the resulting portfolio outperformed the NASDAQ-100, S&P 500, and XLK benchmarks on a cumulative-return basis. These results provide early live-market evidence that a score-based agentic framework can support practical and interpretable short-horizon portfolio construction, while also motivating longer evaluation horizons and broader robustness testing.

## uid: `arxiv:2605.28359v1`

- title: From Knowing to Doing: A Memory-Controlled Benchmark for LLM Trading Agents on Stock Markets
- authors: Taojie Zhu, Wentao Zhao, Rui Sun, Beidi Luan, Jiacheng Lu, Sinuo Wang, Jing Li, Daxin Jiang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-27
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28359v1
- keyword hits: large language model, llm, llms

### abstract

Evaluating whether large language model (LLM) agents can profit in capital markets is increasingly framed as end-to-end trading: place an agent in a historical market, let it trade, and measure portfolio returns. This setup is vulnerable to two evaluation failures. First, long backtests often overlap with the knowledge cutoffs of frontier LLMs, allowing memorized tickers, dates, prices, and market narratives to substitute for investment reasoning. Second, raw returns are a noisy proxy for stock-selection ability, since positive performance may come from market beta, style exposure, or favorable regimes rather than genuine alpha. We introduce KTD-Fin (Knowing-To-Doing Financial Benchmark), an end-to-end stock-market trading benchmark that addresses both issues. KTD-Fin uses a data-side masking protocol to anonymize key identifiers and calendar information consistently across prompts and tools, separating historical market memory from investment decision-making. It also incorporates a Barra-style performance attribution framework that decomposes portfolio returns into market, style, and stock-selection alpha components. Across ten frontier LLM agents evaluated on the Chinese CSI300 over a 2024--2026 window, masking substantially changes agent rationales, pushing them towards anonymized factor-based reasoning. Attribution analysis further shows that LLM agents' cumulative returns under leakage-controlled evaluation are largely explained by passive market and style exposure, with limited evidence of persistent stock-selection alpha. These findings suggest that financial LLM benchmarks should evaluate not only whether an agent makes money, but also whether the source of returns reflects transferable investment skill. We release KTD-Fin as a reproducible template for leakage-controlled and attribution-aware evaluation of LLM trading agents.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6831480`

- title: Equity Considerations in AI-Enabled Conversational Systems: A Cross-Sectional Comparative Study of Clinical Responses
- authors: Lysanne Veerle Michels, Yousef Yousef, Heidi Smith, Hajira Dambha-Miller
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6831480
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, deepseek, gemini

### abstract

Background: Artificial intelligence (AI)-enabled conversational tools are increasingly used for health information and clinical decision support. Concerns persist regarding systematic bias, limited transparency, and the potential to exacerbate health inequity in real-world use. Empirical evaluation of how these tools operationalise equity-relevant factors in clinical responses remains limited. We aimed to compare the inclusion of equity considerations across widely used AI tools in response to standardised health queries. Methods: In this cross-sectional comparative study, we developed clinical and health information queries reflecting patient and clinician perspectives across common scenarios. Responses were generated using multiple widely used AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, DeepSeek, Doubao, Gemini, and Grok). Queries were standardised and geolocation controlled to minimise localisation effects. Responses were assessed using a structured framework with inclusion of equity-relevant factors (age, sex or gender, ethnicity, socioeconomic context, and access to care), clarity of clinical guidance, and specification of non-diagnostic outputs. Findings: Substantial heterogeneity was observed. Grok demonstrated the highest inclusion of equity-relevant factors, followed by Claude and DeepSeek, whereas Doubao showed minimal inclusion. ChatGPT, Copilot, and Gemini showed intermediate performance. Age was referenced in over half of responses, whereas sex or gender and ethnicity were included in fewer than half. Socioeconomic context and access to care were infrequently represented. Although all AI tools provided clinical guidance, fewer than half explicitly specified outputs as non-diagnostic. Patterns of equity inclusion varied by geographic context across tools. Interpretation: AI tools demonstrate inconsistent and limited operationalisation of equity-relevant factors, with implications for clinical safety and differential performance across populations. Strengthening transparency requirements, embedding standardised equity evaluation frameworks, and aligning regulatory oversight with real-world deployment are essential to ensure these technologies mitigate rather than exacerbate health inequity.
