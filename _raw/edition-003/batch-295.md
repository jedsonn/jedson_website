## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6821959`

- title: The Crisis of Information Verification: The Hand, the Network, and the Problem of Knowledge Grounding
- authors: Oleg Nikolaev
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6821959
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

This paper investigates the escalating crisis of information verification within the framework of a materialist ontology of distributed cognition. It argues that human epistemic evolution has relied on a complex of physical, logical, and institutional filters, originally rooted in the sensorimotor grounding provided by the functionally emancipated hand. As cognitive externalization advanced, the rapid increase in information speed progressively outpaced the capacity of these traditional verification mechanisms. The paper introduces the concept of semantic entropy—a heuristic indicator of the growing proportion of unverified statements—and shows how its accumulation drives the cascading degradation of knowledge during iterative transmission. The current semantic stage of externalization, driven by large language models, is shown to generate a massive volume of plausible but ungrounded content. The contemporary crisis of knowledge is thus framed as a systemic breakdown of feedback mechanisms in an era of autonomous semantic generation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6830134`

- title: LANMA: LLM-driven Animal Nutrition Meta-Analysis Workflow — From Nutritional Factors to Meat Quality Outcomes
- authors: Yizhen Wang, Lexuan Shi, Jie Chen, Bocheng Xu, Zhixin Yu, Zhixuan Zhang, Yanzhe Zhang, Yijia Xi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6830134
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Animal nutrition research is characterized by numerous ingredients and highly fragmented evidence, making meta-analysis essential for deriving integrative, production-relevant insights across studies. However, existing meta-analyses are manual, narrowly scoped to single feed ingredients, inefficient, and lack an LLM-driven workflow capable of enabling scalable, multidimensional synthesis. Here, we aim to develop a large language model–driven workflow tailored to animal nutrition (LANMA), enabling a comprehensive and systematic analysis of the effects of common feed additives on pork quality. From nearly two decades of PubMed and Web of Science records, we retrieved 19,594 entries, which were screened within three hours using LANMA, yielding 142 eligible studies comprising 2,893 comparisons for data extraction. These data encompassed seven categories of feed additives and five pork quality outcomes. Among the seven additive categories, plant extracts exhibited the broadest efficacy, significantly improving all five outcomes, with strong effects on drip loss, shear force, and marbling. Protein and energy feeds improved three eating quality–related traits, whereas methyl donor additives and fermented feed enhanced two outcomes spanning eating quality and appearance. Trace Minerals and vitamins showed limited, single-outcome benefits, while fatty acids and probiotics were ineffective. LANMA enables a panoramic understanding of animal nutrition by integrating multi-ingredient, multi-outcome evidence, thereby accelerating field-wide consensus on the effects of nutritional interventions.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6789658`

- title: Token Leverage: A Framework for AI Inside the Firm
- authors: Cayman Seagraves, Stace Sirmans
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6789658
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

Studies of generative AI at work find large gains in some settings, no effect in others, and losses in others. The pattern is less puzzling if the treatment is not access or adoption, but priced inference chosen for a worker-task pair. We call this margin token leverage, billed AI inference spend per dollar of task-allocated labor cost. Inference creates value only when the task lies within the model's frontier, the worker can orchestrate and audit output, the workflow supplies useful context, governance catches errors, and workers know when to abstain. Two measurement results follow. Under voluntary firm choice, positive billed spend, effective-cost dominance, and correct firm expectations, billed token leverage is a revealed willingness-to-pay lower bound on expected AI-created operating value per task labor dollar. Access and adoption estimates instead mix chosen use, optimal abstention, forced overuse, and constrained underuse, so their sign can be positive, zero, or negative even when chosen use is valuable. AI productivity research should therefore measure worker-task inference spend rather than stopping at whether workers had or used AI.

- title: From Distributed Localized AI-Job Crafting to Strategic Meta-Work: RedesigningHR Governance Role in Enterprise-Wide Gen AI Integration and a Research Agenda
- authors: Hongyu Wu, Louise Harder Fischer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6843683
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

The enterprise-wide integration of generative AI (Gen AI) has produced a governance misalignment: employees are independently adapting their workflows through AI-job crafting, yet organizations have no dedicated mechanism to translate these dispersed adaptations into coherent governance architecture. We term this gap the meta-work gap and define meta-work as the second-order HR activity of systematically detecting, interpreting, and institutionalizing employees’ localized AI work adaptations at the organizational level. Drawing on job crafting theory, sociotechnical systems theory, and strategic HRM, we develop the HR Meta-Work Framework, which reconceptualizes HR’s governance role across a three-configuration typology. We advance five falsifiable propositions specifying the conditions under which HR governance authority acquires legitimacy in Gen AI integration. This framework identifies second-order governance learning as an organizational design imperative and establishes a research agenda at the intersection of Gen AI adoption and human capability development.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6845716`

- title: AI Whispers, Students Edit: Tracking Emirati Learners' Revision Journeys
- authors: David Berry
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6845716
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai

### abstract

This study examines the effects of AI-assisted writing feedback on the development of autonomous editing skills among undergraduate students in an English for Academic Purposes (EAP) course at an English-medium university in the **** (***). Prior research has documented the short-term benefits of generative AI tools such as ChatGPT for improving surface-level writing quality, but less is known about whether sustained use supports the internalization of revision strategies or fosters reliance on external feedback.,Using a quasi-experimental design, the study compared two intact classes over a 16-week semester: an experimental group receiving AI-assisted feedback and a control group receiving structured peer feedback. Both groups completed iterative writing tasks aligned with course assessments. A central feature of the design was a feedback fade-out phase in the final weeks, during which external support was progressively withdrawn to assess independent revision performance. Student perceptions of feedback were also examined through post-course questionnaires.,Findings indicate that AI-assisted feedback supported more rapid gains in surface-level writing features, while peer feedback was associated with more balanced development and stronger performance during independent revision. These results suggest that AI feedback functions most effectively when integrated as a scaffold that is gradually withdrawn, rather than as a continuous external support.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6789922`

- title: The Constraint Cascade: A Diagnostic Taxonomy for AI Governance Failure
- authors: Yizi Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6789922
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

AI governance lacks a diagnostic vocabulary for locating failure within the deployment architecture. When a language-model agent produces harm, existing frameworks ask whether the model or the user is at fault-a binary that collapses four structurally distinct layers of constraint into an uninformative dyad. We introduce the Constraint Cascade, a four-layer diagnostic taxonomy that partitions LLM deployment into Environment (L1), Interface (L2), Algorithm & Weights (L3), and Strategic Authority (L4). Between L2 and L3 lies an epistemic boundary: Dual Certificates Zhang [2026] proves mathematically that output-only observation cannot recover internal state, establishing that the governance reviewer sees only what crosses this boundary, not the architecture that produced it. Rather than treating safety operations as a separate layer, the Cascade models them as distributed system properties maintained by human labor at "friction seams" between layers, where misalignment costs are absorbed along strict power gradients. Three cross-layer failure patterns-Override, Contamination, and Compression-are defined and illustrated across four real-world cases spanning the complete mens rea spectrum. The Cascade provides governance institutions with a structural vocabulary for diagnostic auditing, separating the question of which layer failed from the downstream legal question of who bears responsibility.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6792161`

- title: Sequential Structure in Intraday Futures Data: LSTM vs Gradient Boosting on MNQ
- authors: Mathias Mesfin
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6792161
- keyword hits: foundation model

### abstract

This paper compares two machine learning architectures for intraday directional prediction in Micro E-Mini Nasdaq 100 futures (MNQ): a gradient boosting classifier operating on engineered daily and intraday features, and a long short-term memory (LSTM) recurrent neural network processing raw five-minute bar return sequences. The comparison is motivated by recent foundation model research in financial candlestick data, including the Kronos architecture (Shi et al., 2025), which demonstrated that sequential bar-level structure contains predictive information when processed at scale. We implement a tractable approximation of this architecture adapted for a dataset of 944 trading days spanning 2021 through 2025. Four model configurations are evaluated under strict walk-forward validation with expanding training windows across three out-of-sample test periods (2023, 2024, 2025). The target variable is binary: whether the session close exceeds the 10:30 AM bar open by more than ten points. All models are evaluated against a 51.8% base rate. No configuration produces out-of-sample accuracy materially above the base rate. Combined out-of-sample accuracies range from 50.00% to 50.89% across all gradient boosting variants. The LSTM produces a combined OOS accuracy of 50.59%. Permutation test p-values are 0.135 for the best gradient boosting configuration and 0.515 for the LSTM, both failing the p < 0.05 significance threshold. Feature importance analysis across walk-forward folds reveals systematic instability: the features dominating each fold differ substantially, confirming noise fitting rather than consistent structural signal capture. We conclude that five-minute OHLCV bar sequences do not contain sufficient sequential predictive structure for session-level directional forecasting at this dataset size and resolution. The primary contribution is a documented implementation and honest evaluation of a Kronos-inspired architecture on a constrained real-world dataset, contributing an empirical lower bound on the dataset scale requirements for sequential ML-based intraday forecasting. A structural interpretation is offered: the sample size required for transformer-scale architectures to learn non-trivial financial patterns is orders of magnitude larger than what is available in four years of single-instrument intraday data.
