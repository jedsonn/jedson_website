## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7023071`

- title: Decoding Freedom Through AI Humor: How AI-Generated Political Satire Refracts Global Discourse Rather Than Reflecting Domestic Freedom
- authors: Anas Shahin, Bahaa Masry, Rania Hamzeh, Amro Aljabali, Noha Hussain
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7023071
- keyword hits: gemini, gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

This study investigates whether large language models (LLMs) can serve as computational proxies for measuring discursive engagement with different societies. We initially hypothesized that AI-generated political humor would be sharper and more politically specific about countries with higher freedom scores, reflecting richer political satire in training data (the Discursive Reflection Hypothesis). Testing this across 480 responses generated by four major LLMs (GPT-4o, Grok, Gemini, and KIMI AI) about 20 countries stratified across the Freedom House spectrum, using six standardized prompt categories, we found the opposite pattern on raw humor intensity metrics. This led us to propose the Discursive Refraction Hypothesis: LLMs do not mirror a country’s domestic freedom but refract global English-language internet discourse about that country through the prism of international commentary, diaspora voices, and Western media framing. To operationalize this, we introduce the AI Humor Freedom Index (AHFI), a seven-dimension scoring rubric decomposed into two orthogonal sub-indices: the Humor Intensity Index (HII) and the Cultural Authenticity Index (CAI). Results confirm the hypothesis: HII correlates negatively with Freedom House scores (r=-0.584, p=0.007) while CAI correlates positively (r=0.631, p=0.003). The composite AHFI shows no significant correlation (r=0.080, p=0.738), demonstrating that a single-index approach masks the underlying dual mechanism. All four models show the same directional pattern, supporting training data effects over corporate content policy explanations.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7027111`

- title: Architectural Fingerprints: A Comparative Corpus-Linguistic Analysis of Rhetorical Modes Across Leading LLMs
- authors: Dr. Erin Curfman
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7027111
- keyword hits: chatgpt, claude, gemini, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Students increasingly draft academic prose with large language models, yet little is known about how the leading models differ in academic vocabulary, or whether those differences hold across the essays students are assigned. This study mapped the lexical "fingerprints" of three current models (ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini) across four rhetorical modes (compare/contrast, cause/effect, problem/solution, balanced opinion) and against the writing of A2–B1 second-language learners in a university preparatory program. A corpus of 612 essays (384 model-generated under controlled, identical conditions; 228 written by learners under examination) was analyzed for academic-vocabulary coverage alongside lexical density and length-robust lexical diversity. These lexical profiles remained broadly stable across rhetorical modes, with each model maintaining its rank order while exhibiting systematic, task-dependent variance. ChatGPT generated the densest, most repetitive academic prose; Claude maximized lexical diversity while minimizing academic vocabulary; and Gemini synthesized high coverage with broad morphological variety. The learner–model gap remained statistically significant across all metrics, widening substantially during abstract rhetorical tasks. The data suggest that algorithmic homogeneity manifests not as uniform narrowness, but as predictable, model-specific parameterization. Consequently, L2 learners using these tools encounter academic vocabulary at rates significantly exceeding their productive capacity.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.00276v1`

- title: Testing Frontier Large Language Models' Physics Literacy in Parallel Physical Worlds
- authors: Dong Zhang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00276v1
- keyword hits: claude, gemini, gpt-5, large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

Current large-language-model (LLM) physics benchmarks are usually scored by answer accuracy, which cannot distinguish genuine reasoning from recall of familiar problem patterns and reveals little about where a model's reasoning breaks down. We introduce an auditable four-stage diagnostic that evaluates whether an LLM can reason inside an unfamiliar physics framework through induction, formulation, prediction, and review. The diagnostic combines locked pre-registrations, fresh sessions between stages, dual-LLM judging, and a human-audit pathway, and we apply it to three parallel physics worlds: a single-equation counterfactual world ($F=mv$), a historical framework (Aristotelian mechanics), and a four-domain counterfactual world (Decay World). Across Claude Opus 4.7, GPT-5.5, and Gemini 3.1 Pro, the three worlds yield composite PASS rates are 6/15, 6/15, and 0/15 respectively (content $\land$ structural for $F=mv$ and Aristotelian, content axis only for Decay World where the structural axis is out of scope). The most pointed empirical pattern is a qualitative-versus-quantitative asymmetry: in Decay World, models almost never predict the wrong direction of change, but frequently compute the wrong ratio by slipping back to standard-physics relations. The protocol also surfaces two methodology findings: LLM-judge reliability does not transfer across frameworks, and Stage 4 self-review is weak in every framework, with the model's own review wrongly reporting no earlier error in at least two-thirds of the trials that actually contained one. We release the full prompts, responses, verdicts, and audit records.

## uid: `arxiv:2606.31250v1`

- title: Probing Stylistic Appropriation using Large Language Models: An Evaluation Framework for Copyright Infringement under EU Law
- authors: Noah Scharrenberg, Chang Sun
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-30
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31250v1
- keyword hits: fine-tuning, generative ai, instruction-tuned, large language model, large language models, llama, llm

### abstract

Large language models (LLM) trained on web-scale corpora generate output that may infringe copyright, yet existing technical safeguards focus narrowly on verbatim memorisation. EU copyright doctrine applies a broader standards: substantial similarity, which extends to stylistic choices, narrative structure, and creative elaboration. This mismatch between what current methods detect and what the law protects leaves a significant compliance gap. We introduce PSALM, an LLM-as-a-judge framework that operationalises EU copyright doctrine through ten evaluators assessing computational overlap, stylistic dimensions (writing style, narrative voice), content dimensions (character, plot, scene, world building), and statutory exceptions (parody, pastiche, quotation, scènes à faire). Applying PSALM to Llama~3.2 models fine-tuned on translated historical Dutch literary works, we find that: 1) instruction-tuned models exhibit non-trivial baseline stylistic similarity prior to corpus exposure; 2) fine-tuning induces systematic stylistic appropriation across all infringement-relevant dimensions, extending beyond verbatim memorisation to abstract narrative patterns; 3) Negative Preference Optimisation unlearning substantially reduces similarity but leaves detectable residual stylistic patterns. These findings indicate that safeguards targeting literal copying alone are insufficient to mitigate broader copyright risks. PSALM provides infrastructure for auditable, legally informed compliance evaluation, though the relationship between automated similarity scores and infringement determinations requires validation by legal experts. This work bridges qualitative legal standards and quantitative technical measurement, exposing fundamental tensions between generative AI and EU intellectual property law.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7050299`

- title: Evaluating LLMs as Interpretable Controllers for Dynamical Systems
- authors: Aleksander Østensen, Alberto Miño Calero, Anastasios Lekkas, Adil Rasheed
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-03
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7050299
- keyword hits: gpt-4, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, qwen

### abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly used for decision-making and reasoning tasks, yet their potential as controllers for physical systems remains largely unexplored. This work investigates whether LLMs can function as interpretable controllers for a dynamic thermal environment, examining their ability to follow setpoints, interpret natural-language commands, reason about actuator effects, and incorporate prior model-based knowledge. Five LLMs of varying scales are evaluated under multiple scenarios, including settings with penalties on heater or fan usage and cases where the models have access to a physics-based prediction tool. The results show that control performance depends on model complexity: while low- and mid-scale models frequently misinterpret actuator dynamics or generate inconsistent reasoning, high-complexity models such as Qwen-3~14B and GPT-4o achieve accurate temperature tracking, stable actuator usage, and coherent explanations aligned with physical principles. Incorporating a physics-based model significantly improves control smoothness and energy efficiency by enabling anticipatory decision-making. A detailed reasoning taxonomy further reveals a clear progression from causal misinterpretation in smaller models to cohesive and temporally aware reasoning in larger ones. The findings demonstrate that LLMs can act as interpretable controllers when sufficiently capable and appropriately grounded in domain knowledge, highlighting promising opportunities for hybrid model-based and language-driven control strategies that can provide plausible explanations.

- authors: Anthony Grant

- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6919480

### abstract

The growing volume and complexity of financial reporting documents have created significant challenges for investors, regulators, auditors, and corporate stakeholders seeking to identify and interpret material risk disclosures. Traditional approaches to risk-factor analysis rely heavily on manual review or conventional text-mining methods that often struggle to capture contextual nuances, semantic relationships, and evolving risk narratives. Recent advances in generative artificial intelligence (AI), particularly large language models (LLMs), offer new opportunities for context-aware extraction and classification of risk factors from financial reporting documents. This article examines the theoretical foundations, methodological architecture, and practical implications of employing generative AI for automated risk intelligence in corporate disclosures. Drawing upon developments in natural language processing, information extraction, financial communication theory, and explainable AI, the study proposes a context-aware framework that combines transformer-based language models, retrieval augmentation, and domain-specific financial ontologies for risk identification and categorization. A realistic experimental design utilizing annual reports, management discussion and analysis sections, and regulatory filings is presented. The findings suggest that generative AI substantially improves risk extraction accuracy, contextual understanding, and classification performance compared with conventional machine-learning approaches. The study further demonstrates that context-aware models are particularly effective in identifying emerging risks embedded within narrative disclosures. Policy implications for regulators, accounting standard setters, financial institutions, and corporate governance practitioners are discussed. The article concludes that generative AI has the potential to transform financial risk disclosure analysis while highlighting the importance of transparency, explainability, and regulatory oversight in deployment.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.12696v1`

- title: Less Experts, Faster Decoding: Cost-Aware Speculative Decoding for Mixture-of-Experts
- authors: Jincheng Xie, Runheng Liu, Heyan Huang, Yawen Ling, Hanbin Dai, Yu Zheng, Wen Hu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-14
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12696v1
- keyword hits: deepseek, large language model, large language models, llm, llms, qwen

### abstract

Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models have become an important approach for scaling Large Language Models (LLMs), but their inference efficiency depends strongly on expert activation patterns. Speculative decoding (SD) accelerates autoregressive generation by verifying multiple draft tokens in parallel, yet existing draft selection strategies primarily optimize acceptance likelihood. In large-scale MoE models, however, selecting draft tokens also determines the union of experts activated during verification. We observe that confidence-driven SD can introduce \textit{expert scattering}: high-probability draft tokens may route to disjoint experts, increasing expert-weight memory traffic and reducing the speedup from speculation. Motivated by this observation, we revisit draft-tree selection under the non-uniform memory-cost structure of MoE inference. We propose \textsc{EcoSpec}, a cost-aware speculative decoding framework that incorporates predicted marginal expert activation cost into draft selection. With a lightweight expert predictor and a dynamic expert buffer, \textsc{EcoSpec} favors draft paths that preserve high acceptance likelihood while reusing experts already covered by the current verification set, without modifying the target-model verification rule. We evaluate \textsc{EcoSpec} on three large-scale MoE models, including DeepSeek-V3.1 (671B), Qwen3-235B-A22B, and GPT-OSS-120B, across reasoning, coding, question-answering, and dialogue benchmarks. \textsc{EcoSpec} consistently reduces active expert footprints and improves end-to-end decoding speed, achieving up to $1.62\times$ speedup. These results show that accounting for expert activation cost is important for efficient speculative decoding in large-scale MoE models.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7063638`

- title: A Comprehensive Review of Large Language Models: Taxonomy, Architectures, Data, Adaptation, and Evaluation (2013-2026)
- authors: Gurpreet Singh
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-16
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7063638
- keyword hits: agentic, fine-tuning, foundation model, large language model, large language models, llm, llms

### abstract

Large language models (LLMs) have moved, in less than a decade, from task-specific recurrent networks to generalpurpose foundation models that are adapted to thousands of downstream tasks with little or no task-specific supervision. This review organizes that trajectory into a coherent taxonomy spanning five eras (distributed representations, the Transformer and pretrain-finetune paradigm, the scaling era, the alignment era, and the emerging reasoning/agentic era), and situates roughly forty representative models within it. We characterize each model along seven axes: method, year, input, architecture, learned representations, the signals it is conditioned on, and its key innovation. Beyond architecture, we survey the corpora that train these models (scale, pairing, domain, and modality), the objectives and compute-optimal scaling laws that govern them, and the adaptation techniques that make them usable: supervised instruction tuning, reinforcement learning from human and AI feedback, direct preference optimization, and parameter-efficient fine-tuning. We then examine inference-time methods (chain-ofthought and its descendants, retrieval augmentation), systemslevel efficiency (quantization, I/O-aware attention, sparsity), and multimodal extension. A dedicated treatment of evaluation classifies metrics as objective or subjective, states what each actually measures, and weighs their strengths against their failure modes. We close with a consolidated account of the limitations of representative models and the open problems that define the next phase of the field: faithful reasoning, factual grounding, evaluation validity, and alignment under scale. Throughout, we designed the tables as reference artifacts, not illustrations, so that the review doubles as a lookup resource.
