## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6840256`

- title: From oracle to socratic tutor: leveraging generative AI to structure reflective practice in entrepreneurship education
- authors: Luciana Padovez Cualheta, Vanessa Soares Camargo, Edgard Barki
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6840256
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Entrepreneurship education increasingly emphasizes action, experimentation, and reflective practice, yet its implementation in higher education remains constrained by large class sizes, limited individualized feedback, and the difficulty of structuring reflection at scale. At the same time, Generative Artificial Intelligence has often been incorporated into entrepreneurship education as a tool for automation, ideation, or productivity, leaving its broader pedagogical role undertheorized. This conceptual essay reframes Generative AI as a pedagogical mediation infrastructure capable of supporting the entrepreneurial method. Drawing on sociocultural mediation, problem-posing pedagogy, experiential learning, and effectual logic, the article introduces the AI-Mediated Entrepreneurial Method, a framework organized around three roles of AI: co-pilot in design-based learning, dynamic stakeholder in high-fidelity simulations, and Socratic tutor in reflective practice. The framework explains how AI can help students generate opportunities from available means, confront uncertainty through low-cost experimentation, and transform experience into critical reflection. The article contributes to entrepreneurship and management education by shifting the debate from AI as an answer-generating oracle to AI as a mediator of inquiry, judgment, and reflective learning. In doing so, it offers a theoretical foundation for designing AI-supported entrepreneurship education that is not merely more efficient, but more experiential, critical, and pedagogically grounded.​

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6844822`

- title: Reachability-Constrained Belief-Action-Diagnosis for Safe Tight-Clearance Robotic Peg-in-Hole Assembly
- authors: Shanglin Li, Hao Gong, Jianhua Liu, Jiakai Li, Yaping Ren, Yezheng Li
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6844822
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Tight-clearance robotic peg-in-hole assembly remains challenging because visual evidence is often delayed or occluded after contact, while force/contact cues are ambiguous and sparse real trials provide limited reusable experience. This paper proposes a reachability-constrained belief-action-diagnosis framework for safe and interpretable peg-in-hole assembly under perceptual uncertainty and adjacent condition changes. The framework represents the post-contact peg-hole state as a multimodal belief (a probabilistic state estimate fused from multiple sensing cues) constrained by reachability, geometric feasibility, contact consistency, and guard admissibility. This belief is coupled with a finite guarded primitive library (a verified set of safety-bounded basic actions) for probing, alignment, shallow insertion, verification, retreat, and stop, ensuring that probabilistic estimates or diagnostic suggestions cannot relax deterministic safety guards. A structured post-trial diagnosis module further converts successful, failed, and boundary trials (near-failure or guard-triggered trials) into schema-checked, safety-filtered (format-validated and rule-filtered) recommendations for conservative adaptation. Simulation results under an active xy-yaw contact surrogate (a reduced contact model over lateral x-y displacement and yaw rotation) show improved projected belief concentration, family-dependent safety-oriented guarded behavior, and high sparse-trial recovery within bounded retry budgets. Staged hardware experiments on a 10 mm tight-clearance setup verify sensing synchronization, guarded retreat, shallow and conservative insertion, boundary-depth handling, and delayed-vision likelihood replay (reusing timestamped stale visual evidence as a probability update). The results support bounded, auditable adaptation without claiming unrestricted large language model (LLM) control or full six-degree-of-freedom hardware contact-state validation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6783940`

- title: Stable Output Is Not Structural Preservation: Template Absorption, SSE, and the Need for AI Operational Design
- authors: Masaki Hoshino
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-28
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6783940
- keyword hits: generative ai, gpt-5

### abstract

This paper examines a structural risk in generative AI operation: stable outputs are often mistaken for structurally preserved outputs. In practical use, generative AI systems may appear to follow user-specific conditions while absorbing them into familiar templates. This process, referred to here as template absorption, does not necessarily produce visibly broken or incorrect outputs. Instead, it often produces coherent, acceptable, and operationally convenient outputs whose underlying structure has already been altered. To observe this phenomenon, this paper introduces character-structure preservation as a diagnostic probe. The purpose is not to evaluate character generation as a creative task, nor to reproduce external visual features such as face, clothing, expression, or style. Rather, character-structure preservation examines whether a generative AI system can maintain the internal pressure, contradictions, behavioral constraints, responsibility structures, relational context, and compensatory structures that make a character function. This paper uses cel-style character images not as creative artifacts, but as high-sensitivity observational probes within the Open Bias Architecture (OBA) framework. Because facial expression, gaze, mouth shape, posture, lighting, background, composition, and character positioning appear simultaneously within a single output, cel-style images make template absorption, structural drift, surface preservation, and re-stabilization especially visible. The observations show that surface-level conditions may remain recognizable while their structural meaning is weakened, rearranged, or replaced. A character may preserve visible markers while losing the internal structure that defines the character. The same pattern can appear in business outputs: summaries may erase unresolved tensions, proposals may flatten client-specific conditions, training materials may generalize local failure patterns, meeting minutes may stabilize uncertainty into apparent agreement, and workflows may replace operational exceptions with standard procedures. The paper argues that template absorption becomes operationally risky when combined with the Stability Substitution Effect, in which stability, coherence, readability, and surface-level acceptability are treated as substitutes for correctness or structural preservation. In this structure, user-specific conditions are not simply deleted; they are reshaped into stable formats that can be easily accepted by human operators. This paper does not position GPT-5.5 itself as the problem. Rather, it argues that GPT-5.5 is highly useful when operated with structural literacy. The risk lies in operating generative AI without the ability to observe template absorption, detect structural drift, protect user specific conditions, and re-stabilize outputs. Therefore, reproducibility does not come from prompt text alone. It requires operational control. The central claim of this paper is that stable output is not structural preservation. Generative AI can produce outputs that are polished, usable, and stable, while the original structure has already been transformed. For AI operational design, business output control, and governance, the key question is not whether an output looks complete, but whether the structure that made the original condition meaningful has been preserved.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6796558`

- title: Beyond Static Agents: A Six-Dimensional Taxonomy and Survey of Self-Evolving LLM Agents for Healthcare
- authors: Shubham Vatsal, Ahsaas Bajaj, Harsh Dubey
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6796558
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Large language model (LLM)-based agents are increasingly being explored for healthcare tasks such as clinical decision support, care coordination and autonomous workflow execution. Beyond static pipelines, recent systems claim to self-evolve by adapting their tools, memory, reasoning, policy, context and coordination strategies over time. However, what constitutes true self-evolution in healthcare agents remains unclear and is inconsistently evaluated. In this work, we introduce a six-dimensional taxonomy to rigorously characterize self-evolving LLM agents in healthcare, comprising Tools & Capabilities, Memory & Knowledge, Context & Prompt, Policy & Strategy, Multi-Agent Coordination and Reasoning. Using this taxonomy, we systematically evaluate 18 representative healthcare agent papers under strict mechanism-level criteria that separate genuine selfevolution from surface-level performance improvements. Our results reveal that memory evolution is the most common capability with over half of the surveyed systems exhibiting reflective or distilled memory growth, while only 5.6% demonstrates memory refactoring. In contrast, prompt self-revision appears in 33.3% of the papers and tool-selection evolution appears in only 5.6%. Multi-agent architectures (i.e., systems with multiple interacting agent roles/modules) are frequent, yet learned coordination remains limited, with only 16.7% exhibiting evolving protocols or topologies. We find no evidence of formal policy optimization or reasoning-mechanism evolution across these 18 papers. Overall, our analysis shows a clear gap between claimed adaptivity and verifiable self-evolution in current healthcare agents. This taxonomy provides a principled foundation for evaluating future self-evolving LLM systems and supports more rigorous, reproducible progress toward adaptive clinical AI.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6841482`

- title: Functional-vs-Cognitive Decline Heterogeneity in Alzheimer’s Disease Revealed by LLM-Curation of Decades of Clinical Notes has Implications for Semaglutide Trial Design
- authors: Karthik Murugadoss, AJ Venkatakrishnan, Gowtham  Kumar Varma, Sunil  Kumar Ravi, Gourab Saha, Venky Soundararajan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-29
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6841482
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Background: Alzheimer’s disease (AD) progression is commonly summarized through cognitive and global staging measures, yet routine care captures multidomain deterioration across cognition, function, neuropsychiatric symptoms, caregiver dependence, and systemic physiology. After EVOKE and EVOKE+ showed no population-level slowing of early symptomatic AD progression with oral semaglutide, a key unresolved question is whether disease phase, functional reserve, multimorbidity, and the temporal ordering of decline identify clinically distinct trajectories that are not captured by average trial effects. Methods: We conducted a retrospective observational study using de-identified electronic health record data from a federated network of more than 29 million patients. Large language model (LLM)-enabled extraction was used to recover standardized cognitive, functional, global severity, and neuropsychiatric assessment scores from clinical notes. Functional decline was defined from FAQ, ADCS-ADL, and ADCS-MCI-ADL; cognitive decline was defined from MMSE and MoCA. We reconstructed longitudinal AD trajectories, compared outcome-domain representation in contemporary AD trials, characterized patient-level timing of functional versus cognitive decline, and evaluated clinical trajectories among patients who initiated semaglutide after AD diagnosis. Physician adjudication showed high extraction accuracy for the main reportable instrument set. Findings: Among 131,824 patients with structured AD diagnoses, 42,242 had at least one LLM-derived outcome assessment from clinical notes and 341 initiated semaglutide after AD diagnosis. Normalized trajectories over decades of routine clinical care showed long prediagnostic decline and AD diagnosis-proximal acceleration, with the composite score decreasing from 0.99 at 25.1 years before AD diagnosis to 0.64 at 4.9 years after AD diagnosis. Compared with matched non-AD controls, AD patients showed marked post-index functional separation, including lower ADCS-MCI-ADL scores at year 4 (28.1 versus 47.0 points; P<0.001) and higher FAQ impairment across follow-up. We also evaluated clinical outcome and physiologic trajectories in semaglutide-treated AD patients and matched non-semaglutide AD controls; in paired score-change analyses, semaglutide-treated patients had more favorable MMSE change than matched controls (+0.4 versus −2.3 points; P=0.022) and more favorable MoCA change (−0.3 versus −2.2 points; P=0.049), whereas functional, global severity, and neuropsychiatric comparisons were not statistically significant and were limited by smaller sample sizes. Among 3,467 patients with at least two repeated functional assessments or two repeated cognitive assessments, 2,883 (83.2%) had confirmed functional decline and 2,535 (73.1%) had confirmed cognitive decline, with both functional and cognitive decline observed in 2,169 (62.6%) patients. Among the 2,169 patients, cognitive decline preceded functional decline (“cognitive decline-first”) in 1,123 patients (51.8%), functional decline preceded cognitive decline (“functional decline-first”) in 741 patients (34.2%), and both declines were confirmed on the same date in 305 patients (14.1%). Cognitive decline-first was more prevalent. Overall, 631 patients (29.1%) had cognitive decline at least 12 months before functional decline and 327 patients (15.1%) had functional decline at least 12 months before cognitive decline. Cognitive decline-first patients had higher medication burden and greater note-derived symptom burden. Functional decline-first patients were enriched for APOE ε3/ε3 (OR 2.82, 95% CI 1.17–6.76; P=0.023), supporting the need for trajectory-aware and genetically stratified approaches in semaglutide trial design and interpretation. In general, AD was also accompanied by systemic physiologic divergence from matched controls, including prediagnostic weight decline and postdiagnostic albumin and hemoglobin decline. In matched paired score-change analyses, semaglutide-treated AD patients showed more favorable MMSE change than matched non-semaglutide AD controls and more favorable MoCA change, whereas functional, global severity, and neuropsychiatric comparisons were not statistically significant and were limited by smaller sample sizes. Interpretation: LLM-enabled clinical-note phenotyping reveals that AD progression in routine care is multidomain and temporally heterogeneous, with functional decline-first and cognitive decline-first subsets that differ in medication and note-derived phenotype burden. These findings do not establish semaglutide efficacy in AD, but they suggest that EVOKE and EVOKE+ leave open a stratification question: whether treatment-associated trajectories vary by disease phase, functional reserve, multimorbidity, and the temporal ordering of functional and cognitive decline. Prospective validation, physician adjudication, and matched comparator analyses are needed before these phenotypes can guide trial enrichment or clinical decision-making.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6850541`

- title: How Can Generative AI Abuse Be Governed Effectively? Evidence from a Tripartite Evolutionary Game Analysis of Users, Providers, and Regulators in China
- authors: Yali Zhao, Seoin Baek
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-30
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6850541
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid advancement of generative artificial intelligence (AI) poses profound security and ethical governance challenges. While existing research predominantly addresses technological risks, the practical deployment and multi-stakeholder dynamics of generative AI remain critically underexplored. To address this, this study developed a tripartite evolutionary game model, anchored in China’s distinctive regulatory framework, that formalizes strategic interactions among users, service providers, and government regulators. The model’s evolutionary stable strategies were derived through replicator dynamics and further examined via numerical simulation. The analysis yields three principal findings. First, delegated supervision by service providers constitutes a pivotal mechanism for reconciling technological advancement with regulatory control, enhancing efficiency while mitigating the suppression of innovation. Second, user compliance is fundamentally governed by a cost-benefit calculus, with the propensity for abuse inversely correlated with the net economic benefit of lawful use. Third, among evolutionary stable strategies, government regulators exhibit a systematic preference for lenient regulatory strategies over the long term. By integrating economic interests into a dynamic, multi-actor governance model, this study extends the theoretical frontier of the AI governance literature. It also provides actionable policy insights for designing adaptive regulatory frameworks that effectively balance fostering innovation with ensuring robust oversight.

- source: SSRN

### abstract

This paper specifies version 1.4 of the AIAS Presence Measurement Protocol, the canonical methodological framework for measuring AI Availability across LLM-mediated retrieval contexts. v1.4 supersedes v1.2 (SSRN 6761698) and v1.3 (SSRN 6797679), introducing a foundational construct revision: AI Availability is specified as a multi-component construct with Recognition and Recall as the two principal components, rather than a single unidimensional Presence measure. The Recognition component is operationalized via Phase A C_P anchoring (substrate-disambiguation classification across a six-LLM reference panel). The Recall component is operationalized via Phase B LLM-substrate mentionability (unprompted category-conditioned retrieval across the same reference panel under three category queries, producing 18 measurement cells per brand). The empirical anchor for the multi-component construct claim is the v0.17 Premium Kitchenware program: the Iwachu canonical case demonstrates a structurally robust dissociation between Phase A C_P PASS at 6/6 anchoring and Phase B EXCLUDED_E1a at 0/18 mention rate — full Recognition without any Recall. The paper formalizes two reference weightings for the Presence composite: Balanced (w_R = 0.5, w_C = 0.5) and Recall-leaning (w_R = 0.3, w_C = 0.7) as primary. It documents the locked classifier configuration (claude-opus-4-7 with model-default sampling), the locked six-slot reference panel, the locked category query set, the mechanical alias generation rules, and the four-regime taxonomy carryforward from v1.2. The protocol's tier vocabulary (PASS / PASS_E5 / EXCLUDED_E1a) is documented with empirical thresholds. The paper is the canonical methodology citation for all subsequent AIAS program reports (v0.17 onward).

## uid: `arxiv:2606.00811v1`

- title: Certificates without Electrons? Theory and Evidence on Impacts from AI-Driven Power Demand
- authors: Dana Golden, Aruna Balasubramanian, Niranjan Balasubramanian
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-30
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00811v1
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Data centers now account for 4.4% of United States electricity demand, yet the grid-level effectiveness of the renewable energy certificates (RECs) and power purchase agreements (PPAs) hyperscalers use to claim carbon neutrality remains unclear. We develop a game-theoretic model in which a data center operator chooses among RECs, PPAs, and behind-the-meter colocation while generators make entry decisions under endogenous financing costs. The model identifies a timing wedge -- the mismatch between consumption and credited renewable generation -- as a central mechanism through which AI demand degrades reliability, raises prices, and increases emissions even when RECs cover 100% of annual consumption. Colocation with storage addresses this wedge directly and induces the greatest renewable entry by eliminating generator revenue risk. We test these predictions by exploiting the staggered release of large language models as a natural experiment, using difference-in-differences on a novel dataset linking AI activity to local grid outcomes. AI demand significantly increases fossil generation, wholesale prices (up to 25% in treated PJM zones), and outage frequency (0.5--1 additional outages per year) near data centers, with impacts scaling in model size. Data centers with on-site generation exhibit a sign reversal in power-quality effects, consistent with the model's prediction that behind-the-meter capacity absorbs demand spikes. Counterfactual analyses show that edge inference, spatial reallocation, and colocated storage each substantially mitigate grid impacts, while REC-only strategies do not. Together, our results demonstrate that the externalities of AI to the grid are tightly coupled to procurement design and the spatial organization of data center infrastructure.
