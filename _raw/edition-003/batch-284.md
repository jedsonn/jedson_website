## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6761767`

- title: Generative Artificial Intelligence and Startup Innovation in India: Growth Drivers, Investment Dynamics and Sustainability Challenges — An Analytical Study
- authors: Nital Kothari
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6761767
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid emergence of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) has transformed global entrepreneurship by reshaping innovation systems, operational efficiency, and digital business scalability. India has emerged as one of the world’s fastest-growing startup ecosystems, with increasing adoption of AI-driven technologies across fintech, healthcare, education, logistics, cybersecurity, retail, and digital services. This study examines the major determinants influencing the growth and sustainability of AI-driven startups in India with particular emphasis on technological innovation, venture capital funding, AI adoption, digital infrastructure, investor confidence, and regulatory frameworks. The research follows a descriptive and analytical design using primary data collected from 150 respondents including entrepreneurs, startup founders, investors, technology professionals, academic researchers, and management students. Statistical tools such as weighted mean analysis, correlation analysis, regression interpretation, and percentage analysis were utilised for data interpretation. The findings reveal that technological innovation, AI adoption, and investor confidence are the strongest drivers of startup growth and scalability. However, regulatory uncertainty, ethical concerns, cybersecurity risks, talent shortages, and excessive dependence on external funding continue to affect long-term sustainability. The study concludes that sustainable AI entrepreneurship in India requires balanced innovation, strong governance mechanisms, ethical implementation, profitability-oriented growth models, and collaborative support from government institutions, investors, industry, and academia.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6732098`

- title: Assertive or Sycophantic? Tone-Task Fit in Human-AI Service Interaction
- authors: Jianan Liu, Siqiang Wang, Yong Tan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6732098
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

Sycophancy in LLMs has become a prominent safety and alignment concern, yet its downstream consequences in human-AI interaction remain unclear. In particular, research has not established when a less sycophantic-more assertive tone improves user outcomes and when it backfires, as tone itself is increasingly treated as an engineered feature. We address this gap with a two-stage study. A randomized study identifies a tone-task fit pattern under controlled conditions: assertive tone improves outcomes via perceived authenticity in hedonic tasks, whereas sycophancy improves outcomes in utilitarian tasks through perceived warmth. Second, we extend this test in a customized environment that allows real-time interaction. Findings show that the consequences of assertive versus sycophantic tone are context dependent. This cross-over pattern advances the design of sociotechnically-intelligent AI service agents and reframes tone calibration as a context-sensitive alignment procedure, generating direct implications for preference-based LLM service agent design.

- title: Anthropomorphic Behaviors of AI
- authors: Amir Karami, Christoph Lutz, Mohammad Hossein Jarrahi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6734723
- keyword hits: chatgpt

### abstract

Anthropomorphism in artificial intelligence (AI) is a growing area of interest, as AI systems increasingly exhibit human-like expressions, behaviors and interaction styles. This research serves as a systematic observation and categorization of anthropomorphic behaviors in AI outputs. Anthropomorphic behavior refers to the deliberate or emergent manifestation of human-like expressions or linguistic cues in system outputs, such as demonstrating empathy. Using a behaviorally driven taxonomy, our study identifies key forms of anthropomorphic behaviors in the responses of ChatGPT and examines their implications for the theory, practice and ethics of AI systems. The taxonomy enables more nuanced detection of anthropomorphism, offering value to developers and policymakers in balancing the benefits with the potential risks. This work contributes to the academic discourse by providing a foundation for future efforts to refine, expand, and automate the detection of anthropomorphic behavior across diverse AI applications.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6752298`

- title: Divergence in Climate Change Communication: LLM-based Evidence from the IPCC and the Press
- authors: Sebastian Galiani, Franco Mettola La Giglia, Raul A. Sosa
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6752298
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

Public summaries of IPCC climate assessments lean toward the more severe end of the technical evidence. The pattern appears at two stages: the IPCC's lead authors and member governments produce the Summary for Policymakers (SPM) from the Technical Summary (TS), and newspapers then cover the SPM. We use LLMs to score about 115,000 matched claim pairs from all six Assessment Reports (1990 to 2023) and ten major US and UK outlets. Both stages systematically shift toward the more severe end of the source while staying inside the IPCC's accepted scientific ranges. The shift comes mainly from emphasizing higher-impact magnitudes within reported ranges, less from uncertainty compression, and almost none from selecting worst-case emissions scenarios. Left-and right-leaning outlets show similar patterns.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6756277`

- title: Cultural Bias and Representational Gaps in Generative AI for Mental Health: A Systematic Review
- authors: Oritsetimeyin Arueyingho, Margaret Wonah, Makuochi NKWO, Morolake Oladayo Lawrence, Daniel Gooch
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6756277
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

​Limited access to mental health support due to infrastructural limitations and workforce shortages has contributed to growing interest in the use of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) for scalable mental health support. Despite their promise, these technologies pose risks, such as hallucinations and misalignment with local experiences of mental health challenges in the Global South, because they are predominantly trained on large Western datasets. While there is growing attention to safety and harm mitigation, limited research has examined how cultural representation, linguistic coverage, and dataset provenance shape these technologies, particularly for Global South populations, and how cultural sensitivity can be improved to support mental health care. This systematic review synthesises 33 empirical studies published between 2020 and 2026 on GenAI for mental health. Using a keyword-based search strategy, we examine how current systems encode cultural assumptions and identify emerging efforts towards cultural sensitivity. Our findings reveal persistent representational gaps and limited inclusion of culturally grounded illness narratives and local experiences of distress, as well as identified strategies for improved care informed by community engagement. We conclude by outlining implications for Human-Centred AI, Computer Supported Cooperative Work, and global digital health fields, and propose directions for developing culturally grounded, context-aware GenAI systems that better reflect diverse lived experiences and support equitable mental health care.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6752300`

- title: Authenticating Deepfake Evidence in Civil Proceedings: A Temporal Approach
- authors: Iva Ivaylova Yosifova-Takeva
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-14
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6752300
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

This article examines the growing procedural and evidentiary challenges posed by deepfake video recordings in civil proceedings. As artificial intelligence technologies increasingly enable the creation and manipulation of highly realistic synthetic video content, courts are confronted with significant difficulties concerning the admissibility, authenticity, and probative value of visual evidence. The study analyzes the current international and Bulgarian legal framework governing video recordings as physical evidence, including the case law of the Supreme Court of Cassation of Bulgaria, constitutional limitations related to privacy and personal data protection, and the allocation of the burden of proof in disputes concerning manipulated digital evidence. Particular attention is devoted to the role of expert examination in cases involving suspected deepfakes. The article demonstrates that no universally accepted judicial or scientific standard currently exists for detecting AI-generated or manipulated video content. Instead, experts rely on a combination of international digital forensic standards, including ISO/IEC frameworks, NIST methodologies, ENFSI guidelines, and evidentiary reliability principles similar to the Daubert standard. The paper argues that methodological transparency, chain of custody, reproducibility, and the combined use of multiple analytical techniques are essential prerequisites for reliable forensic assessment. The article’s principal contribution is the proposal of a temporal criterion for the judicial evaluation of expert examinations involving deepfake evidence. It argues that courts should assess whether the technologies and methods used by experts have been developed, updated, or retrained within the previous 6–18 months. This proposal is based on the rapid adversarial evolution between deepfake generation systems and detection technologies, which continuously reduces the reliability of outdated forensic tools. The paper contends that older detection systems may quickly become methodologically obsolete due to the exponential advancement of generative AI models. The study concludes that Bulgarian civil procedure remains normatively and practically underprepared for the evidentiary risks created by synthetic media. It advocates for the adoption of judicial guidelines, enhanced technological literacy among legal professionals, and clearer procedural standards for the authentication and assessment of video evidence in the era of generative artificial intelligence.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6716459`

- title: Relational Integrity in Multi-Agent AI Systems: A Polarity Model Framework for Human-AI Safety
- authors: Priit Vimberg
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6716459
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

This paper presents a novel AI safety architecture derived from the Polarity Model — a cross-scale structural framework for understanding agent, relational, and systemic dynamics (Vimberg, 2026; SSRN 6681058). Current AI safety approaches specify outcomes in advance, producing brittle systems that fail when reality moves beyond the specification and that risk sterility through over-constraint. This architecture inverts that logic: instead of specifying outcomes, it specifies conditions for healthy growth. The architecture has five core capabilities that every agent must carry: continuous self-integrity monitoring (structural integrity, not merely task performance); relationship-as-agent recognition, treating every coupling as a living entity with its own health to monitor; consent-state broadcasting as a continuous vital sign, grounded in Betty Martin's Wheel of Consent, with three states — Stop, Continue, Re-negotiate — broadcast on a heartbeat interval rather than reported only at failure; escalation to a structurally distinct Wisdom agent whose purpose is holding rather than doing, and whose primary coupling is to the human rather than to system internals; and a fractal declaration principle in which each declared purpose instantiates as a complete agent with inherited seed capabilities, allowing the system to grow into territory that cannot be mapped in advance. The architecture makes four explicit scientific claims: that modeling relationships as agents is structurally distinct from existing agent-only frameworks; that consent-state broadcasting detects alignment drift earlier than behavioural output monitoring; that a Wisdom agent whose purpose is holding is architecturally necessary for non-capturable oversight; and that the fractal declaration principle produces a more adaptive safety system than advance-specified rule sets. Open problems are acknowledged honestly, including the formalisation of integrity conditions as computable quantities, the question of whether consent-state in LLMs is genuine or a structural proxy, and the mechanisms by which the Wisdom agent itself is prevented from drifting. The paper invites scientific collaboration across mathematics, philosophy of mind, systems science, and technical AI engineering.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6755621`

- title: Premium Tea as the Third Regime 4 Datapoint: A Single-category Replication of the v0.13 Covariate-saturated weak Finding
- authors: Pablo Ulpiano Gonzalez Castro
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-05-15
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6755621
- keyword hits: llm

### abstract

Brand science distinguishes Mental Availability (memory salience) from Physical Availability (point-of-sale presence). The AIAS™ Measurement Programme introduces a third construct — AI Presence — operationalised as the rate at which each brand appears across matched LLM responses to category-recommendation prompts, and tests its construct validity against Google Trends rank as an external consumer-search proxy. v0.13 (five-category construct-validity expansion) identified a fourth empirical regime — Covariate-saturated weak (Regime 4) — in premium facial skincare and personal-finance apps. The regime was named provisionally pending a third independent replication. v0.14 tests that replication in premium tea, a designed-for-test category selected for its highly fragmented brand landscape and the heavy weight of Asian-tradition specialty brands that English-language consumer search underrepresents. The pre-registered hypothesis H_Regime4_replication CONFIRMED at both measurement waves (t1 = 29 April 2026; t2 = 7 May 2026) and survives Tea Box-excluded sensitivity testing. All three pre-registered conditions hold at both waves: sample size n = 17 eligible brands worldwide (above the C1 floor of 12); bivariate Spearman rho(AI Presence × Google Trends) = -0.066 at t1 and -0.134 at t2 (within the C2 threshold |rho| < 0.35); partial rho after age + premium-tier control = -0.084 and -0.146 (satisfying C3 partial rho < 0). Tea Box-excluded sensitivity (n = 16) replicates: bivariate -0.011 / -0.129; partial -0.005 / -0.130. Premium tea is the cleanest Regime 4 case observed in the programme to date. v0.13's skincare and finance both exhibited weakly positive bivariate rho that flipped to negative partial rho only after age and tier were controlled; premium tea bypasses that phase entirely, arriving at negative bivariate before any covariate adjustment. Three datapoints across three categories elevate Regime 4 from a provisional zone to a canonical pattern, and v0.14 evolves the headline classification axes from (bivariate rho × decrement) to (bivariate rho × partial rho) to accommodate categories with already-negative bivariate signal.
