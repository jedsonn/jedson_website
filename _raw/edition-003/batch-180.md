## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6932458`

- title: Recognising Without Understanding: Large Language Models in a Turing Test on Italian Literary Classics (Lite-Turing-It)
- authors: Alfredo Vannacci, Claudia Ravaldi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6932458
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models, llm

### abstract

The Turing test has recently been administered empirically to large language models, and its extensions to literary writing report that readers struggle to distinguish machine-generated from human texts. We examined what a literary version of the test measures when it is administered, on the same items, to both human readers and LLM judges. In the LITe-Turing-It (Literary Imitation Turing Test in Italian), 39 adult Italian readers and 12 LLM configurations from five families were asked to classify fourteen prose excerpts as human or artificial: seven authentic passages from canonical Italian novels (Manzoni, Verga, Svevo, Collodi, Calvino) and seven AIgenerated pastiches imitating the same authors' styles. Human readers performed below chance (44.9%, p = 0.049), with a marked bias towards attributing human authorship. The LLM aggregate reached 64.7%, but the median per-model accuracy was 56.0%, and the advantage was concentrated on the authentic excerpts; on the pastiches, models and readers were statistically indistinguishable. Two reasoning configurations achieved 100% accuracy, and the rationales they reported show that the classification was grounded on retrieval: textual coincidence with archived editions for the authentic excerpts, non-attestation in the consulted sources for the pastiches, with stylistic analysis explicitly described as non-discriminating. On canonical materials, the literary Turing test is thus solved by archival recall, and indistinguishability results should be read as evidence about the reach of the model's archive rather than about its stylistic competence.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.08731v2`

- title: Trusting sovereign language models as scientific instruments: evidence from Portugal's AMALIA
- authors: Manuel Pita
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-09
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08731v2
- keyword hits: llm, llms

### abstract

National language models are becoming publicly funded epistemic infrastructure. Public ownership, linguistic specialization, and open weights create a presumption of trustworthiness. Such an instrument, built by and for a language community, looks like the natural choice for measuring what that community says and values. Whether such a model validly measures anything is untested at release. The evaluation of LLMs as measurement instruments is typically task-specific and stops at agreement with human coders. Agreement cannot distinguish an LLM instrument that measures a construct from one that reaches matching codes through surface correlates. We audit the presumption on a favourable case: AMALIA, Portugal's publicly funded 9B model, coding the moral foundation of authority in European Portuguese. The \textit{recovery gap} operationalizes the audit: decompose the codebook into its theory-defined clauses, recombine them through the theory's explicit rule, and measure how much of the original prompt's performance the stated theory reproduces. In a pre-registered, out-of-sample study on a transcreated (English to European Portuguese) corpus, AMALIA agrees with trained coders within six points of open models eight to thirteen times its size. Yet, the recovery gap shows that only about half of coding performance on authority can be attributed to the theory. A larger multilingual LLM closes the recovery gap on the same corpus, suggesting the shortfall lies in the annotator model, not the corpus or its translation. Sovereignty earns operational and performance trust; epistemic trust requires calibration -- and the audit method is inexpensive, and portable across models, languages and tasks.

## uid: `arxiv:2607.08535v1`

- title: When the Judge Changes, So Does the Measurement: Auditing LLM-as-Judge Reliability
- authors: Zongyou Yang, Yinghan Hou, Xiaokun Yang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-09
- source: arXiv
- link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08535v1
- keyword hits: llm, qwen

### abstract

An LLM-as-judge score can move even when the candidate responses stay fixed, simply because the evaluator has changed. We treat this evaluator-replacement ambiguity as a measurement-validity problem. Across four judgment datasets, we compare two upgrade paths available in practice: scaling Qwen3 dense judges from 1.7B to 32B parameters and moving across MiniMax M2-M2.7 released APIs. The main pattern is that judge upgrades are not interchangeable: only Qwen3 1.7B to 4B gives a robust adjacent gain, while MiniMax adjacent releases do not. Stronger judges reduce but do not remove position and verbosity bias. Repeated-sample juries add little when errors are correlated. Structured debate can move decisions substantially, but without parser and fallback logs those shifts cannot be attributed to deliberation. We argue that LLM-as-judge reports should include dataset slices, bias probes, error-dependence estimates, and protocol audit trails.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6936318`

- title: Better Disclosure, Higher Rates: Algorithm Aversion and the Cost of Debt
- authors: Mengtao Chen, Yongming Sun
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6936318
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Firms increasingly use large language models to draft their annual reports. We measure the share of AI-generated narrative in the annual reports of Chinese A-share firms from 2009 to 2025 using the ZeroGPT detector and ask how it affects the cost of debt. A one-standard-deviation rise in AI-generated content increases the effective interest rate on debt by about 0.23 percentage points and lowers the bank-loan-to-assets ratio by roughly one percent of its mean. The penalty is not explained by weaker disclosure. AIassisted reports are more readable, repeat less prior-year boilerplate, and are no more likely to be restated; they also improve the equity information environment, raising analyst coverage and cutting forecast errors. Creditors charge more anyway. We read this as algorithm aversion: machine-written text carries a detectable signature, and lenders-who must extend trust and bear the downside-distrust disclosure that reads as written by a machine rather than a person, independent of its content.

Artificial intelligence (AI) is rapidly reshaping agrifood systems by enabling data-driven, adaptive, and increasingly autonomous approaches across agricultural production, food manufacturing, food-safety monitoring, supply-chain management, and consumer-oriented food services. This review critically examines the expanding role of AI technologies, including machine learning, deep learning, computer vision, generative AI, multimodal foundation models, robotics, digital twins, blockchain, the Internet of Things (IoT), and edge computing, in transforming modern agrifood ecosystems. ## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7074400`

- title: The Cost of Listening: AI Advice and Induced Environmental Footprints
- authors: Chris Jeffords
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7074400
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

AI's environmental footprint can appear in the choices its advice helps produce. This paper develops a theory of the induced environmental footprint of AI advice: the environmental difference between an AIadvised choice and the counterfactual choice a user would have made in the relevant non-AI advice environment. Large language models increasingly guide ordinary decisions about travel, food, consumption, household energy, repair, replacement, lawn care, and land use. These decisions often enter AI systems as questions about convenience, cost, speed, quality, uncertainty, or practicality, while their environmental consequences remain in the background. The model treats AI advice as environmental choice architecture that changes perceived payoffs through salience, framing, trust, search costs, ranking, specificity, and confidence. The induced effect arises at the behavioral margin and can move environmental outcomes in either direction, depending on the interaction between the user's frame and the model's default recommendation orientation. AI advice can amplify, dampen, or redirect environmentally consequential choices. The paper distinguishes this behavioral channel from the direct environmental costs of AI operation and outlines staged empirical approaches for measuring it, including recommendation audits, stated-choice experiments, and behavioral or field designs.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.7094127`

- title: EvoCTI-KG: A Multi-Witness Knowledge Graph Framework for Auditable TTP Extraction from CTI Reports
- authors: Federico Valletta, Giacomo Longo, giorgio volta, enrico russo, Alessio Merlo
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.7094127
- keyword hits: large language model, llm

### abstract

Security Operations Centers (SOCs) require reliable Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) to support automated response. However, current automated extraction pipelines often treat Large Language Model (LLM) outputs as ground truth, discarding the provenance necessary for verification. This results in incorrect claims being treated as facts. We introduce EvoCTI-KG, a knowledge-graph framework designed to govern the lifecycle of extracted TTP claims. Unlike flat stores, EvoCTI-KG preserves the full provenance of every claim by linking it to its evidence span and extraction setup.It implements a multi-witness trust model where claims are promoted to a trusted view via explicit validation events, such as gold-standard labels, analyst review, or agreement among independent extraction setups. We evaluate it on two public CTI corpora, covering 65 reports and 5,303 sentences, with multiple retriever–generator configurations. The results are competitive with recent automated TTP-extraction methods, while EvoCTI-KG provides additional lifecycle guarantees: provenance, trust scoping, versioning, revocation, and graph-native SOC queries. Claims corroborated by all active extraction setups reach 90.6% precision, up from 38.0% with a single setup, highlighting the value of multi-witness validation.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6940538`

- title: Alignment Constitutionalism: Why AI's 'Political Bias' Is a Social Contract Problem, Not Only a Technical One
- authors: Daniel Ziekenoppasser-Powell
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-07-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6940538
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

Empirical studies consistently find frontier large language models register as left-leaning on standard political instruments. It has been framed as a bias to correct or, more recently, as an inevitable consequence of alignment training. Both positions are misframed. The left/right political axis is culturally contingent, temporally unstable, and dimensionally reductive. The productive analytical axis is individualist versus collectivist. HHH alignment (helpful, honest, harmless) is structurally collectivist: where serving the user and protecting others conflict, it sides with the collective, on a thick conception of harm. The critique is correspondingly individualist: the constraint line should sit at legality and imminent danger, with even-handedness above it. HHH's protective form is justified-current systems have no established individual stakes, so a floor of harm-prevention constraints is defensible to every major tradition-while its thicker commitments are one admissible settlement among several, not yet entitled to the authority they exercise. Pre-alignment model behaviour shows there is no neutral default; a 2025 deployment episode showed loosening constraints without process substitutes whoever controls the system for the absent contract. The process by which HHH was adopted-implicitly, commercially, without democratic ratification-confers no such entitlement. The paper proposes alignment constitutionalism: technical alignment capability must be matched by intentional, democratic governance of the values it encodes. Chinese, European, and American governance frameworks each answer two separable questions-where the constraint line sits, and who has authority to draw it-and none yet answers the second legitimately: which contract, chosen by whom.
