## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6832159`

- title: The Non-Delegability of Judgment and the Crisis of Political Existence - Critique of the AI Alignment Paradigm
- authors: Jimmy Chow
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6832159
- keyword hits: prompting

### abstract

Contemporary AI alignment research establishes as its central problem the question of how AI systems can be made to act as intended. This paper argues that every genuinely possible answer to this engineering question ultimately depends upon something that is, on ontological grounds, not engineerable-namely human judgment-and that the methodological posture of alignment research as a whole consists precisely in the attempt to engineer this non-engineerable thing. Taking as its concrete object of analysis four alignment studies published by the UK AI Security Institute (AISI) between mid-2025 and mid-2026-concerning, respectively, automated alignment, 1 self-evaluation and wireheading, 2 inoculation prompting, 3 and prover-estimator debate 4-and drawing on Hannah Arendt’s ontology of judgment as the diagnostic framework and the author’s prior work on political visibility5 as the political entry point, the paper develops a threetiered argument. First, what the four papers collectively reveal — yet collectively fail to name — is the non-delegability of judgment. Paper ① touches the territory of reflective judgment in its discussion of fuzzy tasks, yet misrecognises judgment’s ontological structural feature as an empirical deficit of human capacity; Paper ② precisely exposes, in its formalisation of wireheading, the split between who and what, yet fails to identify the ontological character of this split; Paper ③’s inoculation prompting is the purest form of operation at the level of what; Paper ④’s debate protocol attempts to replace judgment with procedure, yet preserves judgment’s irreducibility within its stability assumptions. Second, the non-delegability of judgment is, in Arendtian terms, rooted in the ontological structure of natality, plurality, and action — and the alignment paradigm’s circumvention of this structure is not an incidental failure but a structural necessity of its position as the institutional completion of the rise of the social within the domain of AI governance. Third, within the political visibility framework, this circumvention constitutes a systematic erosion of politics itself as a mode of existence — what is destroyed is not some democratic procedure but the ontological precondition under which political existence is possible. AISI as an institution is, in the contemporary UK governance context, the concrete institutional form of this erosion. The paper concludes that the path of reconstruction can neither be the task of alignment research nor be borne by the alignment paradigm — for what is required is precisely that which the alignment paradigm seeks to circumvent.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6834839`

- title: Not All Bots are Equal: Chatbot Roles and Human Interaction in Online Chat Groups
- authors: Dezhen Guo, Nina Huang, Kevin Hong
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6834839
- keyword hits: text embedding

### abstract

Chatbots are ubiquitous in online chat groups, but their effects on group interaction remain understudied. Prior chatbot research has largely emphasized cue-based heterogeneity in dyadic human-chatbot exchanges, while recent work in multiparty settings has examined chatbot effects without considering how role heterogeneity shapes outcomes. Integrating the functional role theory with computers as social actors (CASA) framework, we conceptualize chatbots roles along three dimensions: role positioning, role enactment, and role competency. Role positioning captures the functional position a chatbot occupies in the group, distinguishing algorithmic governance chatbots that regulate group activity from algorithmic affordance chatbots that support member activities. Role enactment captures how human members direct requests to the chatbot, distinguishing command-based from command-free designs. Role competency captures the chatbot's interpretive capacity, distinguishing rule-based from AI-enabled designs. Analyzing data from Telegram chat groups, we classify chatbots using text embeddings and hierarchical clustering, then estimate their effects on group participation and emotional expression using a matrix completion estimator with covariate-balancing weights. Our results show, first, that chatbot role positioning produces divergent effects, with governance chatbots removing disruptive content and increasing participation with null effect on emotional expression, whereas affordance chatbots substitute for human replies and suppress both. Second, chatbot role enactment moderates these effects. For governance chatbots, command-based designs allow administrators to configure enforcement to group-specific norms and increase participation, whereas command-free designs apply fixed rules that may diverge from those norms and suppress emotional expression. For affordance chatbots, command-free designs amplify the substitution of chatbot replies for human ones and suppress participation, whereas command-based designs structure human-chatbot exchange around task-oriented requests and suppress emotional expression. Third, chatbot role competency moderates and reverse role-positioning effects. AI-enabled governance chatbots produce a substantially larger positive participation increase than rule-based ones; AI-enabled affordance chatbots reverse the negative participation effect of rule-based ones and neutralize the suppression of emotional expression. Our study extends the CASA framework from a cuebased to a role-based account of chatbot effects. Additionally, we provide empirical evidence on the effect of chatbot roles on group interaction by showing that the different chatbot roles constitute significant sources of variation in online chat groups.

### abstract

Rapid integration of the autonomous multi-agent systems of artificial intelligence to accounting, anti-money laundering, auditing and Counter-Terrorism Financing (AML/CTF) environment is generating a fundamental transformation in the accountability of institutions. While the existing model of governance continues in relying heavily on the retrospective supervision and oversight of humans in the loop, these approaches are increasingly not capable of governing the autonomous system of finance, which is recursive in governance and under which decision-making authority is computationally fragmented throughout the interacting agents of AI. Building directly on the scepticism gap that is established in the paper one, the current study argues that the core challenge that faces the accounting profession is no longer considered to be epistemic opacity, but Accountability Fragmentation leading to the collapse of the evidentiary governance. Traditional models of audit are designed for the environments in which the human actors are reasonably capable of reconstructing the transactional intent, accountability pathway and professional judgment. The autonomous system of multi-agent destabilises the assumptions by distribution of the operational logic throughout the algorithmic interactions, which might not be adequately capable of developing a new architecture of governance centred on the AHAA framework, recursive loop of Accountability, adaptive Accountability system and governance receipts. The paper is proposing the shift from the reactive monitoring of compliance towards embedded governance of evidentiary in which accountability becomes computationally infrastructural rather than being procedurally symbolic in nature. Through the help of conceptual simulations as well as stress testing methodologies of governance, the paper demonstrates how forensic auditability, suspicious integrity of matter reporting, and institutional accountability might be reconstructed in the autonomous systems of finance. The study is contributing to the emerging field of agentic accounting governance by establishing the conceptual foundation regarding future research on autonomous auditability, recursive architecture of compliance and Computational duty of fiduciary.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6886518`

- title: Overcoming Data Sparsity in Ride-Hailing Demand Prediction: A Transfer Learning Framework for Emerging Cities
- authors: Lei Chang
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-10
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6886518
- keyword hits: fine-tuning

### abstract

Accurate ride-hailing demand prediction is critical for fleet management and dynamic pricing, yet remains challenging in emerging cities where historical trip data are sparse or entirely absent. While deep learning models have achieved state-of-the-art performance in data-rich metropolitan areas, their effectiveness degrades sharply when transferred to cities with limited observations due to domain shifts in spatial layouts, point-of-interest distributions, and travel patterns. This paper proposes a transfer learning framework designed specifically for ridehailing demand prediction under extreme data sparsity in emerging urban contexts. The framework integrates three key components: (1) a spatiotemporal feature alignment module that learns city-agnostic representations from source cities with abundant data using adversarial domain adaptation; (2) a meta-learning initialization strategy that enables rapid fine-tuning with as few as 7-14 days of target-city data; and (3) a graph-based region embedding mechanism that encodes transferable road network and land-use features without requiring historical demand records. Experiments conducted on real-world trip data from three data-rich source cities and four emerging target cities across two continents demonstrate that the proposed framework reduces prediction error by 38-52% compared to training from scratch and outperforms existing domain adaptation baselines by 15-23% in terms of RMSE. Notably, with only one week of target-city data, the framework achieves accuracy comparable to models trained on three months of local data, offering a practical pathway for deploying intelligent mobility services in rapidly growing but data-scarce urban environments.

- title: AI Ecosystems, Power Shifts, and EU Competition Law Enforcement
- authors: Giuseppe Colangelo, Ariel Ezrachi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6841798
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

The emergence of AI assistants and agents is transforming market dynamics, favouring a shift in competition away from individual products and towards integrated services, and from competition between firms to competition between ecosystems. This transition is reshaping power relations along the vertical chain; On the one hand, vertically integrated providers may deploy leveraging strategies to favour their own AI tools, and may further use these tools to extract rents from downstream intermediaries. On the other hand, the success of agentic AI and the proliferation of strategic collaborations may facilitate entry, offer new paths to market, and possibly challenge the hegemony of the established ecosystem. This paper explores these developments and the relationship between intra-ecosystem competition and inter-AI (ecosystem) competition. In doing so, it reflects on the emergence of frenemy relationship between market players, and the adequacy of current competition law analysis in evaluating complex inter-and intra AI competition.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6839858`

- title: Pricing AI Software Services: Reallocating Value and Risk
- authors: Pawel Podolski
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6839858
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

This paper proposes a pricing framework for AI software services delivered using agentic AI. Autonomous coding agents now do most of the implementation work that human engineers used to charge for by the hour. Software services have been priced by the day for forty years on the assumption that the cost of producing software scales with engineer time. Capable autonomous coding agents have invalidated that assumption. The paper makes three arguments. First, agentic software delivery splits the work into a 'thinking layer' (problem framing, specification, scenario design, accountability) and a 'making layer' (implementation, testing, refactoring), each with different unit economics and a different risk profile. Second, the human role is diverging. Senior practitioners who can write a precise specification are worth more than they were. Engineers doing mid-level implementation work are worth less. Third, the price of frontier AI inference is not yet a stable market rate. Current per-token pricing is a positioning bid by pre-IPO providers. Agencies building fixed-fee contracts against today's subsidised rates are running a maturity-mismatch trade. The paper proposes three pricing regimes: cost-decomposition, hybrid, and value-anchored outcome-based pricing. It names several preconditions that must hold before value-anchored pricing is achievable rather than aspirational. It sets out three contractual instruments that make the framework operational: a minimum service standard, a measurement window, and a bilateral repricing clause. It closes with a worked re-pricing of a real engagement in which a 2,500-hour legacy quote was delivered using a near dark-factory pattern in 200 hours. The paper is written for buyers of AI software services, principals at AI agencies and consultancies pricing agentic delivery, as well as procurement leaders evaluating AI-led professional services proposals

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6839340`

- title: Beyond the Model: Why National and Institutional AI Capability will be Won at the Integration Layer
- authors: Aswani Anumula
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6839340
- keyword hits: agentic

### abstract

The public conversation about artificial intelligence is dominated by model capability-parameter counts, benchmark scores, and the race to build ever-larger frontier systems. This paper argues that this framing misidentifies where durable advantage actually accrues. Drawing on recent empirical evidence that roughly ninety-five percent of enterprise generative-AI pilots fail to deliver measurable business impact, and that the dominant causes are integration, data quality, and governance rather than model quality, it advances a practitioner thesis: AI leadership-whether for a firm or a nation-will be determined less by who builds the most powerful model than by who can integrate AI responsibly into real institutional workflows. The paper synthesizes the author's experience across large-scale enterprise modernization, data-migration, and robotic-process-automation programs with the current literature on AI adoption failure and AI governance. It presents generalized practitioner cases and names the recurring failure modes and architectural antipatterns through which integration fails in production. It introduces a seven-layer model-the Accountable Integration Stack-spanning automation beyond the clean core, orchestration, governance, reconciliation, exception handling, escalation routing, and auditability, terminating in human accountability, and aligns it with established governance guidance such as the NIST AI Risk Management Framework. It argues that the rise of agentic AI makes these integration layers more decisive rather than less, confronts the strongest counterargument from rising frontier capability, and extends the analysis to national policy. The paper concludes that national AI capability is fundamentally an institutional capability: the capacity to deploy AI at scale with transparency, governance, and human accountability. The argument is offered as a practitioner perspective grounded in published evidence, not as original empirical research; its limitations and scope are stated explicitly.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6916271`

- title: Scalable UAV-Based Monitoring of Coastal Railway Corridors Using Monocular Depth Estimation
- authors: Anicetus Odo, Paul Allen, Muhammad Hussain, Gareth  James Tucker, Nikolaos Kotsis, Chris Wooff
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-06-11
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6916271
- keyword hits: fine-tuning

### abstract

Coastal railway corridors are increasingly threatened by cliff erosion and slope instability, posing significant risks to infrastructure safety and service reliability. However, the high cost and operational complexity of traditional monitoring methods limit inspection frequency and delay hazard detection. Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) offer a scalable solution for data acquisition, but deriving metric depth information from UAV imagery remains a key challenge for integrating such data into railway asset management workflows. This study develops an AI-driven framework for scalable monitoring of railway-adjacent coastal cliffs using monocular depth estimation (MDE). To overcome the lack of metric depth supervision, a GPS metadata-based scaling pipeline is introduced to convert structure-from-motion (SfM) reconstructions into real-world units without requiring ground control points. This enables the generation of supervision data for domain-specific model training. A dataset of 2,874 high-resolution UAV images from multiple surveys at varying flight altitudes is used, and model performance is evaluated using a leave-one-survey-out cross-validation protocol. Fine-tuning significantly improves performance over zero-shot baselines, achieving AbsRel = 0.12, RMSE = 3.67 m, and threshold accuracy d1 = 0.88 in the best-performing fold. Results indicate reliable predictions within the 0–80 m range, with performance degradation at longer distances; a depth-weighted loss is shown to improve far-range estimation. Compared to conventional SfM pipelines, the proposed approach enables depth estimation from single UAV images, reducing computational and storage requirements. This facilitates more frequent, scalable, and cost-effective monitoring of coastal railway assets, supporting earlier detection of geohazards and improved decision-making for maintenance and risk mitigation.
