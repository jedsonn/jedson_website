## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6391618`

- title: The Fearless Premium: Workplace Psychological Safety and Stock Returns
- authors: Hamid Boustanifar, Saeed Sheykhi
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6391618
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

Organizational research identifies psychological safety-the shared belief that employees can speak up with ideas, questions, or mistakes without fear of negative consequences-as a critical driver of learning, innovation, and team performance. Despite its prominence in the organizational sciences, little is known about its implications for firm value and asset prices. Using textual employee reviews from Glassdoor, we construct a novel firm-level measure of psychological safety (PS) by combining large language model-based annotation with supervised machine learning. Our measure captures nuanced aspects of workplace voice, feedback, and interpersonal risk that are not reflected in traditional corporate culture metrics. We show that PS predicts future innovation but is not associated with higher contemporaneous market valuations. In contrast, a value-weighted long (high PS)-short (low PS) portfolio earns a Fama-French six-factor alpha of 3.57%. These returns are robust to standard asset-pricing factors and controls for intangible-related risk. In the cross-section, the effect of PS on stock returns is strongest and statistically significant among innovation-intensive firms and when PS is measured using white-collar employee reviews. Firms with higher PS also experience systematically positive earnings surprises and stronger announcement returns, consistent with the gradual incorporation of its value into prices. Overall, our findings suggest that psychological safety is an economically meaningful yet underappreciated organizational attribute in financial markets.

- title: Algorithmic Deprioritization Patterns in AI-Generated Content Distribution: The Marketing Agent Decay Model (MAD-M™) as a Predictive Framework
- authors: kristina shrider
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-26
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6213138
- keyword hits: large language model

### abstract

This research identifies the 60% saturation threshold that triggers accelerated visibility decay in AI-generated content across digital discovery layers. By extending the Marketing Agent Decay Model (MAD-M™), the paper provides a predictive framework for managing Narrative Entropy™ and algorithmic deprioritization in generative search (GEO). This paper examines the systematic reduction in content visibility across major digital platforms when AI-generated content is detected or exhibits AI-characteristic patterns. Building on prior work establishing the Marketing Agent Decay Model (MAD-M) as a heuristic for organizational narrative risk[1][2], we extend MAD-M to model cross-platform visibility trajectories and propose it as an early-warning modeling framework for scenario-based forecasting of multi-channel decay patterns in 2026 and beyond. Through integration of platform policy analysis, empirical traffic data, and longitudinal organizational case studies (N=450), we document a convergent pattern: once content is classified as AI-generated-whether through explicit labeling, detection systems, or behavioral signals-it experiences measurable deprioritization across social feeds, organic search, and AI-mediated discovery surfaces. We identify a critical inflection condition occurring when AI-generated content exceeds approximately 60% of total output volume, triggering accelerated visibility decay characterized by reduced initial reach, steeper engagement decline, and systematic exclusion from high-authority citation contexts such as AI overviews and large language model responses. The study contributes (1) extension of MAD-M from internal organizational sensemaking to external algorithmic distribution, (2) documentation of 2024-2026 platform policy shifts toward AI content governance, and (3) strategic guidance for creators, marketers, and platform operators navigating the emerging AI-native content ecosystem. Findings indicate that algorithmic deprioritization of AI-generated content is not a temporary moderation tactic but a structural feature of platform information architecture in 2026, with implications for digital marketing strategy, brand visibility, and equitable access to audience attention.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6423358`

- title: Effective Personalized AI Tutors via LLM-Guided Reinforcement Learning
- authors: Angel Tsai-Hsuan Chung, Botong Zhang, Ling-Chieh Kung, Hamsa Bastani, Osbert Bastani
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-31
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6423358
- keyword hits: generative ai, llm

### abstract

Generative AI (GenAI) is rapidly reshaping education by unlocking the potential for personalized tutoring. Yet, emerging platforms largely focus on GenAI chatbot tutors that reactively answer student questions. We hypothesize that the efficacy of GenAI chatbot tutors can be substantially improved by proactively guiding student learning. To test this, we design a novel tutoring platform that tightly integrates a carefully-designed GenAI chatbot with a reinforcement learning algorithm for sequencing practice problems. Critically, this algorithm leverages rich signals from student-chatbot interactions to adaptively select practice problems of an appropriate difficulty level. In partnership with the Taipei City Government and American Institute in Taiwan, we deployed our tutoring platform in conjunction with a five-month course to teach Python to students across ten high schools. We randomized students between a fixed practice problem sequence and our adaptive sequencing algorithm. We find that adaptive sequencing increased unassisted final exam performance by 0.15 standard deviations (equivalent to 6-9 months of schooling by some estimates); mediation analysis suggests that gains were driven by increased engagement. Our work provides large-scale field evidence that student-chatbot interactions provide valuable signals for proactively optimizing and personalizing student learning.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6479459`

- title: Generative AI and Investor Processing of Financial Media
- authors: Yang Ha Cho, Allen H. Huang, Joseph Pacelli, Kristina M. Rennekamp
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-03-31
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6479459
- keyword hits: generative ai

### abstract

We examine how AI-generated summaries in financial news influence investor information processing. Exploiting The Wall Street Journal's rollout of AI summaries, we find that articles featuring these summaries elicit stronger immediate market reactions, including higher trading volume and amplified price responses. Additional analyses show that the increased trading is concentrated in the content summarized by AI, but not in the content not summarized by AI. We investigate the underlying mechanism using a controlled experiment. Contrary to the concern that AI summaries encourage skimming, we find they increase both perceived confidence and objective recall of the full article text. These findings suggest that AI summaries do not substitute for reading but rather serve as interpretive cues, enhancing overall comprehension while directing investor attention toward highlighted elements. Cross-sectional analyses show that the effects of AIgenerated summaries are strongest in settings with greater information frictions. Finally, articles with AI-generated summaries are associated with significantly greater subsequent information search by both retail and institutional investors. Overall, our study documents how the integration of AI in financial media reshapes the consumption of firm news.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6430698`

- title: The Index Event Horizon: A Chiarella-bouchaud Model for Passive Investing and Market Stability
- authors: Mika Kastenholz
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6430698
- keyword hits: llama

### abstract

We extend the passive share model of Krishnan and Sturm [2025] and Green et al. [2025] in three directions, all motivated by the Black (1986) noise framework. First, we introduce endogenous noise amplification: as passive share p rises and fewer fundamentalists absorb noise, the effective CIR volatility ξ eff (p) grows, yielding an implicit Feller condition that advances the instability threshold from p ≈ 97% to p ≈ 91%. Second, we add trend-following to this extended CIR process while remaining in level space, yielding a generalised Feller condition that shows how even modest momentum trading (β = 0.03) shifts the worst-case (sustained uptrend) threshold down to p ≈ 45%-a level that, under baseline calibration, corresponds to approximately 2017. A probabilistic formulation using the ergodic distribution of the trend signal shows that the likelihood of Feller violation remains low (∼ 0.3%) at current passive share under typical conditions, rising sharply only beyond p ≈ 0.80 (∼2033 under baseline calibration), reconciling the deterministic worst case with the stochastic baseline. Third, we embed the Krishnan and Sturm passive share dynamics into a full Chiarella-Bouchaud agent-based framework, decomposing market volatility into fundamental (σ V ≈ 0.0143) and noise (σ N ≈ 0.0571) components using the empirical excess volatility ratio of Kurth et al. [2025a]. A four-model Monte Carlo comparison (10,000 paths per model, 2025-2085; 50,000 for the first-passage-time analysis) reveals a spectrum of instability: the pure CIR model produces zero-hitting in 42% of paths, endogenous noise raises this to 96%, trend-following pushes it to 98%, while the Chiarella model produces a qualitatively different failure mode: persistent directional mispricing that, under baseline calibration, violates Black's factor-of-two noise bound in 99% of paths by 2085, often under deceptively low realised volatility. We provide microstructural foundations for the endogenous noise specification by connecting to the square-root law of price impact, the Gabaix-Koijen inelasticity multiplier, and the "Ponzi flow" mechanism of van der Beck, Bouchaud & Villamaina (2024), which documents how flow-driven self-inflated returns create endogenous feedback spirals in ETFs. We reformulate the problem as a stopping time: the first passage of the mispricing process to Black's bound yields a stochastic criticality measure-the fragility index Ψ(t)-that unifies all four models under a single metric and functions as a leading indicator of the phase transition from efficient to structurally mispriced markets. Building on the cross-sectional variation in stock-level passive ownership, we propose a Passive Market Fragility (PMF) factor: a long-short portfolio sorted on stock-level Ψ i designed to capture the fragility premium that would arise if Ψ is a priced state variable in the ICAPM. We outline an empirical calibration strategy exploiting cross-sectional variation in stock-level passive ownership and index reconstitution events as quasi-experiments, and propose a simulated method of moments estimator for the full parameter vector.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6543998`

- title: Better Applications, Worse Matching: Artificial Intelligence and Talent Allocation
- authors: Thomas Jungbauer
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-08
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6543998
- keyword hits: generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) raises productivity inside jobs while reducing the informativeness of the screening outputs used to allocate workers across firms. In a stylized matching model, the market sorts efficiently on visible rank, but rank is an imperfect proxy for worker type. Aggregate output, decomposed into average worker output and a misallocation penalty, can therefore fall even when every worker-firm pair becomes more productive. The analysis predicts weaker screening validity, greater use of harder-to-fake assessments, socially excessive applicant AI use as well as greater reliance on probationary hiring and rematching. Empirical design suggestions to test these predictions are provided.

- title: AkaneCore: A Dialogue Control Architecture Centered on State and Responsibility
- authors: Toru Yamada
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-09
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6490259
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

This paper introduces AkaneCore, a dialogue control architecture designed to address the knowledge-action gap in large language models, where appropriate behavior is known but inconsistently applied. AkaneCore maintains consistent response behavior across long-term interactions by treating response generation as a process governed by explicit internal states. The architecture separates response control from response expression, incorporates persistent state variables such as relational phase and responsibility depth, and defines a structured control flow for policy selection. This architectural approach enforces behavioral consistency as a structural property rather than an emergent characteristic.
