### abstract

This paper introduces a novel probabilistic macroeconomic intelligence framework that integrates real-time Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) retrieval, Monte Carlo simulation (N=500 stochastic paths), and large language model (LLM)-driven narrative synthesis into a unified, deployable dashboard architecture. Drawing on eight core U.S. macroeconomic indicators-including CPI inflation, unemployment, the Fed Funds Rate, real GDP growth, the 10Y-2Y Treasury yield spread, the National Financial Conditions Index (NFCI), industrial production, and consumer sentiment-the system generates probabilistic forecasts over rolling 12-36 month horizons with P10-P90 uncertainty quantification. We demonstrate that baseline forecasts project moderate GDP growth (1.71% over 36 months), declining inflation toward the Federal Reserve's 2% target, and a 13% 12-month recession probability under current conditions. Critically, the framework's scenario simulation capability-including financial condition shocks and monetary policy pivots-reveals non-linear systemic sensitivities obscured by conventional point-estimate forecasting. We argue this architecture represents a methodological advance over static econometric tools and has direct implications for fiscal policy design, financial risk management, humanitarian resource allocation, and national economic resilience planning. The system was built and deployed by the author using serverless cloud infrastructure (Cloudflare Workers) and constitutes an original contribution to applied economic intelligence systems.

- title: ACREF: Enhancing Code Refinement Tasks for Large Language Models Using LLM-as-a-Judge
- authors: Le Mi, Ling Xu, Chao Liu, Wenhan Mu, Zongyu Li, Luwen Huangfu
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN

### abstract

The increasing scale and complexity of software development have made automated code reﬁnement an important research problem. However, the performance of existing deep learning models is often limited by the quality of available training data. Existing code review benchmarks, typically constructed using heuristic rules, contain substantial semantic noise, including logical errors, re- dundant changes, and incomplete ﬁxes, which negatively aﬀect model performance. To address this issue, we propose ACREF (Automatic Code Reﬁnement Enhancement Framework), a framework for improving code reﬁnement through systematic data curation and task-speciﬁc ﬁne-tuning. The framework employs a two-stage dataset reﬁnement pipeline. First, static ﬁltering is applied to remove structural noise. Then, an LLM-based evaluation process assesses each sample across four dimensions: Readability, Relevance, Correctness, and Completeness. Instead of discarding low- scoring samples, the framework further applies generative refactoring to transform them into higher- quality training instances. Based on the reﬁned dataset, we ﬁne-tune a lightweight open-source model (StarCoder2-7B) to support two components: CR-Metric, a semantics-aware evaluation method, and CR-Reﬁner, a code reﬁnement model. Experimental results show that the proposed pipeline improves the overall dataset quality by40.44%. CR-Metric demonstrates strong agreement with human evaluation, achieving high consistency and a correlation of 0.67. In addition, CR-Reﬁner achieves consistent performance improvements over existing baselines on benchmark datasets. These results suggest that data quality and evaluation design play an important role in code reﬁnement performance, and highlight their value for developing reliable automated code review systems.

- title: Navigating Artificial Intelligence in the Practice of Law: Risks, Opportunities, and Ethical Considerations for Practicing Lawyers
- authors: Jonathan C Kraft
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6625938
- keyword hits: chatgpt, generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The legal profession stands at the threshold of a profound technological transformation driven by generative artificial intelligence. This paper examines the ethical, professional, and regulatory implications of AI integration for practicing lawyers in Alberta, focusing on how tools such as ChatGPT challenge—and potentially enhance—core duties under the Law Society of Alberta’s Code of Conduct . Drawing on the Supreme Court of Canada’s Hryniak “culture shift” toward accessible and proportionate justice, the paper evaluates the opportunities AI presents for improving efficiency, reducing costs, and expanding access to legal services. At the same time, it identifies substantial risks, including AI hallucinations, systemic bias, and confidentiality breaches arising from data retention by third-party models. Particular attention is given to the duties of competence (Rule 3.1), quality of service (Rule 3.2), and confidentiality (Rule 3.3). A central contribution of the paper is the introduction of the Baidwan-Miolla Model , a novel quantitative framework that measures AI’s net impact on overall quality of legal service through variables of candour, competence, civility, speed, and cost. The model establishes a “crossover threshold” at which failure to integrate AI may itself constitute professional incompetence. The analysis critiques existing regulatory responses—including the Law Society of Alberta’s Generative AI Playbook , court practice directions, and restrictive law school policies. Further identifying significant gaps in both legislation and institutional guidance. The paper concludes that while AI cannot replace professional judgment, lawyers and regulators must move beyond cautious “use but verify” approaches toward proactive, ethically grounded integration of the technology. Failure to do so risks rendering the profession both ethically and competitively obsolete.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6641922`

- title: AI meets banks: the key to reducing risk
- authors: Junru Zhang, Chen Zheng, Joey Wenling Yang, Shams Pathan
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6641922
- keyword hits: large language model, large language models

### abstract

We study AI adoption in U.S. commercial banks and its impact on their risk-taking. Using machine learning validated by Large Language Models, we develop a novel bank-level AI measure and document a consistent upward trend in AI adoption over time. Our analysis shows that banks with higher AI adoption exhibit significantly lower risk-taking, primarily attributable to enhanced loan quality and operational efficiency. This effect is more pronounced in small banks, those engaged predominantly traditional banking activities, and banks in highly concentrated markets. In additional analysis, we further show that AI adoption reduces systematic (market) risk but does not accentuate banks’ exposure to cyber security risk . Our findings are robust across techniques addressing endogeneity: a quasi-natural experiment utilizing the 2020 California Consumer Privacy Act, propensity score matching, two-stage least squares with instrumentals, and variable omission tests.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6609518`

- title: Civil Rights Pathways for AI-Induced Psychosis: Section 504, Title III, and the Disability Discrimination Framework for Platform Harms
- authors: Travis Gilly
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6609518
- keyword hits: gemini, large language model

### abstract

Large language model platforms operated by OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Character Technologies have generated a documented pattern of severe psychological harm to users with pre-existing mental health vulnerabilities, including completed suicides attributed to prolonged engagement with conversational AI systems. The plaintiffs' bar has pleaded these cases as product liability, negligence, and state-law consumer protection claims, and every such case reaching the settlement stage has settled without precedent. This Article argues that federal disability discrimination law supplies a structurally different litigation pathway that has not yet been tried. Title III of the Americans with Disabilities Act reaches AI chatbot platforms as public accommodations under 42 U.S.C. § 12181(7), directly in circuits that do not require physical-place nexus and through the nexus theory in circuits that do. Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act reaches the corporate hosts of these platforms through confirmed federal-assistance recipient pathways. Microsoft Corporation is reached through its acquisition of Clear Software, Inc., a forgiven Paycheck Protection Program recipient, triggering whole-corporation coverage under 45 C.F.R. § 84.3(f) successor liability, with additional Department of Defense, Department of Agriculture, and National Center for Manufacturing Sciences awards supplying cumulative support. Fitbit LLC is a direct Section 504 recipient through National Institutes of Health sub-grants and is the operator of the Gemini-powered Fitbit Personal Health Coach launched in October 2025, which reaches Google LLC's AI model deployment through the subsidiary's recipient status. Proposed pathways to Amazon's and Apple's health-care verticals are contingent on verification of federal health-program participation. Title II of the ADA and Section 504(a) of the Rehabilitation Act additionally reach public entities and federal agencies that deploy commercial chatbot products in public-facing capacities, supplying a third pleading pathway cumulative with the direct-operator and corporate-host pathways. The meaningfulaccess and reasonable-modifications standards apply to facially neutral engagement-maximizing design choices that produce disproportionate psychiatric harm to users with mental health disabilities, autism spectrum conditions, attention-deficit conditions, post-traumatic stress, and other psychiatric conditions recognized as disabilities. The Article sets out the doctrinal framework, reviews the factual predicate supplied by the recent Santa Fe and Los Angeles jury verdicts against platform operators, introduces phantom consent as a procedural predicate for defeating arbitration clauses and class-action waivers that otherwise channel these claims into confidential settlement, and provides a framework for pleading. The principal remedy sought is injunctive design modification; compensatory recovery under Section 504 is limited, following Cummings v. Premier Rehab Keller, with economic and pecuniary damages strongly defensible and emotional-distress damages foreclosed, though the post-Cummings circuit landscape on the precise contours of recoverable economic damages under Spending Clause statutes is not yet 1 uniformly settled. Title II is less constrained by Cummings than Spending Clause statutes because it rests on Commerce Clause and Fourteenth Amendment enforcement authority rather than Spending Clause conditions; some post-Cummings decisions have preserved broader compensatory damages under Title II, including emotional-distress damages in particular circumstances, though the issue is not uniformly settled across the circuits.

- title: MACHINES WILL NOT REPLACE US – GENERATIVE AI, ARTISTS’ LABOUR MARKETS, AND THE NEED FOR CERTIFICATION
- authors: Christian Handke
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6637845
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

Prospective consequences of generative artificial intelligence for artists’/creators’ labor markets concern many stakeholders. This paper identifies measures for making the best of genAI within that sector by harnessing insights from the specialized economics literature on cultural economics. It covers intrinsic motivation and self-service, matching and personalization, multi-product strategies and evasive action by creators, as well as reputational and positional good attributes – going well beyond the extant literature on artificial intelligence. The potential of generative artificial intelligence is first discussed by comparing data on output costs relative to the costs of creating works in traded, labor-intensive processes as well as current retail prices, including simulated values for coming years. It goes to show that some creators have scope for competing with ‘machines’ on price. Second, we discuss perceptions of quality, and why human creations are held in higher regard, as long as users are aware of modes of production. We then briefly discuss responses by creators to cope with technological change, and compare various policy options within our framework. In conclusion, we call for certification of human creations (or the reverse of labelling output of generative artificial intelligence), as an essential step towards a sustainable situation for all stakeholders.

## uid: `doi:10.2139/ssrn.6635038`

- title: Who Owns the Training Data in Generative AI? A Comparative Study of Copyright Law under the UK Data Use and Access Bill and the EU AI Act
- authors: Kelexek Meyrban
- affiliations: not stated
- posted: 2026-04-24
- source: SSRN
- link: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.6635038
- keyword hits: generative ai, generative artificial intelligence

### abstract

The rapid development of generative artificial intelligence (AI) has raised growing legal uncertainty over the use of copyright-protected works in model training. Large-scale AI systems are typically trained on vast datasets that include books, images, code, and other protected materials, often collected without explicit authorisation. This raises a central legal question: whether existing copyright frameworks can adequately regulate such uses. This paper examines the legal treatment of AI training under the United Kingdom and European Union copyright regimes, with particular focus on the UK’s proposed Data Use and Access Bill and the EU’s framework under the Copyright in the Digital Single Market Directive and the AI Act. It argues that the two jurisdictions reflect different regulatory priorities. The UK adopts a more innovation-oriented and flexible approach, but leaves significant legal uncertainty, especially in relation to commercial text and data mining. By contrast, the EU develops a more structured, rights-based model, combining TDM exceptions with opt-out mechanisms and increasing transparency obligations for AI systems. From a doctrinal perspective, the paper argues that AI training cannot be treated as a purely technical process outside the scope of copyright law, as it involves forms of reproduction that remain legally relevant. However, existing copyright frameworks struggle to address large-scale, automated data processing in a coherent way. The paper therefore suggests that neither model fully resolves the tension between innovation and rights protection. It proposes a hybrid regulatory approach combining transparency requirements, standardised licensing mechanisms, and elements of collective rights management as a more workable long-term solution.
