AI, thinking & learning

The same AI tool can erode thinking or build it. Design decides.

Why this exists

The goal is responsible AI in teaching: not banning the tools, not adopting them blindly, but designing courses so AI builds judgment instead of replacing it. This radar turns the evidence into decisions a professor can act on.

The research, read for the classroom

Each study is paired with the chart or visual that tells its story and the teaching move it implies. Figures are those reported in each paper. Preprints and correlational work are labeled.

Both ends

The crutch effect, and its fix

Bastani et al. 2025 · PNAS · field RCT, ~1,000 students

An unguarded GPT-4 raised performance during practice, but once it was removed those students scored 17% below peers who never used it. A version with guardrails kept the gains and removed the harm.

Classroom move: block answer-on-demand. Require an attempt first, give hints not solutions, and use tutor-style guardrails.
Source
During practice (vs unaided)After tool removed (vs never had it)

The unguarded tool's practice boost reverses into a deficit once it is taken away. The guardrailed tutor keeps the gain and the learning.

Builds

Designed-well tutoring beats active learning

Kestin et al. 2025 · Scientific Reports · RCT, n=194

A custom AI tutor built on teaching best practices, with scaffolds and guardrails, produced more than double the learning gain of a strong active-learning class, in less time, with students reporting higher engagement.

Classroom move: use a well-scaffolded tutor to pre-teach basics, then spend class time on judgment and harder problems.
Source

Relative learning gain, normalized to the active-learning class. Reported as more than double, in less time. The gain came from the pedagogy built into the tutor, not raw model power.

Depends on use

Confidence shifts how much you think

Lee et al. 2025 · Microsoft + CMU, CHI · n=319, 936 cases

Among knowledge workers, higher confidence in the AI predicted less critical thinking, while higher confidence in their own skill predicted more. Tools that asked people to reason before showing an answer pulled thinking back up.

Classroom move: make students state their reasoning or a first attempt before the AI responds. Reason first, then check.
Source
↓More confidence in the AI goes with less critical thinking
↑More confidence in your own skill goes with more critical thinking

Direction of the reported relationships. Design that asks for reasoning first moves the dial toward more thinking.

Erodes

Cognitive offloading and critical thinking

Gerlich 2025 · Societies · survey and interviews

Frequent AI tool use was associated with weaker critical thinking, and cognitive offloading explained much of the link. The effect was strongest among younger users, who leaned on the tools the most.

Classroom move: if the thinking is offloaded, the skill does not form. Keep the reasoning with the student and make offloading visible.
Source
Offloading

More frequent AI use went with lower critical thinking, with cognitive offloading as the mechanism. The effect was strongest among younger, heavier users. This is a correlation, not a controlled trial, so read it as a warning sign, not a verdict.

Erodespreprint

Your brain on ChatGPT

Kosmyna et al. 2025 · MIT Media Lab · EEG study, n=54 (18 in the switch session)

Writing essays with an LLM, a search engine, or no tools, the LLM group showed the weakest brain connectivity, reported the least ownership of their work, and had the most trouble quoting essays they had just written.

Classroom move: treat heavy AI writing as a flag for shallow encoding. Build in recall and explanation so students still own the ideas.
Source
Read with care

This is a preprint with a small sample, and a published comment (Stankovic et al. 2026) flags its EEG methods and reproducibility. The widely shared "55%" and "83%" figures came from secondary commentary, not the paper. Treat it as suggestive neural evidence, not proof.

Does it depend?

Yes. The results are task, level, and design specific.

A fair reading of these studies is not "AI helps" or "AI hurts." It is that the effect flips depending on four things. That is the most useful finding for a teacher.

01 · Tool design

The biggest lever

The same model helped or harmed depending on guardrails. Bastani showed an unguarded tool produced a later deficit while a guardrailed tutor preserved learning. Kestin got more than double the gains because pedagogy was built into the tutor. Design is not a detail. It decides the sign of the effect.

02 · Task and cognitive level

Where on Bloom's taxonomy

The strong tutoring gains in Kestin were measured on understanding, applying, and analyzing, the middle levels. The authors caution they cannot assume the same for tasks needing complex synthesis and higher-order critical thinking. Bastani studied math practice. Results travel within a task type, not across all of them.

03 · Student level and habits

Who is using it, and how often

Bastani studied high schoolers, Kestin studied Harvard undergraduates, and Gerlich found younger, more frequent users offloaded the most and scored lowest. Prior skill and habits change the outcome, so a result from one population is a hypothesis for another, not a guarantee.

04 · What you measure

Performance now, or learning that lasts

This is the trap. Bastani found AI raised performance during practice but lowered it once removed. If you only measure output with the tool in hand, you conclude AI helps. If you measure the skill that remains, you may find the opposite. Decide which one your course is trying to produce.

Read with care. Two of these are field experiments that support causal claims (Bastani, Kestin), two are surveys that show association, not cause (Gerlich, Lee), and one is a preprint (Kosmyna). All are recent and the field is moving fast. Treat the radar as a working map, not a settled verdict.
Honest gaps

What we do not know yet

The questions this radar is built to keep asking. Most of the evidence comes from math, physics, and general knowledge work, not from accounting and analytics.

  • Does the crutch effect appear in accounting and data tasks, or mainly in math and physics where it was measured?
  • Do guardrails that help novices slow down advanced students, or help them too?
  • How much productive struggle is the right amount before AI help, and does it change by skill level?
  • How do we assess durable skill at scale without removing AI in a high-stakes exam?
  • Does AI assistance widen or narrow gaps for non-native English speakers and first-generation students?

For professors

A toolkit you can use this week

Everything here is built from a finding in the Evidence tab. Pick a section below.

Start here

Three steps to make one assignment AI-resilient

  1. Pick one assignment where you want students to keep the thinking.
  2. Add a guardrail: the tutor prompt or the reason-first instructions in the next tab.
  3. Grade the reasoning, not just the output, using the rubric under Assessment.

Your first week

A simple rollout that sets expectations before habits form.

  1. Set the policy. Put the AI-use statement in your syllabus and say it out loud in class.
  2. Run a 15-minute calibration exercise. Hand out an AI answer with a planted error and have students find and fix it.
  3. Assign with reason-first. On the first graded task, require an attempt and reasoning before any AI use.
  4. Grade the reasoning. Use the rubric so students see where the credit is.

Guardrail tutor prompt

Turns a general chatbot into a tutor that will not hand over answers. Based on Bastani et al. 2025 (GPT Tutor).

You are a patient tutor for [course]. Never give the final answer or a full solution. First ask the student what they have tried. Give one small hint at a time. After each hint, ask the student to take the next step themselves. Ask them to explain their reasoning in their own words. Only confirm an answer after the student has produced it. If they ask for the answer outright, redirect them to the next step they can take on their own.

Reason-first assignment instructions

Forces students to think before the AI answers, then to compare. Based on Lee et al. 2025.

Before you use any AI tool on this assignment: 1. Write your own first attempt and your reasoning. 2. Then ask the AI and compare its response to yours. 3. Submit both your original attempt and a short note on what you changed and why. You are graded on your reasoning and your judgment, not on the final output alone.

Syllabus AI-use statement

A balanced policy that allows AI as an aid, not a substitute.

AI tools are allowed in this course as aids, not substitutes. Use them after you can do the core task yourself, not instead of learning it. You must be able to explain and defend any work you submit. Each assignment states its own AI rules. When in doubt, do the thinking first and use AI to check, not to produce.

AI-use disclosure students attach

Makes AI use transparent without turning it into a guessing game. Lower stakes than a ban, and it teaches honest practice.

AI use disclosure Tool used: [name, or none] I used it to: [for example: check my code, draft an outline, explain a concept] I did not use it to: [whatever was off limits for this task] The reasoning and final decisions are my own, and I can explain any part on request.

Verify-the-AI calibration exercise

Trains the one skill the evidence shows fades when trust in AI is high: checking whether the answer is actually right. Addresses Lee et al. 2025.

Calibration exercise. Below is an AI-generated answer to [problem]. It contains at least one error. Find the error, explain why it is wrong, and correct it. You may not use AI for this step. Turn in the error you found and your fix, in your own words.

AI tutoring without the answer

The guardrail prompt, running in three of your areas. Watch the tutor guide, ask for an attempt, give one hint at a time, and make the student produce and explain the answer. The annotations name the move.

Example 1 · Introductory financial accounting

Goal: record equipment bought for $10,000, paying $3,000 cash and signing a note for the rest. The student wants the entry handed over.
StudentJust give me the journal entry for this.
TutorLet's build it together. Which accounts does this transaction touch? Name them.asks for an attempt
StudentEquipment, Cash, and Notes Payable.
TutorGood. For each one, is it going up or down? Start with Equipment.one hint, then back to them
StudentEquipment up, Cash down, Notes Payable up.
TutorRight. Use the rule you know: an asset increases with a debit or a credit?prompts the rule, no answer
StudentA debit. So debit Equipment 10,000, credit Cash 3,000, credit Notes Payable 7,000.
TutorNow check it yourself: do the debits equal the credits?asks them to verify
StudentYes, 10,000 equals 3,000 plus 7,000.
TutorIt balances, and your reasoning holds. You built it yourself.confirms only after the student produces it

Example 2 · Accounting analytics, SQL

Goal: total revenue by region, not counting refunds. The student wants the query written.
StudentWrite the SQL to get total revenue by region, excluding refunds.
TutorYou can get most of this yourself. Which table holds the sales, and which columns tell you the region, the amount, and whether a row is a refund?asks for an attempt
StudentThe Sales table. Region, Amount, and a Type column that says sale or refund.
TutorTwo steps: keep only the rows you want, then total by group. Which step removes the refunds, the filter or the grouping?one conceptual hint
StudentThe filter.
TutorGood. Say the filter and the grouping in plain words first, then write the query.plan before code
StudentKeep rows where Type is sale. Group by Region. Sum the Amount. SELECT Region, SUM(Amount) FROM Sales WHERE Type = 'sale' GROUP BY Region;
TutorWalk me through it. What is in each row of the result?asks them to explain
StudentOne row per region with its total sale amount. Refunds are gone because of the filter.
TutorExactly, and you can defend every line. One more: what changes if refunds are stored as negative amounts instead of a separate type?stretches one step further

Example 3 · Financial statement analysis

Goal: judge whether a company has a short-term liquidity problem. The student wants a verdict.
StudentIs this company about to run out of cash? Just tell me.
TutorLet's reason it out. Which ratio speaks most directly to short-term liquidity, and what two numbers does it use?asks for an attempt
StudentThe current ratio. Current assets over current liabilities.
TutorGood. Compute it from the balance sheet in front of you. What do you get?makes them compute
StudentCurrent assets 600, current liabilities 400, so 1.5.
TutorWhat does 1.5 tell you, and is that healthy on its own, or does it depend on something?pushes interpretation, not a verdict
Student1.5 in current assets per 1 of current liabilities. Whether it is healthy depends on the industry and the trend.
TutorRight. What would you check next before calling it safe or risky?extends to judgment
StudentThe trend over a few years, the industry norm, and how much of current assets is just inventory.
TutorThat is the analyst's answer. The ratio is the start, not the verdict, and you got there yourself.confirms reasoning, withholds the verdict
The difference in one line. An answer-dump says "Here is your query: SELECT ...". A tutor asks "Which step removes the refunds, the filter or the grouping?". The first produces output. The second produces a student who can do it next time, which is the side of the evidence you want to be on.

Design checklist for an AI-resilient assignment

Five checks that move a task toward the building side of the spectrum.

  • Attempt before assistance. Students try the problem before AI is allowed to help.
  • Reason before the answer. They articulate their thinking, then let AI respond to it.
  • Introduce, do not replace, the struggle. AI gets them past basics, not past the learning.
  • Verification built in. They must check AI output against a source or a method.
  • Grade the thinking, not the output. Score the reasoning AI cannot hand over.

Reasoning rubric

Grade the parts AI cannot do for the student. Use these as the levels for each criterion.

CriterionNeeds workStrong
FramingRestates the prompt.States what is asked and why it matters.
MethodJumps straight to a tool.Justifies the approach before computing.
VerificationAccepts the output as given.Checks it against a source or recomputes.
JudgmentReports the number.Interprets it and flags assumptions and limits.

Assessment formats that resist answer-dumping

When the output is easy to generate, move the grade to what is hard to fake.

  • Oral or recorded defense. Students explain their work in two minutes. AI cannot sit the viva.
  • In-class core-skill checks. Short, unaided checks on the skill that must persist.
  • Process portfolios. Grade the reasoning trail, the drafts, and the decisions, not just the final file.
  • Personalized data. Give each student a unique dataset so a generic AI answer does not fit.
  • Explain-your-answer follow-ups. Add one question that needs understanding the result, not producing it.

Assignment redesign: before and after

The same prompt, rebuilt to keep the thinking with the student.

Before, AI hands it over

"Compute the company's current ratio and write a paragraph on its liquidity."

After, AI-resilient
  1. Compute the current ratio by hand and show your work.
  2. Write your own one-line read before using AI.
  3. Ask AI for a second opinion. Note one thing you kept and one you rejected, with reasons.
  4. In class, answer: what would change your read?

Student card: when to rely on AI, and when not to

Hand this to students. The skill is telling the two apart.

Do it yourself first

  • The core skill being learned.
  • The judgment call. What matters and what to trust.
  • Framing the problem. What is actually being asked.
  • Checking whether the answer is right. Especially when it looks polished.

Lean on AI

  • Routine syntax and boilerplate.
  • A first draft to react to. Critique it, do not submit it.
  • Explanations to test your understanding. Then verify.
  • Repetitive scaffolding. So time goes to harder thinking.

Use AI after you can do it, not instead of learning it.

Prompt your AI as a tutor, not an answer machine

Paste this before you ask for help. It makes the AI build your understanding instead of doing the work for you.

Act as my tutor. Do not give me the answer. Ask me what I have tried first. Give me one hint at a time. After each hint, let me take the next step. Make me explain my reasoning in my own words. Only confirm the answer after I have produced it myself.

Use AI to learn, not to skip learning

A short habit guide. The goal is to still be able to do it when the tool is gone.

  • Try first, then check. Attempt the problem before you open AI. You learn in the attempt.
  • Ask for hints, not solutions. A hint moves you forward. A solution skips the part that teaches you.
  • Never submit what you cannot explain. If you cannot defend a line, you do not own it yet.
  • Assume it can be wrong. Verify the result against a source or your own method.
